1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 25 as Trump sends 15-point proposal amid continued bombing

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran entered its fourth week with contradictory signals: Trump announced a five-day pause on striking Iranian energy infrastructure after Iran offered a concession tied to the Strait of Hormuz, and the New York Times reported Washington sent Tehran a 15-point diplomatic proposal. Iran denied any direct talks are underway. Meanwhile, the Pentagon is preparing a qualitative military escalation — approximately 1,000 soldiers from the 82nd Airborne Division’s Immediate Response Force are deploying alongside 4,500 Marines already en route, reportedly tasked with seizing Kharg Island, which handles 90% of Iran’s oil exports.

The humanitarian toll continues to mount. Cumulative casualties stand at 1,230 killed in Iran, over 290 in Lebanon, 11 in Israel, and 6 US troops killed. An Iranian ballistic missile carrying approximately 100 kg of explosives evaded US/Israeli air defenses and struck a Tel Aviv residential neighborhood, wounding four — the IDF cited a 92% shoot-down rate and called the failure “not systemic.” According to HRANA, at least 1,443 Iranian civilian deaths have been documented, including 217 children, with a school in Minab struck killing 170. Iran’s telecommunications infrastructure has been destroyed, isolating 90 million people for over three weeks.

Key facts: - Netanyahu: “We are pounding the missile program and the nuclear program… Just a few days ago, we eliminated two more nuclear scientists — and more actions are expected” - Defense Secretary Hegseth: “We negotiate with bombs” — exposing ceasefire rhetoric as cover for escalation - Mohsen Rezaei (IRGC senior adviser): Iran will fight until “all economic sanctions are lifted, and legally binding international guarantees are obtained to prevent US interference in Iran” - Trump’s approval rating has collapsed to 36%, with 61% opposing the strikes - Senator Chris Murphy described insider trading linked to the war as “mind-blowing corruption” — approximately $580 million in oil futures were placed 15 minutes before Trump announced the strike pause

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, the 82nd Airborne deployment represents a shift “from expeditionary operations to a ground-seizure capability,” while Kevin Reed documented evidence that precisely timed bets on oil futures and prediction markets preceded Trump’s military announcements by minutes, pointing to criminal insider trading at the highest levels of government.

4. Colombian military C-130 crash kills 69

A US-donated C-130H Hercules — 43 years old, transferred to Colombia in 2020 — crashed 1.5 km from takeoff at Puerto Leguizamo, Putumayo, on March 23. Of the 126 people aboard, 69 were killed and 57 wounded. Onboard ammunition detonated and set the aircraft ablaze; no enemy action is suspected. It is the deadliest Colombian Air Force crash in decades.

Key facts: - President Petro blamed predecessor Iván Duque for accepting “extremely expensive gifts” from the US — aircraft where “maintenance costs more than a new plane” - Defense Minister Pedro Sánchez confirmed the ammunition detonation caused the fire - Former president Duque called Petro “vile and unintelligent” in response - A separate C-130 crash in Bolivia the previous month killed 20+, raising broader questions about aging US military hardware distributed across Latin America

Explosion rocks Valero’s Port Arthur, Texas refinery

A catastrophic explosion at Valero’s 125-year-old Port Arthur refinery — one of the ten largest US refineries, processing 435,000 barrels per day — triggered secondary fires and forced emergency shelter-in-place orders across the coastal city on Monday evening. The failure originated in the diesel hydrotreater unit, which produces highly toxic hydrogen sulfide, and cascaded into the fluid catalytic cracking unit, resulting in a total facility shutdown with complete loss of water and steam utilities.

Key facts: - No casualties were reported, but the explosion follows a pattern of major refinery disasters: a Chevron blast in Los Angeles in October 2025 - Despite the disaster, Valero’s stock rose approximately 2% the following day - Valero has a documented history of serial safety violations at Port Arthur spanning 2007–2015, including worker deaths and hospitalizations - Trump has eliminated Chemical Safety Board funding, removing the independent body that investigates such incidents

US measles cases surge past 1,487

As of March 19, 1,487 confirmed measles cases have been reported across 32 US jurisdictions in 2026, with 14 new outbreaks declared. Ninety-four percent of cases are outbreak-associated. The full year 2025 saw 2,285 total cases, and 2026 is on pace to far exceed that figure. The Pan American Health Organization has confirmed the Americas have lost their measles-free status, and the US’s own elimination status will be formally reassessed this year. The resurgence is directly linked to declining vaccination rates, amplified by anti-vaccine rhetoric from figures in the Trump administration including HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.

Sources CDC Measles cases and outbreaks — updated March 20, 2026 · CDC CDC reinforces national measles response

H5N1 bird flu confirmed in California marine mammals for first time

Scientists confirmed H5N1 avian influenza in 27 northern elephant seals, 2 California sea lions, and 1 southern sea otter in San Mateo County as of March 16 — the first detection in California’s marine mammal population. In Washington State, approximately 2.2 million birds have tested positive and 14 farmworkers have tested presumptively positive since 2022. The spread into new marine mammal species mirrors the die-offs that killed hundreds of southern elephant seals in Argentina in 2023, raising concerns about mass mortality events and ongoing mammalian adaptation of the virus.

Sources Marine Mammal Center Avian influenza confirmed in northern elephant seals · The Columbian What to know about bird flu in Washington

Nature Medicine: 40% of cancers attributable to modifiable risk factors

A major study published in Nature Medicine, analyzing cancer incidence data from 185 countries, found that 40% of cancers globally are attributable to modifiable risk factors — smoking, obesity, alcohol consumption, and others — with substantial regional and sex-specific variation. The findings quantify a massive, actionable prevention opportunity and provide a roadmap for targeted population-level interventions by region. The study underscores the contradiction between the scientific knowledge base for cancer prevention and the profit-driven healthcare systems that fail to implement it.

Sources Nature Medicine Global cancer burden attributable to modifiable risk factors

NEJM: once-monthly obesity drug shows promise

A once-monthly obesity medication could substantially improve adherence and expand access in the rapidly growing GLP-1 drug market.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — continued promotion of the Democratic Party as a vehicle for socialist politics through DSA electoral campaigns and sympathetic coverage of progressive Democrats
  • PSL / Liberation News — campist orientation on the Iran war, defending Iranian sovereignty without articulating an independent working-class line against both US imperialism and the Iranian bourgeois-clerical regime
  • Left Voice — more critical of class collaboration than other tendencies, but limited by absence of a revolutionary international program; analytical coverage of the Iran war that lacks programmatic conclusions
  • SWP (UK) — rhetorical anti-Labourism without independent party-building; anti-war rhetoric that avoids a programmatic break from the trade union bureaucracy

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin continues to promote the Democratic Party as the operational vehicle for “socialist” politics. The tendency’s output highlights DSA-endorsed candidates and progressive Democratic officeholders as models for the left, effectively channeling socialist sentiment back into the two-party system. Coverage of figures like Brad Lander and Shahana Mamdani frames the DSA’s electoral strategy — running candidates within the Democratic primary — as proof that “democratic socialism” can transform bourgeois institutions from within. This approach systematically avoids the central question: the Democratic Party is a party of Wall Street and the military-industrial complex, and no amount of primary challenges will alter its class character.

The Florida special election result — Emily Gregory’s victory in Trump’s own district — is treated in DSA-adjacent circles as vindication of electoral engagement, reinforcing the illusion that voting for Democrats represents meaningful opposition to the right. Absent from this framework is any analysis of the Democrats’ complicity in the Iran war, their support for the military budget, or their role in the social conditions that produced Trump’s base.

The overall orientation remains that of left-liberal pressure politics within the Democratic Party apparatus, fundamentally opposed to the independent political mobilization of the working class.

Sources Jacobin Recent coverage of DSA electoral campaigns

PSL / Liberation News

Liberation News covers the Iran war from a campist perspective — opposing US military aggression (correctly) but declining to articulate an independent working-class position against the Iranian bourgeois-clerical regime. The tendency’s coverage emphasizes Iranian sovereignty and frames Tehran’s resistance to US imperialism sympathetically, while omitting the regime’s suppression of the Iranian working class, its execution of political prisoners, and its violent repression of the 2022 Woman, Life, Freedom uprising. This one-sided framework — defending the sovereignty of capitalist states targeted by US imperialism without addressing their own class character — is the hallmark of campism. It substitutes geopolitical alignment for class analysis and leaves workers in Iran without any political perspective independent of their own ruling class.

The PSL’s broader approach — large demonstration-oriented activism combined with uncritical defense of states in conflict with the US — continues to function as a pressure group on the Democratic Party’s left flank rather than a revolutionary organization.

Sources Liberation News Recent Iran war coverage

Left Voice

Left Voice published “Trump at a Crossroads: No Regime Change, No Exit Strategy in Iran,” translated from the PTS’s La Izquierda Diario, analyzing Trump’s strategic bind — unable to achieve regime change, unable to exit the conflict. The piece is more analytical than most pseudo-left output on the war, correctly identifying the contradictions in US strategy. A second article on French electoral results (“Socialists Win Big in France”) merits scrutiny for whether Left Voice critically assesses the class content of the “socialist” victory or celebrates it uncritically — a persistent tension in the tendency’s coverage of European elections.

Left Voice remains the most critical of class collaboration among the tendencies reviewed, regularly denouncing the Democrats and the trade union bureaucracy. However, its criticism is limited by the absence of a revolutionary international program — the PTS/FT tendency opposes the ICFI’s perspective on the world party of socialist revolution and substitutes a network model that lacks the theoretical coherence and organizational discipline necessary for the tasks posed by the current period.

Sources Left Voice Trump at a crossroads: No regime change, no exit strategy in Iran

SWP (UK)

The SWP maintains a posture of rhetorical opposition to both the Labour government and the Iran war, but without the political content that would give this opposition a class character. The tendency calls demonstrations, publishes anti-war statements, and denounces Starmer — but proposes no independent political alternative to Labour, no break from the trade union bureaucracy that funds and sustains Labour, and no revolutionary program for the working class. The SWP’s anti-war activity functions within the framework of protest politics: applying moral pressure to bourgeois governments rather than mobilizing workers for political power.

The contrast with the SEP (UK)’s intervention at the Together Alliance march — which explicitly argued that fighting fascism requires opposing the war and breaking with Labour — exposes the SWP’s failure to connect anti-fascism, anti-war politics, and the struggle for socialism into a coherent program.

Sources Socialist Worker Recent coverage

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The IMT’s recently rebranded Revolutionary Communist Party continues its decades-long orientation toward Labour entryism and trade union bureaucracy politics, now dressed in more radical language. Despite adopting the “RCP” name and publishing through communist.red, the tendency’s practical orientation remains unchanged: working within Labour structures, orienting toward trade union officials, and treating the existing labor movement apparatus as the vehicle for socialist transformation rather than as an obstacle to it.

On the Iran war, the IMT produces anti-war content that frames the conflict primarily through the lens of British domestic politics — what it means for Labour, how it affects the trade unions — rather than developing an international perspective rooted in the independent mobilization of the working class across national borders. The tendency’s nationalism, disguised as “practical” orientation toward existing organizations, fundamentally limits its capacity to respond to the international crisis now unfolding.

President Petro’s accusation that US “gifts” drain national budgets invites a broader WSWS examination of US military-industrial relationships with Latin America — how “aid” functions as economic subordination and how the human cost falls on soldiers from working-class and indigenous communities.

While global media focuses on Iran, the Sudanese civil war continues to produce catastrophic civilian casualties with total impunity. An airstrike on a Darfur hospital killed 64 people including 13 children — a war crime by any definition. The absence of international sanctions or pressure on the warring parties reflects a deliberate hierarchy of concern tied to Western geopolitical interests. The WSWS should connect this to its longstanding analysis of imperialist neglect of African conflicts.

The Iran war is providing a live demonstration of this logic to every government that watched the US decision to strike. A WSWS article could trace the proliferation implications across multiple regions.

The New Mexico jury verdict — the first holding Meta liable for acts committed on its own platform — opens a new legal front against the tech monopolies. The WSWS should analyze this through the lens of the profit motive that drives platforms to maximize engagement regardless of harm, the inadequacy of individual lawsuits as a mechanism for accountability, and the need for democratic, public control over social media infrastructure rather than dependence on either corporate self-regulation or the capitalist courts.
