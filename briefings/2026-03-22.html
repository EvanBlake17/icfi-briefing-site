1. US-Israel war on Iran enters week four as Trump threatens to “obliterate” power plants

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its fourth week on March 22 with no ceasefire in sight and escalating threats from both sides. Trump issued a 48-hour ultimatum threatening to “obliterate” Iranian power plants if the Strait of Hormuz is not fully reopened. Iran responded by warning of retaliatory strikes on regional energy and water infrastructure.

The death toll continues to mount. At least 1,444 Iranians have been killed since the war began on February 28, including 204 children. CENTCOM reports that more than 8,000 Iranian targets have been struck — an average of over 360 per day. An additional 2,200–2,500 US Marines are being deployed to the region, even as Trump floats the possibility of “winding down” operations.

Iran announced its 70th wave of attacks, comprising nine separate missile salvoes toward Israel plus two ballistic missiles fired at Diego Garcia, the US-UK base in the Indian Ocean. Iranian missiles struck towns near Dimona and Arad — in proximity to Israel’s nuclear complex — wounding at least 180 people. Israeli air defenses intercepted most incoming fire, but falling debris caused widespread damage across central and northern Israel.

Key developments: - Saudi Arabia expelled Iran’s military attaché and four embassy staff within 24 hours, following Iranian drone strikes on the Red Sea port of Yanbu - The UK has allowed US military use of its bases on Diego Garcia for strikes on Iran - The US Embassy in Baghdad issued an emergency order urging all American citizens to leave Iraq immediately

2. Oil surges past $112/barrel as Strait of Hormuz approaches closure

Brent crude hit $112.19/barrel — a roughly 45% rise since the war began on February 28 — as the Strait of Hormuz, through which approximately 20% of the world’s oil and LNG passes, approaches near-total closure. Iran’s Supreme Leader declared the strait should remain closed, and Kuwait and Iraq have declared force majeure on oil shipments.

Goldman Sachs warned that prices could remain above $100/barrel through 2027 and spike to $200/barrel in a worst-case scenario. There have been 21 confirmed attacks on commercial vessels and offshore infrastructure since March 1.

Key facts: - Iran struck Kuwait’s Mina al-Ahmadi refinery (730,000 bbl/day capacity), causing a major fire - The Trump administration lifted sanctions on Iranian oil already loaded on tankers and separately eased sanctions on Russia and Venezuela to unlock alternative supply - The EU energy commissioner warned of gas price spikes amid storage concerns - The Panama Canal is operating at capacity (36–38 vessels/day) due to surging LNG tanker demand

3. Israel opens ground operations in Lebanon; one million displaced

Israel launched ground operations in southern Lebanon on March 16, following Hezbollah attacks that began March 2. At least 1,001 people have been killed in Lebanon since the renewed assault, including more than 118 children. Nearly one million people have been displaced — approximately 19% of Lebanon’s population.

The regional escalation is drawing in Gulf states. Bahrain has intercepted 143 missiles and 242 drones since February 28. Saudi Arabia shot down 47 drones in a single day, including 38 in a three-hour barrage. Iran has threatened “crushing blows” against the UAE’s Ras al-Khaimah, and Qatar condemned Israeli strikes on Syria as a “flagrant violation of sovereignty.”

Key facts: - Israel struck military infrastructure in southern Syria - Iran threatened retaliatory strikes against UAE territory - The US Embassy in Baghdad ordered all American citizens to leave Iraq

4. Trump mulls “winding down” Iran war while deploying 2,500 additional Marines

Trump told reporters the US is “very close to meeting our objectives” in Iran but ruled out any ceasefire agreement with Tehran. He simultaneously authorized deployment of 2,200–2,500 additional Marines — directly contradicting the “winding down” framing.

Washington is reportedly considering blockading or occupying Iran’s Kharg Island oil hub, which handles the bulk of Iran’s crude exports. Trump said the US has asked China to delay his planned summit with Xi Jinping by “a month or so” due to the ongoing conflict. China has called for an end to the war, warning of severe global economic consequences, and Beijing’s position is complicating any US-China trade framework talks.

As Jordan Shilton argued in the WSWS Perspective, the logic of the war drive is pushing inexorably toward a full ground invasion, regardless of White House rhetoric about “winding down.”

5. Ukraine war: 161 combat engagements in 24 hours as peace talks stall

On day 1,487 of Russia’s full-scale invasion, 161 combat engagements were recorded in the past 24 hours, with the heaviest fighting in the Pokrovsk (32 assaults), Kostyantynivka (22), and Huliaipole (21) directions. Russia deployed 8,107 kamikaze drones and conducted 82 airstrikes dropping 263 guided aerial bombs in a single day. Ukraine shot down 148 of 154 Russian drones.

Ukrainian forces struck Russia’s Saratov Oil Refinery and hit the Kremniy El military microelectronics factory in Bryansk Oblast with Storm Shadow missiles. Russia struck Zaporizhzhia, cutting electricity to 47,000 subscribers.

Key facts: - Cumulative Russian losses estimated at 1.2 million casualties (325,000 killed); Ukrainian military casualties estimated at approximately 600,000 (140,000 killed) - Zelenskyy demanded a clear timeline for the next round of US-mediated peace talks, which have stalled as Washington’s attention is consumed by the Iran war - Russia is pressing Ukraine to cede all remaining Donetsk Oblast territory

6. Gaza: last Israeli hostage recovered as humanitarian catastrophe deepens

The remains of the last Israeli hostage, Ran Gvili, were recovered in Gaza. Total Palestinian deaths since October 7, 2023 have surpassed 72,135, with 171,830 injured. The UN Security Council convened in emergency session on the ceasefire, which remains fragile amid the wider regional war.

Gaza’s economy has been effectively destroyed. Per capita GDP has collapsed to $161/year — roughly $0.44 per person per day — with 80% unemployment. This represents one of the worst economic collapses recorded for any territory in modern history.

Key facts: - 46% of essential medicines and 66% of surgical consumables are completely out of stock - All crossings except Kerem Shalom remain closed - The humanitarian catastrophe continues to deepen as the broader regional war diverts international attention

US measles cases near 1,500 nationally

The CDC confirmed 1,487 measles cases as of March 19 — a 9.2% week-over-week increase that threatens to surpass all of 2025’s 2,285 cases within months. South Carolina leads with 997 cases concentrated in the Spartanburg County epicenter, followed by Utah (443), Texas (147), and Florida (122). Ninety-two percent of cases involve unvaccinated individuals or those with unknown vaccination status.

The US is at risk of losing its measles elimination status, held since 2000, as the outbreak expands amid declining childhood vaccination rates.

Sources AHA News CDC reports measles cases near 1,500 nationally · Contagion Live Measles update March 20, 2026 · US News Tracking the 2026 US measles outbreaks

H5N1 bird flu breaches California marine mammals for first time

H5N1 has been confirmed in 27 northern elephant seals, 2 California sea lions, and 1 sea otter in San Mateo County — the first detection in California marine mammals. The culprit is the A3 genotype, known to adapt to mammalian hosts, which has never before reached California. Pennsylvania has separately lost over 7 million poultry birds in 2026.

The A3 genotype’s westward spread and cross-species jump to otters raises serious concern about mammalian adaptation pathways toward pandemic potential. Marine mammal die-offs have been a key indicator of H5N1 mammalian adaptation globally.

Research breakthroughs and health roundup

  • Shingles vaccine and cardiac events: A major study found that the shingles (herpes zoster) vaccine reduced major adverse cardiac events by 46% and all-cause mortality by 66% within one year of vaccination among high-risk individuals. The finding suggests the vaccine’s benefits extend well beyond preventing shingles, potentially repositioning it as a cardiovascular intervention.

  • In vivo T cell engineering (Nature): Researchers at Nyberg et al. demonstrated stable, cell-specific transgene expression via in vivo site-specific integration of large DNA payloads into T cells using a two-vector system, bypassing the need for costly ex vivo CAR-T manufacturing. This could democratize CAR-T cancer therapy by making it accessible to far more patients.

  • Localized tumor injection triggers systemic remission (Cancer Cell): A phase 1 trial of modified CD40 agonist antibody 2141-V11, injected directly into tumors, showed tumor shrinkage in 6 of 12 patients, with 2 achieving complete remission. The “abscopal-like” effect — clearing tumors beyond the injection site — had long eluded immunotherapy. Ongoing phase 1/2 trials involve approximately 200 patients across bladder cancer, prostate cancer, and glioblastoma.

  • COVID-19 update: CDC projects approximately 4,100 hospital admissions for the week ending March 21, down from 4,500 the prior week. National test positivity sits at 3.4%. The dominant variant is XFG (23%), followed by XFG.1.1 (21%). New RECOVER data shows COVID-19 vaccination reduces Long COVID risk in adolescents, and young people post-COVID have a 24% higher risk of high cholesterol — a metabolic consequence that may pose long-term cardiovascular risk.

Sources Nature In vivo site-specific T cell engineering (Nyberg et al.) · ScienceDaily Localized tumor injection triggers systemic remission · The Sick Times National COVID-19 trends, March 2026 · RECOVER Initiative Research update March 2026

Arts and culture

The cultural landscape this week is dominated by two contrasting tendencies: major studio releases that largely avoid engagement with the wars consuming the world, and smaller independent and arthouse works that grapple directly with questions of dispossession, historical memory, and social disintegration.

Project Hail Mary opens nationwide

The sci-fi adaptation of Andy Weir’s novel, directed by Phil Lord and Christopher Miller and starring Ryan Gosling, opens wide this weekend with a score by Daniel Pemberton. The film, about a lone astronaut racing to save humanity, is the biggest wide release of the year so far. While the premise — one individual rescuing civilization through scientific ingenuity — captures a genuine public yearning for rational problem-solving in an era of political irrationality, it also reflects Hollywood’s persistent retreat into individualist savior narratives. The social organization of science and the collective labor required to sustain it remain invisible in the Weir formula, as they were in The Martian.

Palestine 36 in limited release

A far more consequential cinematic event is the limited release of Annemarie Jacir’s Palestine 36, starring Hiam Abbass and Jeremy Irons with a score by Ben Frost. Jacir, one of the Arab world’s most acclaimed filmmakers, sets her drama in the period of the 1936 Palestinian general strike — a six-month revolt against British colonial rule and Zionist settlement. The film’s release amid an ongoing genocide in Gaza and the regional conflagration gives it an urgency that transcends its period setting. The 1936 revolt was itself a response to the dispossession that the Balfour Declaration and British Mandate had set in motion — the same historical trajectory whose consequences are playing out today in the killing of over 72,000 Palestinians.

Arts roundup

  • NYC Art House Cinema Week (March 20–26): The first-ever citywide initiative offers 5,000 free tickets across nearly 30 independent theaters, with highlights including a Joel Coen and Frances McDormand-introduced screening and a Frederick Wiseman retrospective. The initiative, backed by the NYC Mayor’s Office, represents a significant public investment in preserving independent cinema infrastructure under severe commercial pressure. Wiseman’s inclusion is notable — his documentary method of sustained, unhurried observation of institutions (hospitals, welfare offices, public housing) remains the most powerful cinematic tool for revealing how class society actually functions.

  • Berlinale short films: The WSWS’s Max Linhof and Verena Nees reviewed short films from the Berlinale that grapple with social disintegration, war, and catastrophe, examining how filmmakers are searching for solidarity and meaning in a turbulent world.

  • Courtney Barnett returns: The Australian indie rock artist released her long-awaited new album, her first in several years. Barnett’s observational songwriting — rooted in the anxieties and mundane textures of working-class and lower-middle-class life — has distinguished her from the largely apolitical landscape of contemporary indie rock.

  • Paranormal Activity stage adaptation closes its San Francisco run at the Toni Rembe Theater, a format experiment testing the viability of adapting horror franchise IP for live theater. The production exemplifies the cultural industry’s relentless drive to extract value from existing intellectual property rather than develop original work.

Jacobin / DSA

Jeremy Varon’s “Trump Wants a ‘Video Game War’ in Iran” argues that the war recreates the fantasy of technologically advanced conflict with minimal American casualties designed to insulate the public from accountability. Varon calls for rebuilding a visible antiwar movement through mass protests and direct action, framing this as “a vital plank in a broader antifascist project.” The article explicitly advocates that “the war should be pushed to a close by public pressure,” channeling anti-war sentiment into protest politics without raising the question of the working class as an independent political force or opposing the capitalist system that produces imperialist war.

Other notable Jacobin output includes a cultural piece on Chuck Norris (“With Chuck Norris, the Meme Was the Message”) and a historical article on Namibia’s independence struggle. The tendency’s overall orientation remains one of channeling opposition to war and Trump into the framework of progressive coalition-building — a framework that leads, as it always does, back toward the Democratic Party. There is no discussion of the bipartisan character of US imperialism or the role of the Biden administration in creating the conditions for the current war.

Left Voice

Left Voice has published extensively on the Iran war, making it the central focus of its output in March. Key articles include “Defend Iran, but Don’t Support the Theocracy” (Andrea D’Atri), which argues that revolutionary socialists are “neither pacifists nor neutral” and should defend Iran without offering political support to the Iranian government. The article explicitly polemicizes against the PSL and the Kommunistische Organisation over the question of “campism” — a significant intra-pseudo-left debate.

“Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran” promotes teachers and educators as the leading social force in anti-war organizing, citing footage from the funeral of 160 girls killed in Minab and noting that less than one in four Americans support the strikes. The article calls on educators to “take the lead” but does not address the role of the teachers’ unions in suppressing anti-war action or the need for workers to break from the union bureaucracy.

Left Voice’s Iran coverage is more extensive than other pseudo-left tendencies, but its political framework remains that of the Morenoite tradition: anti-imperialism defined primarily in terms of defending oppressed nations, combined with appeals to existing mass organizations (unions, student groups) to “take the lead” — without confronting the pro-imperialist leaderships of those organizations. The absence of any discussion of building a revolutionary party of the working class as the alternative to both imperialism and the Iranian theocracy is politically decisive.

Sources Left Voice Defend Iran, but don’t support the theocracy · Left Voice Educators must take the lead in opposing the imperialist war on Iran

Socialist Alternative (ISA)

Socialist Alternative’s “11 Days of War on Iran — Trump Has Unleashed a Nightmare” describes the war’s escalation, noting that US Secretary of War Pete Hegseth declared March 10 would be “the most intense day of strikes” so far. The article documents the human and ecological catastrophe as oil refineries were set ablaze, with black rain falling in Tehran. An earlier piece, “Middle East: A Regional War Spiraling Out of Control” (Danny Byrne, March 4), compared the bombing to “roughly double the scale of the ‘shock and awe’ bombings which opened the 2003 invasion of Iraq.”

Socialist Alternative’s coverage correctly identifies the war’s devastating consequences but offers no independent working-class program beyond demands for mass protests. The tendency avoids the question of the bipartisan support for US imperialism and does not raise the need for a political break with the Democratic Party. Its anti-war politics remain confined to the framework of pressuring existing political structures — the same approach that failed to stop the Iraq war two decades ago.

Sources Socialist Alternative 11 days of war on Iran — Trump has unleashed a nightmare · Socialist Alternative Middle East: A regional war spiraling out of control

SWP (UK)

Frankie Murden’s “Unity in Action — How We Can Stop Fascism” contrasts two anti-fascist strategies: the “popular front” (uniting the left with centrist and liberal forces) and the “united front” (mobilizing diverse groups for specific shared goals while maintaining political independence). Murden argues for the latter, citing 1930s examples where popular fronts in Germany, Spain, and France “suppressed radical action to appease liberal partners, ultimately strengthening fascism.”

The article’s historical analysis is superficial and self-serving. The SWP itself has a long record of popular-front politics — from its support for “Respect” (a coalition with George Galloway) to its adaptation to various protest movements. The invocation of the united front is emptied of its Leninist content: for the SWP, “maintaining political independence” means maintaining the SWP’s organizational identity while adapting politically to reformism and identity politics. There is no discussion of the working class’s independent role or the need for a revolutionary party.

Sources Socialist Worker Unity in action — how we can stop fascism

RCP / IMT

The Revolutionary Communist Party (formerly Socialist Appeal, British section of the IMT/now Revolutionary Communist International) is focused on party-building ahead of its national congress, with a goal of 1,400 members. The article “28 March ‘Together Alliance’ Demo: How Do We Fight Racism, Reform, and the Right?” (Adam Booth) argues that “to fight racism, we must fight capitalism” in connection with a coalition demonstration. “The Trade Unions and the War on Iran” (Sean Hodges, March 12) criticizes British trade union leaders for “lagging far behind” the anti-war mood.

The RCP/IMT’s orientation remains centered on building within the existing labor movement structures — criticizing union leaders for insufficient militancy while maintaining political loyalty to the trade union framework. The criticism of union bureaucrats for “lagging behind” avoids the fundamental question: the bureaucracy’s role is not to “catch up” with workers’ militancy but to contain and suppress it. The IMT’s brand of “revolutionary communism” amounts to left-reformist pressure on existing institutions, dressed in revolutionary phraseology.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The working-class cost of $112/barrel oil — who pays, who profits?

As Brent crude surges to $112/barrel following the Hormuz closure, working-class households face spiking energy and food prices globally while oil majors (ExxonMobil, Shell, BP, Saudi Aramco) are on course for record quarterly profits. Goldman Sachs’s warning of potential $200/barrel oil deserves scrutiny — who issued the forecast, what positions do they hold, and how does the financial industry profit from the crisis it predicts? The Trump administration’s chaotic decision to lift Iranian sanctions and ease Russia/Venezuela sanctions to “stabilize” markets primarily benefits energy traders.

2. 204 Iranian children killed — the human cost behind the “8,000 targets” figure

CENTCOM’s claim to have struck 8,000 Iranian targets in 22 days averages to more than 360 strikes per day. Of the 1,444 Iranians confirmed killed, at least 204 are children. Mainstream coverage frames this war through Trump’s ultimatums and Iranian missile salvoes; the mass killing of Iranian civilians — workers, students, families — goes largely unnamed and unquantified. A WSWS article giving names and faces to these casualties would cut through the dehumanizing “targets” language.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war: What is happening on day 22 of US-Israel attacks

3. Gaza’s economic obliteration — $161/year GDP and 80% unemployment as deliberate destruction

Gaza’s per capita GDP has collapsed to $161/year — roughly $0.44 per person per day — while 80% of the population is unemployed and 46% of medicines are completely gone. This is not collateral damage but the deliberate destruction of the economic and social infrastructure of 2.3 million people. The UN figures deserve prominent WSWS framing that connects the economic annihilation to the broader logic of Zionist settler colonialism.

Recommended reading UNRWA Situation Report 213: Humanitarian crisis in Gaza Strip and occupied West Bank

4. One million Lebanese displaced — 19% of the population in weeks

Israel’s renewed assault on Lebanon has displaced nearly one million people — 19% of the entire national population — within weeks of launching ground operations. Lebanon’s infrastructure was already shattered by years of economic crisis and the 2020 Beirut port explosion. The scale of displacement has received a fraction of the coverage given to Israeli missile intercepts or Trump’s ultimatums. A WSWS article could document what is happening to the displaced population and connect it to the broader imperialist war drive.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war: Day 22 of US-Israel attacks

5. Denmark’s Greenland runway demolition plan — NATO fractures under US imperial pressure

The revelation that Denmark — a NATO founding member — secretly planned to sabotage its own territory’s infrastructure to resist a potential US military landing exposes the depth of contradictions inside the Western alliance. That the plan was disguised as a NATO exercise (“Arctic Endurance”) is itself extraordinary. What does it mean when NATO members must plan covert resistance to Washington? The WSWS’s analysis of inter-imperialist conflict and the fracturing of the post-WWII alliance architecture is directly relevant.

6. White House AI framework — preempting state regulation to shield Big Tech from liability

The White House’s six-part AI legislative framework, released March 20, calls for preempting all state AI laws and limiting developer liability. If enacted, it would override regulations in California and Texas while shielding companies like OpenAI, Google, and Meta from accountability for AI harms. The framework represents a direct transfer of regulatory power from state governments to the federal level — at the explicit request of the tech industry. The class dimensions of AI governance deserve WSWS attention.

Recommended reading CNBC Trump AI policy framework

7. Ukraine peace talks sidelined by Iran war — 1.8 million casualties and counting

The US-mediated Ukraine-Russia talks have effectively collapsed because Washington’s military and diplomatic attention has shifted entirely to the Iran war. On day 1,487 of the full-scale invasion, Russia deployed over 8,000 kamikaze drones in a single day. Cumulative casualties — 1.2 million on the Russian side and approximately 600,000 Ukrainian — represent one of the largest military bloodbaths since Korea, yet the story has been largely displaced from front pages by the Iran conflict.
