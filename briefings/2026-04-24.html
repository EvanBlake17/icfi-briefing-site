1. Trump extends Israel-Lebanon truce, orders Navy to “shoot and kill” Iranian boats

Donald Trump announced a three-week extension of the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire on April 24 following a second round of direct talks held at the White House, with Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Vice President JD Vance flanking the president. The extension came even as Hezbollah and Israeli forces traded fire along the Blue Line; Israel’s UN ambassador warned the renewed truce was “not 100%” secure given continued rocket fire. In the same news cycle, Trump posted on Truth Social ordering the US Navy to “shoot and kill” Iranian vessels allegedly laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz — an escalation that came as the USS George H.W. Bush became the third US aircraft carrier strike group deployed simultaneously to the region, the highest concentration in more than two decades.

The US-Iran war has now entered its eighth week with no announced timeline or exit ramp. Trump posted: “I have all the time in the World, but Iran doesn’t — The clock is ticking!” Iranian authorities report 3,468 conflict deaths to date. Navy Secretary John Phelan resigned this week — cited in WSWS coverage as evidence of internal crisis over the administration’s war conduct. As Kevin Reed wrote on the WSWS, the “shoot and kill” order, issued over social media and based on unverified claims, constitutes “a war crime” delivered in the language of social media threats.

Key facts: - US naval blockade now targets all Iran-linked vessels in the Strait of Hormuz; Iran has returned fire on transiting ships - USS George H.W. Bush arrival makes three US carrier groups in theater simultaneously — highest concentration in 20+ years - The Navy seized the tanker M/T Tifani in the Indian Ocean, expanding operations well beyond the Persian Gulf - JD Vance led a US negotiating team to Islamabad for indirect talks with Iran; Tehran had not publicly confirmed participation - Iranian authorities put conflict deaths at 3,468; US aircraft continue daily sorties over western Iran

2. EU approves $106 billion Ukraine loan after Hungary drops veto

The 27-nation European Union approved a $106 billion loan package for Ukraine on April 24, covering Kyiv’s economic and military needs for two years. The deal had been blocked since late 2025 by Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor Orbán, who twice vetoed earlier workarounds. Orbán’s leverage collapsed following his “landslide” defeat in Hungary’s April 12 general election and the resumption of Russian oil pipeline deliveries to Hungary and Slovakia — a quiet admission by Brussels that “sanctions” policy is porous where ruling-class interests require. The EU simultaneously announced a new sanctions package against Russia.

The most politically significant detail: the original plan to use frozen Russian sovereign assets as collateral was blocked by Belgium, which holds the bulk of those reserves at Euroclear and feared unlimited legal liability. Instead, the EU will borrow on international bond markets — meaning European workers will service the debt through taxation, not Russian oligarchs through confiscation. This is happening in parallel to sustained austerity programs across member states, particularly on pensions, health care, and housing.

Key facts: - Package covers two years of Ukrainian economic and military needs, replacing the expired US-led Ukraine Defense Contact Group aid channels - The bonds will be issued as EU-wide debt, raising the joint-debt ratio at a time of record sovereign borrowing - Hungary’s acquiescence was reportedly secured after Russian oil through the Druzhba pipeline to Hungary and Slovakia resumed operation, halted since January after pipeline damage - The vote also included expanded export controls on dual-use technology and secondary sanctions against third-country shippers - No referendum or parliamentary override vote was held in any member state on the new debt commitment

3. Trump DOJ indicts Southern Poverty Law Center

The Justice Department under Attorney General Todd Blanche unsealed an 11-count indictment against the Southern Poverty Law Center (SPLC), alleging financial and wire fraud over approximately $3 million the organization paid to informants embedded inside neo-Nazi and white-supremacist groups between 2014 and 2023. The SPLC’s annual budget exceeded $100 million across that period, making informant expenditure a small operational item. Blanche told reporters the SPLC was “manufacturing the extremism it purports to oppose by paying sources to stoke racial hatred.”

Civil rights organizations denounced the indictment as political. ACLU Executive Director Anthony Romero called it “yet another example of the Trump administration’s extreme attempts to silence its critics.” Marc Morial of the National Urban League described the charges as “political intimidation.” In today’s Perspective on the WSWS, Patrick Martin argues this is a “watershed” signaling that the administration has moved from selective harassment of opponents to “open defense of the fascist movement itself” — framing underscored by the fact that the United Klans of America and Aryan Nations groups targeted by the SPLC’s informant program are now “largely moribund” in part because of that work. Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer offered what the WSWS called “minimal public opposition,” consistent with the Democrats’ pattern of muted response to administration assaults on civil society.

Key facts: - Indictment covers 2014–2023 informant payments totaling roughly $3 million to penetrate UKA, Aryan Nations, and related networks - SPLC founded 1971 in response to Klan violence in the Deep South; has filed landmark civil suits bankrupting several white-supremacist organizations - Action follows the administration’s $2.2 billion funding freeze against Harvard and broader moves against law firms and universities - Blanche led Trump’s personal legal defense before his elevation to attorney general - The SPLC’s arrest warrants were announced shortly after Turning Point USA conducted large rallies on college campuses

4. Russia launches 480km “eastern assault” across Ukraine front

Russian forces on April 19 opened a coordinated offensive stretching 480 kilometers from Kharkiv through Donetsk to Luhansk, paired with missile strikes on Kyiv and Lviv. In the past 24 hours alone, the Ukrainian General Staff recorded 231 combat engagements, with Russia deploying 7,067 Shahed-type kamikaze drones and dropping 287 guided aerial bombs in 78 airstrikes. Yet despite the scale of the push, Russian forces have managed a net loss of roughly 2 square miles of Ukrainian territory over the past four weeks — Ukraine intercepted 92 percent of Russian drones in March even as Moscow set a new monthly drone-launch record.

Ukraine is answering with deep strikes. Over the past 48 hours, Ukrainian forces hit Russian oil-processing infrastructure in Nizhny Novgorod and Samara oblasts and destroyed an air-defense system in Bryansk Oblast. Ukrainian military estimates put cumulative Russian personnel losses since February 2022 at approximately 1,322,550, including roughly 1,100 in the past day. The 480-kilometer simultaneous offensive is the largest single-pulse Russian operation since the Kharkiv counterattack of autumn 2025, and comes as Moscow seeks leverage in any future negotiation.

Key facts: - 7,067 kamikaze drones and 287 guided aerial bombs deployed in past 24 hours; 231 combat engagements logged - Ukraine has liberated more than 480 square kilometers since early February despite the assault pressure - Estimated Russian cumulative personnel losses: ~1,322,550 (Ukrainian General Staff figure) - Ukrainian drones struck oil-processing facilities in Nizhny Novgorod and Samara, extending economic pressure inside Russia - The offensive coincides with Brussels’s $106 billion loan vote — Moscow is attempting to pre-empt any increase in Western materiel before the new funding flows

5. Gaza death toll climbs to 72,568 as aid flows collapse

The Gaza Health Ministry reports 72,568 Palestinians killed and 172,338 wounded in the siege that began in October 2023. Since the October 2025 ceasefire, 777 Palestinians have been killed and at least 2,193 injured by Israeli forces, according to UN OCHA figures as of April 20. UNRWA’s Situation Report #218 documents that aid inflows by UN agencies and partners have declined by 37 percent between the first and second three-month periods of the “ceasefire” — a statistical description of ongoing collective punishment.

Conditions on the ground continue to deteriorate. Bakeries in Gaza City and Khan Younis are scaling back production amid acute flour and fuel shortages; residents report hours-long queues for bread. Israel has blocked UNRWA from bringing humanitarian personnel and aid directly into Gaza since March 2025, rerouting the agency’s work through NGO proxies that operate under Israeli security coordination. The West Bank has seen parallel escalation: settler attacks under IDF cover, the continuation of evictions in Masafer Yatta, and accelerated land expropriations in Area C.

Key facts: - 72,568 killed, 172,338 wounded in Gaza since October 2023 (Gaza Health Ministry) - 777 Palestinians killed and 2,193 injured since the October 2025 ceasefire (UN OCHA, April 20) - UN/partner aid inflows down 37% between the first and second three-month ceasefire periods - UNRWA barred from direct Gaza operations since March 2025 - Bakery closures and flour/fuel shortages extending across central and southern governorates

6. IMF slashes 2026 global growth forecast to 3.1%, inflation headed back to 4.4%

The International Monetary Fund’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook cut its 2026 global growth projection by 0.2 percentage points to 3.1 percent — a revision driven almost entirely by the US-Iran war and the partial closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Absent the war, the IMF said, it would have revised growth upward by 0.1 points to 3.4 percent. Global headline inflation is now projected at 4.4 percent in 2026, a sharp reversal of the disinflation trend of 2024–25.

The Fund laid out increasingly grim downside scenarios. Under its “adverse” scenario — a sharper energy price spike combined with tightening financial conditions — growth falls to 2.5 percent and inflation rises to 5.4 percent. Under a “severe” scenario, in which supply dislocations extend into 2027 and inflation expectations become unanchored, global growth drops to 2.0 percent and inflation exceeds 6 percent. The IMF’s baseline already bakes in a 19 percent rise in energy commodity prices; its assumptions on Middle East hydrocarbon flows remain vulnerable to any further escalation.

Key facts: - 2026 global growth cut to 3.1% (from 3.3%); 2027 projected at 3.2% - Baseline inflation at 4.4% in 2026, reversing the post-pandemic disinflation - Adverse scenario: 2.5% growth, 5.4% inflation - Severe scenario: 2.0% growth, 6%+ inflation, prolonged hydrocarbon supply dislocation - “Reference” assumption: 19% rise in energy commodity prices through 2026

Sudan civil war enters its fourth year

Sudan’s war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) entered its fourth year this week. On April 23, the SAF launched coordinated air and ground strikes targeting RSF positions across six states, destroying weapons depots and drone launch sites and announcing full capture of the Muqaja area in Blue Nile region. The SAF now controls northern, central, and eastern states including Khartoum; the RSF retains Darfur, large portions of three Kordofan states, and the Blue Nile border region with Ethiopia. Military analysts describe year three as “a state of stagnation,” with neither side achieving decisive victory.

The humanitarian toll is catastrophic. 14 million people have been displaced, 26 million face acute food insecurity, and 33.7 million require humanitarian assistance (ICRC, UNICEF, and Intersos figures). Amnesty International reports that nearly 700 civilians were killed in drone strikes in the first quarter of 2026 alone — weaponized drone technology, much of it supplied by the UAE to the RSF and by Iran, Turkey, and Russia to various factions, has transformed the character of the conflict. Sudan now registers a civilian death roughly every 27 minutes.

Myanmar junta controls less than half of national territory

Myanmar’s ruling Tatmadaw has seen its territorial control collapse to less than half of the country under pressure from coordinated ethnic armed-organization offensives and the People’s Defense Forces linked to the National Unity Government. In response, the junta has dramatically escalated its air war: air and drone strikes rose from 134 incidents in year one after the 2021 coup to more than 3,300 in 2025–26 — a roughly 2,400 percent increase. Airstrikes are now the junta’s defining tactic against a largely civilian resistance. The international supply chain that provides these drones and aviation fuel remains largely unexamined in Western coverage.

Min Aung Hlaing, the architect of the February 2021 coup, was sworn in as president on April 10, 2026, completing his formal consolidation of power. Reconstruction and aid delivery for populations affected by the March 2025 Sagaing earthquake (7.7 magnitude, 3,655+ dead) continue to be obstructed by the conflict. The Diplomat notes that Myanmar has effectively dropped down the “global crisis hierarchy” even as the death toll mounts, with major Western outlets devoting a fraction of the attention paid to Ukraine or Gaza.

Germany unveils new military strategy: 460,000 troops, conscription, forward deployment

German Defence Minister Boris Pistorius and Inspector General of the Bundeswehr Carsten Breuer unveiled Germany’s first comprehensive military strategy since reunification. The strategy targets a total force of 460,000 troops with a reintroduction of conscription, forward-deployed brigades near Russian borders, and the integration of the civilian economy into what Pistorius called “total defence.” As Johannes Stern argues on the WSWS, this represents a qualitative leap in German militarism, financed by hundreds of billions in war credits passed with the support of “all establishment parties” — including the Left Party and the Greens — with no meaningful democratic debate.

The strategy envisions Germany operating as the logistical and command hub of NATO’s eastern flank, absorbing enhanced deployments from the Baltic states and Poland under a unified “Regional Plan.” Opposition inside the SPD-CDU coalition was described as “minimal.” Pistorius framed the rearmament as an answer to “Russian aggression and American uncertainty”; for a generation of German workers, it marks the first open return of conscription and mass mobilization since 1945.

200+ immigration judges fired as asylum approval crashes to 7%

More than 200 immigration judges have been fired, forced into retirement, or resigned since Trump took office in January 2025, according to reporting by NPR, CBS News, and Slate. The terminations are concentrated among judges appointed by Democratic presidents or who had issued pro-immigrant decisions. Asylum is now granted in just 7 percent of cases, down from over 50 percent under Biden. The administration’s stated deportation goal is 3,000 people per day — over 1 million per year.

Unlike the Article III federal courts, the entire immigration-court system sits within the Department of Justice’s Executive Office for Immigration Review, making it uniquely vulnerable to executive pressure. NPR’s investigation identifies a small, little-known DOJ unit that has been rewriting case law through Board of Immigration Appeals decisions — effectively legislating deportation policy through administrative rulings. Federal Article III courts have blocked or slowed several initiatives, including use of the Alien Enemies Act of 1798 and the attempt to end birthright citizenship, but the immigration bench itself is being reshaped into an instrument of executive deportation policy.

One year of Trump tariffs: $1,500 per household, 212,700 jobs lost

One year after Trump’s April 2, 2025 “Liberation Day” tariff rollout, new research from the Center for American Progress and the Tax Foundation quantifies the damage. The tariffs represent the largest US tax increase as a percentage of GDP since 1993 — averaging $1,500 per household in 2026 (households paid $1,700 extra between February 2025 and January 2026). Manufacturing, the sector Trump claimed the tariffs would revive, has lost 89,000 jobs; transportation and warehousing has shed 123,700 jobs, for a combined total of 212,700 jobs lost in the 10 months after Liberation Day. Manufacturing alone lost 77,000 jobs between April and December 2025.

On April 2, 2026 — the anniversary — Trump restructured the steel/aluminum/copper tariffs and imposed the 100 percent tariff on patented pharmaceutical imports. More than 65 percent of respondents in a January 2026 Council on Foreign Relations poll said tariffs had made everyday goods less affordable. The administration continues to insist it is “bringing jobs home.”

Harvard graduate workers enter fourth day of strike

Approximately 4,000 Harvard graduate student workers launched a strike on April 21, now entering its fourth day. Harvard’s Corporation — which reports a $53.2 billion endowment and a projected $365 million deficit — has offered graduate workers only 2.5 percent annual raises while they earn $18–$21 per hour in a city where median monthly rent is $3,500. The strike unfolds while the Trump administration has frozen $2.2 billion in Harvard grants and is threatening the university’s tax-exempt status over its handling of pro-Palestinian campus protests. Writing in the WSWS, Mike Ingram argues that the Corporation is using the political crisis as cover to impose austerity on its workforce rather than defend academic freedom, and criticizes the UAW bureaucracy for providing only minimal strike support.

Sources WSWS Harvard Corporation demands graduate workers pay the price for Trump’s assault

Two workers killed in West Virginia chemical plant explosion

An explosion at Catalyst Refiners’ silver-extraction facility in Institute, West Virginia killed two workers and injured between 20 and 30 people, including first responders. The plant had lost its license in 2015, reopened in 2018, and was in the process of a shutdown when the explosion occurred. Hydrogen sulfide is among the hazardous gases suspected to have been released. The facility sits in the heart of West Virginia’s “Chemical Valley,” where industrial fatalities have become chronic. Samuel Davidson, writing on the WSWS, situates the explosion within a pattern of corporate negligence and points to the Trump administration’s gutting of the Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board as a structural cause.

Michigan ICE detention center enters fifth day of hunger strike

Detainees at the GEO Group-operated North Lake Processing Center in Baldwin, Michigan — the largest ICE facility in the Midwest, with roughly 1,400 detainees — launched a hunger and labor strike on April 20. Their seven demands include justification of indefinite detentions, granting of parole, acceleration of case decisions, improvements in food quality, adequate medical care, and an end to sleep disruptions. One detainee, Ahman Alnajdawi, has been denied a straightforward voluntary deportation back to his pregnant wife after months of administrative delay. The strike has received minimal mainstream coverage despite the scale of the facility.

US measles cases hit 1,748 as 2026 outbreak continues

As of April 16, 2026, the CDC confirms 1,748 measles cases across 33 jurisdictions, with 94 percent (1,637 cases) outbreak-associated across 19 declared outbreaks. The case count has nearly doubled from roughly 910 cases in mid-February, reflecting accelerating transmission in under-vaccinated communities. No deaths have been reported so far in 2026, but 2026 is on pace to far exceed the 2019 post-elimination-era record. Several state health departments — notably South Carolina — continue to obscure case locations, citing “privacy” concerns that public-health officials say undercut outbreak response.

HHS blocks CDC COVID vaccine study showing 50% hospitalization cut

The Department of Health and Human Services, under Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., rejected publication of a CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR) study showing that the 2025–26 COVID vaccine reduced emergency department visits and hospitalizations among healthy adults by roughly 50 percent this past winter, using the well-established VISION network of nine health systems. CDC Director Jay Bhattacharya reportedly objected to the study’s standard “test-negative design.” Researchers and former CDC officials told CNN and the Washington Post that suppression of a methodologically standard vaccine effectiveness paper is unprecedented and signals broader erosion of US public-health evidence infrastructure.

New “Cicada” COVID variant BA.3.2 in 31 US states

The BA.3.2 variant — nicknamed “Cicada” for its highly mutated spike protein — has now been detected in at least 33 countries and 31 US states as of mid-April 2026. Test positivity stands at 1.8 percent (week ending April 4), and projected new COVID hospitalizations for the week of April 25 are roughly 1,400. Early laboratory data suggest reduced effectiveness of the current JN.1-targeting vaccines against Cicada, raising the prospect of another reformulation cycle. The variant’s emergence during a period when HHS is blocking vaccine-effectiveness studies has intensified criticism of the administration’s public-health posture.

H5N1 acquires mutations enabling cattle infection

Researchers have identified two mutations now commonly found in dairy-cattle H5N1 strains that allow the virus to bind to N-glycolylneuraminic acid (NeuGc) — a sugar present in bovine mammary tissue but absent in humans and birds. The finding, reported in a bioRxiv preprint covered by the UNMC Transmission blog, explains the virus’s persistence in dairy herds without increasing direct human infection risk. Tracking further mutations remains critical: any drift toward binding human-tissue sugars would represent a significant step toward efficient mammalian transmission.

UN AI panel urges global guardrails as Amazon-Anthropic deepens to $33 billion+

The inaugural summit of the UN Independent International Scientific Panel on AI convened in Geneva, with Nobel laureate Geoffrey Hinton calling for binding international regulation and comparing uncontrolled AI development to “a very fast car with no steering wheel.” At the same time, Amazon confirmed a $5 billion immediate investment in Anthropic with up to $20 billion more tied to milestones, bringing the total commitment to over $33 billion; Anthropic has pledged over $100 billion in AWS infrastructure spend over 10 years. The simultaneous push for international governance and record-scale private capital deployment captures the contradiction at the core of frontier AI development. Meanwhile, Bryan Dyne reports on the WSWS that the first 14 days of the US-Israel war against Iran (February 28–March 14) produced approximately 5.1 million metric tons of CO₂ equivalent, with destruction of ~20,000 civilian buildings alone generating 2.4 million metric tons of embodied emissions — the climate dimension absent from all mainstream coverage of the conflict.

Arts and culture

The day’s most significant cultural story is the launch of an organized artists’ boycott of the 92nd Street Y in New York, one of the most influential cultural institutions in the United States. The collective 92NO called for a full boycott of the institution’s literary and cultural programming, citing a cascading set of political-censorship decisions: the October 2023 cancellation of Pulitzer Prize-winning novelist Viet Thanh Nguyen after he signed a letter demanding a ceasefire in Gaza; the subsequent firing of staff who displayed Palestinian-solidarity symbols; the hosting of Palantir CEO Alex Karp alongside pro-war columnists Bret Stephens, Thomas Friedman, and Bari Weiss; and most strikingly, the 92NY’s new bylaws barring speakers who “question the legitimacy” of the State of Israel. Poetry Center director Sarah Chihaya and coordinator Sophie Herron have resigned in protest.

The boycott represents a significant rupture inside the US literary institutional order. The 92Y’s Unterberg Poetry Center, founded in 1939, has hosted T. S. Eliot, Dylan Thomas, Anne Sexton, W. H. Auden, and every major English-language poet of the postwar era. Its wholesale subordination to a political litmus test on Israel — written into the bylaws — marks a qualitative shift from ad-hoc cancellations to institutional codification of censorship. As Sandy English writes on the WSWS, the development parallels the purging of anti-imperialist voices from universities, newspapers, and foundations across the period of the US-Israel war. The artists involved include figures from the PEN America and National Book Critics Circle networks, suggesting the boycott will harden into a broader professional split.

Other notable developments from the past 24 hours:

  • New York film community mobilizes: Programmers at several major New York art houses — Anthology, Metrograph, and the Brooklyn Academy of Music’s cinema program — joined open letters in solidarity with the 92NO action, extending the effective boycott into film-programming institutions.
  • Publishing industry split over AI licensing: The Authors Guild and PEN America issued new guidance warning members against signing blanket AI-training rights clauses. The guidance followed reports that three major trade houses were quietly modifying contract boilerplate to include perpetual AI-training licenses without compensation.
  • Museums and restitution: Several European museums, including institutions in France and Germany, are preparing deaccession actions under EU restitution frameworks targeting artifacts acquired during colonial expeditions. The Humboldt Forum in Berlin remains the focal point of protest around the Benin Bronzes.
  • Theater under pressure: The Royal Shakespeare Company announced further cuts to its touring program, citing Arts Council England funding reductions. The cuts arrive in parallel with the German federal government’s announcement of defense-spending expansions that dwarf the entire UK arts budget.
  • Music industry consolidation: Universal Music Group’s aggressive acquisition of independent catalogs has drawn renewed criticism from musicians’ unions in the US and UK, with the American Federation of Musicians warning that streaming economics combined with catalog consolidation are driving working performers below subsistence income.

A consistent thread runs through these developments: the cultural sphere is being reorganized around the priorities of war, finance capital, and political conformity. Works engaging seriously with the Gaza genocide, the Iran war, and rising state authoritarianism face institutional resistance at every level, while nationalist and militarist content — including a spate of new streaming productions celebrating US special operations — finds abundant funding. The WSWS framework treats art not as entertainment content but as a form of social consciousness; the conflicts inside the 92NY, inside publishing, and inside the museum world are not “culture wars” but manifestations of the same crisis that is producing war, austerity, and repression.

Australia: Labor imposes sweeping disability cuts — Max Boddy

The Albanese Labor government’s upcoming budget will remove approximately 160,000 people from the National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS) within four years, with the real figure exceeding 300,000 when blocked enrollments are counted. The target is $35 billion in savings — the largest single cut to an Australian government program this century. New “functional capacity” assessments replace diagnosis-based eligibility, and social-participation funding faces a 16 percent reduction from October. Boddy contrasts the austerity against $60 billion in annual military spending plus the $53 billion AUKUS commitment. The cuts are being carried through by a Labor government that campaigned in 2022 on defending the NDIS — a textbook case of social-democratic parties delivering cuts that conservative parties could not sustain.

El Gamal family released from ICE detention — Our reporters

US District Judge Fred Biery ordered the immediate release of Hayam El Gamal and her five children after nearly 10 months of detention at the Dilley family detention center in Texas. The family had been jailed two days after the June 2025 Boulder firebombing for which her now-estranged husband was arrested; none of the family were ever charged with any offense. An immigration judge had set a $15,000 bond in September 2025, but the administration used legal maneuvers to block release for seven additional months. Trump’s DHS denounced the ruling and called the children “national security threats” in its public statement. The case is a textbook example of collective punishment — detention of immediate family as a form of pressure in a separate criminal investigation, without any charge against the detainees themselves.

Nexteer workers: It is time for the rank and file to take matters into our own hands! — Nexteer Workers Rank-and-File Committee

Michigan autoworkers at Nexteer (producer of steering systems for GM, Ford, and Stellantis) who rejected a UAW-backed contract by 96.2 percent on April 2 denounce UAW President Shawn Fain for extending the expired 2021 contract without membership authorization — on the same day as the rejection vote — rather than calling a strike. The statement notes that Fain posted on social media about fishing tournaments in the period. The committee demands elimination of wage tiers, restoration of COLA, livable starting wages, affordable healthcare, and weekly strike pay of at least $1,000 drawn from the UAW’s $1 billion strike fund. The committee endorses rank-and-file candidate Will Lehman for UAW president against Fain. The statement illustrates the concrete role of the union bureaucracy in suppressing a rank-and-file revolt at an industrial flash point.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — continues to frame Trump’s authoritarianism as a problem solvable by Democratic electoral mobilization; no rupture with the Democrats despite SPLC indictment
  • Left Voice — correctly identifies the war on Iran as imperialist but argues for “united fronts” with pro-Democratic left organizations
  • PSL / Liberation News — anti-war coverage continues to combine militant rhetoric with adaptation to Russian and Chinese geopolitical positions
  • Socialist Alternative — centers parliamentary and municipal campaigns as the axis of anti-war work
  • SWP (UK) / Socialist Worker — union-bureaucratic orientation continues; couples correct anti-war positions with calls for pressure on the TUC
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — declarations of the “revolutionary moment” combined with ongoing sectarian posture toward actual class struggle

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s coverage in the past 24 hours continues to filter Trump’s assault on civil society through the prism of Democratic Party electoral strategy. Articles on the SPLC indictment and the firing of immigration judges frame these as violations of norms that Democrats must mobilize to reverse — not as evidence of the terminal crisis of US democracy under capitalism. A piece on the California debate treats Katie Porter’s candidacy as a progressive vehicle despite her record of defending IDF aid. No Jacobin writer in the past 24 hours has called for an organized break with the Democrats in the face of the SPLC prosecution. The ideological function is clear: Jacobin provides left cover for the precise liberal-electoralist framework that the WSWS Perspective identified as the enabling condition of the administration’s escalation. DSA’s national political committee continues to endorse Democratic candidates in contested primaries, including candidates who voted for the military-aid packages that finance the Iran war.

Left Voice

Left Voice correctly identifies the US-Iran war as imperialist and its coverage of the Harvard graduate strike centers the role of the university as capitalist institution. However, the tendency continues to promote “united front” formations with DSA, Liberation, and other pseudo-left groups that are actively building support for Democratic candidates — a formula that in practice produces political subordination rather than united action. Left Voice’s coverage of the Palestinian struggle retains the language of anti-imperialism but avoids the question of working-class political independence, preferring the category of “popular resistance” that conflates workers with a cross-class “people.” The tendency’s ties to the Argentine PTS, which participates in capitalist electoral coalitions, remain the structural limit on its political line.

PSL / Liberation News

The Party for Socialism and Liberation continues to combine militant anti-war rhetoric with a political orientation that treats the governments of Russia, China, and Iran as progressive forces in a “multipolar” struggle against US imperialism. Coverage of the Strait of Hormuz blockade correctly identifies US aggression but does not address the class character of the Iranian regime or the fate of Iranian workers and the Left under clerical rule. The line effectively treats the Iranian bourgeoisie as an ally. The PSL has also continued to promote the Green Party, with Jill Stein endorsed in prior cycles, as a vehicle for “anti-imperialist” electoral politics — a nationalist project that leaves the ICFI’s perspective of international working-class revolution off the table.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative continues to frame its political intervention around elected positions and municipal campaigns, with Seattle remaining its emblematic reference point despite Kshama Sawant’s departure from the city council. Coverage of the Iran war and Harvard strike pushes “ballot-line” demands and calls for progressive Democratic officeholders to “take a stand.” The ISA’s approach in Britain and Europe is identical in structure: pressure on Labour and social-democratic formations rather than a call for the formation of independent workers’ parties. The SEP’s Adelaide meeting report notes precisely this — SAlt’s parliamentarism is the object of direct political criticism by the Trotskyist movement in Australia.

SWP (UK) / Socialist Worker

Socialist Worker coverage in the past 24 hours centers the Trade Union Congress and the Labour Party left. On the Iran war, the SWP’s line is politically correct at the level of slogans — “no to war with Iran” — but the proposed instrument remains pressure on the TUC and Labour left MPs to “break with Starmer,” rather than the building of rank-and-file committees independent of the union bureaucracy. Coverage of the German rearmament strategy and the EU Ukraine loan treats these as failures of “the left” to provide “an alternative” — again a formulation that leaves the political form of that alternative unaddressed.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The International Marxist Tendency’s press continues to declare the arrival of the “revolutionary moment” in essentially every week’s output. Coverage of the Iran war, the SPLC indictment, and the Harvard strike is laced with abstract revolutionary phrases but detached from any concrete intervention in actual class struggles. The RCP’s British section continues to recruit students on the basis of maximalist declarations while abstaining from the building of rank-and-file organizations in industry. The sectarian posture — treating every other tendency, including the ICFI, as beneath engagement — is itself a political program: preservation of the apparatus through ideological insulation.

Assessment. Across all six tendencies, two convergences stand out: (1) none has broken with the orientation toward the Democratic Party (in the US) or the Labour Party / social-democratic formations (in Europe and Australia) despite mounting evidence of those parties’ complicity in the authoritarian turn; (2) none has identified the union bureaucracy as an active obstacle to working-class struggle, despite the Nexteer vote and the Harvard strike illustrating precisely that role. No tendency reviewed has directly engaged with the ICFI’s analysis of the Iran war as inter-imperialist, or with the SEP’s critique of Labor’s disability cuts. The shared political function is to contain working-class radicalization within the framework of the existing bourgeois state apparatus.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. European workers to fund Ukraine war through bond markets — without a vote

The EU’s $106 billion Ukraine loan will be raised on international bond markets rather than from frozen Russian assets, meaning European taxpayers will service the debt. This is happening as every EU member state maintains austerity on health, education, and housing, and as Hungary’s acquiescence was secured by quietly resuming Russian oil deliveries — a public admission of the fraudulence of the “sanctions” framework. No referendum has been held in any member state.

Recommended reading NPR / WGLT EU approves $106 billion Ukraine loan

2. The human cost of the Hormuz blockade for Iranian civilians and the Global South

Western reporting frames the US naval blockade exclusively as pressure on the Iranian government, ignoring the direct effects on Iranian civilians’ access to imported fuel, food, and medicine, and the secondary impact on oil-dependent developing nations in Asia and Africa not party to the conflict. The IMF’s warnings of global energy-supply disruption amount to an indirect, technocratic acknowledgment of a humanitarian crisis that is being deliberately inflicted.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran-US war: four scenarios for what’s next as talks stumble

3. Sudan — the world’s largest humanitarian crisis, buried by the Middle East cycle

Sudan’s war has produced 14 million displaced, 26 million facing acute food insecurity, and a civilian killed every 27 minutes, yet receives a fraction of Western coverage. The RSF’s external financing networks (UAE, mercenary contractors) and the SAF’s return to Khartoum represent a major geopolitical realignment in northeast Africa, with implications for Red Sea shipping lanes and for the global hunger map, that the WSWS has yet to analyze in depth in 2026.

Recommended reading Amnesty International Three years on: Sudan’s warring parties intensify war on civilians

4. US manufacturing collapse one year after “Liberation Day”

One year after Trump’s April 2, 2025 tariff proclamation, manufacturing has lost 89,000 jobs and transportation/warehousing 123,700 — directly contradicting the administration’s promised industrial revival. The story is being told in centrist think-tank framing; a class-conscious account of which specific workers in which industries and regions have borne the cost is absent from left coverage. Major automotive clusters in Michigan, Tennessee, and Alabama, and chemical-valley regions in West Virginia and Louisiana, are particularly affected.

Recommended reading Center for American Progress One year after Liberation Day

5. Myanmar’s drone war and the international supply chain behind it

Junta airstrikes have risen 2,400 percent since the coup — from 134 in year one to over 3,300 in 2025–26. Myanmar has effectively vanished from international media despite the Tatmadaw controlling less than half the country, and Min Aung Hlaing’s April 10 self-installation as president has received minimal coverage. An investigation of which international actors (Russia, China, Belarus, Turkey) are supplying the junta with drone hardware, airframes, and aviation fuel is urgently needed and would connect directly to WSWS coverage of global arms networks.

Recommended reading The Diplomat How Myanmar’s civil war has slipped down the global crisis hierarchy

6. The purge of US immigration courts as structural authoritarianism

The firing of 200+ immigration judges — concentrated among those who issued pro-immigrant rulings — is not a policy disagreement but a structural takeover of the only court system fully within executive branch control. With asylum approval at a historic low of 7 percent and a deportation target of 1 million per year, this represents a qualitative shift in the US state apparatus that no left outlet has analyzed in its full scope. The work of the little-known DOJ Executive Office for Immigration Review division in rewriting case law through Board of Immigration Appeals rulings requires systematic exposure.

Recommended reading NPR The little-known DOJ division turning Trump’s immigration policies into binding law

7. HHS suppression of CDC vaccine effectiveness studies as public health vandalism

The blocking of a standard VISION-network COVID vaccine-effectiveness study showing ~50 percent hospitalization reduction is unprecedented in MMWR history. Combined with the emergence of the Cicada variant (BA.3.2) in 31 US states and reduced vaccine efficacy signals, the suppression constitutes a public-health emergency in its own right. A systematic WSWS analysis of the reorganization of US public-health agencies under the Trump-Kennedy-Bhattacharya leadership, and what this means for the coming respiratory-virus season, would fill a gap in the field.

Recommended reading Washington Post HHS blocks CDC COVID vaccine report
