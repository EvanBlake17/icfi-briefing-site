1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 24 as Trump announces five-day strike pause

The US military campaign against Iran entered its 24th day on March 24 with Trump announcing a five-day postponement of strikes against Iranian power plants, claiming unspecified “conversations” are underway. Iran’s foreign ministry denied any direct dialogue, stating that “points from the US are being reviewed through mediators.” Trump asserted, “They’ve agreed they will not have a nuclear weapon” — a claim Iran has not confirmed.

The scale of the campaign is staggering. The US has conducted over 9,000 combat flights and struck more than 9,000 Iranian targets, including 140-plus naval vessels and missile sites. At least 1,047 Iranian civilians have been killed, including 214 children, according to the Human Rights Activists News Agency. Israeli strikes in Lebanon have killed at least 1,039 people since March 2.

As Andre Damon argued on the WSWS, the “pause” is a tactical deception rather than a move toward peace — a pattern consistent with three prior instances where the administration used negotiation claims as cover for military escalation, including the kidnapping of Venezuelan president Maduro and the assassination of Iran’s Supreme Leader on February 28 while Iranian negotiators sat in Geneva.

2. LaGuardia plane-truck crash kills two pilots

An Air Canada Express Bombardier CRJ-900 carrying 76 people collided with a firefighting truck during landing at LaGuardia Airport, killing both pilots — Antoine Forest and a colleague, both Montreal-based — while all 72 passengers and two flight attendants survived. Over 500 flights were canceled across the New York metropolitan area.

The crash exposed severe systemic understaffing: LaGuardia had four vacancies out of 37 air traffic controller positions, with a single controller managing both landing aircraft and ground vehicle movements simultaneously at the time of the collision. As the WSWS’s Patrick Martin noted, the incident represents the subordination of public safety to cost-cutting across the US aviation system.

Key facts: - The CRJ-900 was on approach when it struck the fire truck on the runway - Four ATC vacancies at a 37-position facility meant inadequate separation of duties - 500+ flights canceled across the New York metro area - The FAA has acknowledged chronic ATC staffing shortages nationwide for years

3. Colombian Air Force C-130 crashes on takeoff, killing 66

A Colombian Air Force Lockheed C-130 Hercules crashed 1.5 kilometers from the runway at Caucayá Airport in Puerto Leguízamo, Putumayo province — a remote jungle region near the Ecuador and Peru borders — killing 66 of 125 people on board. The dead included 6 air force personnel, 58 army soldiers, and 2 national police officers. Fifty-seven were rescued and four remain missing. Onboard ammunition detonated after the crash, engulfing the aircraft.

The aircraft was donated by the US Air Force in September 2020 and had undergone a full engine overhaul in 2023. President Gustavo Petro blamed “bureaucratic difficulties” blocking armed forces modernization, stating: “This should never have happened.” The cause remains unknown, with no indication of attack by armed groups.

Key facts: - General Hugo López Barreto confirmed the death toll: “66 of our military elements died” - The C-130H was part of US security assistance programs to Colombia - The crash is the deadliest Colombian military aviation disaster in years - Investigation into the cause is ongoing

4. French local elections: Socialists win Paris as National Rally fails in Marseille and Toulon

Key facts: - Grégoire defeated Dati in a race seen as a proxy for national political trends - The RN’s failure in Marseille and Toulon undermines its 2027 positioning strategy - Elections were held simultaneously in France, Italy, and Slovenia on March 22–23

7. Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs; Trump shifts to Section 122

The ongoing tariff crisis deepened as the fallout from the Supreme Court’s February 20 ruling — striking down Trump’s use of IEEPA to impose tariffs in a 6–3 decision — continues to reshape US trade policy. Trump responded by invoking Section 122 authority, pushing the average effective tariff rate to 10.5%, the highest since 1943. Twenty-four states led by New York, California, and Oregon are now suing to block the Section 122 tariffs as also circumventing the ruling.

The tariffs constitute the largest US tax increase as a percentage of GDP since 1993, costing an average of $1,500 per household in 2026. Approximately 2,000 businesses are suing for refunds of over $130 billion in IEEPA tariff payments made before the ruling. New Section 301 investigations have been launched against 16 trading partners including China, the EU, Japan, India, Vietnam, and Mexico.

Key facts: - Supreme Court ruled 6–3 on February 20 that IEEPA cannot be used for tariffs - Section 122 tariffs pushed effective rate to 10.5% — highest since 1943 - 24 states suing to block the new tariff authority - ~2,000 businesses seeking $130+ billion in refunds - $1,500 average annual household cost — a regressive burden falling hardest on lower-income families

BP Whiting refinery lockout enters second week

Nearly 900 United Steelworkers members remain locked out of BP’s Whiting, Indiana refinery — the largest inland refinery in the US at 440,000 barrels per day — after rejecting the company’s contract by 98.3% on 94% turnout. BP is demanding the elimination of 115 jobs in “non-core” crafts, pay reclassification cuts, and an unprecedented six-year contract. Three hundred union jobs have already been cut since 2015.

The lockout comes amid extreme volatility in energy markets driven by the Iran war, with refinery capacity taking on heightened strategic significance. BP’s aggressive stance reflects the broader corporate offensive against organized labor in the energy sector.

Kaiser Permanente nurses’ union rams through sellout contract

The UNAC/UHCP union halted the Kaiser Permanente strike — involving tens of thousands of healthcare workers across California and Hawaii — at its strongest point on February 24 without presenting any contract, then withheld the tentative agreement text for over two weeks. When revealed, the contract slashed the wage demand from 38% to 21.5% over four years with no retroactive pay, left the Labor Management Partnership intact, and relegated staffing issues to management-controlled committees.

The outcome exemplifies the pattern of union bureaucracies demobilizing strikes at their point of maximum leverage. Workers had voted overwhelmingly for action, only to have the institutional apparatus negotiate a deal far below their demands.

US measles cases surpass 1,487 in 32 states

The US measles outbreak continues to accelerate, with 1,487 confirmed cases across 32 jurisdictions as of March 19 — up approximately 9% week-over-week. South Carolina alone accounts for roughly 997 cases centered in Spartanburg County, followed by Utah (275), Texas (147), and Florida (122). Ninety-three percent of cases involve unvaccinated or unknown-status individuals, and 94% are outbreak-linked.

The US risks losing its measles elimination status, held since 2000. The CDC will formally reassess in November 2026, and 2026 is already on pace to exceed the 2,285 cases reported in all of 2025. The outbreak is a direct consequence of declining vaccination rates driven by anti-vaccine campaigns that have been amplified by political figures and social media — a public health crisis with clear class dimensions, as undervaccinated communities are disproportionately concentrated in areas with reduced access to healthcare.

H5N1 confirmed in California marine mammals for the first time

H5N1 avian influenza has been confirmed in 27 northern elephant seals, 2 California sea lions, and 1 southern sea otter in San Mateo County — the first detection in California’s marine mammal population. Nationally, 71 total human A(H5) cases have been reported in the US since February 2024. Washington State has seen approximately 14 presumptive positive farmworker cases and about 2.2 million birds affected.

The marine mammal spillover mirrors a catastrophic 2023 southern elephant seal die-off in Argentina, raising concern that the virus is broadening its mammalian host range along the US Pacific coast. The expansion into marine mammals represents a significant escalation in H5N1’s ecological reach.

NEJM publishes phase 3 results for oral GLP-1 drug orforglipron

The New England Journal of Medicine published results from the ATTAIN-1 trial — a 72-week phase 3 randomized controlled trial of 3,127 adults with obesity (no diabetes) — showing that Eli Lilly’s oral orforglipron at 36 mg produced 11.2% body weight loss versus 2.1% for placebo (p<0.001). More than half (54.6%) of the high-dose group lost at least 10% of body weight. A companion Lancet trial (ATTAIN-2) showed similar efficacy in patients with type 2 diabetes.

As a once-daily pill with no food or water restrictions — unlike injectable semaglutide or tirzepatide — orforglipron could dramatically expand access to GLP-1 therapy. FDA action on the obesity indication is expected in the second quarter of 2026.

Sources NEJM Orforglipron for obesity (ATTAIN-1 trial) · Eli Lilly Lilly’s oral GLP-1 orforglipron demonstrated meaningful weight loss

The framework would nullify dozens of state-level AI bills already advancing and consolidate AI governance at the federal level under an innovation-first posture — effectively prioritizing corporate interests over the patchwork of consumer and worker protections emerging from state legislatures.

The past 24 hours saw significant developments across film, opera, literature, and arts policy — all marked by the intersection of cultural production with political crisis, institutional displacement, and the ongoing assault on public funding for the arts.

Project Hail Mary opens to record $140.9 million globally

Phil Lord and Christopher Miller’s adaptation of Andy Weir’s science fiction novel — starring Ryan Gosling and Sandra Hüller — opened to $80.6 million domestically and $140.9 million worldwide, making it Amazon MGM’s biggest-ever opening and the best non-franchise March debut in Hollywood history. The film holds a 95% on Rotten Tomatoes with a CinemaScore of A.

The success of Project Hail Mary is notable as evidence of continued audience appetite for original mid-budget-to-prestige science fiction in a market dominated by franchise properties. Weir’s novel — centered on a lone scientist’s attempt to save Earth from extinction — engages with themes of international cooperation and scientific problem-solving that stand in implicit tension with the nationalist and militarist orientation of the current political moment. Whether the film rises above competent entertainment to genuine artistic achievement depends on whether Lord and Miller bring the same satirical edge they showed in The Lego Movie to the material’s underlying utopianism.

Sources Variety Project Hail Mary box office: biggest debut of 2026 · Hollywood Reporter Project Hail Mary box office blasts off to huge opening

Washington National Opera debuts at new home after Kennedy Center exit

After ending its 55-year residency at the Kennedy Center following the Trump administration’s takeover, the Washington National Opera opened its first independent production — Scott Joplin’s Treemonisha at George Washington University’s Lisner Auditorium — to a sold-out house and a standing ovation of more than two minutes. The choice of Treemonisha is itself a political statement: Joplin’s only opera, completed in 1911, is a work about the liberation of Black Americans through education and community, and was ignored by the white musical establishment during Joplin’s lifetime. The WNO’s selection of this work for its post-displacement debut represents a direct engagement with questions of institutional power, racial justice, and artistic independence.

Sources NPR Kennedy Center canceled: Washington National Opera · WTOP WNO opens first show since leaving the Kennedy Center

Valerie Perrine dies at 82

Actress Valerie Perrine — Oscar-nominated for Bob Fosse’s Lenny (1974), winner of the Cannes Best Actress award for the same role, and beloved as Eve Teschmacher in the Christopher Reeve Superman films — died March 23 at her Beverly Hills home after a 15-year battle with Parkinson’s disease. Perrine’s performance as Honey Bruce in Lenny remains one of the most raw and emotionally exposed performances of the New Hollywood era, a period when American cinema was capable of engaging seriously with the contradictions of American society. She was one of the last active links to that generation of character actors — including Dustin Hoffman, Gene Hackman, and Ellen Burstyn — who brought working-class and marginal lives to the screen with unflinching honesty.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The class composition of casualties in the US war on Iran

Media coverage of the Iran war focuses on military engagements, oil prices, and diplomatic maneuvering. The 1,047 Iranian civilian dead — including 214 children — and the specific class character of communities bombed (workers, farmers, urban poor in industrial zones) receive almost no analytical attention. No major outlet has examined who specifically is dying in Iran or the social composition of the 13 US service members killed. This is precisely the kind of story the WSWS is uniquely positioned to tell — connecting imperial violence to class.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war: What’s happening on day 24 of US-Israel attacks

2. The $200 billion Pentagon war funding request and domestic social spending

The Trump administration’s $200 billion Pentagon supplemental war funding request — which would push annual US military spending above $1 trillion — is proceeding with minimal examination of what is being cut to fund it. The figure dwarfs the Iraq War’s peak of $144 billion even though the Iraq occupation involved 170,000 ground troops. A WSWS article could connect this directly to the DHS shutdown, healthcare underfunding, and the broader austerity regime.

Recommended reading CBS News Iran war live updates

3. Gaza’s Eid al-Fitr amid total humanitarian collapse

Palestinians marked Eid al-Fitr in rubble while the ceasefire that officially ended the war remains a fiction on the ground, with 2,073 Israeli violations recorded. The story has been substantially buried by Iran war coverage despite 72,263 people killed. The specific collapse of the medical system — 46% of medicines out of stock, 66% of medical consumables gone — constitutes an ongoing crime receiving almost no attention.

Recommended reading UNRWA Situation report #212: Humanitarian crisis in Gaza Strip

4. Colombian military plane crash and US-donated military hardware

The C-130 that killed 66 Colombian soldiers was a US Air Force donation, part of the ongoing militarization of Latin American states through security assistance programs. The crash raises questions about the safety of aging US military equipment exported abroad and the accountability gap when it kills allied soldiers. President Petro’s statement about “bureaucratic difficulties” blocking modernization points to the contradiction between US military aid and actual readiness — questions no major outlet is pursuing.

Recommended reading NPR At least 66 killed in military plane crash in Colombia

5. The $1,500-per-household tariff burden as a regressive tax on the working class

The tariff war’s $1,500 average annual household cost is routinely presented as a trade-policy abstraction. No major outlet has examined how this burden falls disproportionately on lower-income families who spend larger shares of income on goods, while corporations and importers receive billions in refunds from the IEEPA court victory. A WSWS piece could connect this to the broader question of who pays for trade war — a textbook case of the working class bearing the costs of inter-capitalist rivalry.

Recommended reading Yale Budget Lab State of tariffs, March 9, 2026

6. ICE at airports and the bipartisan consensus on immigration enforcement

The deployment of ICE agents to airports during a government shutdown — where TSA agents work unpaid — exposes how immigration enforcement is treated as more essential than worker safety and passenger service. Both parties have maneuvered around this question rather than challenging the enforcement apparatus itself. This is a story about the class character of the state’s priorities, not merely a budget dispute — and the bipartisan consensus makes it an ideal WSWS topic.

Recommended reading The Guardian US airports: latest on TSA and ICE

7. French and Italian elections: the limits of bourgeois democracy in the European crisis

The Socialist victory in Paris, the National Rally’s failure in Marseille and Toulon, and Meloni’s referendum defeat all occurred on the same weekend — offering an opportunity for a comprehensive WSWS analysis of the state of European bourgeois politics, the limits of the far right’s institutional advance, and the absence of any genuine working-class political alternative. None of the outcomes involved independent working-class mobilization.
