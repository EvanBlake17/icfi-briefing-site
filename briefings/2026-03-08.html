1. US-Israel war on Iran enters second week with mass casualties and escalation threats

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran entered its eighth day on March 7 with at least 1,332 killed in Iran, 217 in Lebanon, 11 in Israel, and 6 US service members, according to Al Jazeera’s death tracker. US Central Command has struck over 3,000 targets in Iran and destroyed 43 Iranian warships. The UN estimates 330,000 people have been forcibly displaced across the Middle East since the conflict began on February 28.

Trump threatened Iran would “be hit very hard” and suggested expanding targets to “new areas and groups of people.” Iranian President Pezeshkian rejected any prospect of unconditional surrender: “That is a dream that they must take to the grave.” The White House has indicated a 4–6 week campaign timeline.

As Andre Damon reported on the WSWS, Trump met privately with seven major defense contractors and agreed to “quadruple critical munitions production.” The Senate defeated a war powers resolution 47-53; the House rejected a companion measure 212-219.

2. Russia provides satellite intelligence to Iran on US military positions

Four US intelligence sources confirmed to the Washington Post and NBC News that Russia is sharing satellite imagery pinpointing US warships, aircraft, radar sites, and communication posts in the Middle East. Analysts assess that Iranian attacks have become more precise than during the June Iran-Israel conflict, specifically targeting command-and-control infrastructure — a shift that strongly suggests intelligence enhancement.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi confirmed: “They are supporting us politically and otherwise.” Trump dismissed the reports as “an easy problem” and called a question about it “stupid.” Moscow has not confirmed the arrangement; Kremlin spokesman Peskov declined to comment.

The intelligence-sharing arrangement directly connects the Iran war to the broader US-Russia confrontation over Ukraine, raising the prospect of an expanding great-power conflict.

3. Iranian drones and missiles strike Gulf states as energy crisis looms

Iranian strikes hit Kuwait airport, Dubai International Airport and the Dubai Marina, residential neighborhoods in Manama, Bahrain, and Doha, Qatar. An Iranian drone struck a makeshift US operations center in Kuwait, killing six American service members with no warning. At least three were killed and 58 injured in the UAE.

Qatar intercepted 9 of 10 incoming Iranian drones; Saudi Arabia intercepted drones near Riyadh. Pezeshkian apologized for civilian strikes on neighboring states, blaming “miscommunication in the ranks” — but Dubai was hit again hours after the apology. The IRGC warned that all US military bases in the region “will be considered primary targets.”

Qatar’s Energy Minister issued the most alarming economic warning of the conflict: Gulf exports could “come to a halt within weeks” if fighting escalates — a prospect that threatens global GDP given that roughly one-fifth of the world’s oil transits the Strait of Hormuz.

4. Ukraine-Russia ceasefire talks collapse after three rounds in Belarus

Three rounds of direct Ukraine-Russia negotiations in Belarus ended March 7 with no agreement across any of the five tracks: military, political, economic, territorial, and security guarantees. Russia reaffirmed it would accept no ceasefire short of a “comprehensive settlement,” resisting any partial deal.

The UN General Assembly adopted a resolution demanding an “immediate, full and unconditional ceasefire,” but deep divisions among member states underscored its symbolic character. The Western framework now envisions that any Russian ceasefire violations would trigger a coordinated European and US military response. France and the UK have pledged to install “military hubs” in Ukraine as part of a peace security plan — a further step toward direct NATO-country military presence on Ukrainian soil.

5. Sudan famine deepens as global attention diverts to Iran war

The world’s largest humanitarian catastrophe continues to deteriorate largely unnoticed. Some 21.2 million people — 41% of Sudan’s population — face acute food insecurity. Famine has been confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli, with risk of famine in 20 additional areas across Darfur and Kordofan. Famine thresholds for acute malnutrition have been surpassed in Um Baru and Kernoi, North Darfur.

Sudan is now the world’s largest displacement crisis: over 12 million people — one in three Sudanese — have been forced from their homes. Drone attacks on civilian areas in Darfur and Kordofan have intensified since January, displacing over 1 million more. The WFP urgently requires $700 million to continue operations through June 2026; funding shortfalls are forcing triage of aid distribution. UN officials warn: “No corner of Sudan is safe.”

7. Cuba suffers major blackout affecting millions

A fault at the Antonio Guiteras power plant in Matanzas triggered a cascading blackout across much of western Cuba, including Havana. Officials estimate the outage will last at least 72 hours, affecting millions of residents. Cuba’s electrical grid has suffered repeated large-scale failures over the past two years amid fuel shortages and aging infrastructure. The blackout deepens an ongoing energy crisis tied to US sanctions, fuel import restrictions, and infrastructure deterioration — a direct consequence of Washington’s decades-long economic warfare against the island.

Pentagon labels Anthropic a “supply chain risk” over AI weapons refusal

The Department of Defense formally designated Anthropic a supply chain risk on March 5 after the company refused to grant the Pentagon unlimited use of its Claude AI model — specifically objecting to deployment for fully autonomous weapons or domestic mass surveillance. Trump gave the military six months to phase out Claude; defense contractors must now certify they do not use Anthropic’s models. CEO Dario Amodei has vowed to challenge the decision in court.

This is the first time an American company has been designated a supply chain risk under a framework historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei — a precedent-setting collision between AI safety principles and the national security state’s demand for unrestricted technological access.

US measles cases approach 1,300 in worst outbreak in decades

The US has recorded 1,281 confirmed measles cases across 12 outbreaks in 2026, with South Carolina alone accounting for 632 cases. Ninety-four percent of patients were unvaccinated or had unknown vaccine status; 84% were under age 19. This makes 2026 one of the three worst years for measles in the US since 2000, driven by collapsing vaccination coverage — only 10 states maintain the 95% MMR threshold needed for herd immunity. The outbreak is a direct product of the anti-vaccine movement’s political mainstreaming, accelerated by the appointment of Robert F. Kennedy Jr. to lead HHS.

H5N1 surpasses 200 million birds affected and spreads to elephant seals

Over 200.6 million birds have been affected by H5N1 in the US to date, with 11.5 million impacted in the past 30 days alone. Seven northern elephant seal pups in California have now tested positive — the first confirmed H5N1 cases in that marine mammal species along the state’s coast. The continued spillover into new mammal species raises ongoing concern about pandemic potential, even as human cases remain rare and confined to those with direct animal exposure.

NEJM landmark study on cardiovascular risk factors

A landmark analysis published in the New England Journal of Medicine examined nearly 2.1 million participants across 133 cohorts in 39 countries. People with all five major cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, hyperlipidemia, obesity, diabetes, smoking) at age 50 lose 13.3 years (women) and 10.6 years (men) of cardiovascular disease-free life compared to those with none. The five factors account for approximately 50% of the global cardiovascular disease burden. This is the largest-ever harmonized individual-level dataset on lifetime cardiovascular risk.

Sources NEJM Global effect of cardiovascular risk factors on lifetime estimates

Berlinale Golden Bear winner Yellow Letters warns of democratic erosion

The Golden Bear at the 76th Berlinale went to Yellow Letters, a German-Turkish-French co-production by İlker Çatak (whose The Teachers’ Lounge was Oscar-nominated). The film depicts an artist couple destroyed by state persecution in Turkey after the husband refuses a politician’s photo request and faces eight years in prison for “spreading terrorist ideology.” The story references real events: approximately 2,000 Turkish artists and scientists were suspended or tried after signing a peace petition between 2016–2019.

Jury president Wim Wenders called it “a movie that speaks up very clearly about the political language of totalitarianism as opposed to the empathetic language of cinema.” This is the first German Golden Bear in 22 years, since Fatih Akin’s Head-On in 2004. The closing ceremony transformed into an overtly political stage, with filmmaker after filmmaker denouncing Israeli aggression and calling to “free Palestine.”

The film’s significance extends beyond its artistic merits. It arrives at a moment when democratic erosion is not an abstraction but a lived experience for artists, journalists, and dissidents across multiple countries. That the story of Turkish state repression resonated so powerfully with a European jury reflects the growing consciousness — however uneven — that the assault on democratic rights is an international phenomenon, rooted in the crisis of the capitalist order.

German government pressures Berlinale programming

Simultaneous with the Golden Bear win, the WSWS documented the German government’s attempt to impose “recommendations” on Berlinale festival programming, including an “independent advisory forum” with mandated Jewish representation. As WSWS critic Bernd Reinhardt argued, this amounts to a mechanism for Zionist veto power over film selection, threatening festival director Tricia Tuttle’s editorial independence. The juxtaposition is striking: a festival honoring a film about state censorship of artists is itself being subjected to state pressure over its programming decisions — particularly regarding films addressing the Palestinian question.

NEA funding cuts continue to devastate US cultural institutions

The Trump administration’s assault on arts funding continues to deepen. The 2026 budget proposal would eliminate the National Endowment for the Arts entirely. The House Interior Appropriations Subcommittee has proposed a 35% reduction, cutting the NEA budget from $207 million to $135 million. The agency has cancelled grants for organizations with DEI programs, terminated the Challenge America grant that funded small arts organizations in underserved communities, and issued termination notices across dozens of programs.

Key staff departures include directors overseeing dance, design, folk and traditional arts, and theater. Lawsuits have been filed to reverse the cuts. The systemic defunding of public arts infrastructure — alongside the simultaneous proposal for a $1.5 trillion military budget — lays bare the priorities of the ruling class.

“Trial begins in Hanover: The death of rail apprentice Simon Hedemann” — Marianne Arens

On September 8, 2023, 19-year-old rail apprentice Simon Hedemann was killed at the Hanover-Linden freight yard, struck by a freight train on a track that should have been closed. Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin/DSA — focused on electoral organizing and a “not about oil” reframing of imperialism that avoids the question of capitalism itself
  • Left Voice — maintains anti-war posture but channels opposition toward “mass movements” without identifying the working class as the revolutionary subject
  • Socialist Alternative (ISA) — characterizes the Iran war as a “regional war spiraling out of control” but offers no independent working-class program
  • SWP (UK) — centers IWD and anti-war coverage but remains trapped in movementism, promoting the SWP’s electoral candidates rather than a revolutionary program
  • RCP/IMT — IWD feature critiques Silvia Federici from a nominally Marxist position while the tendency continues to adapt to bourgeois national movements

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s most prominent piece is “The Iraq War Was Not About Oil” by Matt Huber (March 8), which argues that US imperial aggression is driven by geopolitical strategy rather than resource extraction. While the article challenges crude economic reductionism, it conspicuously avoids identifying capitalism itself as the systemic driver of imperialist war — reducing the question to one of “geopolitical strategy” that could, implicitly, be reformed. This framing converges with the bourgeois foreign policy establishment’s own self-understanding.

“America’s Holy War in Iran” by Ryan Zickgraf (March 7) examines evangelical theology’s influence on the Iran conflict. The article treats Christian nationalism as an autonomous ideological force rather than a political instrument of the ruling class, obscuring the material interests behind the war.

“America’s Next Generation of Socialist Organizers” by Trey Cook and Charlie Muller (March 7) covers the YDSA conference, with student organizing focused on campus worker unionization and planning “mass action” for May Day 2028. The DSA’s orientation remains firmly within the orbit of the Democratic Party: a Burlington city council profile celebrates working “within the system” alongside Democrats.

The day’s overall orientation: Jacobin treats the Iran war as a policy failure amenable to Democratic Party correction rather than a systemic expression of imperialism. The labor coverage channels worker discontent into electoralism.

Sources Jacobin America’s next generation of socialist organizers · Jacobin A young socialist in Bernie’s backyard

Left Voice

Left Voice has maintained consistent anti-war coverage of the Iran conflict but channels opposition toward “mass movements” and street protests without identifying the working class specifically as the social force capable of halting the war. Their framework — rooted in the PTS/FT tradition — emphasizes “self-organization” in the abstract while avoiding the central question of building an independent revolutionary party. The tendency’s coverage of international solidarity demonstrations treats protest activity as an end in itself rather than a starting point for political mobilization of the working class against the capitalist system.

Sources Left Voice Homepage

Socialist Alternative (ISA)

Socialist Alternative published “Middle East: A Regional War Spiraling Out of Control” (March 4), which provides a reasonable factual summary of the conflict’s expansion — noting the scale exceeds the 2003 “shock and awe” campaign — but offers no independent working-class program. The article calls for “international working class opposition” in the abstract without specifying organizational forms, strategy, or the necessity of breaking with the trade union bureaucracies that have refused to oppose the war. Their earlier piece “Trump & Netanyahu’s War on Iran” (February 28) similarly describes the war’s horrors while limiting the political conclusion to vague calls for “revolutionary socialist alternatives” without confronting the role of the pseudo-left organizations themselves in disorienting the working class.

The ISA’s approach treats the war as a moral outrage requiring protest rather than a systemic crisis requiring the political reorganization of the working class.

Sources Socialist Alternative Middle East: A regional war spiraling out of control · Socialist Alternative Trump & Netanyahu’s war on Iran

SWP (UK)

The SWP’s Socialist Worker published prominently on both the Iran war and International Women’s Day. “Attacks on Iran: Imperialism, repression and resistance” frames the conflict in anti-imperialist terms but remains within the movementist paradigm — calling for demonstrations and “resistance” without addressing the political bankruptcy of the trade union leadership or the need for independent working-class organization. “Iran demonstration rages against Donald Trump’s attack and Britain’s complicity” reports on UK protests but treats the demonstration itself as the political goal.

The IWD coverage — “International Women’s Day: Women and Revolution” and “Breaking Our Chains: Smashing sexism and the system” — combines feminist rhetoric with vague revolutionary language. The SWP is also promoting its electoral candidates for May elections “on a short but radical programme,” confirming the tendency’s continued oscillation between movementism and parliamentary opportunism.

“Germany’s school students walk out against war” reports on student protests without noting the political role of the pseudo-left organizations in channeling student anger into impotent protest formats.

Sources Socialist Worker Homepage

RCP/IMT

The Revolutionary Communist Party (formerly Socialist Appeal) published “Women, capitalism, and the struggle against oppression — A Marxist critique of Silvia Federici” for International Women’s Day. The article nominally critiques Federici’s autonomist feminism from a Marxist standpoint, but the IMT’s own practice — characterized by adaptation to bourgeois national movements (including its equivocal positions on various national liberation struggles) and its refusal to draw clear lines against Stalinism — undermines the theoretical credibility of such critiques. The tendency’s rebranding as the “Revolutionary Communist Party” represents a leftward verbal shift without a corresponding change in political program or practice.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. War profiteering in the Iran conflict — $891 million per day, and who’s collecting

The first 100 hours of the US-Iran war cost an estimated $3.7 billion, nearly entirely unbudgeted. Defense contractors — Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, Lockheed Martin — stand to gain massively from weapons replenishment, particularly after Trump’s meeting with seven contractors to “quadruple critical munitions production.” This dimension is almost entirely absent from mainstream coverage, which focuses on military operations. A WSWS article could trace the specific contracts, stock price movements of defense firms, and the class interests driving the war.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war: What is happening on day eight

2. Migrant workers and the Gulf’s 30 million labor underclass caught in the crossfire

The Gulf states under Iranian attack — UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain — are home to an estimated 30 million migrant workers, mostly from South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa. These workers have no evacuation rights, limited consular protection, and are trapped near bombed airports and industrial targets. This story has received virtually no mainstream coverage. The WSWS could connect the kafala system’s structural exploitation to the war’s differential impact on working-class populations.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera After Iran’s salvo hit their skylines, will the Gulf states enter the war?

3. USAID cuts under DOGE are accelerating Sudan’s famine

The Trump administration’s gutting of USAID — one of the largest funders of WFP Sudan operations — has directly worsened the funding shortfall that now threatens food aid to 21 million people. The connection between domestic austerity policy and mass death abroad deserves a dedicated WSWS article tracing the specific funding cuts, their timeline, and their measurable impact on aid delivery in Darfur and Kordofan.

Recommended reading WFP Sudan emergency · Amnesty International The international community’s silence amid the worst humanitarian crisis

4. American workers bearing the brunt of Trump’s tariffs — not foreign exporters

Multiple economic studies confirm that US businesses and consumers have paid nearly the full cost of Trump’s tariff regime. The effective import tax rate has risen to 13.7% under the new Section 122 tariffs. Despite this, mainstream media frames the issue as a “trade war” between nations, obscuring the class dimension: working-class Americans pay through higher prices and slower wage growth. A WSWS piece could integrate the tariff data with the February jobs collapse to show how trade policy functions as a mechanism of wealth transfer.

5. European rearmament drive after Ukraine talks collapse

The collapse of the Belarus ceasefire talks is fueling a massive European rearmament push, with France and the UK pledging military hubs in Ukraine. Germany lost over 250,000 manufacturing jobs since 2019, partly due to energy price shocks from NATO’s war in Ukraine, yet military spending continues to escalate. This spending surge benefits European arms manufacturers and is being driven through parliaments with minimal democratic debate. The WSWS could trace the specific defense budget increases across European NATO members and their impact on social spending.

Recommended reading Carnegie Endowment Russia’s negotiations purpose

The Paris IWD march explicitly tied feminist demands to cancellation of the Macron-era pension reform — the same austerity measure that triggered massive strikes in 2023. The re-emergence of this demand in 2026, now framed through a feminist lens, signals deepening working-class anger at structural inequality. A WSWS article could analyze the political significance of the protest’s anti-austerity character and the role of the union bureaucracies in channeling this anger into a single-day demonstration.

The designation of Anthropic as a “supply chain risk” — the first American company to receive this label, historically reserved for foreign adversaries — marks a new frontier in the militarization of technology. The case raises fundamental questions about the relationship between the tech industry and the national security state.
