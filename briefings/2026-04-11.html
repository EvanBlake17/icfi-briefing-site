1. US-Iran ceasefire unravels as Strait of Hormuz remains closed

The two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan and announced April 8 — barely 90 minutes before Trump’s deadline, after which he warned “a whole civilization will die tonight” — is fraying over the status of Lebanon. Pakistan’s ceasefire language explicitly included Lebanon; Trump and Netanyahu both publicly declared Lebanon excluded. Iran says this discrepancy voids the agreement and has refused to reopen the Strait of Hormuz.

As of April 11, more than 100 commercial vessels remain stalled near the strait, which normally handles roughly 20 percent of global oil supply and significant LNG volumes. Reports indicate Iran is conditioning or charging fees for passage, and possible mines were detected in the waterway as of April 9. Brent crude has fallen from a war peak of $119 per barrel to approximately $95, but remains 35 percent above pre-war levels of roughly $70 in late February.

Key facts:

  • Trump stated US military forces will remain “near Iran” until a “real agreement” is honored and warned any breach triggers a response “larger than anything seen before”
  • Russia and China blocked a UN Security Council resolution on the Strait of Hormuz on April 7
  • Iran struck UAE gas infrastructure, Kuwait oil facilities (causing “severe material damage” to oil, power, and desalination plants), and Saudi Arabia’s east-west pipeline during the conflict
  • UAE intercepted 17 ballistic missiles and 35 drones in a single engagement
  • US military conducted strikes on Kharg Island, Iran’s main oil export terminal

2. Israel continues Lebanon assault, strikes government building in Nabatieh

Israel continued its bombardment of Lebanon on April 10, killing at least 14 people. Thirteen of the dead were security personnel struck in an attack on a government building in Nabatieh. Lebanon’s President Joseph Aoun condemned the strike, stating that “targeting state institutions would not deter Lebanon from defending its sovereignty.” The attack on a state building — not a Hezbollah installation — marks an escalation in Israel’s willingness to target Lebanese government infrastructure directly.

The assault follows the deadliest single day of the Lebanon campaign on April 8, when Israeli forces launched strikes on more than 100 Hezbollah targets in approximately 10 minutes using 50 fighter jets and roughly 160 munitions, killing at least 303 people and wounding 1,165. Netanyahu declared Lebanon operations fall outside the US-Iran ceasefire — a position that has become the central obstacle to reopening the Strait of Hormuz, since Iran refuses to comply with a ceasefire that excludes Lebanon.

Key facts:

  • Cumulative death toll since March 2: at least 1,888 killed, 6,092 wounded
  • Netanyahu stated Israel “supports Trump’s decision to suspend strikes against Iran” but declared Lebanon operations continue independently
  • Lebanon’s ceasefire exclusion is the principal reason Iran has not reopened the Strait of Hormuz

Sources Al Jazeera Israel strikes residential areas, destroys homes in southern Lebanon · Washington Post Israeli strikes kill hundreds in central Beirut · PBS News Israeli strikes kill more than 180 in central Beirut

3. Russia and Ukraine agree to Easter ceasefire beginning today

Putin announced a 32-hour ceasefire running from 16:00 April 11 through the end of April 12 (Orthodox Easter), ordering Defense Minister Belousov to halt operations “across all directions.” Zelenskyy agreed, stating: “People need an Easter without threats and a real move towards peace.”

The historical precedent is not encouraging. Putin declared a similar 30-hour truce last Easter; both sides accused each other of violations within hours. Ukraine has been on the defensive since losing its Kursk foothold, while Russia continues grinding forward along the front line.

Key facts:

  • Putin’s caveat: “We proceed on the basis that the Ukrainian side will follow the example of the Russian Federation”
  • The ceasefire comes as Ukraine faces mounting pressure after the loss of its positions inside Russian territory
  • No framework for broader peace talks has been established; the truce is limited to the Easter holiday

4. North Korea conducts 3-day weapons blitz with cluster-warhead missiles

North Korea carried out a multi-day weapons testing spree from April 7–9 including ballistic missiles fitted with cluster-bomb warheads, electromagnetic pulse weapons, carbon-fiber bombs, and anti-aircraft systems. The cluster munitions were mounted on the nuclear-capable Hwasong-11 short-range ballistic missile, modeled on Russia’s Iskander. Pyongyang stated the system “can reduce to ashes any target covering 6.5–7 hectares.”

The tests underscore North Korea’s strategy of expanding its nuclear-capable force while global attention is fixed on the Middle East. Missiles flew 240–700 km before falling into the sea, with at least one launched near Pyongyang.

Key facts:

  • Japan’s Defense Ministry confirmed no projectiles entered its exclusive economic zone
  • US military assessed “no immediate threat to the United States or its allies”
  • The Hwasong-11 is a short-range system designed for theater-level operations against South Korea and Japan
  • The three-day blitz represents the most concentrated weapons testing campaign since the 2022 series

5. US inflation surges to 3.3% as war-driven fuel costs soar

Consumer prices rose 0.9 percent in March — triple the prior month’s rate — pushing annual inflation to 3.3 percent, driven overwhelmingly by a 21.2 percent monthly surge in gasoline prices, the largest single-month increase since the Bureau of Labor Statistics began tracking the series in 1967. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) rose a more modest 0.2 percent, with the annual core rate at 2.6 percent, indicating the headline spike is almost entirely energy-driven.

The surge reflects the Strait of Hormuz closure and the broader disruption caused by the US-Iran war. California pump prices averaged $5.92 per gallon, with some counties reaching $6.91. As Jacob Crosse noted on the WSWS, the inflation falls hardest on the 26.6 percent of civilian workers who depend on driving for income, while over 50 million workers earn $20 per hour or less against a federal minimum wage of $7.25 that has not increased in years.

Key facts:

  • March CPI: +0.9% month-over-month, +3.3% year-over-year
  • Core CPI: +0.2% month-over-month, +2.6% year-over-year
  • Gasoline: +21.2% in March — largest monthly increase on record
  • Billionaire wealth rose 120% from 2017–2025 versus 45% for the prior equivalent period; the top 1% holds approximately 50.2% of corporate equities

Sources BLS Consumer Price Index — March 2026 · WSWS Fuel shock sends inflation soaring while the oligarchy grows richer · Washington Post Corporate bankruptcies hit 15-year high

6. Gaza: 571,000 in emergency hunger as IPC projection period ends

The IPC food security projection period for Gaza (December 2025–April 15, 2026) is ending with approximately 571,000 Palestinians in emergency (Phase 4) hunger conditions and roughly 1,900 in catastrophic (Phase 5) conditions. An estimated 101,000 children aged 6–59 months suffer acute malnutrition, with more than 31,000 cases classified as severe.

Famine was technically averted after the October 2025 ceasefire allowed limited humanitarian access, but the IPC warns that “renewed hostilities or a halt in commercial inflows could trigger famine across the entire Strip rapidly.” The Iran war and Strait of Hormuz crisis have further complicated regional supply chains, disrupting humanitarian logistics at a critical juncture.

Key facts:

  • 571,000 in IPC Phase 4 (emergency); ~1,900 in Phase 5 (catastrophe)
  • 101,000 children under five in acute malnutrition; 31,000+ severe
  • No international mechanism exists to prevent a return to famine after the projection period lapses
  • Regional supply chain disruptions from the Hormuz closure are compounding the crisis

Sources UN News Gaza food crisis update · IPC Gaza acute food insecurity report, December 2025–April 2026 · WFP Palestine emergency

Chinese researchers face McCarthyite persecution at US universities

Youhuang Xiang, a 32-year-old Chinese postdoctoral researcher at Indiana University, was sentenced to time served after four months in detention and ordered deported for receiving plasmid DNA — circular, non-infectious molecules routine in biological research — from a colleague in China. The Justice Department pursued felony charges carrying up to 25-year sentences for a matter that would normally be handled as an administrative Agriculture Department issue.

Similar cases at the University of Michigan saw five Chinese researchers charged over harmless materials including plant fungi and roundworms; three had charges dismissed. One University of Michigan researcher died by suicide in March after what has been described as “severe and hostile questioning” by federal agents. Both universities reportedly cooperated with authorities, immediately revoking positions and visas.

The pattern reflects the escalation of anti-Chinese targeting in the context of US-China great-power rivalry, echoing the McCarthy-era persecution of scientists.

US measles surges to 1,714 cases across 33 states

The United States has confirmed 1,714 measles cases as of April 9, making 2026 one of the worst years for measles since the disease was declared eliminated in 2000. Ninety-four percent of cases are outbreak-associated, and roughly 92 percent involve unvaccinated individuals or those with unknown vaccination status. No deaths have been reported in 2026. The country is now at risk of losing its measles elimination status, which requires keeping case counts below a sustained threshold over 12 months. The surge is directly linked to declining vaccination rates, exacerbated by anti-vaccine sentiment promoted by figures in and around the Trump administration.

Sources CDC Measles data and research · Healthbeat Where measles is spreading, April 2026

New COVID variant BA.3.2 “Cicada” detected in 25+ states

BA.3.2, a highly mutated COVID variant with 70–75 spike protein mutations, has been detected in at least 25 states and now accounts for approximately 20 percent of sequenced sewage samples per Stanford monitoring. The variant represents a genetically distinct new lineage that may better evade existing immunity. Current antivirals remain effective. Overall COVID activity is declining, with 38 states trending down, but the emergence of a heavily mutated variant warrants close surveillance given the dismantling of public health infrastructure.

Sources Today New COVID variant BA.3.2 “Cicada” symptoms 2026 · Balloon Juice Plagues and pandemics update, April 8, 2026

H5N1 bird flu entrenched globally

H5N1 is circulating in more species across more continents than ever before, with sporadic cases continuing among US dairy and poultry workers. CDC surveillance shows no unusual influenza activity in humans as of early April 2026, but scientists warn the virus is “completely out of control” in wildlife globally, raising pandemic risk even as current human transmission remains low. The combination of H5N1’s widening host range with the gutting of CDC and WHO surveillance capacity creates a dangerous gap in pandemic preparedness.

Gut bacteria linked to ALS and frontotemporal dementia onset

A new study has found that harmful sugars produced by gut microbes can trigger immune responses that damage the brain, potentially playing a causal role in ALS and frontotemporal dementia. If confirmed, this gut-brain axis mechanism could open entirely new therapeutic targets for two devastating and currently untreatable neurodegenerative diseases.

OpenAI releases AI economy blueprint

OpenAI published a policy blueprint proposing a Public Wealth Fund to give Americans a direct stake in AI company gains, a potential robot tax, and a four-day workweek as AI displaces labor. The framework argues superintelligence is imminent and is the most detailed AI governance proposal from a major lab to date. It is shaping Congressional debate as over 600 state-level AI bills are under consideration in the 2026 legislative sessions, following Trump’s December 2025 AI Preemption Executive Order. The proposal notably does not address the class dynamics of AI ownership — framing workers as passive beneficiaries of wealth redistribution rather than as a force capable of determining how technology is deployed.

Afrika Bambaataa, founding figure of hip-hop, dies at 68

Afrika Bambaataa, one of the three founding figures of hip-hop alongside DJ Kool Herc and Grandmaster Flash, died April 9 in Pennsylvania of prostate cancer at age 68. His 1982 track “Planet Rock” — which fused hip-hop with Kraftwerk-influenced electro-funk — was a landmark recording that helped define an entire genre and influenced generations of electronic and hip-hop artists. He founded the Universal Zulu Nation, originally conceived as a vehicle for channeling Bronx gang culture into artistic expression.

His legacy was profoundly tarnished after multiple men accused him of sexually abusing them when they were boys during the 1980s and 1990s. The accusations confronted hip-hop culture with the contradiction between its origins as an art form of the oppressed and the predatory behavior of one of its founders — a contradiction the industry largely avoided reckoning with. His death will inevitably produce hagiographic retrospectives that elide the abuse; the fuller truth is that Bambaataa’s story illustrates how cultural movements born from genuine social need can be deformed when individual power goes unchecked.

Michael Jackson biopic spent $15 million to remove all child abuse allegations

The upcoming biopic “Michael,” directed by Antoine Fuqua and starring Jaafar Jackson, underwent 22 days of reshoots costing $15 million — paid for by the Jackson estate — to remove all mention of child molestation accusations. The film was originally structured to begin in 1993 when Jackson was first accused, with much of its third act devoted to the scandal. The sanitized version now ends at Jackson’s zenith during the “Bad” tour, transforming a potentially complex portrait into a promotional vehicle. Paris Jackson, Michael’s daughter, has publicly stated she had no involvement and criticized the production. The film is projected to open above $60 million, potentially a record for a musical biopic.

The episode is a textbook illustration of the entertainment industry’s approach to historical truth: $15 million to erase inconvenient reality from a narrative, funded by the estate that profits from maintaining the myth. The cultural function is identical to the censorship it replaces — not an absence of information, but its active suppression in the service of commerce.

US museums in crisis as NEA funding cuts deepen

One year after the Trump administration began terminating NEA grants — nearly 560 grants totaling $27 million rescinded — the fallout continues across US cultural institutions. The Museum of Fine Arts Boston laid off 33 employees, including its only Black, Muslim, and Indigenous curators. DePaul University’s art museum will close permanently on June 30. A survey found 34 percent of US museum directors have lost grants, and 63 percent are concerned about further disruptions. The NEA stated terminated grants “no longer aligned with administration priorities” — a formulation that makes explicit the subordination of cultural funding to political orthodoxy.

Gore Verbinski’s Good Luck, Have Fun, Don’t Die — David Walsh

David Walsh reviews Verbinski’s 2025 sci-fi black comedy as “a dreadful work” and “a libel against humanity.” The film stars Sam Rockwell as a time traveler from a dystopian AI-ruined future who recruits present-day people to install safety software in an emergent AI. Walsh’s central critique targets the film’s message — “It’s all your fault … everyone from your time. You’re all equally complicit” — which he identifies as reactionary technological determinism. Walsh concludes that technology properly understood is “a conquest of humanity” and “a basic requirement for the liberation of humanity,” not an apocalyptic threat. The review is a useful example of the WSWS’s method of connecting cultural criticism to fundamental questions of political economy.

The article explicitly presents municipal reform within the capitalist state as a path to building support for socialism — classic DSA reformism that presents socialism as better municipal management rather than working-class seizure of power. No mention of the Democratic Party apparatus within which Mamdani operates.

“It’s Tech Versus Teachers as Strike Looms Over LA Schools” presents the April 14 UTLA strike deadline as a fight against EdTech privatization. It supports union demands and frames the conflict as corporate interests versus public education, but does not mention the Democratic Party despite LA being a Democratic stronghold with deep UTLA leadership ties to the party. Validates “majority-participation strikes” without raising the question of rank-and-file independence from the union bureaucracy.

“Iran Is Stuck in Permanent Crisis” offers analysis of Iran’s “compression” under external and internal pressures, warning intervention will create “another failed state.” The article includes class analysis of Iran’s state-connected elite and notes that the 1979 revolution was driven by striking oil workers — but offers no call for independent working-class political action or revolutionary party-building. Imperialism is treated as a policy choice rather than a systemic feature of capitalism.

Overall: Jacobin’s day reflects the DSA’s fundamental orientation — channeling working-class discontent into reformist municipal politics, trade union activism within the existing bureaucratic framework, and geopolitical analysis that stops short of anti-imperialist conclusions. The consistent omission of the Democratic Party from labor coverage is not an oversight but a political choice.

1. Strait of Hormuz closure and the global working class: who pays for imperialist war?

The Strait of Hormuz closure has raised global oil prices 35 percent or more since late February, driving inflation in fuel, food, and transportation costs that disproportionately fall on workers worldwide. Insurance costs, shipping delays, and the potential for Iran-imposed transit fees represent a massive transfer of wealth from workers to energy and finance capital. While the WSWS has covered the war itself extensively, a dedicated piece on the mechanisms by which the costs of imperialist war are systematically shifted onto the working class — through gas prices, food costs, and insurance premiums — would connect the geopolitical crisis to everyday economic experience.
