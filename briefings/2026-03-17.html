1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 17 with over 2,300 dead across the region

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran entered its 17th day on March 16 with no signs of abating. Iran’s health ministry reported 1,444 killed and 18,551 injured as of Sunday. In Lebanon, where Israel has simultaneously escalated its military operations, 850 people have been killed, including over 100 children. The total regional death toll now exceeds 2,300, with 13 US service members killed and approximately 200 US troops injured across seven countries since the conflict began on February 28.

The IDF has conducted more than 7,600 strikes across Iran in nearly 5,000 aerial sorties. Israel confirmed killing IRGC Basij force commander Gholamreza Soleimani and Kata’ib Hezbollah security chief Abu Ali al-Askari in Baghdad on March 14. The IRGC issued a notable statement suggesting Tehran retains significant retaliatory capacity: “The missiles we’ve used are from a decade ago — most of our weapons cache remains intact.”

Key facts: - Saudi Arabia intercepted 37 Iranian drones targeting its eastern oil region on Monday; Dubai airport briefly shut after a drone struck a fuel tank - Israel says it has “thousands of targets” remaining and plans strikes over the next three weeks - 13 US service members killed, approximately 200 US troops injured across seven countries - Iran claims its air defense systems remain partially functional despite US/Israeli strikes

As Patrick Martin and David North wrote in the WSWS Perspective, Trump used an appearance at the Kennedy Center to deliver a rambling, hour-long account of the military assault, boasting that over 7,000 Iranian targets had been struck and that Kharg Island’s oil facilities could be destroyed “on five minutes’ notice” — remarks the authors characterized as reflecting “the criminal underworld in power.”

2. Diplomatic deadlock hardens as both sides publicly reject ceasefire

Both Washington and Tehran continued to publicly reject ceasefire talks while contradictory signals emerged about back-channel contacts. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi stated flatly: “We never asked for a ceasefire, and we have never asked for negotiation… every time we negotiated with them, they attacked us in the middle of negotiations.” Trump told reporters that Iran’s terms are “not good enough yet” and that he doesn’t think “they are ready.”

However, an Axios report indicated that US envoy Steve Witkoff and Araghchi have quietly resumed direct contact — a claim Araghchi publicly denied on X, writing: “The last time I was in contact with Witkoff was before his employer decided to end diplomacy by launching another illegal military attack against Iran.”

Key facts: - Trump has rebuffed repeated efforts from Gulf Arab allies to open negotiations, per three sources cited by Reuters - Iran’s preconditions for any talks: permanent end to US/Israeli strikes and financial compensation - Israel says it still has “thousands of targets” planned for the next three weeks - Treasury Secretary Bessent told CNBC the US is allowing Iranian oil tankers to transit the Strait, saying “we’ve let that happen to supply the rest of the world”

3. Iran war triggers largest oil supply shock in decades as Hormuz traffic collapses

The war has produced the most severe oil supply disruption in decades, with tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20 million barrels per day normally transit — reduced to a trickle. The IEA confirmed global supply has fallen by approximately 8 million barrels per day, and its 32 member countries announced plans to release 400 million barrels from strategic reserves over 120 days beginning next week.

Brent crude settled at just over $102 per barrel on Monday, up roughly 40% from $72 on February 27. LNG prices have surged nearly 60% since the war began. Goldman Sachs projected the war will reduce global GDP by 0.3% and push headline inflation up 0.5–0.6 percentage points.

Key facts: - Gulf states have cut oil production by at least 10 mb/d - Global stocks down 5.5% since February 28; Tokyo Nikkei down 11%, Saudi Tadawul down 9.6% - Petrol prices up 68% in Cambodia, 50% in Vietnam, 35% in Nigeria, 28% in Canada — at least 85 countries reporting increases - Average US gasoline prices rose 48 cents per gallon in the first week; jet fuel up from $85–90 to $150–200/barrel

4. Allied nations refuse Trump’s call to deploy warships to Strait of Hormuz

Trump’s efforts to assemble a multinational naval coalition to secure the Strait of Hormuz have met near-universal refusal. Australia and Japan both stated they are not planning to send warships. The UK said it was “working with allies” without committing forces. EU foreign ministers on Monday voted against expanding the bloc’s naval operations in the region.

Trump publicly “chastised” allies for their lack of “enthusiasm,” shaming countries that benefit from Gulf oil trade. China, which receives over 40% of its oil through the Strait, has said nothing publicly on military deployment. Iran’s FM said Tehran is open to discussions with countries wanting safe access to the strait — an apparent bid to split the potential coalition.

The diplomatic isolation underscores how years of unilateral trade wars, tariff threats, and diplomatic provocations have eroded Washington’s capacity to build coalitions even in a crisis that threatens global energy supplies.

5. Israel launches ground invasion of southern Lebanon

Israel formally launched a ground invasion of southern Lebanon on March 16, expanding from five positions occupied since November 2024 to seize the entire area south of the Litani River. The IDF described the operation as “limited” ground operations, but Axios reported that Israel is planning a “massive” expansion aimed at dismantling Hezbollah’s military infrastructure across the region.

The death toll in Lebanon has reached at least 850, including 107 children and 66 women. Nearly one million people — roughly 19% of Lebanon’s entire population — have been displaced. An Israeli strike killed 12 doctors, paramedics, and nurses at a healthcare center in the town of Burj Qalawiya. The IDF announced it killed Hezbollah intelligence chief Hussein Makled in overnight strikes in Beirut.

Key facts: - Between 750,000 and 1 million Lebanese civilians displaced - 329 children killed and 1,632 injured over 28 months of conflict; 412 child fatalities in the last six days alone - France, Canada, Germany, Italy, and the UK expressed they were “gravely concerned” and called for “immediate de-escalation” - Israeli officials vowed to “do what we did in Gaza”

As Kevin Reed reported on the WSWS, the operation represents an extension of genocidal methods fully backed by Washington, conducted under cover of the broader US-Iran conflict.

6. Cuba protests erupt as demonstrators torch Communist Party headquarters in Morón

Protesters in Morón, Ciego de Ávila province, ransacked and set fire to the local Communist Party headquarters in one of the most significant outbreaks of public unrest since the July 11, 2021 uprising — itself the largest since 1959. Chants of “Libertad!” were audible on video circulating on social media. A young man was seen falling wounded on video after gunfire was heard; state media denied any shooting, calling it “a fall.” Five people were arrested.

The protests are rooted in material desperation. Cuban President Díaz-Canel confirmed that no petroleum shipments have arrived for three months — a direct result of Trump’s January 2026 executive order threatening penalties against any country supplying Cuba with oil. Cuba’s aging thermoelectric infrastructure has collapsed, triggering island-wide blackouts lasting 12–18 hours daily.

Key facts: - No petroleum shipments for three months due to Trump’s executive order - Blackouts lasting 12–18 hours daily across the island - Government mobilized military around Communist Party buildings in multiple cities - These are the most significant protests since the July 11, 2021 uprising

7. DHS partial shutdown triggers mass TSA resignations and hour-long airport security lines

The Department of Homeland Security partial shutdown, which began on February 14 after Congress failed to agree on funding, has produced cascading effects on airport security. More than 300 TSA officers have resigned since mid-February, and unscheduled absences have jumped from 2% to 6%. Over 100,000 DHS workers — including TSA, FEMA, and CISA personnel — missed their first full paycheck.

Wait times exceeded one hour at multiple airports, with New Orleans advising travelers to arrive three hours early. Similar conditions were reported at Fort Lauderdale, Philadelphia, Austin, and Chicago airports. Airline CEOs from American, Delta, JetBlue, Southwest, and United wrote jointly to Congress demanding resolution.

Key facts: - Workers forced to withdraw $1,850–$3,700 each from retirement savings for rent, food, and medical co-pays - TSA officer Johnny Jones: “The TSA officers are tired of being used as political pawns in the game” - DHS blames “Democrats’ reckless shutdown”; Democrats blame Republicans for blocking bipartisan funding bills - Shutdown is fundamentally a labor rights crisis — over 100,000 workers denied wages for political leverage

JBS meatpacking workers strike in Greeley, Colorado

Over 3,800 workers at JBS’s Greeley, Colorado meatpacking plant — the largest such strike since the 1985–86 Hormel strike in Austin, Minnesota — walked off the job demanding wages of $33/hour against a current starting rate of $23–24/hour. Workers process approximately 2,600 cattle per shift in dangerous conditions, with supervisors reportedly forcing workers to wait 30 minutes before allowing bathroom breaks.

The plant, built in 1960 and acquired by Brazilian multinational JBS in 2007, lost at least six workers to COVID-19 in 2020. The strike is led primarily by immigrant workers despite the Trump administration’s intensified immigration enforcement — a fact that underscores the centrality of immigrant labor to US food production.

Pentagon-Anthropic standoff escalates over AI access

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic — whose Claude AI runs on classified military platforms — a “supply-chain risk” after the company refused unlimited Department of Defense access. Anthropic is now suing the Pentagon for First Amendment violations. At least 100 enterprise customers have sought to pause or cancel contracts amid the dispute.

The standoff reveals how a single Pentagon contract decision can function as de facto AI regulation in the absence of federal law, with ripple effects across pharma, fintech, and government technology sectors. Meanwhile, a wave of state AI legislation — now being challenged by a DOJ AI Litigation Task Force — marks the most active period of AI lawmaking in US history.

US measles outbreak surpasses 1,362 cases in 2026

The United States confirmed 1,362 measles cases in 2026 as of March 12, spread across 31 jurisdictions with 14 new outbreaks. The South Carolina outbreak stands at 996 cases and Utah’s has risen to 405. Combined with 2025, the cumulative two-year total is 3,637 cases — 94% in unvaccinated or unknown-status individuals. The country is on track to lose its measles elimination status, held since 2000, driven by kindergarten MMR coverage declining to 92.5%, leaving approximately 286,000 children unprotected nationally.

Sources CDC Measles data and research · AHA News Measles cases continue nationwide spread

Pennsylvania bird flu outbreak kills 7+ million birds; mammal-adapted A3 genotype reaches California

Over 7 million birds have died in Pennsylvania in 2026, with single sites losing 2.6 million and 1.4 million birds respectively. Separately, the more mammal-adapted A3 genotype of H5N1 has been confirmed for the first time in California, spreading from elephant seals to a sea otter and sea lion at Año Nuevo State Park. PAHO reports H5N1 detections now span 37 mammalian species across the Americas since January 2025. The A3 genotype’s mammalian spread and the scale of US poultry losses heighten concerns about a potential human spillover event.

Sources South Philly Review Pennsylvania bird flu outbreak: 7 million birds in 2026 · PAHO/WHO Epidemiological update: avian influenza A(H5N1) in the Americas

NEJM: CAR T-cell therapy achieves drug-free remission in refractory autoimmune disease

A study published in the New England Journal of Medicine (Vol. 394, No. 11) reported that in 11 patients with multirefractory autoimmune hemolytic anemia, CD19 CAR T-cell therapy produced a complete response and drug-free remission lasting a median of 11.5 months, with expected but manageable toxicity. The finding extends the emerging use of CAR T-cell therapy beyond cancer into severe autoimmune disease where conventional treatments had failed.

A second NEJM study found that in patients with high-grade asymptomatic carotid stenosis, adding stenting to medical therapy produced a lower 4-year stroke risk compared to medical therapy alone, while carotid endarterectomy did not show a significant benefit — a result that could shift treatment guidelines for millions of Americans at stroke risk.

Sources NEJM Original articles — Vol. 394, No. 11 (March 12/19, 2026)

One Battle After Another sweeps the 98th Academy Awards

Paul Thomas Anderson’s One Battle After Another — a time-hopping drama depicting ICE raids and state repression — swept the 98th Academy Awards on March 15–16, winning six Oscars including Best Picture, Best Director, and Best Adapted Screenplay. These were Anderson’s first career Oscar wins. Ryan Coogler’s Sinners also made history with four wins, including Michael B. Jordan for Best Actor and Autumn Durald becoming the first woman to win Best Cinematography.

The ceremony’s most politically significant moment came when Javier Bardem declared “No to war. Free Palestine” before an audience of roughly 20 million viewers. Palestinian actor Motaz Malhees, barred from attending due to his citizenship, sent a statement read aloud: “You can block a passport. You cannot block a voice.” These interventions represented rare instances of direct political speech at a ceremony that has increasingly policed such expression.

As WSWS arts editor David Walsh noted, the ceremony reflects a tentative but significant shift in cultural consciousness away from identity politics toward broader opposition to state violence and imperial war — though he cautioned against overstating the depth of this shift. Anderson’s choice of subject matter — the machinery of immigration enforcement as a system of state terror — represents a noteworthy departure from the safely biographical or nostalgic films that have dominated recent Best Picture winners.

Sources Hollywood Reporter One Battle After Another wins Best Picture · WSWS One Battle After Another wins the top Academy Awards

Cats: The Jellicle Ball begins Broadway previews

The acclaimed Off-Broadway production — which reconceives Andrew Lloyd Webber’s Cats as a fierce drag ballroom competition set to house music — began previews at the Broadhurst Theatre ahead of its April 7 opening. Co-directed by Zhailon Levingston and Bill Rauch, with Tony winner André De Shields leading the cast, the production earned near-universal critical praise during its PAC NYC run. The show represents a significant cultural reclamation: a commercial Broadway property reimagined through the lens of Black and Latinx ballroom culture, bringing a subculture that has long existed at the margins of mainstream entertainment onto one of its most prominent stages.

Spring Broadway season and film

Broadway’s spring 2026 slate features an unusually high concentration of screen talent making stage debuts: The Fear of 13 (Adrien Brody and Tessa Thompson) begins previews March 19; Proof features Ayo Edebiri and Don Cheadle; Dog Day Afternoon with Jon Bernthal and Ebon Moss-Bachrach opens March 30. While celebrity casting carries commercial logic, the specific material is notable — Dog Day Afternoon dramatizes a bank robbery motivated by the cost of a partner’s gender-affirming surgery, and The Fear of 13 addresses wrongful imprisonment. These are not apolitical choices.

In film, Phil Lord and Christopher Miller’s adaptation of Andy Weir’s Project Hail Mary (Ryan Gosling) opens March 20 with a 96% Rotten Tomatoes score, and Boots Riley’s I Love Boosters premiered at SXSW’s 40th anniversary edition — Riley’s first major film since Sorry to Bother You (2018), which offered one of the most explicitly anti-capitalist visions in recent American cinema.

SXSW celebrates 40th anniversary

SXSW’s landmark 40th edition (March 12–18) is the first to run its Music, Film & TV, and Innovation tracks concurrently under the theme “All Together Now,” featuring 4,400 musicians, 375+ screenings, and 850+ sessions. The structural merger reflects the festival’s effort to maintain competitive position against both Sundance and the tech-conference circuit. The festival’s evolution from a regional music gathering into a global multi-disciplinary event mirrors the broader fusion of culture, technology, and capital that characterizes the contemporary entertainment industry.

Flying Lotus (Steven Ellison) remains one of the few figures in popular music whose work consistently refuses commercial compromise, maintaining an artistic practice rooted in experimentation across jazz, hip-hop, and electronic traditions.

American Axle workers speak out against UAW bureaucracy ahead of contract talks — George Kirby

Workers at American Axle & Manufacturing’s Three Rivers, Michigan facility describe deep frustration with the UAW ahead of new contract negotiations. The 2008 strike — lasting 87 days — ended in catastrophic defeat, cutting wages from $27–28/hour to as low as $10/hour and costing roughly half the company’s 3,700 Michigan and New York workers their jobs. Current wages ($22–25/hour) have not kept pace with inflation. Workers report lifting 45–70 pounds regularly with existing injuries, and management is already stockpiling parts in anticipation of a potential strike. One worker called for abolishing “the bureaucracy” that “embezzles money from the strike fund.” The article advocates rank-and-file committees independent of UAW structures.

Tom Henehan: A revolutionary life — David North

David North commemorates Tom Henehan, assassinated at age 26 on October 15–16, 1977, in Brooklyn. Henehan was shot five times by a professional gunman (Angelo Torres) while the Workers League was investigating the 1940 assassination of Leon Trotsky. Having joined the Workers League in 1973 after graduating from Columbia University, Henehan’s life — North argues — embodied the uncompromising internationalist Marxist principles subsequently vindicated by the Soviet collapse, the failure of nationalist liberation movements, and the resurgence of class struggle globally. The piece is tied to what would have been Henehan’s 75th birthday.

Sources WSWS Tom Henehan: A revolutionary life

40 years since the People Power ouster of the Marcos dictatorship in the Philippines — John Malvar

Malvar argues the 1986 People Power Revolution is systematically mythologized as a democratic triumph when it preserved capitalist rule under new management. Poverty rose from 28% in 1965 to 70% by February 1986 under Marcos; unemployment affected roughly one in three workers. The Communist Party of the Philippines and BAYAN subordinated mass struggle to elite military factions (Enrile and Ramos), with BAYAN members “more inside than outside Crame” mediating between masses and generals. The article calls for construction of a Philippine section of the ICFI grounded in an internationalist socialist program.

Australian labor coverage

Three articles cover Australian labor developments:

University of Newcastle educators strike — Staff are preparing a 24-hour strike on March 18, the third stoppage in recent months, amid approximately 4,000 positions eliminated nationally over 18 months. Management proposed a 10% wage increase over three years — a real wage cut given 5% predicted inflation — while demanding teaching loads rise to 80% of time.

Victorian educators at a crossroads — 98% of AEU members voted for a March 24 stopwork, the first major action since 2013. Victorian teachers earn the lowest wages and work in the worst-funded schools nationally — $2,253 per student below the national average. The AEU president earns $240,000/year. The Committee for Public Education calls for rank-and-file school-based committees.

238 jobs axed at Tahmoor Colliery — GFG Alliance owner Sanjeev Gupta liquidated 238 of 328 jobs after operations were suspended in February 2025. Despite the mine reporting $85.7 million in annual earnings as recently as 2024, Gupta extracted a $427 million dividend while stripping the mine’s finances.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — focused on the Iran war through a geopolitical-realist lens, carefully avoiding any call for independent working-class action or break with the Democratic Party
  • Left Voice — combined anti-war rhetoric with emphasis on mass movements and “self-organization,” while promoting its French sister group’s electoral gains as a model
  • Liberation News / PSL — defended Cuba against US blockade while framing the Iran conflict primarily through the lens of anti-imperialism without class analysis of the Iranian regime
  • Socialist Worker / SWP (UK) — emphasized Western “weakness” exposed by the Iran war while channeling political energy into the March 28 “Together” anti-far-right demonstration
  • RCP / IMT — focused inward on party-building toward a May national congress, claiming 1,400-member target, with limited engagement on the Iran war

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s most significant piece on the Iran war is an interview with Andreas Krieg (“Trump will not march quickly to victory over Iran”) arguing the conflict will be “longer and messier” than expected, with Iran demonstrating retaliatory capacity that raises costs for US regional allies. The analysis is conducted entirely within a geopolitical-realist framework — the question of working-class opposition to the war is never raised. Jacobin treats the conflict as a matter of strategic miscalculation rather than the organic product of imperialism.

Separately, Juliano Fiori’s “A post-order world” argues US hegemony has collapsed into a period of “zonal geopolitics” — unstable competition across regions with no replacement hegemon. While correctly identifying the erosion of American dominance, the article’s framework is purely academic, offering no political perspective for the working class. Max Sawicky’s “Socialists need a distinctive economic policy agenda” proposes five “pillars” of socialist economic policy (labor power, industrial policy, social insurance, public ownership, reduced federalism) explicitly designed to be “neither revolutionary nor frightening” — a reformist program that amounts to a left-Keynesian wish list positioned within the existing capitalist state.

Jacobin’s overall orientation remains that of a left-liberal publication adapting socialist vocabulary to Democratic Party-adjacent politics. Its Iran coverage avoids any call for independent working-class mobilization against the war.

Sources Jacobin Trump will not march quickly to victory over Iran · Jacobin A post-order world · Jacobin Socialists need a distinctive economic policy agenda

Left Voice

Left Voice’s lead article (“Trump at a crossroads: No regime change, no exit strategy in Iran”) argues the war has entered a critical phase with US missile-defense systems deteriorating under Iranian barrages. The piece calls for a “mass movement” against the war, positioning educators as playing “a crucial role.” A separate article critiques Eric Blanc’s call to “cut the military budget in half” as a reformist confusion about the role of the US military — a rare instance of Left Voice criticizing a DSA-adjacent figure from the left, though the critique remains limited to tactical disagreements rather than fundamental programmatic differences.

Most notably, Left Voice highlighted its French sister group Révolution Permanente’s electoral gains in municipal elections, including two city council seats in Saint-Denis. The promotion of electoral victories for a Trotskyist tendency within municipal government reveals the fundamental contradiction of the PTS/FT tendency: it claims revolutionary politics while celebrating integration into bourgeois state institutions at the local level. Left Voice remains oriented toward “mass movements” and “self-organization” without addressing the question of revolutionary party leadership.

Sources Left Voice Trump at a crossroads: No regime change, no exit strategy in Iran · Left Voice We need a mass movement to defeat Trump’s imperialist war on Iran

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL issued a statement on the “Israeli aggression against Iran” framing the conflict primarily through the lens of anti-imperialism and national sovereignty, characterizing the US-Israeli campaign as unprovoked aggression. A separate statement (“The U.S. blockade plunges Cuba into darkness”) defends the Cuban government against the oil embargo, correctly identifying the devastating impact of Trump’s January executive order but offering no criticism of the Cuban government’s own failures to develop energy independence over six decades or its deployment of security forces against protesting workers.

The PSL’s broader March output includes pieces on deportations (“an attack on all workers”), military production as “parasitic,” and the Epstein case. The tendency’s consistent orientation is toward popular-frontist anti-imperialism that defends bourgeois-nationalist and Stalinist regimes against US pressure without applying class analysis to those regimes themselves. The absence of any reference to independent working-class organization or socialist internationalism in the Cuba statement is characteristic.

Sources Liberation News PSL statement on the Israeli aggression against Iran · Liberation News The U.S. blockade plunges Cuba into darkness

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

Socialist Worker’s lead piece (“Trump looks for way out as Iran war exposes weakness,” by Arthur T) argues the war has backfired, exposing Western military and economic vulnerability rather than projecting imperial strength. The article notes 43% of Americans oppose the war and cites international reluctance from allies as evidence of declining US hegemony. The SWP’s framing of the crisis as primarily a story of Western “weakness” avoids the more fundamental question of why imperialist powers wage wars that produce such outcomes — namely, the crisis of the profit system itself.

The paper’s other major focus is the March 28 “Together” anti-far-right demonstration in London, with multiple articles organizing transport and building attendance. Coverage of the NHS’s £780 million trust deficit and demands for Labour PM Starmer’s resignation reflect the SWP’s orientation toward broad-front protest politics and pressure on the Labour government rather than building an independent revolutionary alternative. The SWP’s characteristic method — channeling political energy into single-issue demonstrations organized through front organizations (Stand Up To Racism, Together Alliance) — remains unchanged.

Sources Socialist Worker Trump looks for way out as Iran war exposes weakness · Socialist Worker Workers unite for Together demo

RCP / IMT

The Revolutionary Communist Party (formerly Socialist Appeal), the British section of the IMT, is focused primarily on internal party-building ahead of its May national congress, with the headline appeal “Forward to 1,400 members!” The tendency claims to have grown from 700 to 1,200 members in recent years through its “Are you a communist?” recruitment campaign. Workers’ Liberty has noted the RCP “has all but disappeared from recent Gaza and anti-far-right demonstrations” and is “scarcely visible in unions,” suggesting the organization is consolidating recruits through internal “Lenin schools” rather than engaging in external class struggle.

The RCP’s statement that 2026 has been marked by “titanic events” shaking the world remains at the level of abstraction — the tendency’s output shows limited concrete engagement with the Iran war, the global oil crisis, or the labor struggles documented elsewhere in this briefing. The IMT’s characteristic method of building a revolutionary organization through cadre education rather than class-struggle intervention remains its defining feature.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Cuban workers under a double siege — US oil embargo meets decade-long infrastructure collapse

The Morón protests are being framed by Western media as anti-communist uprising and by Cuban state media as foreign-directed vandalism. Neither framing addresses the material foundation: Trump’s January 2026 executive order has cut off all oil supply for three months, collapsing Cuba’s thermoelectric grid. A WSWS article could analyze the protests as an expression of working-class desperation — not a US-backed color revolution — while also examining the Cuban government’s failure to develop energy alternatives over six decades and its deployment of security forces against protesting workers.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Protests erupt in Cuba as US restrictions spark food, energy shortages

2. 100,000 federal workers used as hostages in immigration funding standoff

The DHS shutdown is being reported as an airport-disruption story, obscuring that over 100,000 workers are being denied wages for political leverage — a practice that in any other context would constitute wage theft. TSA workers are rationing food, skipping medical care, and depleting retirement savings. The legal obligation to work without pay while Congress debates immigration enforcement reveals deep structural vulnerabilities in how the US state treats its public-sector workforce.

Recommended reading NPR TSA workers miss paycheck amid DHS shutdown

3. North Korea’s $14.4 billion war economy and the collapse of the international sanctions regime

A new report estimates North Korea has earned up to $14.4 billion in hard currency and advanced military technology from supplying Russia with troops and weapons since 2023. This represents a near-total collapse of the sanctions architecture designed to economically isolate Pyongyang, and illuminates how Russia is financing an increasingly global war coalition. The WSWS could analyze what the Russia-DPRK axis means for the broader imperialist confrontation and the arms trade that sustains it.

Recommended reading Korea Times N. Korea estimated to have earned up to $14.4 billion from Russia-Ukraine war

4. Asian workers bear the heaviest burden of the Hormuz oil shock

84% of Hormuz oil exports transit to Asia. While coverage focuses on US and European economic projections, the real human cost falls on working-class people in Cambodia (+68% petrol), Vietnam (+50%), Nigeria (+35%), and India, where the rupee is under severe pressure and subsidized fuel programs are strained. The WSWS could report on workers who cannot afford to get to their jobs and farmers who cannot afford fertilizer for the coming planting season — the class dimensions of the oil shock that IMF forecasts obscure.

Recommended reading Time How the Iran war is impacting economies in Asia

5. The FCC’s wartime censorship threat and the collapse of press independence

FCC Chair Brendan Carr’s threat to revoke broadcast licenses over Iran war coverage warrants sustained WSWS analysis as a qualitative escalation in state management of wartime information — comparable to the Espionage Act prosecutions of World War I or post-9/11 pressure on networks to air Pentagon-approved coverage. Even Republican Senator Ron Johnson has objected, but the structural power of the FCC over broadcast licenses means the threat is real and represents a new stage in the erosion of democratic rights under conditions of war.

Recommended reading Guardian Republican rebukes FCC chair’s threats to revoke broadcast licenses over Iran war

6. Iran’s civilian death toll and the legal vacuum surrounding US-Israeli strikes

With 1,444 confirmed dead in Iran — including at least 206 children per human rights monitors — and 7,600+ Israeli strikes in 17 days, virtually no mainstream coverage addresses whether these strikes comply with international humanitarian law. The UN General Assembly has not been convened in emergency session. The absence of any legal accountability framework is itself a major story that the WSWS is uniquely positioned to analyze from the standpoint of imperialist war and international law.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Map shows how 17 days of attacks have evolved in the US-Israel war on Iran

7. Peru’s indefinite transport strike over fuel prices and Petroperu privatization

Truck and taxi drivers across Peru have launched an indefinite strike over gasoline prices exceeding $6.40/gallon — driven by the Hormuz disruption — and the government’s planned privatization of state oil company Petroperu. The strike connects the global oil crisis directly to working-class resistance in Latin America, and the privatization dimension links it to the WSWS’s broader coverage of austerity and state asset stripping across the continent.
