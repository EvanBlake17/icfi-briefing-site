1. US-Israeli war on Iran enters day 65 as Trump launches “Project Freedom” naval escort

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its 65th day on Sunday with Donald Trump announcing on Truth Social that the US Navy would commence “Project Freedom” — a program to escort commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz, with any Iranian interference to be “dealt with forcefully.” Trump described the operation as a “humanitarian gesture.” Iran has submitted a 14-point peace plan to mediators demanding an end to all hostilities within 30 days (rejecting Washington’s two-month proposal), guarantees against future US military aggression, withdrawal of US forces from Iran’s periphery, and an end to the naval blockade. Trump said discussions are “very positive” but had previously rejected Tehran’s earlier proposal.

As Kevin Reed wrote on the WSWS, behind the rhetoric of “freedom of navigation” the Pentagon is “weighing the deployment of ground forces to occupy portions of the strait” while requesting hypersonic missile production accelerated for the theater.

Key facts: - Preliminary death tolls: ~3,375 killed in Iran, 2,509 in Lebanon, 28 in Gulf states since the offensive began February 28; Lebanon’s Health Ministry reports 2,618 deaths and 8,094 wounded since March 2 alone, with recent strikes killing 41 over several days - Approximately 300 commercial ships carrying ~20,000 seafarers remain stranded in the Persian Gulf, with the shutdown halting 12 million barrels of daily oil exports — roughly 20% of global daily oil and LNG transit - US average gas prices have risen to $4.45/gallon, up $1.47 (+49.3%) since February 28 - The blockade has disrupted one-third of the world’s essential fertilizer chemical supply - Iran’s 14-point plan includes ceasefire terms and blockade easing; US signals on whether the plan is being taken seriously have been contradictory

2. Israel threatens to resume full Gaza war as truce frays

Israel signalled over the weekend that it is prepared to resume large-scale military operations in Gaza unless Hamas accepts a Cairo-mediated framework for complete disarmament within 281 days, broken into five stages. Hamas has refused to decouple disarmament from a political horizon — concretely, a path to a Palestinian state. Israel has expanded its territorial control to 59% of the strip by progressively pushing the truce-established “Yellow Line” westward, while at the same time intercepting Gaza-bound humanitarian aid boats in international waters, drawing international condemnation.

The “ceasefire” that nominally took effect in October 2025 has never fully held: 828 Palestinians have been killed in Gaza since it began, and at least 1,081 Palestinians have been killed in the West Bank between October 7, 2023 and April 22, 2026 — including at least 235 children.

Key facts: - Cairo mediators are pressing Hamas to accept a five-stage disarmament timeline of 281 days - Israel now controls roughly 59% of Gaza territory, up from the original Yellow Line - 828 Palestinians killed in Gaza since October 2025 ceasefire; the pattern indicates a slow-motion expulsion under cover of the truce - Israeli interception of aid flotillas has prompted condemnation from multiple European foreign ministries - 235 of the 1,081 Palestinians killed in the West Bank since October 7, 2023 were children

3. Russia launches mass drone and missile assault on Ukraine; Trump-Zelensky deal reportedly 90–95% agreed

Russian forces conducted a sweeping offensive across the front and rear of Ukraine over the past 24 hours: 1 missile strike, 79 airstrikes dropping 254 guided aerial bombs, 9,870 kamikaze drones, and 3,203 shelling attacks (44 with multiple-launch rocket systems) according to Ukrainian military figures, alongside 141 recorded combat engagements. A Russian strike on a student dormitory at Dnipro National University killed at least one student. A separate strike on Chornomorsk in the Odesa region damaged 35 residential houses.

In parallel, Trump and Zelensky have reportedly agreed on 90–95% of a peace framework whose details remain undisclosed. A new UN special rapporteur report finds that at least 50,000 Russian soldiers — roughly one in ten of those deployed — have deserted since February 2022, exposing what Al Jazeera describes as the deepening class character of Putin’s war: conscripts drawn predominantly from poor regions and ethnic minorities are being thrown into “human wave” assaults while elites remain insulated.

4. Spirit Airlines collapses, eliminating 17,000 jobs in first major US airline failure in 25 years

Spirit Airlines ceased all operations at 3:00 AM Eastern on May 2, stranding thousands of passengers nationwide and eliminating 17,000 direct and indirect jobs. The carrier had filed for bankruptcy twice since 2024; its collapse was triggered by jet fuel costs that more than doubled — from roughly $2.10 to over $4 per gallon domestically and from €68 to €153 per barrel in Europe — driven by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. A failed JetBlue acquisition (blocked by the Biden DOJ) and a Pratt & Whitney engine defect that grounded large parts of the fleet had already weakened it.

The Trump administration proposed a $500 million federal bailout that would have given the government a 90% stake. It was blocked by Spirit’s major bondholders, including Ken Griffin’s Citadel and Ares Management Corp.

As Tom Hall wrote on the WSWS, oil majors face projected $234 billion in additional 2026 profits while major banks reported $47.6 billion in Q1 profits — financial flows that “could be expropriated to compensate every Spirit worker many times over.”

Key facts: - 17,000 direct and indirect jobs eliminated; first major US airline collapse in approximately 25 years - $500 million federal bailout vetoed by hedge fund creditors led by Citadel and Ares - Spirit was the largest US ultra-low-cost carrier serving price-sensitive working-class travelers - Spirit had carried $8.1 billion in debt from its August 2025 Chapter 11 filing and operated without fuel hedging - United Airlines projects $11 billion in additional annual fuel costs; Lufthansa is cutting 20,000 short-haul flights this year

5. US oil blockade pushes Cuba to brink of societal collapse

UN Secretary-General António Guterres said over the weekend he is “extremely concerned” about Cuba’s humanitarian situation and warned it “will worsen, or even collapse” without imminent oil deliveries. Since February 2026, the US has been intercepting oil tankers bound for the island and threatening Mexico’s state-owned Pemex and other suppliers with tariffs. Mexico announced what it called a “sovereign decision” to halt shipments, leaving Cuba with a 90% reduction in total fuel imports.

The UN is now coordinating relief for ~300,000 elderly Cubans living alone, 100,000 people with disabilities, and 32,000 pregnant women. China’s Xi Jinping approved $80 million in aid plus 60,000 tonnes of rice; Canada has pledged C$8 million for food programs. The fuel collapse is rapidly producing the conditions of mass mortality.

Key facts: - Over 96,000 surgeries postponed, including 11,000 for children - The National Immunization Programme has been delayed for thousands of infants - Roughly one million Cubans now depend on water trucking, severely constrained by diesel shortages - 90% reduction in total Cuban fuel imports since the US began intercepting tankers in February - Fuel-dependent hospitals, dialysis units, and water pumping stations are progressively going offline

6. Federal appeals court issues nationwide ban on mail and telemedicine access to mifepristone

A three-judge panel of the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals ruled on May 1 that mifepristone — used in nearly two-thirds of US abortions — can only be dispensed through in-person clinic visits nationwide, eliminating the telemedicine and mail-order access reinstated by the FDA in 2021. All three judges were Republican appointees (two Trump, one Bush). The case is now expected to move to the Supreme Court.

The ruling disproportionately affects more than 100,000 patients annually in abortion-ban states who relied on mail services, as well as working-class women in every state who lack transportation, childcare flexibility, or paid time off. Mifepristone has been on the US market since FDA approval in 2000 with mortality now standing at 0.0005% — comparable to common over-the-counter analgesics. As Kate Randall wrote on the WSWS, HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. initiated the underlying FDA “safety review” citing a non-peer-reviewed paper from a conservative think tank.

Key facts: - Mifepristone used in roughly two-thirds of all US abortions - 100,000+ patients per year in ban states relied on mailed pills - All three Fifth Circuit panelists are Republican appointees; mifepristone mortality at 0.0005% - Decades of Democratic Party failure to codify Roe v. Wade and continued enforcement of the Hyde Amendment created the legal terrain for the ruling - Telemedicine appointments — disproportionately used by rural and low-income patients — are immediately stripped of legal protection

8. IMF warns Iran war has darkened global outlook; growth cut to 3.1%

The IMF has cut its 2026 global growth forecast to 3.1% with headline inflation at 4.4%, citing the simultaneous closure of the Strait of Hormuz and the Afghanistan-Pakistan war’s disruption of Central Asian trade routes. Global freight rates have risen ~18% since hostilities against Iran began. Trump’s compounded China tariffs — raised by 145 percentage points through April 2025 — represent the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, and the average per-household tariff burden in 2026 is approximately $1,500. US imports from China are now roughly half their pre-tariff levels, and Beijing has effectively halted purchases of US agricultural and manufactured goods. The IMF warns these are not transitory shocks but a structural rewiring of global trade along militarised geopolitical lines.

Afghanistan-Pakistan war displaces 115,000 in Operation Ghazab

Pakistan launched Operation Ghazab lil Haq in late February with airstrikes on Nangarhar, Paktika and Khost provinces, targeting alleged TTP and ISIS-K camps. Taliban forces have retaliated by striking Pakistani military border outposts. UNHCR estimates 115,000 people have been displaced inside Afghanistan with another 3,000 in Pakistan; 56 Afghan civilians have been confirmed killed including 24 children and 6 women, with 129 wounded. The conflict is disrupting Central Asian airspace and overland trade and, alongside the Hormuz blockade, is one of two factors the IMF cites for its downgraded 2026 outlook. The root dispute remains the British-imposed Durand Line of 1893, which the Taliban government has never recognised as a legitimate border. UN High Commissioner for Human Rights Volker Türk has appealed for “dialogue” while Western governments — Pakistan’s military is a longtime recipient of US arms — remain largely silent.

Hantavirus deaths aboard polar expedition vessel MV Hondius

Six passengers aboard the polar expedition vessel MV Hondius — carrying ~170 passengers and 70 crew en route from Argentina to Cape Verde — fell ill in the past week; three have died and one remains in intensive care. The WHO has confirmed hantavirus in one case and classified five others as suspected, working with national health authorities in affected countries. Hantavirus is rodent-borne, transmitted via infected rodents’ urine, droppings, or saliva; some strains carry case-fatality rates as high as 40% and there is no approved vaccine or specific antiviral. Human-to-human transmission, though rare, has been documented in some strains, which is why WHO is treating the outbreak as a possible cluster. This is the first major hantavirus cluster aboard a passenger vessel in recent memory and raises questions about rodent control in long-haul expedition shipping.

Australian Royal Commission interim report on Bondi attack suppresses chapter on intelligence services

The interim Royal Commission report on the December 14 Bondi Beach attack, which killed 15 people at a Hanukkah event in Sydney, was released over the weekend with Chapter 5 — dealing with federal intelligence and policing agency activities — suppressed in its entirety. WSWS’s Oscar Grenfell documents that attacker Naveed Akram had been investigated by ASIO in 2019 after associating with a known Islamic State supporter, and a former ASIO informant claims he reported both attackers’ extremist links to his handlers. Despite a 31% increase in intelligence agency budgets from 2020–2025 and ASIO warnings in August 2024 that terrorism was “probable,” counter-terrorism funding declined and the Hanukkah event was assigned only minimal, intermittently-present police protection. The Albanese Labor government immediately accepted all 14 interim recommendations, including five kept from public view — confirming, the WSWS argues, that the Commission’s purpose is to expand security-state powers and suppress pro-Palestinian dissent under the cover of anti-antisemitism rhetoric.

Australian Labor government blocks return of citizens interned in Syria

The Albanese Labor government has twice blocked Australian citizens — predominantly women and children — from leaving the Al Roj internment camp in Syria. In February 2026 a group of eleven women and twenty-three children was turned back fifty kilometres into their journey toward Lebanon; in April a separate group of thirteen women and children with plane tickets to Australia was turned back after the Syrian regime stated “the Australian government had refused to receive them.” Prime Minister Anthony Albanese justified the policy by stating the citizens “made decisions to leave Australia to fight against Australia’s national interest for an ideological group.” The government has signalled readiness to impose Temporary Exclusion Orders or revoke passports — establishing, the WSWS argues, a precedent for stripping citizenship from anyone deemed to threaten “national interest,” a formulation that could readily extend to political dissent.

“May Day Strong” demonstrations sweep US cities

May Day 2026 demonstrations under the “May Day Strong” banner drew large crowds across US cities — Chicago, New York, Los Angeles, Detroit and others — invoking the 1886 Haymarket martyrs and protesting the Iran war, ICE raids and the Trump administration’s social cuts. Mainstream coverage focused on attendance and the union-Democratic Party alliance. As Kristina Betinis and Jerry White wrote on the WSWS, the rallies were tightly choreographed by the UAW, AFL-CIO and Democratic Party to contain rather than catalyse working-class resistance: UAW President Shawn Fain limited current industrial action to 10% of the workforce despite a 97% strike authorization vote, while Governor Tim Walz has overseen ICE cooperation in Minnesota and Senator Chuck Schumer has been advising the Trump White House on ICE “crisis management.” Congress meanwhile approved a $70 billion infusion to ICE and Border Patrol — making the Democratic Party, in the WSWS’s framing, an “active enforcer” of Trump’s deportation programme.

Harvard graduate student strike enters its third week

Harvard’s 4,000-member HGSU-UAW has been on strike since April 21 over wage increases of roughly 3% over four years — far below the 75% increase required for pay equity in Boston — alongside protections for non-citizen workers and an agency shop provision. Mike Ingram on the WSWS documents that Harvard’s non-tenure-track faculty bargaining committee cancelled a sympathy strike despite a 53% member vote to proceed, and that Columbia’s graduate workers, who voted 91.5% to authorize a strike, were prevented from walking out when UAW regional director Brandon Mancilla threatened trusteeship. Will Lehman, the rank-and-file UAW reformer, said: “The problem is the bureaucracy itself. A union leadership that refuses to respect strike votes…cannot be reformed. It must be abolished by a rank-and-file movement.”

Little Lake teachers’ strike shut down after 10 days

The Little Lake Education Association ended a 10-day walkout on April 29 — the first strike in the district’s 154-year history — before teachers had reviewed the full tentative agreement. The district serves a predominantly Hispanic working-class community near Los Angeles where 80% of students qualify for free or reduced lunch. The core dispute was a district proposal to end 55 years of full premium healthcare coverage, with one teacher calculating she would pay “$580 per month for coverage” under the new plan. United Teachers Los Angeles cancelled a threatened 77,000-person sympathy strike two days before the Little Lake strike began, eliminating potential citywide leverage. Teachers received only sparse details before ratification — a $1,000 bonus and a 90/10 healthcare split — with salary adjustments and class-size terms still undisclosed.

Ypsilanti utility board halts $1.25 billion University of Michigan nuclear-weapons data centre

The Ypsilanti Community Utilities Authority unanimously approved a 12-month moratorium on water and sewage services to new data centres on April 22, blocking a $1.25 billion facility jointly proposed by the University of Michigan and Los Alamos National Laboratory. The project would have occupied 144 acres, consumed 100 megawatts of electricity (enough for 75,000–100,000 homes), and required between 500,000 and 1 million gallons of fresh water per day — directly competing with civilian water needs. LANL Deputy Director J. Patrick Fitch confirmed that the supercomputers would support nuclear weapons research, including processing “secret restricted data.” WSWS reporter Stephen St. Clair connects the project to broader university militarisation, including the recent firing, smuggling charges, and deportation of five Chinese researchers and the suicide in March of Chinese postdoctoral researcher Danhao Wang following federal interrogation. Democratic Governor Gretchen Whitmer supports the data centre.

US measles cases approach 1,814

As of April 30, the US has confirmed 1,814 measles cases across 37 states and jurisdictions in 2026, with 93% (1,688) outbreak-associated. At the current pace, 2026 is on track to surpass 2025’s record 2,288 cases. PAHO has pushed its review of US measles elimination status — a status held since 2000 — to November 2026 as the CDC works to determine whether the 24 active outbreaks constitute endemic transmission. The acceleration is occurring against the backdrop of HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s sustained campaign against MMR vaccination uptake.

H5N1 fatal child case in Bangladesh

WHO tracked 13 avian and swine influenza cases in humans in Q1 2026, including one newly disclosed fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh. Domestically, no unusual influenza activity is currently detected in US surveillance systems, but H5N1 remains entrenched in US dairy cattle and poultry. Scientists warn the virus is now circulating in more species across more continents than ever previously recorded, and that its ongoing mutation trajectory continues to raise pandemic risk.

COVID-19 declining in 31 US states; WHO antigen meeting this month

As of April 28, COVID-19 is declining or likely declining in 31 US states; the CDC forecasts approximately 1,500 new laboratory-confirmed hospital admissions for the week ending May 2. Omicron-lineage variants continue to dominate globally. The WHO’s TAG-CO-VAC meets this month to decide the antigen composition for the next vaccine formulation cycle — a decision that will shape fall 2026 vaccination programmes worldwide.

Pentagon awards classified AI contracts to seven companies, blacklisting Anthropic

On May 1, the Pentagon signed classified-network AI agreements with OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, Nvidia, SpaceX, and the startup Reflection AI — explicitly excluding Anthropic, which Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth formally designated a “supply chain risk” in March after the company refused to accept terms permitting use of its Claude models for autonomous lethal weapons and mass domestic surveillance. Anthropic has sued the Trump administration to reverse the designation. CEO Dario Amodei recently held White House talks that both sides described as “productive,” with Trump suggesting a deal is possible. The fracture is the most significant to date between the US government and a major AI lab over safety guardrails — and it confirms how rapidly the leading commercial AI infrastructure is being absorbed into the US war machine.

NEJM HOPE-B and SURPASS-CVOT

The five-year final analysis of the HOPE-B trial (NEJM, n=54) found a single infusion of etranacogene dezaparvovec (Hemgenix) reduced annualized bleeding rates in hemophilia B patients by 63% and cut exogenous factor IX consumption by 96% (from ~257,000 IU/year to ~10,900 IU/year), with mean factor IX activity sustained at 36.1 IU/dL at five years — the longest follow-up to date for any approved hemophilia B gene therapy. Separately, the SURPASS-CVOT trial in 13,000+ patients with type 2 diabetes and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease found tirzepatide non-inferior to dulaglutide for the composite of CV death, MI or stroke (12.2% vs. 13.1%; HR 0.92; p=0.003 for non-inferiority), establishing tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) as cardiovascularly safe and comparable to the GLP-1 standard of care.

“Two Prosecutors”: Loznitsa film on the 1937 Great Purge banned in both Russia and Ukraine

Sergei Loznitsa’s Russian-language film Two Prosecutors, set during the 1937 Great Purge, follows a young provincial prosecutor named Kornyev who discovers a blood-written letter from imprisoned old Bolshevik Stepniak documenting NKVD-orchestrated torture and coerced false confessions. Kornyev travels to Moscow to present the evidence to chief prosecutor Andrey Vyshinsky, then returns home to be arrested himself. Reviewing the film for the WSWS, Fred Mazelis praises the lead performances and the film’s unflinching atmosphere of totalitarian dread, but identifies a critical political weakness: by lacking historical context explaining how Stalinism betrayed Bolshevism — specifically the doctrine of “socialism in one country” and the bureaucracy’s degeneration — the film leaves open the reactionary conclusion that revolution itself “inevitably” produces tyranny. The work’s dual banning in both Russia and Ukraine, where it has unsettled nationalists on both sides, is itself revealing: the historical truth of 1937 cuts across the official ideologies of both belligerent capitalist states. Loznitsa’s own liberal politics — and his expulsion from the Ukrainian Film Academy — illustrate the difficulty of treating this material from a perspective outside the narrow framework of bourgeois anti-communism.

Evan Blake’s announcement of an expanded Socialism AI platform — accessible in over 100 countries and capable of providing Marxist texts and analysis in real-time dialogue — situates the project within an older socialist tradition of universal popular education while using contemporary AI infrastructure that overwhelmingly serves military and commercial ends.

“From Port Huron to postmodernism: The class politics of the ‘New’ Students for a Democratic Society” — Nancy Hanover

Hanover traces SDS across six decades, arguing both its original (1960s) and revived (2006–present) forms have systematically substituted other social forces for the working class as the agent of revolutionary change. The 1962 Port Huron Statement, authored by Tom Hayden under the influence of ex-Trotskyist/social democrat Max Shachtman, explicitly disparaged a “labor metaphysic” and promoted liberal pluralism; this theoretical vacuum left the 1960s organization vulnerable to Maoism and Third-Worldism, culminating in the 1969 split and the Weather Underground’s terrorism. Herbert Marcuse provided intellectual cover by arguing American workers were too integrated into consumer capitalism to be revolutionary. Former SDS leaders subsequently joined the political establishment: Todd Gitlin backed NATO intervention in Yugoslavia; Tom Hayden became a California Democratic assemblyman; over 60 former SDS figures signed a 2020 letter endorsing Joe Biden. The 2006 SDS revival, founded by the Maoist-descended Freedom Road Socialist Organization, explicitly rejects having “a special political view” and focuses on identity-politics campaigns while collaborating with DSA and avoiding Democratic Party criticism. Hanover characterises this as representing the class interests of privileged middle-class layers serving “the need of the bourgeoisie to intercept a radicalization and subordinate it to the political requirements of the Democratic Party.”
