1. US launches second wave of strikes on Iran as ceasefire teeters

The fragile US-Iran ceasefire signed April 8 came close to collapse on May 28. US Central Command reported that Iran launched five attack drones and one ballistic missile at Kuwait, all intercepted by US and Kuwaiti forces, and said it had prevented a sixth drone launch from a ground-control site in Bandar Abbas. The exchange followed a reported second wave of US strikes on Iranian targets near the Strait of Hormuz, which sent oil futures sharply higher and rattled global markets.

Trump, who on Saturday claimed he had “largely negotiated” a memorandum of understanding to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, walked the claim back on Monday, posting only that “Negotiations with the Islamic Republic of Iran are proceeding nicely!” He has separately described the truce as on “life support,” with intermittent fighting continuing since May 4. The US naval blockade of Iranian shipping remains in force.

Key facts: - Sticking points include reopening the Strait of Hormuz, Iran’s uranium stockpile, and billions in frozen Iranian assets - The Hormuz closure has severely disrupted regional shipping and energy markets, with WTI crude up more than 1.7% intraday - Secretary of State Marco Rubio is leading the US negotiating posture; both sides claim talks are “progressing”

2. Israel escalates assault on Lebanon, killing at least 16 in a day

Israeli forces killed at least 16 people and wounded 58 across southern Lebanon on May 28, conducting more than 135 strikes in 24 hours. Six victims were from a single family, killed by a drone strike on the Adloun Highway as they fled north; a Lebanese soldier was killed near Nabatieh. Israel struck an apartment building in southern Beirut — its first strike on the capital in three weeks — and hit a cafe and residential building in Tyre.

The IDF issued mass displacement orders requiring evacuation north of the Zahrani River, roughly 40 km from the border. Since February 28, Lebanese health authorities report 3,269 killed and 9,840 wounded. A US-brokered ceasefire that began last month has failed to hold; US-mediated talks between Israel and Lebanon are scheduled to resume Thursday.

Key facts: - The renewed Beirut strike marks a sharp escalation after a relative lull - Displacement orders affecting territory up to 40 km from the border amount to the de facto depopulation of southern Lebanon - The strikes are coordinated with the broader US-Israeli regional offensive against Iran and its allies

3. Ebola declared a global emergency as Uganda seals DRC border

The WHO’s Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC), declared May 17, governs an Ebola epidemic in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda that has become the third-largest on record. As of May 27, there were 1,205 suspected and confirmed cases (1,077 suspected, 128 confirmed) and at least 264 deaths, including 18 confirmed. On May 22 the WHO upgraded the risk in the DRC to “very high.”

The outbreak is caused by the Bundibugyo virus, with an estimated fatality rate of 25–50% and no approved vaccine or treatment. Uganda closed its border with the DRC on May 27 for at least four weeks and imposed a 21-day isolation requirement for arrivals after cases reached the capital, Kampala. Conventional hospitals in Ituri Province are overwhelmed; field hospitals have been set up in Bunia. Ten countries are considered at risk of spillover. As Benjamin Mateus noted on the WSWS, the outbreak is rooted in “chronic underfunding of African health infrastructure, displacement from regional conflicts, and malnutrition” — and the Trump administration has responded with travel bans rather than treatment capacity (see WSWS coverage below).

4. Gaza catastrophe deepens as aid stays blocked

Roughly 1.6 million people — 77% of Gaza’s population — face acute food insecurity, the UN told the Security Council, while 94% of hospitals are destroyed or damaged and 51% of essential medicines are at zero stock. When the US-Israeli operations against Iran began on February 28, Israel closed all crossings into Gaza; weekly truck entries collapsed from an average of 4,200 to just 590.

UN High Representative Nickolay Mladenov warned that Gaza risks “permanent limbo,” with the phased ceasefire plan paralyzed over Hamas’s refusal to disarm. The UN 2026 Flash Appeal seeks $4 billion for nearly 3 million people but is only 13% funded. Human Rights Watch documented that Israel has continued to curb aid and kill civilians during the nominal ceasefire.

Key facts: - 80% of Gaza’s buildings are damaged or destroyed; over 1 million people have no permanent shelter; 1.8 million are in displacement sites - The ceasefire framework imposed no binding reconstruction or access obligations on Israel - Attention has shifted to the Iran war while the humanitarian collapse accelerates

Sources UN News Security Council hearing on Gaza · Human Rights Watch Gaza: Israel curbs aid, kills civilians during ceasefire · UNRWA Situation Report #220

5. Russia loses ground in Ukraine as Moscow and Minsk hold nuclear drills

Between April 28 and May 26, Russian forces suffered a net loss of 100 square miles of Ukrainian territory, with Ukraine gaining 38 square miles in the final week alone, according to the latest war report card. Russia still occupies roughly 20% of Ukraine and continues heavy bombardment of Ukrainian cities. A Ukrainian strike collapsed a five-story dormitory in Russian-occupied Starobilsk, killing at least four; Russia claims six dead and blames Kyiv, which denies targeting civilians.

Moscow and Minsk conducted strategic nuclear drills, with both Vladimir Putin and Alexander Lukashenko participating by video. Trump’s 20-point draft peace plan carries a June deadline; Russia has repeatedly refused ceasefire calls and direct negotiations with Volodymyr Zelensky.

Key facts: - 62% of Russians support peace negotiations; 61% of Ukrainians support territorial compromises to end the war - The nuclear drills signal escalation even as battlefield lines stagnate and both publics tire of the war - The June deadline injects new instability into an already volatile front

Sources Russia Matters Russia-Ukraine War Report Card, May 27, 2026 · CFR Global Conflict Tracker: War in Ukraine

6. Trump-backed Paxton ousts Cornyn in record Texas Senate runoff

Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton defeated incumbent Senator John Cornyn in the Republican Senate runoff on May 26, ending more than two decades of Cornyn’s electoral dominance. The Associated Press called the race shortly after polls closed. Cornyn — first elected in 2002 — becomes the first Republican senator from Texas ever denied his party’s renomination. Trump’s eleventh-hour endorsement, praising Paxton as a “true MAGA warrior,” proved decisive despite Republican-aligned PACs spending $85 million for Cornyn, nearly three times Paxton’s war chest.

At $128 million total, it was the most expensive state primary in US history. Cornyn’s vote total collapsed from roughly 900,000 in the April primary to barely 500,000 in the runoff, while Paxton held at 886,000. Paxton — previously impeached by the Republican-controlled state House on corruption charges but acquitted by the state Senate — now faces Democratic state Representative James Talarico, who raised $27 million in the first quarter alone, in a race polls show is close. As Patrick Martin wrote on the WSWS, Paxton helped lead efforts to overturn the 2020 election and addressed the January 6 rally.

7. US maritime strikes kill nearly 200 as Ecuadorian fishermen describe attacks

More than 50 US maritime strikes across the Eastern Pacific and Caribbean have killed nearly 200 people since operations began, the overwhelming majority working-class fishermen and sailors. Ecuadorian crews fishing near the Galápagos Islands told NPR they were attacked by drones launched from a US-flagged vessel, taken aboard, handcuffed, and handed over to Salvadoran officials. One fisherman, Palacios, was among 20 crew aboard the Don Maca in mid-March; NPR confirmed similar accounts from at least two other incidents off Ecuador’s coast.

The Trump administration claims the operations target narco-traffickers under US anti-drug authority in international waters but has provided no public evidence and has not identified the victims.

Key facts: - Crews describe being detained without charge, due process, or consular notification before rendition to third-country custody - The killings have gone largely unreported in the mainstream US press - The operations represent an extension of extrajudicial killing as a routine instrument of US policy in Latin America

Sudan: 20 million face acute hunger as famine spreads

Nearly 20 million people across Sudan face acute hunger as the civil war enters its fourth year, the UN and the IPC reported. More than 800,000 children risk severe malnutrition in 2026, and 135,000 people in North Darfur, South Darfur and South Kordofan — including in el-Fasher and Kadugli — face “catastrophic” (IPC Phase 5) conditions. Roughly 14 million people have been displaced, one of the largest displacement crises on earth.

  • No ceasefire is in sight; UN agencies warn conditions will deteriorate during the June–September lean season

China: gas blast kills 82 at illegal Shanxi coal mine

A gas explosion on May 22 at the privately owned Liushenyu Coal Mine in Qinyuan County, Shanxi Province killed at least 82 of the 247 workers who were underground; more than 120 were hospitalized. State media reported that executives of the operating company were detained and that the mine had been listed in 2024 among 1,128 facilities cited for “severe safety hazards.” The disaster — the deadliest Chinese mining accident in over a decade — was reported by NPR, PBS and CNN; the WSWS analysis by Peter Symonds details the systemic character of the failures (see WSWS coverage).

  • The mine secretly ran hidden shafts concealed behind fake walls of wire mesh sprayed with mortar
  • It failed to log 123 of the 247 underground workers in mandatory tracking systems
  • A retired mine manager told the WSWS the mine “had clearly breached a dangerous threshold, yet no one noticed”

Italy braces for third transport strike in a month

A national 24-hour general strike on May 29, called by grassroots base unions (CUB, SGB, SI Cobas, ADL Varese and USI-CIT), is set to disrupt Italy’s transport network for the third time in a single month, with air, rail, ferry and motorway services affected and walkouts also expected in schools, healthcare and public administration. Organizers cite declining real wages, welfare cuts, rising military spending and complicity in Gaza. The strike caps nine months of mass action that began with a general strike in September 2025 and a one-million-strong demonstration in Rome on October 4, during which dockworkers refused to load weapons bound for Israel.

  • Absolute poverty has reached an all-time high of 5.7 million people, or 9.8% of the population
  • The Meloni government has slashed social services to meet EU fiscal rules and NATO spending targets
  • Bourgeois coverage frames the strike as traveler inconvenience; today’s WSWS Perspective calls for rank-and-file committees independent of the CGIL, CISL and UIL bureaucracies

Chemical leak forces evacuation of 50,000 in Garden Grove, California

A methyl methacrylate (MMA) tank began overheating and off-gassing at GKN Aerospace’s Garden Grove facility, forcing the evacuation of about 50,000 residents across Garden Grove, West Anaheim, Cypress, Buena Park and Stanton amid the risk of a thermal-runaway explosion. Evacuation orders were issued, lifted, reissued and only fully lifted Tuesday night after the tank cracked and released pressure. A class-action lawsuit was filed against GKN on May 23. At a city council meeting, residents demanded the facility — which manufactures F-35 components and has previously paid $5 million in negligence compensation — be permanently closed.

  • One displaced family with two infants was living in their van after evacuation funds ran out; a medical worker with asthma reported persistent gas odors

US measles cases top 1,950 as elimination status wavers

The US has recorded 1,952 confirmed measles cases as of May 21 across 40 jurisdictions, with 93% outbreak-associated — already on pace to surpass the 2,288 cases recorded for all of 2025. MMR vaccination coverage in children has fallen from 95.2% (2019–20) to 92.5% (2024–25). The CDC and WHO will formally evaluate in November whether the US retains its measles elimination status; losing it would be the first such reversal since elimination was declared in 2000.

First human death from H5N5 avian flu reported in NEJM

A case report in the New England Journal of Medicine describes the first globally confirmed human infection with HPAI A(H5N5) — a 75-year-old Washington State woman with Waldenström macroglobulinemia who died on illness day 28. Contact tracing found no secondary cases. Separately, the WHO logged 13 avian and swine influenza human cases in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh and three additional H5N1 cases in Cambodia. US dairy-herd outbreaks continue with sporadic farm-worker cases and no confirmed human-to-human transmission.

Arts and culture

The death of Sonny Rollins, the tenor saxophonist universally known as the “Saxophone Colossus,” dominates the cultural week. Rollins died May 25 at his home in Woodstock, New York, at the age of 95. With John Coltrane and Charlie Parker, he was among the most influential saxophonists of the twentieth century and one of the last surviving giants of the bebop and hard-bop eras.

Born September 7, 1930, in New York City, Rollins grew up on Harlem’s Sugar Hill, the “strivers’ row” where many of the era’s leading jazz musicians lived. His 1956 album Saxophone Colossus — and its defining track “St. Thomas,” drawn from his Caribbean heritage — established him as a peerless improviser whose long, architecturally constructed solos rewrote the language of the instrument. His artistic seriousness was legendary: at the height of his fame in the late 1950s he withdrew from public performance for two years, practicing alone at night on the walkway of the Williamsburg Bridge; his 1962 return, the album The Bridge, was greeted as a cultural event.

Rollins embodied the deep connection between jazz and the social struggles of the Black working class and the broader democratic movements of the mid-twentieth century — his 1958 Freedom Suite was one of the first explicit musical statements against racial oppression in the United States. His passing severs one of the last living links to a period in which a popular American art form reached extraordinary heights of collective and individual creativity. The tributes from the Washington Post, NPR, Rolling Stone and CNN reflect a recognition, even in the bourgeois press, that an irreplaceable artist has been lost.

The month has been heavy with losses across the arts:

  • Dennis Locorriere, lead singer of Dr. Hook, died May 16 of kidney disease at 76; the group scored six US Top 10 hits including “Sylvia’s Mother”
  • Tom Kane, the prolific voice actor (Professor Utonium in The Powerpuff Girls), died May 18 at 64 of complications from a stroke
  • Grizz Chapman, the actor known from 30 Rock, died May 22 at 52 after years of illness and dialysis — a reminder of the toll of inadequate healthcare access
  • Rob Base, the hip-hop artist, died of cancer at 59
  • Claudine Longet, French singer and actress, died May 15 at 84

On the festival circuit, the Lanzarote International Film Festival (May 13–23) spotlighted debut features from Romania, Bulgaria and Ukraine, while the Smithsonian’s IlluminAsia festival continued in Washington. Such programming, foregrounding work from smaller national cinemas and immigrant communities, stands in contrast to the increasingly consolidated, franchise-driven output of the major studios — a tension between genuine artistic exploration and the commodification of culture that the WSWS arts coverage consistently examines.

Perspective published: May 29 general strike in Italy: What way forward for the working class? by the European Editorial Board of the WSWS — read in full on wsws.org. WSWS speaks to Australian disability advocate Megan Spindler-Smith on Labor’s NDIS cuts — Richard Phillips and Mark Wilson

PWDA Acting CEO Megan Spindler-Smith details the Albanese Labor government’s proposed $35–38 billion in NDIS cuts — the largest reduction to any Australian social program in history — which would remove roughly 300,000 disabled people from the scheme. New “functional assessment” criteria replacing diagnosis-based eligibility create socioeconomic barriers, since those who cannot afford private treatment face greater difficulty demonstrating disability, while ministerial discretion to eliminate entire support categories guts appeal rights — even though 90% of 2024 tribunal appeals favored participants. Two deaths are directly attributed to funding cuts: Koa Gibson, age 4, and Noah Johnston, age 22, both losing nursing care. The article’s conclusion is that Labor is administering the same austerity agenda as the Coalition, dismantling a program that returns $2.25 to the economy for every dollar spent.

Tony Abbott to become Australian Liberal Party president in further lurch to right — Oscar Grenfell

Former PM Tony Abbott is set to be elected unopposed as federal president of the Liberal Party following its catastrophic May 2025 result — its lowest primary vote since 1944, retaining just nine of 88 metropolitan seats. The WSWS argues the Liberals’ pivot toward One Nation-style anti-immigration rhetoric is a dead end because the party cannot credibly pose as anti-establishment, and that the rightward drift of all bourgeois parties reflects capitalism’s structural crisis.

‘You’re setting an example for everybody’: Big Three workers back Nexteer rank and file — Jerry White

Nexteer Automotive workers in Saginaw, Michigan are preparing to vote on a third tentative agreement after rejecting two previous ones, with 86% voting to authorize a strike after the second rejection. Workers note that production workers earning $27/hour by 2030 would earn the same in nominal terms as in 2005 — a real-terms cut of roughly $1/hour annually over two decades, during which a pickup’s price nearly doubled from $36,000 to $65,000. Ford Rouge workers pledged through delegate candidate Martaz Crutchfield to block scab parts if Nexteer strikes. The article cites 85-year-old retired GM worker Lyle criticizing UAW President Shawn Fain for “rhetoric” while advancing company interests, and recalls that 1930s labor victories were won through rank-and-file wildcat action. A Nexteer strike could cascade through just-in-time supply chains to halt GM, Ford and Stellantis assembly plants.

ICE brutalizes protesters at detention center in New Jersey — Erik Schreiber

Supplementing the US section: the detainees launched their hunger and labor strike May 22 over conditions including live worms in food, no air conditioning, denial of bond and poor medical care; guards cut phone and tablet access after detainees publicized them. Trump weaponizes public health as Ebola epidemic expands — Benjamin Mateus

Beyond the Ebola facts in Top stories: the Trump administration invoked Title 42 on May 18 to bar noncitizens from the DRC, Uganda and South Sudan, then extended the restrictions to lawful permanent residents (green card holders). The foreseeable catastrophe: How GKN Aerospace, regulators and the Teamsters built the Garden Grove disaster — Marc Wells

This investigation traces nearly two decades of negligence. Cal/OSHA’s 2018 citation for ten chemical-storage violations sought just $2,898 in penalties for a plant with £136 million in annual revenue. Crucially, Teamsters Local 952 filed an unfair labor practice charge against GKN in April 2024 — then withdrew it without explanation one month later, abandoning leverage before any remedy. Cal/OSHA operated at a 32% staff vacancy rate and routinely reduced fines on appeal.

Unifor President Lana Payne named Ford Motor Canada as the lead company for master bargaining (beginning June 22) for 16,000 Canadian autoworkers, with both sides praising their “strong, long-term relationship.” Since Unifor’s 1985 split from the UAW, nine Detroit Three plants have closed in Canada; the 2023 pattern contract barely passed at 54% and introduced an Alternative Work Schedule surrendering eight-hour-day and overtime protections. Bargaining chair John D’Agnolo told workers they “mustn’t expect too much.” The piece situates the talks within the Trump trade war and EV restructuring that have already cut thousands of jobs.

Jacobin and the DSA

Jacobin’s lead pieces this cycle were “The Democrats Are Determined Not to Learn From Their Failure” and “The Era of Central Bank Independence Is Coming to an End.” The first criticizes the Democrats’ 2024 autopsy for ignoring Gaza and dismissing the “left-populist upsurge embodied by Zohran Mamdani” — the essential program: not a break from the Democratic Party but its rehabilitation through a progressive figurehead. The second treats the politicization of the Federal Reserve as a regulatory problem to be managed rather than an expression of capitalist breakdown. Jacobin also ran sympathetic labor coverage — “The Long, Grueling Strike at Henry Ford Genesys Hospital” and “Workers Are Demanding a Share of Samsung’s AI Windfall” — that documents strikes while studiously avoiding any critique of the union officialdom that contains them. Overall orientation: to channel working-class radicalization back into the Democratic Party and the trade union apparatus.

Sources Jacobin The Democrats are determined not to learn from their failure · Jacobin The long, grueling strike at Henry Ford Genesys Hospital

Left Voice

Left Voice continues to revolve around “The Mamdani Moment and the Fight Against the Far Right,” the framing of its magazine, alongside “Zohran Mamdani’s Primary Win: What Does It Mean for the Left?” Despite Trotskyist phraseology and formal criticisms of Mamdani’s Democratic Party affiliation, the group treats his campaign as the central arena for “the rank and file of the Left,” lending it left cover rather than fighting for an independent revolutionary party. Its internationalism amounts to drawing “lessons from the workers’ movement in Milei’s Argentina” via the PTS while abstaining from the concrete fight to build sections of the Fourth International. Overall orientation: centrist adaptation to the same Democratic Party milieu as the DSA, dressed in more militant language.

Sources Left Voice Introducing Left Voice Magazine: The Mamdani Moment · Left Voice Zohran Mamdani’s primary win: What does it mean for the left?

Liberation News and the PSL

The PSL’s output centers on “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” and “Why Trump failed in his criminal war against Iran — and why we need to keep up the pressure.” While correctly opposing the US war, the PSL frames events through a campist lens that presents the Iranian bourgeois-clerical regime as an anti-imperialist force and obscures the class interests of Iranian workers, who confront both imperialism and their own ruling class. The party simultaneously promotes its expanding network of “Liberation Centers,” with new openings planned for Houston and Washington — organizational growth substituting for a worked-out revolutionary program. Overall orientation: Stalinist-derived campism that subordinates the working class to bourgeois nationalist regimes abroad and protest politics at home.

Sources Liberation News PSL statement: Stop Trump’s war on Iran! · Liberation News Why Trump failed in his criminal war against Iran

Socialist Alternative

The ISA-affiliated group’s featured piece, “Uprising in Bolivia — Bring Down Rodrigo Paz By the Power of the Streets!”, captures its characteristic method: militant-sounding calls to topple a government through mass mobilization, with no perspective for the conquest of power by the working class or the building of a revolutionary party. The “power of the streets” formulation substitutes spontaneous protest for the question of state power and political leadership. Overall orientation: left-populist spontaneism that repeatedly leaves mass movements without a strategy once the immediate target falls.

Sources Socialist Alternative Uprising in Bolivia — bring down Rodrigo Paz by the power of the streets!

Socialist Worker (SWP)

The British SWP’s coverage — “Elections 2026: Reform UK surges as mainstream politics fractures” alongside reports on school strikes in Waltham Forest and Brent and on attacks on trans+ rights — registers the collapse of the political center but answers it with appeals to build a “broad left” out of electoral fragments rather than a revolutionary party. Its conference materials (“resisting the right, building the left”) subordinate the SWP’s identity to amorphous unity projects. Overall orientation: chasing left-reformist regroupment in response to the far right, without a fight for working-class political independence.

Sources Socialist Worker Elections 2026: Reform UK surges as mainstream politics fractures · Socialist Worker SWP conference 2026: resisting the right, building the left

The British RCP’s output remains dominated by self-referential organizational announcements — its 2026 Organisational Resolution (“Raising our sights and our profile”), Congress reports, and the May Day call to “Make May Day Communist Again!” The relentless emphasis on recruitment and “profile” substitutes a propaganda-society conception of party-building for intervention in the actual struggles of the working class — notably absent from its featured material are the live battles at Nexteer, in Italy, or among Australian disabled workers. Overall orientation: abstract propagandism and triumphalist self-promotion detached from the concrete class struggle.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. US foreign aid cuts linked to more than 350,000 deaths since January

The dismantling of USAID and the firing of 250 State Department foreign service officers in May have been associated with over 350,000 deaths globally — collapsing HIV treatment, maternal health and famine-relief pipelines across Africa and Asia. The mainstream press frames this as a policy debate; the class content is that the world’s poorest workers and peasants are dying to finance US domestic tax cuts and military buildup. This is a defining indictment of imperialist barbarism that demands sustained WSWS coverage.

Recommended reading CNN As global crises multiply, scores of US diplomats forced out

2. Bundibugyo Ebola and the missing vaccine: a pharmaceutical industry indictment

The third-largest Ebola outbreak in history is spreading through one of the world’s poorest regions, yet there is no approved vaccine or treatment for the Bundibugyo strain — because the industry has no profit incentive to develop one. The existing Ervebo vaccine was developed for a different strain and offers only partial cross-protection. The WSWS covered the Title 42 travel bans; a companion piece on the structural failure of for-profit pharmaceutical development would deepen the analysis.

Recommended reading WHO Ebola in DRC and Uganda determined a PHEIC

3. First confirmed human death from H5N5 avian flu

The NEJM has documented the first-ever fatal human H5N5 infection, expanding the avian-influenza surveillance target beyond H5N1 even as a fatal pediatric H5N1 case surfaces in Bangladesh. With US public health agencies being gutted, the emergence of a novel fatal H5 subtype underscores the danger of dismantling pandemic preparedness — a key theme of WSWS science coverage.

Recommended reading NEJM Human infection with HPAI A(H5N5) virus

4. Trump’s tariff: the largest US tax hike in 33 years falls on workers

Trump’s 10% global tariff is the single largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, but it is sharply regressive — working-class households spending more of their income on apparel, toys, furniture and electronics (all up 0.6–0.8%) bear a disproportionate burden. The “trade war” framing obscures that it functions as a consumption tax on workers to fund corporate tax cuts, and the court fight to strike it down is led by business, not labor.

Recommended reading Tax Foundation Trump tariffs and the trade war, 2026

5. The eight-month nurses’ strike at Henry Ford Genesys

One of the longest nurse walkouts in recent US history has continued for eight months at Henry Ford Genesys Hospital in Michigan, even as the corporate hospital chain and the union bureaucracy let it grind on in isolation. The strike exemplifies both the militancy of healthcare workers and the role of the union apparatus in containing it — a natural subject for the WSWS Health Care Workers Newsletter and the rank-and-file committee perspective.

Recommended reading Jacobin The long, grueling strike at Henry Ford Genesys Hospital

6. Sonny Rollins (1930–2026): an appreciation of the Saxophone Colossus

The death of Sonny Rollins severs one of the last living links to the golden age of bebop and hard bop and to a popular art form bound up with the social struggles of the Black working class. A full WSWS appreciation — situating his Freedom Suite, his artistic integrity and his Williamsburg Bridge years in their historical and social context — would honor a genuine artistic achievement while examining the conditions under which such creativity flourished and is now endangered.

Recommended reading NPR Sonny Rollins, colossus of the saxophone, has died at 95
