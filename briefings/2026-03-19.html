1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 19 as Gulf energy infrastructure is struck

The US-Israeli air campaign against Iran entered its 19th day on March 19 with a dramatic escalation in targeting of energy infrastructure across the Persian Gulf. Israel struck Iran’s South Pars gasfield — the world’s largest natural gas field, providing roughly 75% of Iran’s gas output — prompting Iranian retaliatory missile strikes on Qatar’s Ras Laffan LNG terminal that caused “extensive damage.” Qatar expelled Iranian military and security attachés in response, while Saudi Arabia declared “the little trust that remained in Iran has completely shattered.”

Iran’s Ministry of Health reports 1,444 civilians killed and 18,551 injured since the campaign began February 28. The Hengaw Human Rights organization estimates over 4,800 Iranian military personnel have been killed. Iran’s Red Crescent reports at least 47,000 residential units destroyed.

Key developments:

  • Kent resignation: Senior counterterrorism official Joe Kent resigned, telling Tucker Carlson: “We started this war due to pressure from Israel and its powerful American lobby.” Trump called the resignation “a good thing.” VP Vance personally attempted to dissuade Kent before the resignation.
  • Intelligence contradictions: DNI Tulsi Gabbard testified that Iran was “not rebuilding uranium enrichment” prior to the war, undermining the official casus belli. She stated the Iranian regime “appears intact, although largely degraded.”
  • NATO fractures: German Chancellor Friedrich Merz stated Berlin “would have advised against” the war. Trump threatened to quit NATO over allies’ refusal to send warships to the Strait of Hormuz.
  • Regional spread: Iraqi armed group Saraya Awliya al-Dam claimed 28 drone strikes across Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Jordan over 15 days. Thirteen US soldiers have been killed so far.
  • $200 billion funding request: The Pentagon has requested over $200 billion for Iran operations — exceeding total Ukraine aid and surpassing peak annual Iraq War costs — with 50,000 US troops deployed and three carrier strike groups in the region.
  • Russia-Iran intelligence link: US intelligence reports confirm Russia is supplying Iran with targeting intelligence on US troops, warships, and aircraft, while Ukraine is helping intercept Iranian drones for the US and Arab allies.

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, Democratic leaders voted for the defense budgets funding this war machine, and criticize Trump only for insufficient aggression toward Russia and China — revealing bipartisan commitment to imperial warfare.

The Federal Reserve held its benchmark rate at 3.50–3.75% on March 18 in an 11-1 vote, with Governor Stephen Miran the lone dissenter favoring a 25 basis point cut. The FOMC’s updated dot plot placed the 2026 median fed funds rate at 3.4%, signaling virtually no easing this year. The statement explicitly cited “elevated uncertainty” and flagged Middle East developments as a risk factor.

3. BP locks out 900 workers at Midwest’s largest refinery

BP initiated a lockout of approximately 900 United Steelworkers Local 7-1 members at its Whiting, Indiana refinery at midnight on March 19 — the largest refinery lockout in the US in years. The Whiting facility is the Midwest’s largest, processing 440,000 barrels of crude oil daily. The lockout follows workers’ decisive rejection of BP’s “last, best and final” offer by 98.3% on 94% turnout.

Key facts:

  • BP’s rejected offer would have eliminated over 100 union positions, expanded contract labor, cut wages by $8–10 per hour, reduced the signing bonus from $7,500 to $2,500, and removed the facility from national pattern bargaining
  • The company is also seeking to install AI surveillance systems without worker protections and strip seniority protections for layoffs
  • USW president Eric Schultz stated: “They continue to demand that we cut more than 100 jobs, accept pay cuts to nearly all positions and give up our bargaining rights”
  • BP claims operations will continue with a trained replacement team and no disruptions are expected
  • Analysts warn any operational disruptions could exacerbate an already tight global refined-products market, with gasoline and diesel costs rising due to the Middle East war

The previous three-year collective bargaining agreement expired January 31, 2026, and the refinery had been operating under rolling 24-hour contract extensions.

4. JBS meatpacking strike enters third day in largest beef industry walkout since 1980s

Approximately 3,800 workers at the JBS meatpacking plant in Greeley, Colorado entered their third day on the picket line on March 19 in the first strike at a US beef slaughterhouse since the 1980s. The Greeley facility processes 5,000–6,000 head of cattle daily, representing approximately 8% of US beef production. Workers voted 99% to authorize the strike after eight months without a contract.

Key facts:

  • The UFCW Local 7 contract with JBS expired last July; the union accuses JBS of failing to meet demands on wages, safety equipment, and healthcare
  • Workers report being forced to use dull knives that increase the risk of blade slippage and wearing protective clothing with holes in the steel mesh
  • Sanitation workers earn $23.75/hour but are typically paid for only six of their overnight hours
  • JBS’s workforce is largely immigrant, with many facing deportation threats under current immigration enforcement
  • Beef prices have risen 15.2% in the last year due to the smallest US herd size in 75 years
  • JBS S.A. is the world’s largest processor of beef and pork with over $50 billion in annual sales

CNN described the strike as the first at a major US meatpacking plant in roughly 40 years. A worker told the WSWS: “People have died working this job, and it’s not safe.”

6. Sudan: UN documents genocide in Darfur as estimated death toll passes 400,000

A UN fact-finding mission has declared that the Rapid Support Forces’ campaign in Darfur bears the “hallmarks of genocide” — the first time a UN-backed body has leveled that allegation against the RSF. The former US envoy estimates the true death toll, including famine and disease, may exceed 400,000 since April 2023. The story continues to receive a fraction of the international attention directed toward the Iran and Ukraine conflicts.

Key facts:

  • UN OHCHR documented 6,000 killings in three days after El Fasher fell in October 2025; the actual toll is described as “undoubtedly significantly higher”
  • At least 11,300 civilians were killed in 2025 alone — nearly triple the 2024 figure
  • RSF drones struck a kindergarten and hospital in Kalogi, South Kordofan in December 2025, killing 114 people including 63 children
  • 21.2 million people face acute food insecurity; 9.5 million are internally displaced; 4.35 million have fled the country; 10 million children are out of school
  • The UN Security Council imposed sanctions on four RSF leaders in February, including the “Butcher of el-Fasher,” but the measures are largely symbolic
  • As recently as late February 2026, RSF forces stormed the town of Misteriha, killing 28 people including 10 women

Ethiopia landslides kill at least 125 in Gamo Zone

Multiple landslides struck the Gamo Zone districts of Gacho Baba, Kamba, and Bonke beginning around March 10 following days of intense rainfall. The death toll climbed from approximately 50 in initial reports to 125 confirmed dead as of March 16, with 128 people still missing and more than 11,000 residents displaced. Ethiopian PM Abiy Ahmed visited affected communities and pledged government support. Ethiopia declared three days of national mourning.

The disaster is part of a broader pattern: neighboring Kenya simultaneously recorded 62 dead from flash floods. The East African rainy season is driving cascading regional disasters, compounded by underdeveloped emergency infrastructure and the absence of early warning systems — a legacy of colonial underdevelopment that postcolonial states lack resources to address.

Sahel crisis: jihadists blockade Bamako as Burkina Faso faces regime collapse

Since September 2025, jihadist forces have imposed a partial blockade on Bamako, Mali’s capital, signaling a new phase in the Sahel’s broader war. Italy’s Meloni government pushes judicial reform ahead of March 22 referendum

A constitutional referendum scheduled for March 22–23 will vote on the “Nordio Reform,” which would separate judges’ and prosecutors’ career paths, split the High Council of the Judiciary, and establish a new disciplinary court. These safeguards were deliberately designed after fascism’s defeat to prevent political subordination of the judiciary. The reform complements the “Premierato” project for direct prime ministerial election with a guaranteed parliamentary majority.

Italy has seen three major general strikes since summer 2025 in opposition to war budgets and austerity. As Marc Wells argued on the WSWS, the reform is designed to build a state apparatus capable of suppressing anticipated social upheaval — a warning that extends beyond Italy to similar moves across Europe.

Russia sentences 19 in Crocus City Hall attack, 15 to life in prison

A Russian military court sentenced all 19 defendants tried in connection with the March 2024 Crocus City Hall terrorist attack, which killed 145 people and was one of Russia’s deadliest terrorist incidents in two decades. Fifteen defendants received life sentences; the remaining four received terms of 19–22 years. The attack was claimed by ISIS-K, though Russian authorities initially alleged — without evidence — Ukrainian involvement.

Kaiser strike betrayed: UNAC/UHCP ends 31,000-worker walkout

UNAC/UHCP leadership terminated the largest open-ended healthcare strike in US history — involving 31,000 Kaiser Permanente workers across California and Hawaii — on February 24 before any tentative agreement existed and without a membership vote on ending the walkout.   • No retroactive pay was secured — the union claimed this “put money in your pockets faster”
  • Safe-staffing demands were replaced with vague “improvement strategies”
  • Union officials earn six-figure salaries while providing limited strike pay

Columbia student workers vote 91.5% to authorize strike

The Student Workers of Columbia (UAW Local 2710) voted 91.5% — 1,129 to 105, with over 75% membership participation — to authorize a strike against Columbia University after seven months of stalled contract negotiations. The union’s first contract expired June 30, 2025, and the SWC accuses Columbia of “making zero substantive concessions.”

Key demands include pay increases to approximately $76,000 (based on the MIT Living Wage Calculator), protections for noncitizen workers facing ICE enforcement, restrictions on AI replacing labor, and adequate healthcare coverage. The union alleges Columbia pays student workers 61% of cost-of-living compared to 95–100% at Brown, Dartmouth, and Yale.

UAW Region 9A officials have threatened receivership if the local refuses to narrow its demands, forcing workers to drop requests to end campus surveillance and remove police. Region 9A Director Brandon Mancilla, affiliated with the DSA, and Representative Courtney Bither (earning $133,000 in 2024) were named in the dispute.

Separately, 950 contract faculty at New York University, also UAW-covered, are poised to strike March 23.

Ford worker Gregory Knopf killed by press machine at Sharonville plant

Gregory Knopf, 61, was killed on March 16 when a press machine activated during maintenance at Ford’s Sharonville, Ohio transmission plant, pinning him against equipment. He was pronounced dead at Bethesda North Hospital. Knopf is survived by his wife, three children, and eight grandchildren.

US measles cases hit 1,362 across 31 states in 2026

As of March 12, the CDC reports 1,362 confirmed measles cases in 31 states plus New York City, with 94% outbreak-associated and 14 new outbreaks declared this year alone. For reference, the full-year 2025 total was 2,284 cases — the current pace suggests 2026 will far exceed that figure.

The Pan American Health Organization has declared the Americas has lost its measles-free status, with endemic transmission reestablished in Canada. The US faces formal reassessment of its own elimination status this year, a development that would represent a historic public health failure driven by declining vaccination rates.

Sources CDC Measles cases and outbreaks

H5N1 devastates Pennsylvania poultry; A3 genotype reaches California marine mammals

Over 7 million birds have died in Pennsylvania this year from H5N1, with 8.97 million affected nationally in the past 30 days. In a separate and potentially more concerning development, the A3 genotype of H5N1 — known for mammalian adaptation — has been confirmed in elephant seals, a sea otter, and a sea lion at Año Nuevo State Park in California. This is the first detection of the A3 genotype on the US West Coast.

On the human side, PAHO reports 75 total human cases in the Americas since April 2022, with no new human cases since November 2025. The A3 genotype’s spread among marine mammals raises concern about broader mammalian transmission vectors that could eventually increase human risk.

Anthropic sues Pentagon over “supply chain risk” blacklisting

The Pentagon designated Anthropic a “supply chain risk” on March 6 — a label historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei — after contract negotiations broke down over Anthropic’s refusal to allow its Claude AI system to be used for mass citizen surveillance or autonomous weapons targeting. Anthropic filed two federal lawsuits (N.D. Cal. and D.C. Circuit) alleging First Amendment violations. CFO Krishna Rao warned the blacklist could cost “multiple billions of dollars” in 2026 revenue. A hearing is set for March 24.

Microsoft, Google’s Jeff Dean, and OpenAI researchers filed amicus briefs in Anthropic’s support. This is the highest-stakes AI policy confrontation in the US to date, testing whether the executive branch can use national security procurement law to punish an American company for its AI safety policies.

Separately, a unanimous Colorado task force released a proposed framework holding AI developers and deployers jointly liable for harms from systems making “consequential decisions” in healthcare, employment, housing, education, and financial services — the state is emerging as the de facto US template for state-level AI regulation at a moment when the White House seeks to preempt state AI laws.

NEJM: antisense therapy reduces Dravet syndrome seizures by up to 91%

A study published in the New England Journal of Medicine reports that zorevunersen, an antisense oligonucleotide developed by Biogen and Stoke Therapeutics, cut median convulsive seizure frequency by up to 90.91% over 20 months in Phase 1–2a trials (MONARCH and ADMIRAL, n=81). Multi-dose patients at 70 mg saw an 84.8% reduction at three months, with significant improvements in communication skills at 36+ months. This is the first clinical data showing potential disease modification in Dravet syndrome — targeting the SCN1A gene root cause rather than symptom management. A Phase 3 sham-controlled trial (EMPEROR) is now enrolling.

Arts and culture

The 98th Academy Awards ceremony on March 16 was dominated by Paul Thomas Anderson’s One Battle After Another, a political thriller about left-wing ex-revolutionaries adapted from Thomas Pynchon’s novel Vineland. The film took six Oscars including Best Picture, Best Director, Best Adapted Screenplay, Best Supporting Actor (Sean Penn, absent), Best Casting, and Best Film Editing. After nearly 30 years and 14 nominations since Boogie Nights, Anderson finally claimed his first Oscar — three in one night.

The film’s subject matter — former radicals navigating the wreckage of defeated movements — resonates with the present political moment. Anderson told the press backstage: “I wrote this movie for my kids to say sorry for the housekeeping mess that we left in this world we’re handing off to them. But also, with encouragement that they will be the generation that hopefully brings us some common sense and decency.” The Best Picture victory over Ryan Coogler’s Sinners (which won Best Original Screenplay, with Michael B. Jordan taking Best Actor) represented a preference for introspective political engagement over genre filmmaking, however accomplished. Jessie Buckley won Best Actress for Hamnet.

Russia’s escalating censorship of pop culture represents one of the most significant cultural developments of 2026. New laws that took effect March 1 prohibit mentions of drugs in any form, non-heterosexual relationships, criticism of “traditional values” or the Russian army, references to “foreign agents,” questioning of the state’s WWII narrative, or discussion of terrorism, suicide, or modern Russian politics. As the Moscow Times reported on March 16, publishers are rushing to pre-censor their portfolios, amounting to “the systematic destruction of modern Russian pop culture as it has existed since the early 1990s.” Russians are reportedly buying MP3 players to download pirated, uncensored versions of tracks — a digital samizdat for the streaming age. Meanwhile, Moscow experienced near-total mobile internet disruptions for weeks in what the Kremlin calls “security measures,” fueling speculation of a coming Great Firewall.
