1. US-Iran talks open in Islamabad as Vance leads highest-level meeting since 1979

Vice President JD Vance leads the US delegation in Islamabad on April 10, joined by Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, for the highest-level direct US-Iran meeting since the 1979 revolution. Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi lead Tehran’s delegation, with Pakistan’s mediation having brokered the two-week ceasefire that took effect April 8.

The gap between the two sides remains enormous. Iran’s 10-point proposal demands the lifting of all US sanctions, withdrawal of US combat forces from regional bases, UNSC ratification of any agreements, and full war-damage compensation. Trump insists on “no uranium enrichment” — a non-starter for Tehran, which seeks sanctions relief and non-aggression guarantees. At least 1,497 people have been killed since the war began February 28, including 57 health workers in Lebanon.

Key facts: - Iranian FM Araghchi: “If attacks against Iran are halted, our Powerful Armed Forces will cease their defensive operations” - Trump: “Almost all of the various points of past contention have been agreed to” — a claim contradicted by the scope of Iran’s demands - Pakistan’s mediation role marks a significant geopolitical shift, with Islamabad positioning itself as a broker between Washington and Tehran - The Vance-Kushner delegation signals that Middle East policy remains concentrated in Trump’s inner circle rather than the State Department

Sources Al Jazeera US-Iran talks in Pakistan: Who’s attending, what’s on the agenda · Axios US-Iran peace talks: Vance heads to Pakistan · CNN Trump-Iran ceasefire: Vance-Pakistan talks analysis

2. Israel kills over 300 in Lebanon on deadliest day of the war

Israel killed over 300 people and wounded more than 1,150 in Lebanon on April 9 in its deadliest wave of strikes since resuming large-scale attacks in early March. The strikes hit Beirut’s Corniche promenade — a civilian area far from Hezbollah positions — and killed four Lebanese Army soldiers. Lebanon declared a national day of mourning.

Netanyahu announced Lebanon talks after claiming “repeated requests from the Lebanese government,” with the US State Department confirming Washington will host talks the following week. Trump explicitly excluded Lebanon from the US-Iran ceasefire: “Because of Hezbollah. They were not included in the deal.” As Andre Damon wrote in the WSWS Perspective of April 9, the massive Israeli escalation in Lebanon — occurring within hours of the ceasefire announcement — exposes the fraudulent character of the agreement and the deep divisions within the US-Israeli alliance over its terms.

Key facts: - Iran’s IRGC said the Israeli strikes on Lebanon constitute a ceasefire violation - Over 100 ships remain stalled at the Strait of Hormuz, with the IRGC citing Israeli actions as justification for the continued slowdown - The death toll since February 28 has reached at least 1,497 across all fronts - Israel’s assault came within 24 hours of the ceasefire announcement — a pattern consistent with its use of “diplomatic cover” for military escalation

Sources Al Jazeera Iran war live: Israel kills 254+ in Lebanon · NPR Iran war updates · WSWS Amid deep divisions over ceasefire agreement, Israel launches massive attack on Lebanon

3. Strait of Hormuz remains blocked as oil surges back toward $100

Despite ceasefire terms requiring Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, over 100 ships remain stalled and ship operators cite uncertainty about Iran’s decentralized military command structure and new toll procedures. Brent crude surged 3.7% to $98.27/barrel on April 9, recovering from a brief ceasefire-driven drop, while US oil futures raced back toward $100.

The Strait handles approximately one-fifth of global oil and gas supplies. Iran and Oman may charge transit fees with revenues earmarked for Iranian reconstruction — a term disputed by Washington. The IRGC’s claim that the continued blockage is a response to Israeli violations in Lebanon creates a direct linkage between the two fronts that complicates diplomatic efforts.

Key facts: - Pre-war average: 120–150 daily transits through the Strait - Oil peaked above $109/barrel during the conflict before plunging 15% on the ceasefire announcement, then rebounding - The blockage’s impact falls hardest on energy-importing countries in the Global South — Bangladesh, for instance, imports 95% of its energy with 70–90% transiting Hormuz

4. Ukraine faces mass desertion crisis: 200,000 AWOL, 576 desert daily

Ukraine’s Defense Minister disclosed that 200,000 Ukrainians are listed as absent without leave, with 576 soldiers deserting daily — ten times Russia’s rate. Root causes include forced mobilization, exhaustion, and what soldiers describe as “suicide mission” orders. The crisis reflects a mass social refusal by Ukrainian workers and youth to continue dying in a war now grinding through its fifth year.

On the battlefield, April 9 saw 164 combat engagements. Russia launched 75 airstrikes and dropped 250 guided aerial bombs while losing an estimated 1,040 soldiers. Russian forces gained 17 square miles of territory between March 10 and April 7, but Ukraine claims to have recaptured 400 square kilometers since beginning counteroffensive operations — February 2026 was the first month since 2024 that Ukraine regained more land than it lost.

Key facts: - Ukrainian drone strikes have halted at least 40% of Russia’s oil export capacity by hitting all three major western export ports - Trump and Zelenskyy agreed on “90 to 95 percent” of a peace proposal, but analysts say Russia remains unwilling to make substantial concessions - 10.6 million Ukrainians displaced — 24% of the pre-invasion population - The class character of forced mobilization — where men with fewer resources or connections are conscripted — remains largely unexamined in bourgeois coverage

5. Gaza ceasefire declared “failing” after 689 killed and 2,073 Israeli violations

Five major humanitarian organizations released a progress scorecard on April 9 declaring the Gaza ceasefire — endorsed by UNSC Resolution 2803 — is “failing.” Israel has violated the ceasefire 2,073 times between October 10, 2025 and March 18, 2026: 973 bombings and shellings, 750 civilian shootings, 87 residential area raids, and 263 property demolitions.

The toll since the October truce: 689 Palestinians killed, with at least 2 children killed or injured every day according to Save the Children. On April 9, Israel struck a bicycle in Gaza City’s Sheikh Radwan neighborhood, killing one Palestinian and critically injuring a child. Palestinians continue to face what the humanitarian organizations described as “extreme deprivation, hunger, injury, and death” due to movement restrictions and aid obstruction.

Key facts: - The ceasefire was endorsed by UNSC Resolution 2803 — its systematic violation without consequence exposes the framework of international law as toothless in the face of US-backed Israeli impunity - 973 bombings/shellings in five months amount to roughly 6 per day - The humanitarian scorecard was issued by Oxfam and four other major organizations

6. North Korea tests cluster-bomb warheads on nuclear-capable ballistic missiles

North Korea concluded a three-day weapons testing spree that included cluster-munition warheads on Hwasong-11 ballistic missiles — nuclear-capable systems modeled on Russia’s Iskander design for low-altitude maneuverable flight. KCNA stated the warhead system “can reduce to ashes any target covering an area of 6.5–7 hectares (16–17.2 acres).”

Other systems tested included anti-aircraft weapons, electromagnetic weapons systems, and carbon-fiber bombs. Missiles flew 240 to 700 kilometers before falling into the sea. South Korea’s Joint Chiefs confirmed tracking the launches while sharing data with the US and Japan.

Key facts: - The tests are framed as part of Pyongyang’s push to expand nuclear-capable forces amid heightened regional tensions - The Iskander-modeled design enables low-altitude maneuverable flight to evade missile defense systems - Cluster munitions are banned by 112 countries under the Convention on Cluster Munitions — the US, Russia, and South Korea are not signatories

7. Trump threatens 50% tariffs on nations supplying Iran with weapons

Trump announced via Truth Social on April 8 that any country supplying Iran with military weapons will face immediate 50% tariffs with “no exclusions or exemptions.” No countries were named, but primary targets are understood to be China and Russia. The legal basis is disputed: the Supreme Court struck down Trump’s use of IEEPA for broad global tariffs in February 2026, forcing refunds of approximately $166 billion collected over a year.

The threat comes as the US average effective tariff rate stands at 11.0% — the highest since 1943 — costing households an average of $1,050 per year. Manufacturing has lost a net 89,000 jobs in the 10 months since “Liberation Day” tariffs took effect, contradicting the administration’s protectionist rhetoric.

Key facts: - Analysts called the threat “empty” given the Supreme Court ruling - The tariff regime’s weaponization for geopolitical coercion — targeting arms suppliers to Iran — marks a further escalation in the use of trade policy as an instrument of imperialist war - The $1,050/household cost and 89,000 lost manufacturing jobs demonstrate that protectionism imposes its costs on the working class

Sudan: hundreds killed by drone strikes as world’s largest humanitarian crisis deepens

Over 200 civilians have been killed by drone strikes since March 4 alone in Sudan’s Kordofan region and White Nile state. Strikes have hit markets, health facilities, and residential areas. Chad closed its eastern border with Sudan indefinitely on February 23 after repeated armed incursions, while hosting 1.3 million Sudanese refugees — over 900,000 of whom arrived since the civil war began in April 2023.

The military-led government returned to Khartoum on January 11 but city services remain devastated. Neither the Sudanese Armed Forces nor the RSF has been able to prevail after three years. The International Rescue Committee warns 2026 could see further deterioration. The conflict’s direct links to UAE, Russian, and Egyptian interests — which have supplied arms and support to competing factions — have kept the war going while it receives a fraction of the attention directed at Ukraine or the Middle East.

Sources UN News World news in brief: Deadly drone attacks in Sudan · International Rescue Committee Sudan is already the world’s largest humanitarian crisis

DRC: M23 conflict escalates with mass civilian killings in Ituri

At least 30 people were killed in a single attack in Bafwakoa, Ituri Province, while over 100 civilians have been killed and nearly 390 abducted since mid-March. M23 withdrew from its most forward position in Lubero district (North Kivu) while pro-government Wazalendo fighters recaptured several villages in Kalehe district.

A Red Cross-mediated deal for M23 to release 5,000 Congolese soldiers remains unimplemented a month after announcement; Kinshasa cites security concerns. Rwanda-backed M23 continues to expand control over strategic eastern DRC areas despite a nominal peace framework — a proxy conflict in which imperialist interests in the region’s mineral wealth remain the driving force.

Alberta premier announces anti-immigrant referendum for fall 2026

Alberta Premier Danielle Smith announced a fall 2026 provincial referendum that would restrict healthcare and education access to citizens and permanent residents, bar non-permanent residents from provincially funded social programs for one year, impose service fees on temporary residents, and require citizenship proof for voting.

The scapegoating offensive obscures the actual causes of service deterioration: per capita hospital spending declined 4% in real terms between 2014 and 2023; student enrollment grew by 89,000 from 2019–2024 while teacher numbers stagnated and wages rose only 3.8% against price inflation. Smith’s own predecessor ran the “Alberta is Calling” campaign offering $5,000 tax credits to attract migrants, contributing to population growth of nearly 600,000 between 2021 and 2025. The referendum reflects the broader adoption of nativist rhetoric across Canadian politics, including the federal Bill C-12.

ICE detention deaths reach deadliest pace since 2004

Thirty-two detainees died in ICE custody in 2025 — the deadliest year since 2004 — while 2026 has already registered 15 deaths. The detained population has grown to 73,000 as of mid-January 2026, a 75% increase since Trump’s return, with a 2,400% increase in detainees with no criminal record.

Individual cases documented in inspection records and autopsies include: Nenko Gantchev, a 56-year-old Bulgarian immigrant whose requests for medical assistance were ignored before dying on December 15 at North Lake, Michigan; and Oudone Lothirath, a 57-year-old Laotian refugee with terminal Hodgkin’s lymphoma, who was arrested January 6, missed chemotherapy sessions, and was held in a tent with approximately 60 others until officials released him only after hospital documentation confirmed he would “quickly succumb to cancer” without treatment.

Key facts: - An internal ICE inspection of Camp East Montana (Fort Bliss, Texas) on February 10–12 found detainees with possible tuberculosis not quarantined, one-third of medical grievances unaddressed, and no documentation of medical care following use-of-force incidents — then rated the facility “Acceptable/Adequate” - The bipartisan character of the detention system is documented in the expansion of capacity under both Obama and Trump administrations

Idaho constructs remote-controlled firing squad chamber

Idaho is retrofitting its F Block execution unit with remote-operated firing mechanisms designed to “minimize correctional staff involvement.” House Bill 37 makes the firing squad Idaho’s primary method of execution by July 1, 2026. US measles cases top 1,671

The United States has recorded 1,671 confirmed measles cases in 2026 as of April 2, up 6.1% week-over-week, with 94% outbreak-associated. This makes 2026 one of the two worst years for measles in the US since elimination was declared in 2000, already surpassing the historically severe 2019 outbreak. Utah’s outbreak is the largest still-growing cluster at approximately 600 cases, while a Florida university outbreak tied to Ave Maria University has reached 106 cases.

Sources Healthbeat Where measles is spreading: April 2026 · Contagion Live Measles update: April 3, 2026

CDC delays COVID vaccine effectiveness report

Acting CDC director Jay Bhattacharya blocked a scheduled March 19 MMWR report finding the 2025–26 COVID vaccine reduced emergency department and urgent-care visits by approximately 50% and hospitalizations by approximately 55% in healthy adults. The report had cleared the agency’s full scientific review process. Current and former officials warn this represents an unprecedented politicization of public health science — the methodology is the same used in NEJM, JAMA, and Lancet studies for decades. The suppression of evidence demonstrating vaccine efficacy, under an administration led by RFK Jr. at HHS, represents a direct threat to public health.

Sources Washington Post CDC delays publication of report showing COVID vaccine cuts hospitalizations ~55% · The New Republic RFK Jr.’s CDC delays report COVID vaccine worked

New COVID variant BA.3.2 “Cicada” spreading in 25 states

BA.3.2 carries 70–75 mutations in its spike protein and has been detected in 20% of sequenced sewage samples in early April, spreading across at least 25 states. Current vaccines target JN.1 and LP.8.1, which are genetically distant from BA.3.2. The high immune-escape potential raises questions about whether the current vaccine formulation will need updating ahead of fall 2026.

Sources NBC News / Today New COVID variant BA.3.2 “Cicada”: symptoms, spread in 2026

H5N1 surging in NYC urban wildlife

H5N1 is surging in New York City urban wildlife, jumping from migratory birds into a growing number of mammal species. Separately, the virus has evolved an improved ability to use alternative receptors abundant in cattle — though these receptors are not found in humans or birds. The unprecedented mammalian spillover rate and urban wildlife spread increase opportunities for further adaptation, keeping pandemic risk elevated even as direct human cases remain rare.

AI layoffs hit 80,000 in Q1 2026

A total of 78,557 tech workers were laid off in the first quarter of 2026, with 47.9% of cuts (37,638 positions) explicitly attributed to AI automation — the first quarter where AI-attributed layoffs account for nearly half of all tech sector cuts. Over 76% of affected workers are US-based. Separately, OpenAI published a 13-page policy document proposing a “robot tax,” a public sovereign wealth fund, and a government-subsidized four-day workweek — framed around the imminent arrival of superintelligence. Critics across the political spectrum called the proposals “regulatory nihilism” designed to shape favorable policy rather than accept meaningful oversight.

Arts and culture

The most culturally significant event of the week is the opening of MoMA’s Marcel Duchamp retrospective on April 12 — the first US retrospective of the Dada artist since 1973, featuring nearly 300 works spanning his entire career from 1900 to 1968. Organized jointly by MoMA, the Philadelphia Museum of Art, and the Centre Pompidou, the exhibition offers today’s audiences the first opportunity to view the full breadth of Duchamp’s creative output, from his early Cubism-influenced paintings to the readymades that upended the definition of art itself. That this retrospective arrives at a moment when AI threatens to automate creative production — and when 37,600 tech workers have been displaced by automation in a single quarter — gives renewed urgency to Duchamp’s central question: what constitutes art, and who decides?

Also opening this month: LACMA’s Peter Zumthor-designed David Geffen Galleries — a $724 million, decade-in-the-making building spanning Wilshire Boulevard with 10,220 square meters of exhibition space. The ribbon-cutting on April 19 marks the culmination of one of the most expensive museum construction projects in US history. Forty-five curators collaborated on the inaugural installation spanning 6,000 years of art history. The lavish expenditure stands in stark contrast to the ongoing defunding of arts education and public cultural institutions across the country.

Jacobin’s output in the past 24 hours centers on two themes: AI regulation and healthcare. “Bernie and AOC Pump the Brakes on Artificial Intelligence” (Nicholas Liu) promotes the Sanders-Ocasio-Cortez proposal to halt new AI data centers until federal oversight is established. The article treats congressional legislation as the vehicle for controlling AI — channeling legitimate worker anxiety about automation-driven layoffs (37,600 in Q1 alone) into faith in the Democratic Party’s regulatory apparatus, without questioning whether a capitalist state can meaningfully regulate technology deployed for profit maximization. “Medicare for All Is an Electoral Winner” (Jared Abbott) argues that universal healthcare should be framed as a “social insurance benefit” to win over working-class voters — reducing the question of healthcare as a social right to one of electoral messaging strategy for Democratic candidates.

“The Fight Against Trumpism Can Reinvigorate Labor” (Stephen Lerner and Joseph McCartin) is the most revealing piece: it argues explicitly that “defending democracy” against authoritarianism creates “opportunities for labor advancement” — the classic DSA formula of subordinating the working class to the defense of bourgeois democratic institutions rather than building an independent political movement. The overall orientation remains firmly within the Democratic Party orbit, offering reformist policy proposals as substitutes for socialist politics.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Gaza ceasefire collapse as a crisis of international law and US complicity

The Oxfam-led scorecard documenting 2,073 Israeli ceasefire violations and 689 Palestinian deaths in five months demands analysis beyond the tally. The structural US role — UNSC Resolution 2803 was endorsed across the Biden-Trump transition and is now failing under systematic Israeli violation — and the legal framework that enables this impunity (US veto power, continuing arms supply) have gone largely unanalyzed. The WSWS should examine how the “rules-based international order” functions when the rules are systematically broken by Washington’s closest ally.

Recommended reading Oxfam International Humanitarian scorecard: Six months in, Gaza ceasefire is failing

2. Ukraine’s desertion crisis as a working-class revolt against a proxy war

The 200,000 AWOL soldiers and 576 daily desertions represent not merely a military logistics problem but a mass social refusal by Ukrainian workers and youth to die in a war shaped by NATO geopolitical interests. The class character of forced mobilization — where men with fewer resources or connections are forcibly conscripted — deserves examination. This is a story the WSWS is uniquely positioned to cover, connecting to its analysis of the war as an inter-imperialist conflict.

Recommended reading PBS NewsHour Ukraine faces military desertions as Russian invasion grinds through 5th year

3. AI-driven mass layoffs reach inflection point: 37,600 workers displaced in Q1

The first quarter of 2026 marks a qualitative shift — nearly half of all tech layoffs are now explicitly attributed to AI automation, with 76% of affected workers US-based. OpenAI’s simultaneous publication of a policy blueprint proposing “robot taxes” and wealth funds reveals the ruling class’s awareness that mass displacement is coming. The WSWS should analyze the class dimensions of both the layoffs and the corporate proposals to manage them.

Recommended reading Tom’s Hardware Tech industry lays off nearly 80,000 in Q1 2026, almost 50% due to AI

4. Pakistan as a new imperial broker: how Islamabad engineered the US-Iran ceasefire

Pakistan’s mediation of the highest-level US-Iran meeting since 1979 marks a significant geopolitical shift. Islamabad is positioning itself as an interlocutor between Washington and Tehran while managing its own domestic political crisis through foreign policy assertion. The WSWS should analyze the deeper changes in regional alliances this reflects and Pakistan’s class dynamics.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera How Pakistan managed to get the US and Iran to a ceasefire

5. Sudan’s ignored catastrophe: drone warfare, displacement, and great-power indifference

Sudan’s civil war has produced the world’s largest humanitarian crisis — 1.3 million refugees in Chad alone, 200+ civilians killed by drones since March 4, daily strikes on civilian markets and health facilities — yet it receives a fraction of the media attention directed at Ukraine or the Middle East. The RSF-SAF conflict has direct links to UAE, Russian, and Egyptian interests. The WSWS should expose the imperialist dimensions driving the conflict and the cynical indifference of the “international community.”

Recommended reading UN News World news in brief: Deadly drone attacks in Sudan

6. CDC suppression of COVID vaccine data under RFK Jr.’s HHS

The blocking of a fully reviewed MMWR report demonstrating 55% reduction in COVID hospitalizations represents an unprecedented politicization of public health science. The WSWS, which has led coverage of the pandemic through the Global Workers’ Inquest, should analyze this as part of the systematic dismantling of public health infrastructure under conditions where COVID continues to circulate, new variants emerge, and the ruling class has abandoned all mitigation.

Recommended reading Washington Post CDC delays publication of report showing COVID vaccine cuts hospitalizations ~55%

7. US tariff regime at one year: $1,050/household cost, 89,000 manufacturing jobs lost

A year after “Liberation Day,” the Yale Budget Lab documents that the US tariff regime has cost households over $1,000 each while destroying the manufacturing jobs it was supposed to protect. The effective tariff rate of 11% — highest since 1943 — and the Supreme Court’s ruling striking down IEEPA authority create an important moment for the WSWS to analyze the gap between protectionist rhetoric and economic reality for workers.

Recommended reading Yale Budget Lab State of U.S. tariffs: April 2, 2026
