1. US-Israel war on Iran enters second month with widening strikes

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran entered its second month on March 28 with no diplomatic resolution in sight and an accelerating tempo of strikes. Israel struck two Iranian nuclear facilities on March 28, including a uranium processing plant, prompting Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi to vow a “heavy price for Israeli crimes.” Iran retaliated by striking Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia with six ballistic missiles and 29 drones on March 27–28, wounding 15 US troops — two seriously — bringing total US casualties since February 28 to 13 killed and over 300 wounded.

Secretary of State Rubio declared: “We are going to basically destroy their ability to make missiles and drones in their factories,” claiming objectives would be complete “in the next couple weeks.” Trump paused strikes on Iranian energy plants until April 6, with Pakistan mediating multilateral talks. As Andre Damon reported on the WSWS, the administration plans to deploy an additional 10,000 troops to the Middle East, potentially bringing the total to 17,000+, while Pentagon officials warn that Tomahawk cruise missile supplies are “alarmingly low” after 850+ fired.

2. “No Kings” protests sweep US in largest-ever anti-Trump mobilization

The third wave of “No Kings” demonstrations on March 28 constituted what organizers called “the single largest non-violent day of action in American history,” with over 3,200 marches across all 50 US states and several continents. The previous round in October 2025 drew more than 7 million participants; this round is projected to exceed that figure. The flagship rally in St. Paul, Minnesota featured Bruce Springsteen, who told the crowd: “They picked the wrong city. Your strength and your commitment told us that this is still America.”

At least 70 arrests and tear gas deployment were reported in Los Angeles, with arrests also in Portland and Dallas. Protesters cited the Iran war, rising gas prices, and mass deportations. An NBC poll shows majorities disapprove of Trump on all three issues. The White House dismissed the protests as a “Trump Derangement Therapy Session.”

7. Global tariff war escalates as IMF projects US growth at 1.5%

The US effective tariff rate has reached 10.3% — the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993 — costing the average US household an estimated $1,500 in 2026. Retaliatory measures are escalating: Canada imposed 25% tariffs on US steel and consumer goods, the EU reinstated duties on bourbon and motorcycles, and Mexico imposed surcharges up to 50%.

In a significant diplomatic rebuff to Washington, Canada and China reached an EV deal admitting 49,000 Chinese electric vehicles per year at reduced tariffs, bypassing the 2024 100% surtax. The IMF projects US growth at 1.5% and global growth at 3.3%, while UNCTAD is more pessimistic at 2.6% global growth. The Iran war is compounding trade disruption — 6,000–8,000 tonnes of Kenyan tea worth $24 million are stranded at Mombasa port, while Egypt imposed a 9 PM business curfew over doubled energy bills.

Oral GLP-1 pill orforglipron achieves 11% weight loss in phase 3 trial

A landmark study published in the New England Journal of Medicine found that once-daily oral orforglipron at 36mg produced −11.2% body weight loss versus −2.1% for placebo in a 72-week phase 3 randomized trial of 3,127 patients with obesity (no diabetes). Over 54% of patients in the highest-dose group lost ≥10% of body weight. A parallel trial (ATTAIN-2), published in The Lancet, showed superiority over oral semaglutide in type 2 diabetes. With no injection requirement or food-timing restrictions, FDA obesity approval is expected as early as Q2 2026, potentially transforming access to GLP-1 treatments currently limited by injection delivery and cost barriers.

Sources NEJM Orforglipron ATTAIN-1 trial (obesity) · Eli Lilly Lilly’s oral GLP-1 orforglipron demonstrated meaningful weight loss

Sarcopenic obesity raises mortality risk by 83%

A new study finds that combining excess abdominal fat with low muscle mass — sarcopenic obesity — raises all-cause mortality risk by 83% compared to neither condition alone. The condition can be identified using basic anthropometric measurements rather than expensive imaging, offering a low-cost screening pathway for a high-risk phenotype increasingly common in aging populations and relevant to the expanding use of GLP-1 drugs, which raise questions about muscle mass preservation during rapid weight loss.

Project Hail Mary opens nationwide

The long-awaited adaptation of Andy Weir’s 2021 novel, directed by Phil Lord and Christopher Miller and starring Ryan Gosling, opened wide this weekend. World Theatre Day 2026: Willem Dafoe on theater as connection

Willem Dafoe delivered the annual World Theatre Day message on March 27, reflecting on theater as connection rather than commerce — a fitting theme given his roots co-founding the experimental Wooster Group in 1980, one of the most important American avant-garde theater companies of the late twentieth century. Dafoe’s message arrives at a moment when live performance globally faces existential pressures: declining public funding, the displacement of collective cultural experience by algorithmic entertainment, and the growing subordination of theatrical institutions to corporate sponsorship. That the annual address went to a figure known primarily for film underscores theater’s ongoing struggle for mainstream visibility and cultural authority.

Market Theatre of South Africa marks 50th anniversary

Johannesburg’s iconic Market Theatre, founded in 1976 during apartheid as a deliberately multiracial cultural refuge, is celebrating its 50th anniversary this month. The institution served as one of the most politically consequential theater spaces of the twentieth century, staging works by Athol Fugard, Barney Simon, and others that directly confronted racial oppression and class exploitation when doing so carried real personal risk. Its anniversary is an occasion to consider the fate of politically engaged theater institutions in the post-apartheid period, where the ANC’s embrace of neoliberal economics has created new forms of inequality that demand artistic reckoning.

La Biennale di Venezia announces 2026 Golden Lions

Venice’s Biennale announced its 2026 honorees: Mamela Nyamza (Silver Lion, Dance), Sarah Davachi (Silver Lion, Music), and Emma Dante receiving the Golden Lion for Lifetime Achievement in Theater. Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s most significant piece is “We Can’t Income-Tax Ultra-Elites. We Must Tax Their Wealth” — an advocacy article for the Sanders/Khanna Congressional wealth tax proposals and parallel California legislation. The article presents parliamentary reformism as the outer boundary of socialist ambition: wealth taxation through existing Democratic Party legislative maneuvers, with no independent class program and no acknowledgment that the same capitalist state being asked to tax the ultra-rich is currently waging war on Iran and deploying ICE to airports. A second article, “Mental Health Workers Fight for AI Protections in California,” covers the Kaiser Permanente strike but operates entirely within a trade-unionist framework, absent any critique of healthcare as a commodity under capitalism. A third, “The Afroman Ruling Is a Victory for Artistic Speech,” offers a civil-liberties analysis of a police defamation case within a bourgeois legal framework, with no class analysis of the repressive state apparatus.

Jacobin continues to function as the ideological organ of the DSA current, systematically subordinating working-class politics to the Sanders-AOC wing of the Democratic Party. The wealth-tax piece is emblematic: demands that could be called socialist are laundered as bipartisan-adjacent reform proposals.
