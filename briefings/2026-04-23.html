1. US-Iran war: Iran seizes ships, peace talks collapse, oil surges past $100

The US-Iran war, now in its eighth week, escalated sharply on April 22–23 as Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps seized two foreign container ships in the Strait of Hormuz and fired on a third — just hours after President Trump had announced an indefinite extension of the shaky ceasefire. The seizures came as scheduled Islamabad peace talks were cancelled and the US naval blockade of Iran-linked vessels, begun April 13, entered its eleventh day. Trump told reporters on April 23 there is “no time frame” on the war, sending WTI crude above $94 and Brent briefly above $103 for a fourth consecutive day of gains.

The Pentagon confirmed 13 US service members have been killed since fighting began February 28. Iranian authorities put the death toll inside Iran at 3,636, including 254 children. International Energy Agency chief Fatih Birol called the conflict “the worst energy crisis the world has ever faced.” As Jordan Shilton wrote on the WSWS Perspective, “the material consequences of imperialist war fall most devastatingly not on those who launch or direct it, but on the international working class.”

Key facts:

  • Strait of Hormuz traffic has dropped far below the pre-war baseline of more than 100 ships per day; Iran declared the strait closed to US-linked vessels on April 18
  • Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi called the US blockade “an act of war”; President Masoud Pezeshkian said dialogue is “desired” but impossible under “blockade and threats”
  • Brent crude closed April 23 at $103.38 (+1.44%); WTI at $94.46 (+1.67%) — a fourth straight up day driven by the ship seizures
  • The S&P 500 set its fifth all-time high in eight sessions on April 22 (Nasdaq also hit a fresh record at 24,657.57) before pulling back as the talks collapsed; major oil corporations stand to gain more than $230 billion in additional 2026 profits from the war, per figures cited on the WSWS
  • The Containerised Freight Index rose 10 percent within a month of the war’s outbreak; UN Development Programme estimates the conflict could push 8.8 million additional people in 36 Asia-Pacific countries into poverty

2. Israel-Lebanon truce collapses; Lebanese journalist killed, teenagers shot in West Bank

The 10-day truce between Israel and Hezbollah that began April 18 effectively collapsed on April 22–23. Hezbollah fired rockets and drones at Israeli forces for the first time since Friday, with both sides accusing the other of triggering the breakdown. At least five people were killed in Israeli strikes on Lebanon on April 23, including Lebanese journalist Amal Khalil — a killing condemned by the Lebanese government as a “heinous crime.” In the occupied West Bank, an Israeli army reservist shot and killed 14-year-old Aws al-Naasan and Jihad Abu Naim, 32, near Al-Mughayyir village; witnesses described a joint settler-soldier attack that left three others wounded as part of “a displacement operation.”

Key facts:

  • Israeli army reservists were sentenced to only 30 days detention after one smashed a statue of Jesus in southern Lebanon — drawing international condemnation from Catholic and Orthodox authorities
  • Senior Hamas officials told the New York Times the group could surrender its police-force weapons to a Trump-aligned oversight body — a significant but partial concession that falls short of Israeli demands for full disarmament
  • The West Bank killings come amid an accelerating pattern of fatal settler-soldier attacks on Palestinian villages, with human rights groups documenting record displacement in 2026

3. Sudan enters fourth year of war as 33 million face hunger

The civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces has now entered its fourth year. The UN’s top Sudan official on April 22 called the international community’s response “unacceptable” and described the war as “not a forgotten crisis — an abandoned one.” Coverage has been almost entirely displaced by the Iran war, even as Sudan constitutes the gravest humanitarian catastrophe on the planet.

Key facts:

  • 14 million people — a quarter of Sudan’s population — have been forcibly displaced; 4.4 million have fled to Chad, South Sudan, and Egypt
  • More than 33 million Sudanese now require humanitarian assistance, the world’s largest hunger crisis; two formal famines were declared in El Fasher and Kadugli in 2025
  • RSF targeting of non-Arab ethnic groups in Darfur has prompted genocide warnings at the UN General Assembly
  • Germany pledged €1 billion in humanitarian aid at a Berlin conference this week; US, UK and French pledges remain a fraction of UN-requested funding
  • Action Against Hunger reports malnutrition rates in displaced camps now exceed 30% in some localities, the threshold for “extreme” emergency classification

4. US partial shutdown hits record 67 days; DHS warns payroll will collapse in early May

The US partial government shutdown reached a record 67 days on April 23 with no resolution in sight. DHS Secretary Markwayne Mullin warned the department will exhaust emergency funds by early May, telling reporters: “My payroll through DHS is just over $1.6 billion every two weeks.” Hundreds of thousands of federal workers and contractors continue to face delayed or absent paychecks. The shutdown coincides with the Iran war and continued strain on every arm of federal government capacity.

Key facts:

  • The shutdown exceeds the previous record by more than two weeks and has now surpassed every such impasse in US history
  • Food banks across the Washington, DC region have reported record demand from furloughed federal employees
  • Essential workers — TSA, Border Patrol, air traffic controllers — are reporting to work without pay; absenteeism is rising at major airports
  • Congressional Republicans and Democrats remain locked over military supplementals for the Iran war, with White House demands for expanded wartime spending blocking bipartisan continuing-resolution talks

5. DOJ indicts Southern Poverty Law Center on 11 federal counts

A federal grand jury in Montgomery, Alabama, on April 21 returned a 14-page indictment against the Southern Poverty Law Center on six counts of wire fraud, four counts of false statements to a federally insured bank, and one count of conspiracy to commit concealment money laundering. DOJ alleges the SPLC funneled more than $3 million in donor funds between 2014 and 2023 to paid informants inside the KKK, Aryan Nations, and National Socialist Party of America, using shell entities such as “Fox Photography” and “Rare Books Warehouse.” FBI Director Kash Patel announced the charges personally.

The case is widely read as a Trump administration move to dismantle the country’s pre-eminent civil rights monitoring organization. As Jacob Crosse notes on the WSWS, when pressed, Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche acknowledged that the money did not actually benefit extremist groups — the charge concerns disclosure of informant payments, a routine law enforcement practice.

Key facts:

  • SPLC Interim CEO Bryan Fair called the charges “false allegations,” saying the group has “stood as a beacon of hope fighting white supremacy for 55 years”
  • The indictment comes as figures previously tracked by the SPLC — including Stephen Miller and Jack Posobiec — occupy positions of power in the second Trump administration
  • SPLC’s Intelligence Project publishes the “Hatewatch” database used by journalists and researchers globally; its survival as an active monitor is now in question

6. Virginia voters approve Democratic redistricting map, 51.5% to 48.6% — circuit judge immediately blocks certification

Virginia voters on April 22 approved a Democratic-drawn congressional map 51.5% to 48.6% (97% of ballots counted), making Virginia the second state after California to counter Trump’s mid-decade Republican redistricting push. A Virginia circuit court judge immediately blocked certification of the results; Attorney General Jay Jones vowed to appeal, and the state Supreme Court is expected to rule within days.

Key facts:

  • The new map would give Democrats an electoral advantage in 10 of Virginia’s 11 House districts, potentially netting up to 4 additional seats in the November midterms
  • Trump urged GOP-led states to redraw maps last year as Republicans feared losing their House majority
  • Democratic legal theory holds that Virginia’s mid-decade constitutional amendment authorizing the map is valid; Republican plaintiffs argue it violates state constitutional limits on redistricting frequency
  • The ruling sets up a high-stakes showdown before a Virginia Supreme Court whose members have mixed appointments from governors of both parties

  • The WSWS documents at least 53 recorded US strikes since September, killing no fewer than 181 civilians, most identifiably fishermen
  • New FBI offices have opened in Quito and Costa Rica; five additional lethal strikes occurred in the Eastern Pacific between April 11–19 alone
  • Sheinbaum’s simultaneous fracking reversal signals a sharp rightward turn in energy policy coinciding with deepening US-Mexico security cooperation

US-brokered talks in Abu Dhabi and Geneva remain stalled over Russia’s insistence on Ukrainian territorial concessions in Donbas, which Kyiv has rejected.

  • Russian forces have continued to grind forward at Pokrovsk and Toretsk, exploiting the collapse of the truce to reset artillery positions
  • Polish and Baltic officials this week warned of a “renewed spring offensive window,” with NATO planners quietly drawing up contingency air-defense reinforcements

Progressive Bulgaria wins absolute majority as GERB collapses

Progressive Bulgaria — a coalition formed in March 2026 and formally unified on April 17 under President Rumen Radev — won 43.91% of the vote and 130 of 240 National Assembly seats in snap elections, enough to govern without coalition partners. The previously dominant GERB party’s vote collapsed from 25.52% to 13.18%, and the Bulgarian Socialist Party fell below the 4% threshold for the first time since the restoration of capitalism in 1989.

The elections were triggered by mass December protests against the outgoing government’s military-heavy 2026 budget. Two-thirds of previous non-voters and 34% of voters aged 18–30 backed Radev. Despite his anti-war rhetoric, Radev is a former US military-trained officer whose presidency has overseen continued Bulgarian arms shipments to Ukraine and one of the EU’s worst COVID death tolls (548 deaths per 100,000, as the WSWS documents).

LAUSD rank-and-file reject UTLA-SEIU “sellout” contracts that cancelled 77,000-worker strike

Tentative contracts that halted a planned 77,000-worker Los Angeles Unified School District strike are facing widespread rank-and-file opposition. Florida executes Chadwick Willacy — fifth state execution of 2026

Florida on April 21 executed Chadwick Willacy, 58, who maintained his innocence for more than 30 years. It is Florida’s fifth execution of 2026 and the eighth nationally. The WSWS’s Kate Randall documents that four prior 2026 Florida executions involved non-unanimous jury verdicts, intellectual disability claims, the execution of a defendant with Fetal Alcohol Syndrome at 18, and a traumatic brain injury case — all processed under Governor Ron DeSantis’s accelerated schedule. Justice Sonia Sotomayor has identified what she calls a systemic catch-22: “the state denies condemned prisoners access to the records that would allow them to challenge the execution protocol, and then the courts dismiss those challenges for lack of evidence.” Florida leads the nation in both executions and exonerations (30+ since 1973).

Chinese physicist Danhao Wang dies by suicide after federal interrogation; “100 percent political” prosecution of Chinese researchers widens

The death of 30-year-old University of Michigan semiconductor physics postdoctoral researcher Danhao Wang — who took his life on March 19, the day after federal agents interrogated him — is being documented by the WSWS as the casualty of a systematic government campaign against Chinese researchers. A parallel case detailed in the reporting: doctoral student Chengxuan Han, detained for six hours at Detroit Metro Airport and charged over laboratory plasmid DNA that Professor Roger Innes described as “one hundred percent political.” The WSWS’s Stephen St. Clair reports that the University of Michigan administration “facilitated the purge of its own researchers” to protect roughly $100 million in annual Pentagon contracts.

US measles cases reach 1,748 across 33 jurisdictions

As of April 16, the US has reported 1,748 confirmed measles cases across 33 states and jurisdictions in 2026, with 94% (1,637 cases) outbreak-associated. Weekly growth has slowed to just 0.40% — seven new cases in the most recent week, the slowest rate since the surge began. South Carolina alone accounts for 997 cases, centered in Spartanburg County, where state officials have been accused of suppressing case-level data. 2026 is already on track to be one of the two worst measles years since 2000, threatening the country’s endemic-elimination status for the first time in decades.

Cambodia confirms fourth human H5N1 case of 2026

Cambodia’s National Institute of Public Health on April 21 confirmed H5N1 avian influenza in a 66-year-old woman from Svay Rieng province, now in isolation in intensive care. Investigators found sick and dead backyard chickens at her home from April 2–13 that had been used for cooking; no human-to-human transmission has been identified. Four Cambodian human cases in roughly 90 days reinforces scientific concern that H5N1 continues to spill over from poultry to humans at a persistent low-level rate in Southeast Asia.

Stanford AI Index: US private investment hits $285.9 billion, public trust collapses

Stanford HAI’s 2026 AI Index, released this week, documents US private AI investment at $285.9 billion in 2025 — roughly 23 times China’s $12.4 billion. AI coding benchmarks hit near-100% on SWE-bench Verified (up from 60% in one year), and frontier models now exceed human baselines on PhD-level science questions. Simultaneously, only 31% of Americans trust their government to regulate AI — last among surveyed nations — and Foundation Model Transparency Index scores fell from 58 to 40. Organizational adoption has hit 88%; 80% of university students now use AI tools.

OpenAI’s “Superintelligence New Deal” reignites federal vs. state AI preemption fight

OpenAI’s 13-page “Superintelligence New Deal” blueprint, published April 5–6 under CEO Sam Altman’s byline, proposes higher capital-gains and corporate taxes on AI-driven returns, a nationally managed public wealth fund seeded by AI companies, a four-day workweek with no pay cut, and federal guardrails on government AI use. It explicitly warns that superintelligence may arrive “soon” and that advanced AI could enable a “world-shaking cyberattack” as early as 2026. Critics — including AI governance researchers — argue the proposals are strategically vague in ways that favor regulatory capture by frontier labs. Over 100 state AI laws were enacted in 2025 and 600+ new state bills are active this session.

COVID at near-record low, NIST opens agentic AI standards process

COVID-19 activity is at a near-record low in the US. As of April 14, transmission is declining or stable in 45 of 46 states monitored; test positivity was 1.8% for the week ending April 4; COVID accounted for just 0.3% of all emergency department visits as of April 17. The anticipated spring wave has not materialized.

NIST’s Center for AI Standards and Innovation has issued a Request for Information on secure development of agentic AI systems and published a concept paper on “agentic identity standards” — a new domain focused on how autonomous AI agents authenticate and authorize actions. The push signals that regulators see a qualitative risk shift as AI moves from answering questions to autonomously taking actions.

Two independent films on the incarcerated and the working poor tested against Belinsky

The most substantive arts commentary of the 24-hour window came from Joanne Laurier’s WSWS review of two independent American films — Ricky, directed by Rashad Frett, following a 30-year-old formerly incarcerated Connecticut man after 15 years in prison, and Mad Bills to Pay (or Destiny, dile que no soy malo), directed by Joel Alfonso Vargas, tracking a 19-year-old Dominican American navigating poverty and early fatherhood in the Bronx.

Laurier finds both films worthy in intent but insufficient in execution. Ricky flattens social reality into what she calls “grey monotone”; Mad Bills to Pay remains claustrophobically individual, failing to indicate “any objectively existing impulse for social breakthrough.” Invoking the 19th-century Russian critic Belinsky — “Society is also something real, and its essence is made up not only of costumes and hairstyles, but also of customs, habits, concepts, relations” — Laurier argues that genuine realism must depict the structural forces (mass incarceration as a systemic phenomenon involving “approximately two million people,” the labor market, the housing crisis) that shape individual lives, not merely the surface details of those lives. The review is a polemic against the contemporary indie tradition’s retreat from the social totality into lifestyle documentation.

Rocky Horror Show revival opens on Broadway at Studio 54

The Richard O’Brien rock musical The Rocky Horror Show opened April 23 at Studio 54, under the direction of Sam Pinkleton and starring Luke Evans, Amber Gray, Harvey Guillén, and Rachel Dratch. The revival lands at a moment when Broadway musical revivals have become the primary financial engine of the industry, displacing the risk of new work — a structural trend sharpened by the post-pandemic retreat of smaller producers.

Fuqua’s Michael Jackson biopic arrives amid legacy debates

Antoine Fuqua’s long-awaited biographical film Michael, with Jaafar Jackson in the title role, arrives in theaters April 24 after years of production and public controversy over estate involvement and casting. The release comes alongside the Beaches: A New Musical stage adaptation (opened April 22 at the Majestic), Netflix’s Stranger Things: Tales From ’85 spin-off (April 23), the opening of the OutShine LGBTQ+ Film Festival in Miami (April 23–May 10), and a new Charlize Theron Australian-wilderness thriller for Netflix debuting April 24. Several of these releases consolidate the now-dominant pattern of capital concentration on recognizable IP over original artistic work. The OutShine Festival, by contrast, continues to program independent queer cinema at a moment of intensifying political attack on LGBTQ+ cultural institutions in the United States.

  • India, already facing severe inequality, saw fuel prices climb 10–15 percent within weeks; Indonesia, a major nickel exporter, cut output by at least 10 percent due to gas shortages from disrupted imports
  • Nigeria — a major oil producer that might be expected to benefit from rising prices — saw domestic fuel prices rise over 50 percent due to disrupted refinery inputs; Kenya raised petrol prices 16 percent and diesel 24 percent in mid-April
  • Iran itself has experienced approximately 2 million direct job losses attributable to the conflict
  • The Wall Street Journal’s own phrase — an era of “mega layoffs” across multiple sectors — is cited with deliberate irony as capitalist journalism documenting capitalism’s breakdown
  • The article frames the current moment in historical comparison to 1917 Russia, when food shortages and military exhaustion created objective conditions for socialist revolution, arguing that the globally integrated economy means these conditions are forming simultaneously across dozens of countries
  • Explicit rejection of relief from any faction of the bourgeoisie or existing trade unions; call for independent working-class political mobilization on a socialist program and participation in the upcoming International May Day Rally

Australia: Two workers killed on the job in Brisbane in three days — Mike Head

Head’s report on two Brisbane workplace fatalities (a man crushed between trucks at a construction site; a forklift fatality at Karreman Quarries) brings Brisbane’s total to five deaths in six months and the national total to 30 by April 9, 2026, against 180 in all of 2025. Industrial manslaughter laws introduced across Australian states by late 2024 have produced only six convictions since 2017; companies have received fines as low as $625,000 for two deaths — insignificant relative to profits. Head attributes the pattern to corporate speed-ups, subcontracting, and casualization of labor, calling for rank-and-file safety committees and socialist reorganization of production.

US ramps up boat strikes in Latin American waters — Andrea Lobo

Beyond the figures cited in Top Story 7, Lobo’s investigation documents the March 26 drone destruction of the Ecuadorian vessel Don Maca 200 miles northwest of the Galápagos. Survivor Jhonny Sebastián Palacios told investigators: “We were just working, waiting for the last trawler to return. Everything was perfectly fine.” Lobo connects the escalating operations not to counter-narcotics but to: (1) countering Chinese economic influence in South America, (2) threatening Peru’s Chancay megaport (China’s largest infrastructure investment in the region), and (3) reasserting control over the Panama Canal.

Growing number of financial-system flashpoints — Nick Beams

Beams’s core analytical contribution — distinct from the economic data reported above — is that each flashpoint (BIS stablecoin warnings, the $39 trillion US debt load, doubled prime brokerage lending, concentrated non-bank leverage, private credit liquidity mismatches) would in isolation be serious, but their simultaneity under wartime conditions represents a systemic threat to the entire financial order, not a sum of manageable risks. This is the frame bourgeois financial reporting conspicuously refuses to apply.
