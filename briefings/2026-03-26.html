1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 26 as Tehran rejects ceasefire plan

The US-Israel war on Iran, launched with surprise strikes on February 28 that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and senior officials, entered its 26th day with no ceasefire in sight. Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi declared flatly: “We do not want a ceasefire,” calling the US 15-point peace plan “maximalist, unreasonable, and unworkable.” Iran issued its own five counter-conditions, including a full US-Israeli military withdrawal from the region and lifting of all sanctions — demands the US has categorically rejected.

The war continued to expand on multiple fronts. Iran fired six missile salvos at Israel on March 26, injuring nine, while also striking Saudi Arabia (28 drones and a ballistic missile intercepted), Bahrain, Kuwait, and the UAE, where two were killed and three injured by debris in Abu Dhabi. Iraqi resistance factions carried out 23 attacks on US bases in 24 hours, killing seven US soldiers and wounding 13 when an airstrike hit a military clinic and engineering unit at the Habbaniyah base west of Baghdad.

Key facts: - Some 8,000 tons of armaments have been airlifted and shipped to Israel since the war began; approximately 1,000 US 82nd Airborne troops are deploying imminently - The Pentagon is weighing whether to divert weapons earmarked for Ukraine to the Middle East as the war depletes critical US munitions stockpiles - Ben Gurion Airport closure extended through April 16; no direct US-Iran talks have occurred — Pakistan is serving as intermediary - Secretary of State Rubio is traveling to France to brief skeptical G7 allies amid soaring global fuel prices; European partners have been notably cool to the campaign - Israel’s KAN broadcaster reports Trump may announce a unilateral pause by Saturday if Iran makes a “substantial concession” - Only 35% of Americans support the strikes; 14 US service members have been killed, per the WSWS’s Andre Damon

As Damon noted on the WSWS, the 15-point ultimatum — including demands that Iran dismantle its nuclear facilities — is designed for rejection to justify further escalation, with military analysts describing operations as “preparing the battlefield” for a strike on Kharg Island, which controls 90% of Iran’s oil exports.

2. Strait of Hormuz crisis strands 2,000 vessels as oil prices surge

The Strait of Hormuz crisis deepened as approximately 2,000 vessels and 20,000 seafarers remained stranded in or near the waterway. Iran is charging ad hoc transit fees of up to $2 million per voyage on commercial ships it deems non-hostile, while waving through vessels from Pakistan and India. China and Iraq are in negotiations over passage.

The economic fallout is accelerating. Brent crude is expected to average $90/barrel in March, but an S&P Global “oil shock” scenario projects a peak of $200/barrel in Q2 2026 if the strait remains effectively closed through April. The WTO warned sustained high energy prices could cut 2026 global GDP growth by 0.3%. An Iranian strike destroyed approximately 17% of Qatar’s LNG export capacity, sending European natural gas prices to a historic €78/MWh before falling back below €50 on March 26.

Key facts: - Goldman Sachs projects Kuwait and Qatar could contract by 14%, UAE by 5%, Saudi Arabia by 3% - US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent indicated Washington could consider removing sanctions on Iranian oil to ease the energy crisis - South Korea established two emergency economic situation rooms in response to global market disruptions - The war has simultaneously rerouted shipping away from both the Strait of Hormuz and the Red Sea

3. Lebanon death toll surpasses 1,072 as Israel threatens ground invasion

Lebanon’s Health Ministry reported at least 1,072 killed and 2,966 wounded since Israel expanded its offensive on March 2, with 33 deaths in the last 24 hours. IDF Staff Sgt. Ori Greenberg was killed in a southern Lebanon gunfight on March 26. Israel continued airstrikes on Beirut’s southern suburbs while ordering mass evacuations.

Prime Minister Netanyahu has framed expanded operations as establishing a “buffer zone” in southern Lebanon. Lebanese officials warn a ground invasion south of the Litani River is imminent. UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned the fighting “must not condemn Lebanon to the same fate as Gaza,” calling the broader regional conflict “out of control.” French President Macron stressed “safeguarding Lebanon’s territorial integrity and protecting civilians.” The WSWS reported 1.2 million displaced within Lebanon.

4. France’s National Rally makes historic municipal gains

The second round of French municipal elections on March 22 produced a dramatic expansion of the National Rally’s institutional power. RN multiplied its total mayoralties sixfold nationally, taking control of more than 60 municipalities. RN chief Jordan Bardella called it “the biggest breakthrough in its entire history.”

RN retained Perpignan and won smaller southern towns including Carcassonne, Menton, and Orange, but was blocked by “Republican Front” coalitions in Toulon and Nîmes. In Marseille, incumbent Socialist Mayor Benoît Payan was re-elected with 56.3% despite RN polling neck-and-neck going in. In Paris, Socialist Emmanuel Grégoire was elected, extending the left’s 25-year hold on the capital. Former PM Édouard Philippe was re-elected in Le Havre, boosting his 2027 presidential ambitions.

Key facts: - RN gained 3,000+ new municipal councillors who will reshape the electoral college for Senate elections later in 2026 - Sciences Po political scientist Anne Muxel: the result “stands to confirm [RN’s] territorial integration in France” - The methodical accumulation of institutional power through local office-holding mirrors strategies used by fascist parties historically

5. UK slashes foreign aid by over 50% to fund defense buildup

Chancellor Rachel Reeves confirmed the UK’s Official Development Assistance budget will fall from 0.5% to 0.3% of GNI by 2027/28 — cuts totaling £500 million in 2025/26, £4.8 billion in 2026/27, and £6.5 billion in 2027/28. UK aid at 0.3% of GNI will be the lowest share since 1999 and the lowest in cash terms since 2012. The FCDO programme budget drops from £9.3 billion to £6.19 billion.

International Development Minister Anneliese Dodds resigned in protest, warning the cuts will mean “a UK aid pull out from numerous African, Caribbean, and Western Balkan nations.” Save the Children analysis estimates 55.5 million of the world’s poorest people will lose access to water, food aid, education, or family planning. The stated rationale is £2.2 billion in new defense spending. Climate finance was cut 13% from prior commitments, and the £3 billion nature earmark has been eliminated entirely.

6. Argentina officially exits WHO

Argentina’s withdrawal from the World Health Organization became legally effective on March 17, 2026, one year after formal UN notification and following the US exit completed in January. President Milei has called the WHO “a nefarious organisation” that carried out “the greatest experiment in social control in history” via COVID measures. Foreign Minister Pablo Quirno confirmed Argentina will pursue bilateral health agreements and regional frameworks instead.

The WHO had already cut its 2026–27 budget from $5.3 billion to $4.2 billion following the US exit. Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus warned the dual withdrawal creates a “dangerous void” in global disease surveillance. Analysts warn other right-leaning Latin American governments — particularly El Salvador and Paraguay — may follow. Argentina loses access to WHO technical support, funding channels, and pandemic coordination.

7. Sarah Mullally installed as first female Archbishop of Canterbury

Sarah Mullally was installed as the 106th Archbishop of Canterbury on March 26, becoming the first woman to hold the position in the Church of England’s nearly 1,500-year history. Mullally previously served as Bishop of London. She takes the role at a time of significant division within the Anglican Communion over doctrine, sexuality, and the church’s social role. The installation ceremony took place at Canterbury Cathedral.

US measles cases hit 1,487 — elimination status at risk

The CDC reports 1,487 confirmed measles cases across 32 jurisdictions from 14 outbreaks as of March 19, with 94% outbreak-associated. South Carolina accounts for over 600 cases; Utah over 275. Some 93% of case-patients are unvaccinated or have unknown vaccine status. The US is on pace to surpass 2025’s 2,285 total — a 35-year high — before summer. PAHO is expected to assess whether the US loses its measles elimination status (held since 2000) in November.

Sources CDC Measles data and research · CIDRAP US measles outbreak approaches 1,500 cases

Oral GLP-1 pill achieves 11% weight loss in phase 3 trials

Two landmark phase 3 trials of Eli Lilly’s oral GLP-1 receptor agonist orforglipron were published simultaneously in the New England Journal of Medicine and The Lancet. The ATTAIN-1 trial (NEJM; ~1,500 participants without diabetes over 72 weeks) found that orforglipron at 36 mg produced 11.2% mean body weight loss versus 2.1% for placebo; 54.6% of patients lost 10% or more of body weight. Among participants with prediabetes, 91% achieved near-normal blood sugar versus 42% on placebo.

The ATTAIN-2 trial (Lancet; 136 sites across 10 countries) confirmed efficacy in adults with obesity and type 2 diabetes. As a once-daily pill with no food or water restrictions, orforglipron could dramatically expand access to GLP-1 therapy beyond the current injectable-only options. Lilly’s FDA New Drug Application is under review with a potential decision in Q2 2026.

Sources NEJM ATTAIN-1: Orforglipron in adults with obesity · The Lancet ATTAIN-2: Orforglipron in obesity with type 2 diabetes

H5N1 spreads to 37 mammalian species in the Americas

Since April 2022, 75 human H5N1 infections (including 2 deaths) have been confirmed in five countries across the Americas, per PAHO’s March 11 epidemiological update. No new human cases have been reported since November 24, 2025. However, the virus has now been detected in 37 mammalian species and 94 bird species across the region, including — for the first time in California — northern elephant seals, sea lions, and a sea otter. The deepening spread into marine mammals and domestic swine signals H5N1 is entrenching across species barriers even as immediate human risk remains low.

Sources PAHO Epidemiological update: Avian influenza A(H5N1) in the Americas — March 11, 2026 · CDC Bird flu situation summary

Arts and culture

The most significant cultural development this week is the opening of Project Hail Mary, the long-anticipated adaptation of Andy Weir’s 2021 novel, directed by Phil Lord and Christopher Miller and starring Ryan Gosling. The film — about a lone astronaut who must save Earth from an extinction-level stellar phenomenon — arrives at a moment when its themes of international scientific cooperation and existential risk carry unavoidable political weight. With the real world consumed by a war that threatens global energy supplies and has already killed over a thousand Lebanese civilians, a narrative built on the premise that human survival depends on cross-species cooperation rather than military domination offers an implicit rebuke to the logic of imperialism, whether or not its creators intended it. The score is by Daniel Pemberton (Spider-Verse, Steve Jobs).

Broadway is experiencing one of its most eventful weeks of the season. The Rocky Horror Show begins previews tonight at Studio 54 in a star-studded revival featuring Luke Evans, Stephanie Hsu, Juliette Lewis, Harvey Guillén, Rachel Dratch, and Michaela Jaé Rodriguez, directed by Sam Pinkleton. Richard O’Brien’s 1973 creation — a transgressive celebration of sexual liberation set against the backdrop of Cold War paranoia — returns to a cultural landscape in which LGBTQ rights are once again under coordinated political assault from the far right. Whether this revival engages with that reality or retreats into nostalgic camp will determine its cultural significance.

Its commercial success reflects audiences’ appetite for comic relief during a period of sustained geopolitical anxiety.

Henson and Cedric the Entertainer, directed by Debbie Allen. Its revival under a Black woman director represents a serious engagement with historical memory.

In independent cinema, New York City’s inaugural Art House Cinema Week concludes today after a week-long program offering 5,000 free tickets and deep discounts at nearly 30 independent theaters across all five boroughs. The city-funded initiative represents a meaningful public investment in independent cinema infrastructure at a time when art house theaters face existential financial pressure from streaming platforms and rising real estate costs.

On streaming, the Duffer Brothers (Stranger Things) debut a new psychological thriller series on Netflix, their first post-Stranger Things project.
