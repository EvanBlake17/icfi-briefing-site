1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 21 as ground invasion preparations accelerate

The US-Israel war against Iran entered its 21st day on March 20, with confirmed deaths surpassing 1,444 in Iran — including 204 children — alongside 18 Israeli, 13 American, and 21 Gulf state fatalities. Iran’s Red Crescent reports over 18,000 civilians injured. The war, which began on February 28 less than two days after US-Iranian diplomatic talks in Geneva, has entered a critical new phase as White House sources indicate ground troop deployment is “under serious consideration.”

Approximately 7,500 Marines are arriving via three amphibious assault groups — the USS Tripoli redeployed from the Asia-Pacific, the USS Boxer group from California, and a third from the Mediterranean. One likely invasion target is Kharg Island, which processes 90% of Iran’s oil exports and lies just 15 miles offshore. As the WSWS’s Jordan Shilton warned in today’s Perspective, “one of the most likely scenarios is an attempt to capture Kharg Island,” an operation that would mark a dramatic escalation from the air campaign.

Key facts: - A US F-35 made an emergency landing after being struck by what CENTCOM believes was Iranian fire; pilot in stable condition - Iran killed IRGC top spokesman Ali Mohammad Naini; security chief Ali Larijani also confirmed killed - Bahrain has intercepted 139 missiles and 238 drones since February 28 - Trump branded NATO allies “COWARDS” on Truth Social for refusing to help control the Strait of Hormuz - Trump administration considering asking Congress for up to $200 billion in war funding - US temporarily lifting sanctions on Iranian oil already loaded on ships through April 19, expected to add ~140 million barrels to global supply - Netanyahu: “This war ending a lot faster than people think… You can’t do it only from the air… there has to be a ground component”

2. Iran strikes Qatar’s Ras Laffan LNG facility, cutting 17% of global gas capacity

Iranian strikes on Qatar’s Ras Laffan complex — the country’s main LNG export hub — have cut approximately 17% of Qatar’s LNG export capacity, with damage expected to last up to five years. The estimated financial losses reach ~$20 billion annually, representing a 9% hit to Qatar’s GDP. The strike came in retaliation after Israel targeted Iran’s South Pars gas field, from which Iran derives 70% of its natural gas.

The energy crisis extends far beyond the Gulf. The Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil and LNG transits — has been effectively closed for 19 days. Gas prices surged 35% across the UK and Europe. Brent crude peaked at $115/barrel after the South Pars strike and was trading at $112/barrel on March 21. Goldman Sachs projects elevated prices through 2027 if flows do not recover.

3. Pakistan airstrike kills 400+ at Kabul drug rehabilitation hospital

On March 16, the Pakistan Air Force struck the Omid Addiction Treatment Hospital, a 2,000-bed facility in Kabul, at approximately 9 p.m. local time. Afghanistan’s Interior Ministry reports 408 dead and approximately 265 injured. The UN’s UNAMA confirmed at least 143 dead and 119 injured in an ongoing investigation, while the Norwegian Refugee Council stated: “We found hundreds of civilians dead and injured.”

Pakistan’s military denied hitting the hospital, claiming it “precisely targeted” Camp Phoenix — an ammunition and drone storage site — and that secondary explosions caused fires at the hospital. The conflict began in late February when Pakistan launched “Operation Ghazab Lil Haq,” striking Taliban positions across six Afghan provinces. A temporary ceasefire is in place through Eid al-Fitr at Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkey’s request.

Key facts: - UN: 289 Afghan civilians, including 104 children, killed or injured since fighting began in late February - Tens of thousands of Afghans have been displaced by the fighting - India condemned Pakistan’s strikes as “a massacre”; EU said attacks on medical facilities violate the Geneva Conventions; China said the strike “raises serious concerns” - The conflict between two nuclear-armed states has received a fraction of the media attention devoted to the Iran war

4. EU summit collapses as Hungary and Slovakia veto €90 billion Ukraine loan

Hungary’s Viktor Orbán and Slovakia’s Robert Fico vetoed the €90 billion ($103 billion) EU loan to Ukraine for 2026–2027, causing the collapse of an EU summit and the cancellation of a planned press conference with President Zelenskyy. 6. US Congress twice rejects war powers resolution on Iran

The Senate voted 47–53 to reject a war powers resolution that would have forced Trump to end strikes on Iran absent congressional authorization. Only one Republican — Rand Paul — crossed over, while Democrat John Fetterman voted against. The House voted 212–219 against a companion resolution. This was the second such failed vote; Democrats previously tried in June 2025.

The United States is now waging a major regional war with no Authorization for Use of Military Force, no congressional authorization, and no defined end date. Senate Majority Leader Thune argued Trump already had authority. Sen. Chris Murphy (D-CT): “We shouldn’t be acting like this is business as usual… This is war and peace.” Sen. Tim Kaine stated Democrats plan to use the appropriations process as a next lever.

7. Lancet study projects 9.4–14 million deaths from USAID dismantlement by 2030

A peer-reviewed Lancet study analyzing 133 countries projects 14 million additional deaths by 2030 from the dismantlement of USAID — including 4.5 million children under 5, approximately 700,000 extra child deaths per year. A second CIDRAP study projects 9.4 million deaths under moderate scenarios and 22.6 million under severe scenarios. The tracking model ImpactCounter estimates 762,000+ people have already died since USAID was officially closed on July 1, 2025, including over 500,000 children; PEPFAR cuts alone account for 174,000 deaths.

The US slashed foreign aid disbursements from $68 billion in 2024 to $32 billion in 2025. Cascading cuts followed from the UK, Germany, and Canada; sub-Saharan Africa faces a 16–28% reduction in net bilateral aid. A US State Department official called The Lancet a “failed journal” and dismissed the findings, arguing the prior system “created a global culture of dependency.”

US measles cases hit 1,487 as elimination status comes under review

As of March 19, 1,487 confirmed measles cases have been reported across 32 US jurisdictions in 2026 — a dramatic surge that has prompted the Pan American Health Organization to declare the Americas has lost its verification as a measles-free region. The US elimination status is under formal assessment. The CDC reports 94% of cases are outbreak-associated, with 96% of cases in the ongoing South Carolina outbreak involving unvaccinated individuals or those with unknown vaccination status.

H5N1 spreads to California marine mammals

H5N1 has been confirmed in 27 northern elephant seals, 2 California sea lions, and 1 southern sea otter in San Mateo County — the first detection in California marine mammals. The causative strain is the A3 genotype, known to adapt in mammalian hosts and appearing in California for the first time. Nationally, over 8.97 million birds have been affected in the past 30 days. Pennsylvania alone has lost 7 million birds in 2026, the largest single-state poultry toll of the ongoing national H5N1 outbreak, driven by outbreaks in Lancaster County commercial egg-layer facilities where one site lost 2.6 million birds.

White House AI framework seeks federal preemption of state laws

The Trump administration released a six-point AI legislative blueprint on March 20 calling on Congress to establish a single federal AI standard and explicitly preempt state-level AI laws. The framework proposes child-safety guardrails, AI data center permitting and energy standards, and a hands-off approach to copyright liability for AI training data — leaving that to the courts. House Democrats immediately introduced the GUARD AI Laws and Standards Act to prohibit federal preemption. The framework triggers a direct federal-versus-state clash, with House GOP leadership aligning with the White House position.

Nature Medicine: 40% of cancers from modifiable risk factors

A study published in Nature Medicine analyzing cancer incidence data from 185 countries found that 4 in 10 cancers worldwide are attributable to modifiable risk factors, with substantial variation by country and cancer type. The findings strengthen the case for prioritizing upstream public health policy and population-level interventions over treatment-focused funding.

RECOVER study: COVID linked to diabetes risk for 3 years

New analysis from the RECOVER COVID initiative, studying over 2 million patients, found that COVID-19 infection is associated with increased risk of type 2 diabetes for up to 3 years post-infection, with risk scaling with disease severity. The persistent metabolic sequelae represent a long-tail public health burden not captured in acute case counts.

NEJM: Zorevunersen cuts seizures by up to 91% in Dravet syndrome

Published in the New England Journal of Medicine, Phase 1–2a trial results show the antisense oligonucleotide zorevunersen achieved a median reduction in convulsive seizure frequency of 58–91% in children with Dravet syndrome across 20 months of follow-up. Patients started with an average of 17 seizures per month. This is the first data suggesting disease modification — not just symptom control — in this severe genetic epilepsy. A Phase 3 global trial (EMPEROR) is now enrolling.

UK meningitis outbreak signals public health erosion

A deadly meningitis outbreak in the UK has prompted an infectious disease expert to note it represents “one of the greatest clusters outside Africa” in recent memory. The outbreak points to the erosion of public health infrastructure across Britain.

Arts and culture

The past 24 hours in arts and culture have been dominated by the intersection of war, institutional power, and the contested meaning of cultural production. From the closure of America’s premier performing arts center to the postponement of the Gulf’s flagship art fair, the Iran war’s shockwaves are reshaping the cultural landscape — while significant new works and revelations open questions about art’s relationship to society.

New Museum reopens with OMA expansion in New York

The New Museum on Manhattan’s Bowery reopened to the public on March 21 after a two-year closure, unveiling a $82 million expansion designed by OMA (Rem Koolhaas and Shohei Shigematsu) with executive architect Cooper Robertson. The project, a decade in the making, doubles the museum’s gallery space to 11,148 square meters and creates what is one of the few cultural institutions worldwide to unite the work of two living Pritzker Prize–winning practices (OMA alongside the original 2007 SANAA building).

The reopening exhibition, New Humans: Memories of the Future, brings together more than 200 artists, writers, scientists, and filmmakers — including Hito Steyerl, Anicka Yi, Francis Bacon, and Salvador Dalí — to explore how technological and societal changes have redefined what it means to be human. Opening weekend offers free admission. The question of who has access to such institutions remains pertinent: standard adult admission is $25, though visitors under 18 and SNAP/EBT recipients enter free.

Art Dubai postponed to May amid Iran war

Art Dubai has postponed its 20th anniversary edition from mid-April to May 14–17 in response to the Iran war, transforming the event from a full-scale commercial fair into a “more focused, curated, and flexible” cultural gathering. Several international galleries had already pulled out, art shipments had halted, and airlines suspended Dubai routes after the UAE was forced to temporarily close its airspace due to Iranian retaliatory strikes.

The postponement joins a growing list of Gulf cultural events disrupted by the conflict — ISEA2026 in Dubai, the annual March Meeting in Sharjah, and the Arabian Travel Market have all been rescheduled. The disruption lays bare the fragility of the Gulf’s cultural infrastructure, built on petrodollar wealth now directly threatened by the war whose energy consequences drive the very crisis.

Banksy identified as Robin Gunningham in Reuters investigation

A major Reuters investigation published March 13 identified the world’s most famous anonymous street artist, Banksy, as Robin Gunningham, 51, from Bristol. The evidence includes handwritten police confessions from a 2000 arrest in New York, court documents, and immigration records showing Gunningham (who changed his name to David Jones in 2008) crossed into Ukraine in 2022 just days before new Banksy murals appeared in Horenka.

Banksy’s lawyer, Mark Stephens, stated his client “does not accept that many of the details contained within the enquiry are correct,” while Pest Control, Banksy’s company, said the artist “has decided to say nothing.” Reuters argued the public interest justified publication given Banksy’s “profound and enduring influence on culture, the art industry and international political discourse.” The unmasking raises questions about the commodification of anonymity itself in an art market where Banksy’s identity-as-brand has functioned as both critique and commercial strategy.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output in the past 48 hours has centered on the economic dimensions of the Iran war and the Trump administration’s cultural attacks, but consistently channels opposition back toward Democratic Party legislative processes.

“The US Is Spending Billions to Bomb Iran” (Veronica Riccobene) details approximately $24 billion in anti-Iran operations and questions resource allocation — but frames the issue as a budgetary priority dispute within Congress rather than as an expression of the imperialist character of the American state. The article’s implicit solution is for Democratic legislators to redirect funds, not for the working class to independently oppose the war.

“Celebrities Can’t Save Opera. Public Funding Can.” (Jarek Paul Ervin) argues for “robust public investment and democratic control” of the arts — a reformist demand that treats the capitalist state as a neutral instrument capable of serving cultural needs, ignoring the class character of state funding decisions demonstrated by the Kennedy Center closure.

“Europe Is Sanctioning Critics of Israel and Militarism” (Caspar Shaller) documents how European sanctions are used to suppress free speech regarding Israel and military policy — useful documentation, but the analysis avoids connecting European militarism to the class interests driving it, instead presenting it as a democratic rights issue in isolation.

The overall orientation is one of loyal opposition: criticizing Trump’s spending priorities while directing readers toward Congressional advocacy and Democratic Party channels, with no perspective for independent working-class political action.

Left Voice

Left Voice has published extensively on the Iran war, with Juan Chingo providing the most substantive analysis among the pseudo-left tendencies. However, the political conclusions consistently fall short of a revolutionary perspective.

“The U.S. and Israel Are Winning Battles but Losing the War” (Juan Chingo, March 19) argues that tactical military victories are not yielding strategic advantage, with the South Pars/Ras Laffan exchange illustrating a “central paradox.” This is the strongest piece in Left Voice’s output — genuinely analytical about the military dynamics — but it evades the central question of the class forces capable of stopping the war. The article warns Iran’s strength lies in exploiting “structural vulnerabilities in the global system” rather than military symmetry, but offers no program for the international working class.

“Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran” calls on the labor movement to oppose imperialism, referencing the funeral of 160+ girls killed by Israeli strikes on an all-girls school in Minab. It notes less than one in four Americans support the strikes — useful data — but the call for educators to “take the lead” channels anti-war sentiment into trade union structures rather than building an independent political movement of the working class.

“We Need a Mass Movement to Defeat Trump’s Imperialist War on Iran” explicitly calls for bringing together “the No Kings protests, the movement for Palestine, communities organizing against ICE.” This coalition-of-movements approach is characteristic of Left Voice’s adaptation to middle-class protest politics — subordinating the working class to a grab-bag of identity-based and single-issue campaigns without raising the question of political power or socialist revolution.
