1. Trump says Iran peace deal “largely negotiated” as House Republicans pull war powers vote

President Trump announced Saturday that a deal with Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz is “largely negotiated” and will be announced “shortly.” Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baqaei confirmed that a 14-clause memorandum of understanding is in its final drafting stage. The agreement reportedly pairs a gradual reopening of the strait and discussions on Iran’s enriched-uranium stockpile with phased sanctions relief. Trump launched the war on Iran roughly three months ago — in late February 2026, alongside Israeli strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — without congressional authorization. A “shaky” ceasefire has held since early April.

In Washington, House Republican leadership abruptly cancelled a scheduled vote on a Senate-passed war powers resolution once it became clear the measure would pass. The capitulation underscores that an antiwar majority exists in Congress that the leadership of both parties is determined to suppress.

Key facts: - A prior procedural vote ended in a 212–212 tie; Democrats had secured at least three Republican defectors, including Rep. Brian Fitzpatrick (R-PA). - In the Senate, four Republicans crossed party lines to advance the resolution. - Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries called the cancellation “cowardly”; Rep. Gregory Meeks said, “We had the votes without question and they knew it.” - Under the 1973 War Powers Resolution, the president’s 60-day authority to wage unauthorized war has lapsed; the White House claims the ceasefire suspends the clock. - Polling shows most Americans oppose the war and do not believe it is going well.

As Jordan Shilton noted on the WSWS, the war has fractured the Western alliance — Spain has formally refused to permit US military bases to be used for Iran operations, and Rubio has openly complained that allies failed to rally behind Washington.

2. Strait of Hormuz crisis enters its third month as worst oil shock since the 1970s

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 25% of global seaborne oil and 20% of LNG normally transit — has become what the International Energy Agency calls “the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.” Iran closed the strait on February 28 following the US-Israeli airstrikes, with the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps attacking merchant vessels and laying sea mines. The crisis is now in its third month.

Key facts: - Brent crude peaked at $126/barrel; Dubai crude hit a record $166 on March 19; California gasoline topped $5/gallon by mid-March. - As of mid-May, more than 600 tankers are stranded inside the Gulf with another 240 waiting outside. - Iran established a “Persian Gulf Strait Authority” charging tolls exceeding $1 million per vessel. - Twelve seafarers have been killed or are missing; 17 merchant ships damaged; 104 Iranian Navy sailors were killed when the USS Charlotte torpedoed the IRIS Dena. - Saudi Arabia cut oil output by 20%; European natural gas more than doubled, rising from €30/MWh to above €60/MWh.

The downstream effects fall hardest on the working class and poor: fertilizer (urea) shortages threatening food production across South Asia and Africa, soaring heating costs in Europe, and food inflation worldwide — consequences that have received almost no analytical treatment in the bourgeois press.

3. Gaza ceasefire at six months: more than 800 killed, reconstruction stalled

Six months into the ceasefire known as the “Washington Accord,” the UN Security Council was told this week that conditions in Gaza remain “far from perfect,” with no tangible recovery underway and the phased peace plan paralyzed. Envoy Nickolay Mladenov warned the Council that Gaza risks “permanent” division and a state of limbo if the transition continues to stall.

Key facts: - More than 800 people have been killed and 2,600 injured since the ceasefire began in October 2025. - Israel has not withdrawn its troops and continues to restrict humanitarian aid; 80% of Gaza’s buildings are damaged or destroyed. - Over one million people lack permanent shelter; health and education systems have not been rebuilt. - Only $540 million of the more than $4 billion required for reconstruction has been received; aid “remains far below actual need.” - The plan is deadlocked: Hamas has not accepted the disarmament roadmap, and Israel has made any troop withdrawal contingent on Hamas disarmament.

The “ceasefire” thus functions as a managed continuation of the siege — a permanent occupation enforced through the deliberate withholding of reconstruction.

4. Ebola outbreak in DR Congo spreads to Uganda, with more than 150 dead

A Bundibugyo-strain Ebola outbreak centered in the Democratic Republic of Congo’s Ituri Province has generated more than 650 suspected cases and over 150 suspected deaths, with confirmed seeding in three major cities — Bunia, Goma and Kampala. At least one death has been confirmed in neighboring Uganda. The WHO declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) on May 16 — its first ever for this strain, for which no approved vaccine or treatment exists and which carried 30–50% fatality rates in prior outbreaks.

Key facts: - A candidate vaccine is estimated to be 6–9 months from availability. - Provincial authorities have banned public gatherings in Ituri; Africa CDC Director-General Jean Kaseya said the agency is in “panic mode,” lacking PPE, medicines and vaccines. - Ituri already endures roughly 6 million conflict-related deaths, 7 million internally displaced people and 27 million facing acute hunger, with cholera, measles and mpox endemic.

As Evan Blake argued on the WSWS, the catastrophe is not a natural disaster but “social murder”: the Trump administration terminated the $100 million STOP Spillover surveillance initiative and dismantled PEPFAR-linked infrastructure, while pledging a “derisory” $13 million in response. The eastern DRC conflict that has hollowed out the health system is driven by competition over coltan, gold and cobalt feeding Western technology and defense supply chains.

Simultaneously, Ukrainian forces launched a mechanized counterattack in the Borova direction, reportedly penetrating up to 5 kilometers into Russian defenses, according to the Institute for the Study of War (ISW).

The escalation unfolds against the backdrop of the NATO powers’ commitment to a vastly expanded war footing in Europe even as Washington’s attention is consumed by the Iran conflict — a convergence of two major theaters of imperialist conflict that raises the danger of a wider war.

6. 50,000 evacuated in Orange County as overheating chemical tank nears explosion

Roughly 50,000 residents across Garden Grove and portions of West Anaheim, Cypress, Buena Park and Stanton remained under evacuation orders as of Saturday as an overheating tank of approximately 7,000 gallons of methyl methacrylate — a highly volatile, toxic and flammable compound — threatened to explode or rupture at a GKN Aerospace Transparency facility. Governor Gavin Newsom declared a state of emergency Saturday and deployed additional state resources; the district attorney’s office has launched a probe into the cause.

Key facts: - Officials described two scenarios: a “massive fireball” or a toxic chemical leak across the evacuated neighborhoods. - GKN Aerospace is a British defense contractor that manufactures landing gear and aircraft components; no injuries were reported as of Saturday. - The plant’s proximity to Disneyland, Knott’s Berry Farm and Angel Stadium has heightened public concern.

The episode underscores how multinational defense industries operate hazardous facilities embedded in dense working-class residential areas, with regulatory oversight subordinated to corporate cost-cutting.

Bolivia’s general strike enters fourth week as military repression kills four

Bolivia’s indefinite general strike — led by the Central Obrera Boliviana (COB) and now in its fourth week — was met on May 18 with the deployment of 2,500 police and 1,000 soldiers in La Paz. At least four protesters were killed and 127 detained in the single largest demonstration of the conflict, which drew miners, teachers, peasants, factory workers and indigenous communities. The strike was triggered by President Rodrigo Paz’s Decree 5503, an austerity package that follows an IMF and Harvard Growth Lab roadmap and has driven fuel costs sharply higher.

Key facts: - Arrest warrants on terrorism charges have been issued against COB executive-secretary Mario Argollo and 24 other union leaders. - As of May 21, Bolivia’s road administration registered 46 active blockades at strategic access points to La Paz and El Alto. - Foreign ministers of ten countries condemned the broader regional crackdown; the repression has not broken the movement.

As Tomas Castanheira documented on the WSWS, Washington is coordinating the response: Rubio declared US support for the “legitimate constitutional government,” Argentina’s Javier Milei allegedly sent weapons to blockaded cities, and only Colombia’s Gustavo Petro called the events “a popular insurrection” — and was punished with the expulsion of Colombia’s ambassador.

Arts and culture

Romanian director Cristian Mungiu won the Palme d’Or at the 79th Cannes Film Festival for Fjord, starring Sebastian Stan and Renate Reinsve. The film follows a mixed Romanian-Norwegian couple who relocate to a remote fjord village and collide with locals holding a “very different outlook on life” — widely described as a culture-war drama. Mungiu becomes only the 11th filmmaker to win the Palme twice, following 4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days (2007), his unsparing portrait of clandestine abortion under the Ceaușescu dictatorship. US distributor NEON claimed its seventh consecutive Palme d’Or.

That a director who emerged from the Romanian New Wave — a movement rooted in honest reckoning with the Stalinist bureaucracy and its aftermath — should now win for a study of rural insularity and cultural friction is itself telling. The best of this tradition has insisted on examining ordinary people caught in the machinery of social crisis; the question for Fjord is whether it interrogates the reactionary “outlook” it depicts or merely stages it as spectacle. NEON’s commercial dominance of the Cannes prize also illustrates how even the festival’s gestures toward serious cinema are absorbed into a tightly controlled distribution market.

The other major cultural event of the week was a monument to that same market logic. As David Walsh wrote on the WSWS, a single Christie’s evening saw $1.1 billion in art sold, headlined by Jackson Pollock’s Number 7A, 1948 at $181.2 million — three times his previous record — and a new record for a Constantin Brancusi work, drawn from the estate of media billionaire S.I. Newhouse, whose widow explained she was “downsizing her life.” Walsh invokes Marx and Diderot to argue that when monetary exchange dominates art’s purpose, something essential to human creativity is destroyed. The global art market — $59.6 billion in 2025 — represents, in his words, capitalism’s “extreme, parasitic decay,” with masterworks that could enrich public museums locked away in private vaults.

Roundup: - The juxtaposition of the two stories is instructive: at Cannes, art as a contested terrain of social meaning; at Christie’s, art as a pure asset class for a parasitic financial aristocracy. Both are products of the same epoch of social polarization. - The concentration of cultural ownership — record sums for canvases by Pollock and Brancusi — mirrors the concentration of wealth that has driven equity markets to record highs even amid mass layoffs and falling living standards.

Perspective: No Perspective was published today, May 24 (Sunday). The most recent Perspective — “The Ebola epidemic, imperialism and the political economy of social murder” by Evan Blake (May 23) — is summarized below; its core analysis is integrated into the Ebola top story above.

The Ebola epidemic, imperialism and the political economy of social murder — Evan Blake

Beyond the facts cited above, the Perspective develops a century-long historical arc: under Belgian King Leopold II, an estimated 10 million Congolese died under forced-labor regimes; HIV is traced by researchers to colonial-era Kinshasa circa 1920; the CIA orchestrated Patrice Lumumba’s 1961 assassination, installing Mobutu Sese Seko, whose rule gutted health infrastructure. Blake connects the present outbreak to the “eugenicist normalization of mass death” institutionalized during COVID-19, which he says killed “more than 30 million people worldwide” with 400 million suffering Long COVID. He cites a 2022 Nature study projecting that climate- and land-use-driven shifts in animal ranges will generate thousands of new opportunities for cross-species viral transmission, concentrated in Asia and Africa. The political conclusions are programmatic: emergency restoration of surveillance, free vaccine development and the deployment of medical resources — steps impossible under capitalism — require “the international unification of the working class in the struggle for world socialism,” organized through cross-border rank-and-file committees to expropriate the mining and technology conglomerates.

Management boasts deal to end Long Island Rail Road strike “within the MTA’s financial plan” — Tom Hall

MTA CEO Janno Lieber announced a tentative agreement ending a three-day strike by 3,500 LIRR workers, boasting that the deal fell “within the MTA budget” — an admission that directly refutes management’s earlier claim that the workers’ demands were unaffordable. The union shut the strike down Monday evening without a membership vote and withheld full contract details, providing only “brief email bullet points.” The agreed 4.5% wage increase in the contract’s final year falls below New York City’s official 4.6% inflation rate, and management retains work-rule changes “on the table.” The WSWS calls for workers to reject the deal and demand full transparency before ratification, situating the sellout within a national pattern of union bureaucracies suppressing strikes.

NATO summit in Sweden exposes sharpening inter-imperialist antagonisms — Jordan Shilton

The piece frames these conflicts as symptoms of “the complete breakdown of the post-World War II equilibrium,” driven by US economic decline and producing rivalries reminiscent of pre-WWI conditions. (The summit’s specifics on military spending and the US-Canada defense rift are incorporated into the International section above.)

A Turkish court unlawfully removes the CHP leadership from office — Barış Demir, Ulaş Sevinç

Ankara’s 36th Civil Chamber declared the CHP’s November 2023 and April 2025 congresses “absolute nullities,” retroactively removing chairman Özgür Özel and reinstating former leader Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu. Fifteen bar associations jointly condemned the ruling as “unlawful,” noting that constitutional authority over party-congress legality rests with the Supreme Election Board, not regional civil courts. The authors argue the ruling is part of systematic Erdoğan-government repression accelerated after the CHP’s strong March 2024 local-election performance and Istanbul Mayor Ekrem İmamoğlu’s rising poll numbers, concluding that democratic rights can be defended only through independent working-class mobilization, not appeals to existing state institutions.

Anger in Australia over brutal treatment of Gaza flotilla activists — Oscar Grenfell

Grenfell situates the Labor government’s posture within Australia’s deepening military alignment with the United States and its complicity in the Gaza siege.

A $1 billion evening for the art world — David Walsh

Summarized in the Arts and culture section above. The crisis of public health in the era of the COVID-19 pandemic: an interview with Arijit Chakravarty — Benjamin Mateus

Beyond the framing cited in the Science section, Chakravarty identifies three structural consequences of the COVID failure: institutional (governments now use communication alone as disease management); immunological (repeated COVID infections cause lymphocytopenia, with white blood cells taking 6+ months to recover, producing population-level immune impairment); and behavioral (mass unwillingness to mask or quarantine). His prescriptions — air-quality infrastructure for all US public buildings at the cost of roughly one aircraft carrier, honest risk communication, and treating every zoonotic outbreak as potentially aerosol-transmissible by default — remain unimplemented.

New revelations show San Diego neo-Nazi mosque attackers were known to police and FBI before massacre — Jacob Crosse

Three Muslims were killed at the Islamic Center of San Diego by Caleb Vazquez (18) and Cain Clark (17). Chula Vista police had issued an emergency gun-violence protective order against the Vazquez home in January 2025 after documenting that Caleb “had engaged in suspicious behavior idolizing Nazis and mass shooters” — 30 firearms were in the home — and Bloomberg reported he was flagged in an FBI eGuardian alert in 2025. No warning was issued to the mosque. Despite Clark’s mother calling police at 9:40 a.m. to report her son had stolen a car and taken weapons, officers failed to locate him. Crosse argues the FBI’s characterization of the manifesto as reflecting “various ideologies” deliberately obscures its grounding in neo-Nazi “Great Replacement” theory, and connects the failure to the administration’s counterterrorism strategy, which targets anti-fascists while ignoring the far right.

Audit report documents Australian government’s betrayal of 2022 flood victims — Mike Head

An NSW auditor-general performance audit found that four years after the 2022 Lismore and Northern Rivers floods killed 13 people and left 4,055 properties uninhabitable, zero of 4,382 promised replacement homes or housing lots have been delivered — only 793 buy-backs finalized and 54 resilience grants completed. The Resilient Homes program, initially promised at up to $1.5 billion, has been repeatedly cut to just 1,000 houses. Head attributes the failure not to administrative incompetence but to the systemic subordination of working-class needs to capitalist priorities, pointing to simultaneous spending on nuclear submarines and new fossil-fuel approvals.

Sri Lankan president uses war commemoration to declare “economic war” on working people — Wasantha Rupasinghe

At the 17th National Victory Day ceremony marking the 2009 defeat of the LTTE, President Anura Kumara Dissanayake declared his government must win an “economic war,” framing IMF-dictated austerity as national necessity. The program has already raised fuel prices ~40%, gas ~31% and electricity tariffs ~32% since February, with a doubling of energy prices anticipated. Rupasinghe parallels the rhetoric to Mahinda Rajapakse’s 2009 “nation building” posture and documents the JVP-led government’s banning of strikes, military deployment against postal workers and emergency laws against electricity workers. The WSWS advocates debt repudiation, bank nationalization and a workers’ government.

New Zealand woman held for weeks in ICE detention — John Braddock

Everlee Wihongi, a 37-year-old New Zealand citizen and 25-year US green-card holder, was detained by ICE for six weeks after returning from a family visit, on the basis of a marijuana conviction more than a decade old. She was held across multiple facilities — including a 46-person shared room with 22-hour confinement and Camp East Montana at Fort Bliss (3,000 detainees, 2,000 beds) — shackled in overcrowded transport and denied food. NZ Foreign Minister Winston Peters refused to intervene, saying he would act only if she faced “the death penalty.” Braddock contextualizes the case within the 60,310 people in ICE custody as of April 4 and 468,450 deportations since Trump’s inauguration.

Having canceled Los Angeles schools strike, SEIU circulates petition to ask that layoffs stop — Marc Wells

After SEIU Local 99 canceled a 77,000-worker Los Angeles schools walkout at 2:30 a.m. on April 15 — hailing it as an “historic victory” — the union is now circulating a letter-writing petition against layoffs that are proceeding anyway, amid an $877 million LAUSD deficit for 2026–27. The district spends an estimated $1 billion annually on private contractors doing work identical to that of laid-off union employees. Wells situates LAUSD within a national pattern of Democratic-aligned union bureaucracies suppressing strikes (LIRR, UC service workers, transit) and calls for independent rank-and-file committees through the IWA-RFC.

UPS shuts down midnight shift at Manhattan’s 43rd Street Hub — A. Woodson, Robert Milkowski

UPS eliminated 103 jobs at its Manhattan 43rd Street hub on April 17, offering remaining workers split shifts with five-hour unpaid gaps — conditions one worker said “in some countries would be considered torture.” It is part of a company-wide elimination of ~48,000 jobs since 2024, with 30,000 more planned for 2026, even as CEO Carol Tomé received $19 million in 2024 compensation, the company reported $5.5 billion in net income, and robots already outnumber humans 15-to-1 at the Louisville hub. The article attacks the Teamsters for negotiating a 2023 contract with no automation safeguards and maintaining silence about the closures.

Four weeks of general strike in Bolivia — Tomas Castanheira

The factual core and US-coordination analysis are integrated into the International section above. The article’s distinctive contribution is its accounting of regional governmental complicity: Brazil’s Lula offered no condemnation of the repression, Deputy Secretary of State Christopher Landau pressed Brazil and Colombia to back President Paz, and only Petro broke ranks — demonstrating, the WSWS argues, that no faction of the Latin American bourgeoisie will defend the working class against IMF austerity enforced at gunpoint.

Pro-independence Parti Québécois strengthens its ties with the far right — Laurent Lafrance

PQ leader Paul St-Pierre Plamondon gave a one-hour interview to Rebel News — which the Quebec Press Council has ruled is not a news organization but “an activist organization with ties to far-right circles” — during which he promoted what Lafrance characterizes as “Great Replacement” theory, claiming federal immigration aims to “drown” French-speaking Quebecers, and proposed restricting immigrant access to public services. The article argues the PQ has become “the most virulent promoter of anti-immigrant ethno-chauvinism in official Quebec politics” since 2020, with Quebec unions continuing to channel workers toward it. Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — doubling down on electoralism after Mamdani’s mayoral win, with DSA cochairs floating a “left-labor” presidential candidate inside the Democratic orbit
  • Left Voice — celebrating the Minneapolis anti-ICE revolt while subordinating it to a movementist, campus-centered framework
  • Party for Socialism and Liberation — protest-perpetualism on Palestine and “Liberation Centers” as a substitute for revolutionary perspective
  • Socialist Alternative — economic agitation around graduate joblessness and war spending without a break from reformist demands
  • Socialist Worker (SWP) — interpreting Reform UK’s surge as a call to “build the left” through cross-class anti-racist fronts
  • Revolutionary Communist Party (IMT) — self-promotional “party-building” triumphalism around its Third Congress

Jacobin and the DSA

Jacobin’s output continues to orbit the electoral success of New York Mayor Zohran Mamdani. In “We Need a Left-Labor Presidential Candidate,” DSA national cochairs argue that Mamdani’s victory — and the organization’s growth past 100,000 members — proves “the Left can win,” and call for a coalition of social movements, labor and “the Left” to recruit and run a presidential candidate. “Socialists Aim for Assembly Seat in Kathy Hochul’s Backyard” profiles a Buffalo DSA member running for the state assembly with union and tenant-group backing.

The unifying political line is the subordination of the working class to the Democratic Party. The “left-labor candidate” formulation is a mechanism for channeling radicalization back into the very party of war and austerity, dressing capitalist politics in socialist phraseology. The orientation toward winning seats “in Hochul’s backyard” treats the capitalist state as a neutral instrument to be captured rather than overthrown.

Left Voice

Left Voice’s May magazine foregrounds the January anti-ICE revolt in Minneapolis, with “Image/Revolt: Minneapolis From Below” offering a street-level chronicle, alongside CUNY May Day coverage demanding “Full Rights for Immigrants” and “Down with U.S. imperialism.” The PTS-affiliated group is to its credit hostile to the Democrats and raises the call for working-class methods.

Its weakness is characteristic of Morenoism: a movementist, spontaneist framework that elevates episodic struggles and campus mobilizations over the fight to build a politically independent revolutionary party. The emphasis on “rebellion” and imagery substitutes radical atmospherics for the patient construction of a Marxist leadership rooted in the working class, leaving its militant phrases disconnected from a strategy for the conquest of power.

Party for Socialism and Liberation

Liberation News continues its Palestine coverage with statements such as “We will stay in the streets until Palestine is free!” and addresses the collapse of Spirit Airlines and the Purdue Pharma settlement. The PSL’s expanding network of “Liberation Centers” — now more than two dozen — is presented as the organizational future.

The political line is one of perpetual protest divorced from a struggle against the Democratic Party and the trade-union bureaucracy. The PSL’s enthusiasm for “anti-imperialist” campism leads it to political accommodation with bourgeois nationalist regimes rather than to a perspective of international socialist revolution. Its center-building substitutes a self-sufficient activist apparatus for the political education and mobilization of the working class.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative published agitational pieces on young graduates’ inability to find work and on the share of income tax consumed by the military — “more than a third of 2025 income tax was used to fund war and military weapons” — alongside commentary on China’s 15th Five-Year Plan.

The exposures are factually useful, but the political conclusions remain trapped within reformism: demands for taxing the rich and redirecting the budget that leave the capitalist state and the profit system intact. Having dissolved its earlier orientation into broad “left” formations, Socialist Alternative continues to substitute pressure politics and single-issue campaigns for the building of a revolutionary party.

Socialist Worker (SWP)

Following the May local elections, Socialist Worker leads with “Elections 2026: Reform UK surges as mainstream politics fractures,” documenting Reform’s capture of over 950 council seats and Labour’s “humiliation,” alongside coverage of 2,700 threatened jobs and home-office repression.

The SWP’s response — “resisting the right, building the left” — points toward cross-class anti-racist popular fronts and left-Labour realignment rather than the independent political mobilization of the working class. By treating the rise of the far right as a problem of insufficient “unity” rather than a product of the bankruptcy of Labourism and the trade-union bureaucracy, the SWP disarms workers before the very forces it claims to oppose.

Revolutionary Communist Party (IMT)

The British RCP’s press is dominated by its Third Congress (held May 1–4, coinciding with the centenary of the 1926 General Strike) and documents such as the Organisational Resolution 2026, which boasts of growth from 813 to 1,200 members, and British Perspectives 2026. The tone is one of party-building triumphalism.

Behind the revolutionary branding, the IMT’s perspective rests on a syndicalist faith in a spontaneous radicalization and the recruitment of youth on the basis of impressionistic “communist” branding, detached from the historical struggle of the Fourth International against Stalinism and Pabloite revisionism. Its substitution of organizational metrics for a worked-out program leaves it adapting to the existing labor bureaucracies it claims to fight.
