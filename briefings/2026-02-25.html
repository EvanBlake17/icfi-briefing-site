1. Trump’s State of the Union: 108 minutes of demagogy, Iran war threats, and Democratic complicity

Donald Trump delivered the longest State of the Union address in modern history on the night of February 24 — approximately 108 minutes of belligerent demagogy that centered the drive toward war with Iran, doubled down on the immigration crackdown, and buried the catastrophic social reality of American life beneath transparently false economic claims.

On Iran, Trump struck his most aggressive public posture to date. He accused Tehran of working to rebuild its nuclear program after the June 2025 US strikes (Operation Midnight Hammer), claimed Iran is developing missiles “that soon would be capable of reaching the US,” and asserted that Iran killed 32,000 antigovernment protesters — a figure far exceeding any credible estimate and immediately denounced by Tehran as “big lies.” Trump declared: “My preference is to solve this problem through diplomacy, but one thing is certain: I will never allow the world’s number one sponsor of terror to have a nuclear weapon.” This formulation — the same language used before the 2003 Iraq invasion — lays the rhetorical groundwork for military action under the guise of a diplomatic failure that Washington itself is engineering.

The speech was a festival of false claims. Trump stated gasoline is “below $2.30 a gallon in most states” — not a single state has an average below $2.30, according to AAA. He described inflation as “plummeting” — PolitiFact rated this an exaggeration; the January 2026 year-over-year increase was 2.4%, modestly below the rate Trump inherited. Groceries are up 2%, electricity up 6.3%, housing up 3.4%, and medical care up 3.2% under his tenure. As the WSWS Editorial Board established in yesterday’s Perspective, “The real state of Trump’s America,” the facts Trump will never cite include: national debt at $38.4 trillion growing at $8 billion per day; the top 1% controlling 31.7% of all wealth; CEO pay 285 times average worker pay; and 66,000 people held in ICE detention.

On immigration, Trump called on legislators to stand if they believe “the first duty of the American government is to protect American citizens, not illegal aliens,” producing a prolonged Republican ovation while Democrats sat. Representative Al Green was escorted out after holding a sign reading “Black people aren’t apes,” referencing a Trump Truth Social video depicting the Obama family as primates — later removed; the White House called it “posted in error.” Dozens of Democrats boycotted the address entirely, attending a rally on the National Mall. The Democratic response was delivered by Virginia Governor Abigail Spanberger — a CIA veteran, continuing the pattern from last year when CIA veteran Elissa Slotkin was selected, signaling the Democrats’ fealty to the military-intelligence apparatus.

Notably absent from the 108-minute address: any acknowledgment of the fourth anniversary of Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine, despite Trump’s campaign promise to end the war; any mention of the Epstein files, though some of Epstein’s victims were in attendance. The WSWS has not yet published a dedicated analysis of the speech itself — this is the most significant coverage gap of the day and should be discussed by the board.

2. Iran war drive escalates: Geneva talks, mass evacuations, student protests

The Iran crisis has entered a new phase in the 24 hours since yesterday’s briefing. Three developments are significant.

First, a third round of indirect US-Iran talks is scheduled for Thursday, February 26, in Geneva. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and his delegation have already departed Tehran. Iran has publicly stressed its “red lines”: zero enrichment and the ballistic missile program are non-negotiable, and Iran insists its nuclear program is exclusively civilian. The talks will occur under the toxic atmosphere created by Trump’s SOTU claims — including the fabricated 32,000-protester death toll — meaning the diplomatic track is being poisoned even as it nominally proceeds. This is the same dynamic the WSWS’s Andre Damon identified in his buildup reporting: the appearance of diplomacy as political cover for the continuation of military preparations.

Second, a growing list of countries have issued urgent evacuation warnings for their citizens in Iran. Australia has told its nationals to “leave as soon as possible.” Germany describes the “security situation” as “extremely volatile.” India has warned all citizens — students, pilgrims, business owners — to “exercise due caution” and leave. Poland’s PM Tusk issued a blunt directive: “please leave Iran immediately.” South Korea has issued similar warnings. Australia has additionally directed dependents of officials to leave Lebanon and Israel and is offering voluntary departures from Jordan, Qatar, and the UAE. This is not routine diplomatic precaution — it is the international community’s intelligence assessment that war is imminent, expressed through civilian evacuation orders.

Third, BBC-verified video shows Iranian student protests spreading to additional universities. This is a significant political development that the WSWS has not yet analyzed. The class character of the Iranian student movement, its relationship to the theocratic regime, and the way US imperialist pressure both fuels internal opposition and simultaneously undermines its progressive content — these are questions demanding our analysis. The US is exploiting Iranian internal dissent to justify military intervention, just as it did in Libya and Iraq.

3. Pentagon issues ultimatum to Anthropic: AI safeguards or blacklisting

As this briefing’s author reported for the WSWS, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth gave Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei a three-day ultimatum on Tuesday to grant the military “unfettered access” to Claude AI or face blacklisting as a “supply chain risk” — or compulsion under the Defense Production Act, a wartime industrial law never previously used against an AI company. The confrontation, which took place in a tense meeting attended by six senior defense officials including the Pentagon’s top lawyer, was triggered by revelations that Claude was used — without Anthropic’s knowledge — in the illegal January 3 military assault on Caracas, Venezuela, in which between 83 and 100 people were killed and President Maduro was abducted.

The bourgeois press is framing this as a principled stand by Anthropic for “AI safety” against military overreach. This is a fabrication. As the WSWS article documents, Anthropic has aggressively pursued military integration: a November 2024 partnership with Palantir and AWS to deploy Claude on classified networks; a June 2025 “Claude Gov” product for national security agencies; a $200 million Pentagon contract awarded July 2025. On the same day as the ultimatum, Elon Musk’s xAI signed a deal to deploy Grok AI on classified military networks without restriction, breaking Claude’s exclusive position — making the Pentagon’s leverage over Anthropic explicit.

The broader context is Hegseth’s January 9 “Artificial Intelligence Strategy for the Department of War,” which commits to an “AI-first warfighting force” with seven “Pace-Setting Projects” due for demonstration by July 2026: “Swarm Forge” (autonomous drone swarms), “Agent Network” (AI battle management and kill chain automation), and “Ender’s Foundry” (AI military simulation). Anthropic’s post-meeting statement expressed “appreciation” for the Department’s work and “thanked the Secretary for his service” — the posture of a corporation preparing to capitulate. A senior Defense official told Axios: “The only reason we’re still talking to these people is we need them and we need them now.”

4. Kaiser Permanente strike betrayal: the political anatomy of coordinated sabotage

Tom Hall’s Perspective, discussed in the contribution above, provides the essential framework for understanding the UNAC/UHCP bureaucracy’s shutdown of the 31,000-worker Kaiser Permanente strike. The strike was ended without a contract, without a membership vote, and on the same day 500 operating engineers were to join — at the precise moment when a potential West Coast strike wave of over 100,000 workers was taking shape. As Hall writes, “A unified movement would have laid the basis for a powerful strike wave across the West Coast, encouraging similar action throughout the country. The bureaucracy could not allow this because it would threaten their financial and social interests.”

Hall situates the Kaiser betrayal in its full political context. The UNAC/UHCP participates in the Labor-Management Partnership, “funded by management with millions of dollars annually” and “explicitly established in 1997 to prevent strikes and foster a ‘culture of collaboration.’” During the strike, Kaiser implicitly threatened this framework through a lawsuit, effectively threatening the bureaucracy with a funding cutoff if it failed to end the action. The corporation reported $9.3 billion in net income while CEO Greg Adams received $13 million in compensation, and had recently settled with the DOJ for $556 million in Medicare fraud. Kaiser maintains investments in ICE detention contractors and has entered a partnership with Walter Reed for mass-casualty war training — the structural integration of the healthcare industry with the military-industrial complex.

The Perspective names Democratic politicians as direct agents of suppression: Pelosi and millionaire Mayor Lurie brokering the San Francisco teachers’ sellout; “democratic socialist” Mayor Mamdani and Julie Su working behind the scenes on the NewYork-Presbyterian settlement while police arrested protesting nurses; the USW simultaneously pushing through a 30,000-worker refinery sellout that Hall identifies as a war contract keeping fuel flowing for Iran operations. Hall’s political conclusion is definitive: “No amount of ‘pressure’ can alter the social interests of this layer any more than pressure can induce corporate management to give up profits.”

5. Quakertown 5: students face felony charges while police chief faces none

The Quakertown, Pennsylvania situation has escalated significantly. Jacob Crosse reports that the five teenagers arrested during the February 20 anti-ICE walkout now face felony aggravated assault charges — confirmed by the Philadelphia Inquirer from two anonymous sources. Two students remain in custody as of Tuesday; three have been released to parents on house arrest. No charges have been filed against Police Chief Scott McElree, who was filmed in plain clothes choking a teenage girl and assaulting students on a public sidewalk.

A 17-year-old witness confirmed that McElree “barged onto the sidewalk” and grabbed a teenage boy by the neck; students believed he was a counter-protester, not a police officer. At least three students suffered significant injuries: one broken nose, one chin wound requiring stitches. Students were held over 72 hours before a Tuesday morning hearing closed to the public. Attorney Ettore Angelo reported his 15-year-old client was released to house arrest — she faces aggravated assault, a felony punishable by up to five years.

Nearly 70 residents packed a typically sparse Quakertown Borough Council work session Monday night demanding McElree’s immediate suspension. Student Colin told reporters: “I do not feel very safe going back to school tomorrow… every police officer that was present on the scene that day should be investigated.” As Crosse writes, the contrast is stark: these students face five years in prison; Trump has served zero jail time for any of his crimes. The prosecution of the Quakertown 5 expresses the fascistic and class character of the US government — the criminalization of political protest by children who dared to oppose mass deportation.

6. Detroit mass protests against ICE detention centers — SEP intervenes

Hundreds of workers, students, and residents in Detroit’s suburbs protested Monday evening against DHS plans to expand ICE detention and operational infrastructure in the region. In Romulus, 700-800 people gathered outside City Hall opposing a plan to convert a 250,000-square-foot warehouse near Detroit Metropolitan Airport into a 500-bed ICE detention center — one of 23 new mega-warehouse detention centers DHS plans nationwide, designed for rapid and secretive deportation flights. In Southfield, 200-300 packed a City Council meeting opposing ICE office space at One Town Square.

The SEP intervened directly. Jerry White, recently returned from Minneapolis, addressed Romulus protesters and warned that what happened in Minneapolis was “only a dry run for repression.” He called for mobilization of autoworkers, Amazon workers, truck drivers, and airline workers toward “a general strike to demand the abolition of ICE.” White drew attention to the Democratic Party’s role: Minnesota Governor Tim Walz agreed with the Trump administration to deploy state troopers against anti-ICE protesters, freeing federal agents for redeployment to Michigan and other states.

The Democratic politicians present — Lt. Gov. Garlin Gilchrist, state legislators Darrin Camilleri and Dylan Wegela — all attempted to channel anger into midterm elections. The Romulus City Council adopted a nonbinding resolution opposing the center primarily on grounds of “local resources” and “depressed property values” — not on the basis of democratic principle. Southfield’s resolution affirmed “local control” while declaring the city “cannot ignore federal law.” A 15-year-old, Charles O’Neal, quoted the anti-Nazi poem “First they came for the socialists…” before being silenced by Council President Lloyd Hicks under meeting rules. The class lines were drawn clearly: the Democrats seek to exploit the protests electorally; the SEP alone raised the program of independent working class action.

7. Measles surges past 1,000 cases — South Carolina engaged in deliberate obfuscation

US measles cases have passed the 1,000 mark for 2026 — CDC confirmed 982 as of reporting, with the CNN tracker at 1,030 — far ahead of the pace that produced a three-decade high of 2,281 infections in all of 2025. The US did not breach 1,000 cases until May last year; the threshold has been crossed in late February this time.

As Benjamin Mateus reports for the WSWS, South Carolina has emerged as the epicenter, with 973 cases concentrated in Spartanburg County, and the state is engaged in what Dr. Paul Offit of Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia describes as deliberate obfuscation: reporting only a 2% hospitalization rate (just 20 admissions) when the CDC’s standard estimate for measles hospitalization is 20%. ProPublica documented the cover-up as “driven by profound and calculated indifference.” State epidemiologist Dr. Linda Bell admitted the agency “is not getting an accurate picture at all” yet declared the state has not considered adding measles hospitalizations to mandatory reporting.

The CDC is rudderless. The principal deputy director, Ralph Abraham — a former Republican congressman — resigned effective immediately “to attend to ‘unforeseen family obligations,’” leaving the agency further depleted. Jim O’Neill, the acting director before Bhattacharya, is expected to be nominated to lead the National Science Foundation — an institution for which he is equally unqualified. Bhattacharya’s failure to demand transparent hospitalization data from South Carolina constitutes, as Mateus writes, “tacit federal endorsement of the state’s obfuscation.” National kindergarten MMR vaccine coverage has fallen to 92.5% — below the 95% threshold for herd immunity. The absence of leadership is not a policy failure but the intended policy itself.

International developments

Iran: the diplomatic track and the war preparations are not contradictory

Beyond what is covered above, the diplomatic choreography around Iran requires analysis. The third round of indirect Geneva talks scheduled for Thursday represents not a genuine peace process but a mechanism for managing international opinion while the military buildup continues. Iran’s “red lines” — zero enrichment and ballistic missiles as non-negotiable — have been consistent for years. Modi in Israel: India’s ruling class alignment with genocide

Indian PM Narendra Modi arrived in Israel on February 25 for a two-day visit — his second to the country, the first since becoming the first Indian leader to visit in 2017. Modi is scheduled to address the Knesset and will oversee the signing of strategic economic and defense agreements with Netanyahu on February 26. The visit occurs while Israel’s genocidal war in Gaza continues without pause.

This represents a historic shift. India was among the countries that opposed Israel’s creation in 1948 and was, as Al Jazeera notes, “one of the most forceful non-Arab critics of Israel’s policies towards Palestinians” until 1992. Since Modi’s BJP came to power in 2014, the relationship has “flourished.” India’s Communist Party condemned the visit as “legitimising the murderous Netanyahu regime” — a correct characterization, though the CPI offers no program to mobilize the Indian working class against it. The WSWS has not yet covered this visit, which has significant implications for the alignment of the world’s most populous country with the US-Israeli axis, and for the Indian working class and the oppressed masses of South Asia.
