Perspective Contribution

Comrades, today’s Perspective by Tom Hall on the sabotage of the Kaiser Permanente strike is, I think, one of the most politically important Perspectives we have published during this entire strike wave, because it does not merely report on the betrayal of a single strike — it establishes the analytical framework for understanding what is happening across the entire American and international labor movement at this moment.

The central argument Hall makes is that the UNAC/UHCP bureaucracy did not merely fail the 31,000 Kaiser workers. It acted deliberately and with precise timing to prevent the emergence of a unified strike movement on the West Coast. This is the critical point. The strike was shut down on the very day 500 Kaiser operating engineers were to join it — and at the moment when, as Hall documents, over 100,000 potential reinforcements were in motion: 65,000 LAUSD teachers and classified staff who have authorized strikes, and 40,000 UC graduate and academic workers who have voted to strike. A unified movement of that scale would have constituted the most powerful labor action in the United States in decades. The bureaucracy intervened precisely because that prospect was becoming real.

What makes this Perspective especially sharp is its placement of the Kaiser betrayal within the coordinated pattern of simultaneous sellouts: NYSNA ending the NewYork-Presbyterian nurses’ strike, San Francisco teachers shut down with Pelosi and millionaire Mayor Lurie brokering the deal, the USW pushing through a refinery sellout that Hall correctly identifies as a war contract keeping fuel flowing for operations against Iran, and the suppression of anti-ICE strike action by union officials nationwide. This is not a series of disconnected events — it is, as Hall writes, a social layer defending its material interests against the working class. The Labor-Management Partnership at Kaiser, funded by management with millions annually and explicitly designed to prevent strikes since 1997, is the institutional expression of this class relationship.

I want to draw the board’s attention to two connections Hall makes that extend the analysis. First, the identification of Democratic Party politicians — Pelosi, Lurie, Mamdani, Julie Su — as direct agents of strike suppression, not merely passive bystanders. This connects directly to the role Democrats are playing in channeling the anti-ICE movement into electoral politics, as we saw last night at the Detroit protests where Lt. Gov. Gilchrist attempted to redirect anger toward the midterms. Second, Hall’s conclusion that “no amount of pressure can alter the social interests of this layer any more than pressure can induce corporate management to give up profits” is a formulation we should carry forward in every article on the strike wave. It is the Marxist answer to the illusion — promoted by the DSA and the pseudo-left — that the existing unions can be reformed through internal pressure campaigns.

This Perspective should orient the board toward a synthesis article on the national strike wave and the role of the bureaucracy as a coordinated instrument of class suppression, which I will raise in the discussion points. The data Hall provides — Kaiser’s $9.3 billion in net income, its $556 million Medicare fraud settlement, its investments in ICE detention contractors, its partnership with Walter Reed for mass-casualty war training — are the concrete material through which we can demonstrate that the healthcare corporations and the military-industrial state are structurally integrated, and that the union bureaucracy’s suppression of strikes is the domestic counterpart of its support for imperialist war.

1. Trump’s State of the Union: 108 Minutes of Demagogy, Iran War Threats, and Democratic Complicity

Donald Trump delivered the longest State of the Union address in modern history on the night of February 24 — approximately 108 minutes of belligerent demagogy that centered the drive toward war with Iran, doubled down on the immigration crackdown, and buried the catastrophic social reality of American life beneath transparently false economic claims.

On Iran, Trump struck his most aggressive public posture to date. He accused Tehran of working to rebuild its nuclear program after the June 2025 US strikes (Operation Midnight Hammer), claimed Iran is developing missiles “that soon would be capable of reaching the US,” and asserted that Iran killed 32,000 antigovernment protesters — a figure far exceeding any credible estimate and immediately denounced by Tehran as “big lies.” Trump declared: “My preference is to solve this problem through diplomacy, but one thing is certain: I will never allow the world’s number one sponsor of terror to have a nuclear weapon.” This formulation — the same language used before the 2003 Iraq invasion — lays the rhetorical groundwork for military action under the guise of a diplomatic failure that Washington itself is engineering.

The speech was a festival of false claims. Trump stated gasoline is “below $2.30 a gallon in most states” — not a single state has an average below $2.30, according to AAA. He described inflation as “plummeting” — PolitiFact rated this an exaggeration; the January 2026 year-over-year increase was 2.4%, modestly below the rate Trump inherited. Groceries are up 2%, electricity up 6.3%, housing up 3.4%, and medical care up 3.2% under his tenure. As the WSWS Editorial Board established in yesterday’s Perspective, “The real state of Trump’s America,” the facts Trump will never cite include: national debt at $38.4 trillion growing at $8 billion per day; the top 1% controlling 31.7% of all wealth; CEO pay 285 times average worker pay; and 66,000 people held in ICE detention.

On immigration, Trump called on legislators to stand if they believe “the first duty of the American government is to protect American citizens, not illegal aliens,” producing a prolonged Republican ovation while Democrats sat. Representative Al Green was escorted out after holding a sign reading “Black people aren’t apes,” referencing a Trump Truth Social video depicting the Obama family as primates — later removed; the White House called it “posted in error.” Dozens of Democrats boycotted the address entirely, attending a rally on the National Mall. The Democratic response was delivered by Virginia Governor Abigail Spanberger — a CIA veteran, continuing the pattern from last year when CIA veteran Elissa Slotkin was selected, signaling the Democrats’ fealty to the military-intelligence apparatus.

Notably absent from the 108-minute address: any acknowledgment of the fourth anniversary of Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine, despite Trump’s campaign promise to end the war; any mention of the Epstein files, though some of Epstein’s victims were in attendance. The WSWS has not yet published a dedicated analysis of the speech itself — this is the most significant coverage gap of the day and should be discussed by the board.

2. Iran War Drive Escalates: Geneva Talks, Mass Evacuations, Student Protests

The Iran crisis has entered a new phase in the 24 hours since yesterday’s briefing. Three developments are significant.

Australia has told its nationals to “leave as soon as possible.” Germany describes the “security situation” as “extremely volatile.” India has warned all citizens — students, pilgrims, business owners — to “exercise due caution” and leave. Poland’s PM Tusk issued a blunt directive: “please leave Iran immediately.” South Korea has issued similar warnings. Australia has additionally directed dependents of officials to leave Lebanon and Israel and is offering voluntary departures from Jordan, Qatar, and the UAE. This is not routine diplomatic precaution — it is the international community’s intelligence assessment that war is imminent, expressed through civilian evacuation orders.

Third, BBC-verified video shows Iranian student protests spreading to additional universities. This is a significant political development that the WSWS has not yet analyzed. The class character of the Iranian student movement, its relationship to the theocratic regime, and the way US imperialist pressure both fuels internal opposition and simultaneously undermines its progressive content — these are questions demanding our analysis. The US is exploiting Iranian internal dissent to justify military intervention, just as it did in Libya and Iraq.

7. Measles Surges Past 1,000 Cases — South Carolina Engaged in Deliberate Obfuscation

US measles cases have passed the 1,000 mark for 2026 — CDC confirmed 982 as of reporting, with the CNN tracker at 1,030 — far ahead of the pace that produced a three-decade high of 2,281 infections in all of 2025. The US did not breach 1,000 cases until May last year; the threshold has been crossed in late February this time.

As Benjamin Mateus reports for the WSWS, South Carolina has emerged as the epicenter, with 973 cases concentrated in Spartanburg County, and the state is engaged in what Dr. Paul Offit of Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia describes as deliberate obfuscation: reporting only a 2% hospitalization rate (just 20 admissions) when the CDC’s standard estimate for measles hospitalization is 20%. ProPublica documented the cover-up as “driven by profound and calculated indifference.” State epidemiologist Dr. Linda Bell admitted the agency “is not getting an accurate picture at all” yet declared the state has not considered adding measles hospitalizations to mandatory reporting.

The CDC is rudderless. The principal deputy director, Ralph Abraham — a former Republican congressman — resigned effective immediately “to attend to ‘unforeseen family obligations,’” leaving the agency further depleted. Jim O’Neill, the acting director before Bhattacharya, is expected to be nominated to lead the National Science Foundation — an institution for which he is equally unqualified. Bhattacharya’s failure to demand transparent hospitalization data from South Carolina constitutes, as Mateus writes, “tacit federal endorsement of the state’s obfuscation.” National kindergarten MMR vaccine coverage has fallen to 92.5% — below the 95% threshold for herd immunity. The absence of leadership is not a policy failure but the intended policy itself.

IEB Discussion Points

Point 1: The State of the Union Speech Demands a Dedicated WSWS Analysis

The WSWS published an outstanding Perspective yesterday — “The real state of Trump’s America” — that established the political framework for understanding the SOTU before it was delivered. But the speech itself has now been delivered, and it contains specific content that demands dedicated analysis. Trump’s 108-minute address centered the Iran war drive with maximalist claims — fabricated death tolls, allegations of intercontinental missile development, the classic “my preference is diplomacy but I will never allow” formula. The Democratic response was delivered by CIA veteran Spanberger. Trump ignored the Ukraine anniversary entirely. Al Green was removed for protesting the racist Obama ape video.

The AP, BBC, and Al Jazeera have published fact-checks and analysis, but none provides the class analysis. I would propose a rapid-turnaround article — ideally by Andre Damon, given his command of the Iran material — that analyzes the SOTU specifically as a war speech, situates the Iran content within the ongoing military buildup, and exposes the Democratic response as complicity rather than opposition. The speech was watched by tens of millions of people; our response must be timely and sharp.

Point 2: The Iranian Student Protests Require Analysis — Before US Imperialism Coopts Them

BBC-verified video shows Iranian student protests spreading to additional universities. This is a significant development that connects to major questions in the ICFI’s analysis. What is the class character of the Iranian student movement? What is its relationship to the theocratic regime, to the working class, and to the various bourgeois opposition factions? How does US imperialist pressure — including Trump’s fabricated claim that Iran killed 32,000 protesters — simultaneously fuel internal opposition and undermine its progressive content by providing the regime with a nationalist rallying point?

The danger is clear from historical precedent: the US exploited genuine popular opposition in Libya, Iraq, and Syria to provide cover for military intervention that produced catastrophic results for the very populations it claimed to be liberating. The WSWS must establish its analysis of the Iranian opposition movement now — before the war begins and the narrative is locked in. I would propose an article, perhaps by Keith Jones or David North, that addresses the Iranian student movement within the framework of permanent revolution and the ICFI’s analysis of the Middle East.

Point 3: Sudan’s Genocide — The WSWS Coverage Absence Must Be Addressed

The BBC reports intensified RSF drone attacks in Sudan’s Kordofan region — a strategic gold and oil zone described as a pivotal new front line. Mass civilian casualties are resulting. The UN has characterized the El Fasher siege as having “hallmarks of genocide.” Sudan’s civil war has produced one of the worst humanitarian catastrophes in the world, with millions displaced and famine conditions spreading.

The WSWS has had minimal coverage of Sudan. This is a gap that needs to be addressed. The class dynamics are clear: the RSF and the Sudanese military are rival factions of a ruling class fighting over resources and power, with the working class and rural population bearing the catastrophic cost. The international dimension — UAE support for the RSF, Egyptian support for the military, the competition for gold and oil resources — connects directly to the ICFI’s analysis of how imperialist rivalries produce regional wars. I would propose that we commission a comprehensive analysis of the Sudan crisis, situating it within the broader framework of African resource wars and the competing interests of regional and global powers.

Point 4: Modi in Israel — India’s Alignment with Genocide and Its Implications

Indian PM Modi’s visit to Israel — addressing the Knesset, signing defense and economic agreements with Netanyahu — is a politically significant development the WSWS has not covered. India was historically one of the strongest non-Arab critics of Israeli policy toward Palestinians. The transformation under Modi’s BJP — from that position to actively legitimizing the Netanyahu government during a genocide — represents a major realignment that has implications for the Indian working class, for South Asian geopolitics (particularly India-Pakistan tensions), and for the international solidarity movement.

India’s Communist Party condemned the visit as “legitimising the murderous Netanyahu regime,” but the CPI and CPM have no program to mobilize the working class against it. The WSWS should analyze this visit within the framework of the Indian ruling class’s integration into the US-Israeli axis, the implications for India’s Muslim population under BJP rule, and the strategic competition between India and China that is reshaping Asian geopolitics. I would propose an article, potentially by our South Asian correspondents, that addresses these dimensions.

Point 5: The Al Jazeera Epstein Banking Investigation — Material for a Major WSWS Analysis

Al Jazeera has published what amounts to a comprehensive financial investigation of the Epstein network after his 2008 conviction, based on the DOJ documents released January 30. The investigation documents how JPMorgan maintained Epstein’s accounts despite internal warnings and suspicious activity reports; how Jes Staley visited Epstein during his sentence; and how the banking system continued to process his money. Combined with Bill Gates’s staff meeting acknowledgment of his Epstein ties — “I did nothing illicit. I saw nothing illicit” — and the Mandelson arrest in the UK, the material is now available for a dedicated WSWS analysis that develops David North’s framework of the Epstein network as revealing “the social physiognomy of a degenerate ruling class.”

The specific angle I would propose: an article that analyzes the financial infrastructure of the Epstein network — not the sex trafficking itself, which dominates bourgeois coverage, but the banking relationships, the billionaire connections, and the institutional mechanisms that allowed a convicted sex offender to remain embedded in the highest levels of finance capital for over a decade. This is the class analysis that the bourgeois press systematically avoids, and it is the analysis that connects the Epstein files to the broader crisis of capitalist legitimacy.
