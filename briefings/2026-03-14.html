1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 14 with Kharg Island strikes and mounting civilian toll

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran entered its fourteenth day on March 13, with intensified strikes on Kharg Island — the facility handling roughly 90% of Iran’s crude oil exports. Trump declared the US had “totally obliterated every military target” at the site. The Pentagon confirmed war costs exceeded $11.3 billion in the first six days alone.

The human toll continues to mount. At least 1,444 people have been killed and 18,551 wounded in Iran since the war began on February 28. The UN Refugee Agency reports 3.2 million Iranians displaced in under two weeks. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth stated Iran’s new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei is “wounded and likely disfigured,” while Vice President Vance confirmed “we know that he’s hurt.”

As the WSWS Editorial Board warned in its March 14 perspective, the administration is actively preparing a ground invasion of Iran’s coastline to seize control of the Strait of Hormuz — a move the Australian Strategic Policy Institute described as “Gallipoli times ten.”

Key facts: - A US Marine expeditionary unit of approximately 2,200 personnel is deploying from Okinawa; the US has deployed 10,000 Merops interceptor drones at $14,000–$15,000 each - Bahrain intercepted 114 missiles and 190 drones; Saudi Arabia shot down 56 Iranian drones overnight; NATO intercepted a ballistic missile fired toward Turkey - A coalition of 250+ US organizations — including ACLU, SEIU, Greenpeace, and Jewish Voice for Peace — called on Congress to halt the administration’s $50 billion supplemental war funding request - At least 175 children were killed in a single US strike on an elementary school in Minab

2. Iran’s new Supreme Leader orders continued Strait of Hormuz blockade

Mojtaba Khamenei issued his first public statement since becoming Supreme Leader following the assassination of his father Ali Khamenei on February 28, ordering forces to continue blocking the Strait of Hormuz and calling on Middle Eastern nations to close US military bases. In a significant geopolitical development, Iran is considering allowing limited tanker passage through the Strait only if oil is traded in Chinese yuan, according to a senior Iranian official.

The economic consequences are severe. Brent crude surged above $103 per barrel, a multi-year high. The IEA announced a record release of strategic reserves to offset the disruption, with global oil supply projected to fall by 8 million barrels per day in March. US gas prices reached $3.63 per gallon, a 22-month high.

Key facts: - The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20 million barrels per day — approximately 20% of global oil supply - Australia ordered non-essential diplomatic staff to leave the UAE and Israel - Qatar Airways scheduled 140+ special repatriation flights; Dubai International Airport reported severe disruptions - The World Food Programme warned the blockade is disrupting fertilizer supplies, threatening long-term global food prices

3. Israeli bombardment kills 687 in Lebanon in under two weeks

Israeli strikes have killed 687 people in Lebanon — including 98 children — since March 2, with between 700,000 and 750,000 people displaced. Strikes hit the Bachoura neighborhood of central Beirut on March 13, killing at least 12 people. A separate strike on the town of Arki killed 9 people including 5 children. Israel’s designated evacuation zone for southern Lebanon has nearly doubled in the past 48 hours.

In Gaza, the UN reported that nearly all humanitarian aid movements through the Kerem Shalom crossing were denied in the past two days, sharply limiting delivery of critical supplies to the besieged population.

Key facts: - Iceland and the Netherlands joined South Africa’s genocide case against Israel at the International Court of Justice - Spain permanently withdrew its ambassador to Israel over the Iran war - Israel dropped charges against five soldiers accused of sexually assaulting a Palestinian detainee at Sde Teiman prison, where the victim suffered broken ribs, a punctured lung, and rectal damage; Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich called the soldiers “heroes” - The former military lawyer who leaked surveillance video of the Sde Teiman abuse was arrested

4. UN General Assembly passes Ukraine ceasefire resolution 107–12

On the fourth anniversary of the war, the UN General Assembly adopted a resolution titled “Support for lasting peace in Ukraine” by 107 votes in favor, 12 against, with 51 abstentions. The US abstained, warning that “some language in the resolution is likely to distract from ongoing negotiations.” The resolution demands an immediate, full, unconditional ceasefire, the release of all unlawfully detained persons, and the return of deported children.

US-brokered peace talks led by Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner have been put on hold due to the Middle East escalation. Three prior rounds in the UAE and Switzerland produced no breakthrough.

Key facts: - A UN Commission estimates 20,000 Ukrainian children have been abducted to Russia or Belarus and subjected to military training; Commission chair Erik Møse called these acts “enforced disappearance as a crime against humanity” - A Russian aerial attack struck the eastern Ukrainian city of Sloviansk, killing 4 people and injuring at least 16 - Russia also launched drone attacks on three other Ukrainian cities overnight, wounding at least 17 - Ukraine’s deputy minister stated Russia has shown “no genuine willingness to stop its aggression”; Russia warned against a “hasty truce”

5. DOGE mass federal layoffs shrink US government workforce 12 percent

The US government civilian workforce shrank by 12% between September 2024 and January 2026, representing approximately 212,000 fewer federal employees — roughly 9% of the 2.3 million civilian workforce. The Defense Department lost 60,000+ employees, Treasury lost 30,000+, Agriculture lost 20,000+, and HHS saw a 20% staff reduction.

The effects are cascading into the private sector. Employers have cited the “DOGE impact” as the direct reason for 63,583 private-sector layoffs. The Trump administration has since attempted to rehire some workers in critical positions — including USDA staff combating H5N1 bird flu and National Nuclear Security Administration personnel — who were laid off in error.

Key facts: - Tariffs are simultaneously imposing an average tax increase of $1,500 per US household in 2026 - US GDP growth projected to fall by 0.5 percentage points from tariff effects alone - PBS reports that some purged federal employees are now receiving messages asking if they want to return

Sources Chicago Sun-Times Trump economy: DOGE layoffs, wages, tariffs · Reuters US government shed workers in Trump’s first year · PBS NewsHour Federal employees purged by DOGE asked to return

6. Sudan: SAF recaptures Khartoum Presidential Palace amid mass civilian massacres

The Sudanese Armed Forces recaptured the Presidential Palace in Khartoum, which had been under Rapid Support Forces control since the civil war began in April 2023. The recapture marks a significant shift in the conflict’s dynamics, though both sides continue committing atrocities against civilians.

The RSF attacked Al-Maliha in North Sudan, approximately 200 km north of El Fasher, killing at least 45 people on an apparent ethnic basis, according to the Sudan Doctors Network. Separately, SAF allegedly conducted an airstrike on the Tur’rah market, killing hundreds of civilians — videos showed “smoking ruins of market stalls and bodies charred beyond recognition.”

Key facts: - The UN Human Rights Council is holding an enhanced interactive dialogue on Sudan as part of its 61st regular session through March 31 - Both warring factions continue to target civilian populations with impunity - The conflict has produced one of the world’s largest displacement crises, with millions forced from their homes since April 2023

Sources Global R2P Atrocity alert no. 432 · UN Human Rights Council 61st regular session

7. 4,000 JBS meatpackers in Colorado authorize first major meatpacking strike in decades

Workers at JBS — the world’s largest meat producer — in Greeley, Colorado voted to authorize a strike set to begin Monday. Approximately 4,000 workers are involved, making this potentially the first major meatpacking industry strike in decades in the United States.

Workers cite low wages, forced purchases of personal protective equipment, and immigration-based discrimination as key grievances. JBS settled a $4 million child labor violation suit last year without admitting wrongdoing, underscoring the exploitative conditions in the industry.

Key facts: - JBS is the world’s largest meat producer, with global operations spanning multiple continents - The strike authorization comes amid a wave of labor militancy across US industries - The company’s record of child labor violations highlights the broader exploitation characterizing the meatpacking sector

Sources Havana Times International news briefs — March 13, 2026 · Democracy Now Daily news coverage

Lufthansa pilots conduct 48-hour strike

Approximately 5,300 Lufthansa pilots conducted a 48-hour warning strike with 95% participation among Cityline pilots, cancelling 75% of short-haul and 70% of long-haul flights. The pilots demand 11% cumulative pay increases owed retroactively since February 2024.

Management has responded by expanding the low-cost subsidiary City Airline as a pressure mechanism and threatening 800 cockpit and cabin crew jobs. The strike is the latest in a wave of European labor action amid the economic disruptions caused by the Iran war and rising energy costs.

US measles cases hit 1,362 across 31 jurisdictions

The CDC reports 1,362 confirmed measles cases as of March 12, spread across 31 jurisdictions. A striking 94% of cases (1,281) are outbreak-associated, with 14 new outbreaks declared in 2026 alone. The geographic spread extends from Alaska to Florida, reflecting sustained community transmission driven by outbreaks that originated in 2025.

The scale of the outbreak — already exceeding the total for any recent full year — underscores the consequences of declining vaccination rates and the gutting of public health infrastructure through DOGE-imposed federal layoffs, including the firing of CDC personnel.

Sources CDC Measles data and research

H5N1 bird flu kills 7 million+ birds in Pennsylvania

Over 7 million birds have died in Pennsylvania in 2026, with 51 flocks confirmed infected nationwide in the past 30 days. The most concerning development is in California, where the A3 genotype — known for mammalian adaptation — has spread from elephant seals to a sea otter and sea lion. Regionally, 75 human H5N1 infections (2 deaths) have been recorded since April 2022, though no new human cases have occurred since November 2025.

The mammalian spread of the A3 genotype is the key watchpoint for pandemic risk assessment. The simultaneous DOGE-driven firing and rehiring of USDA bird flu response staff creates dangerous gaps in surveillance.

Sources PAHO/WHO Epidemiological update: avian influenza A(H5N1) · South Philly Review Pennsylvania bird flu outbreak — 7 million birds

Fish oil cuts cardiovascular events 43% in dialysis patients

A major international trial found that 4 grams per day of omega-3 fish oil supplements reduced life-threatening cardiovascular events by 43% in dialysis patients — a population with extremely high cardiac risk and few evidence-based interventions. The clinically striking result has potential immediate practice implications for the millions of patients worldwide on dialysis.

Sources ScienceDaily Health and medicine news

PCSK9 base editing validates in-vivo gene therapy

A phase 1 study published in Nature Medicine demonstrated that liver-targeted PCSK9 base editing can lower cholesterol in patients with familial hypercholesterolemia. The result validates in-vivo gene editing as a therapeutic strategy, moving the field beyond one-off CRISPR cuts toward precise base editing for a common, life-threatening genetic condition.

Sources Nature Medicine PCSK9 base editing study

The UK government must publish two AI/copyright reports by March 18 under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, covering AI training rights and protections for AI-generated outputs — a key data point in the global divergence between the EU’s prescriptive approach and the US deregulatory posture.

Key data: a 2020 depot explosion, 2021 wheel arch fractures that grounded the entire 12-tram fleet, and an Independent Rail Review finding that incidents and delays are at their highest level in a decade. The WSWS argues that the Labor government, safety regulators, and the Rail Tram and Bus Union have all failed workers, calling for independent rank-and-file safety committees.

Fiji’s HIV epidemic worsens amid deepening social crisis — John Braddock

Fiji recorded 2,003 new HIV diagnoses in 2025, up from 1,583 in 2024 — a 3,091% increase since 2010 and a 17-fold increase in the diagnosis rate since 2019, now reaching 226 per 100,000. One baby per week is diagnosed through mother-to-child transmission; an estimated 8,900 Fijians are living with HIV. The WSWS locates the catastrophe in Western imperialist domination and domestic austerity policies that have gutted public health infrastructure, arguing only socialist transformation can resolve the crisis.

  • Jacobin / DSA — focuses on neocon influence networks and AI in warfare; avoids calling for independent working-class action against the war

  • Liberation News / PSL — issues high-profile “Stop Trump’s War on Iran” statement while channeling opposition through ANSWER Coalition protest circuit and congressional lobbying
  • Socialist Alternative — limited recent output; January Greenland article reflects the tendency’s adaptation to bourgeois geopolitics
  • Socialist Worker / SWP (UK) — opposes Labour’s Al-Quds Day march ban and analyzes the “Your Party” failure; frames opposition through street activism rather than revolutionary politics

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s March 13 output centers on the Iran war through two distinct lenses. “Neocons have shaped Washington’s Iran war plans” by Katya Schwenk traces neoconservative influence on the current military campaign through the Vandenberg Coalition, chaired by Elliott Abrams. The article draws parallels to the Project for the New American Century’s role before the Iraq War, identifying dark money networks and donor-advised funds funding the war drive. While the exposé is journalistically competent, its political framework treats the war as a product of neoconservative influence networks rather than the structural imperatives of American imperialism — effectively presenting the problem as one of corrupted policy-making rather than capitalist crisis. The implicit solution is transparency and accountability within the existing political system.

“Artificial intelligence is already making war more horrific” by David Moscrop examines AI in military operations, drawing on Eric Hobsbawm to argue that technology extends the “impersonal cruelty” of industrial warfare. The article notes the Pentagon’s use of the Maven Smart System for target identification in “Operation Epic Fury” but offers no concrete political strategy — no call for working-class action, no mention of the labor movement’s potential to halt arms production. It functions as a moral warning without programmatic content.

“Defend Cuba from US efforts to crush it” by Helen Yaffe calls for international solidarity against Trump’s fuel blockade of Cuba, framing the issue within a defense of Cuban sovereignty. The article avoids any critique of the Cuban Stalinist bureaucracy’s role in the island’s crisis.

Jacobin’s overall orientation remains characteristic: competent reportage on symptoms of imperialist decay, combined with a systematic avoidance of revolutionary conclusions. The Iran war is treated as bad policy rather than an expression of capitalist crisis, and the Democratic Party’s role as war enabler receives mild criticism rather than the fundamental opposition the situation demands.

Sources Jacobin Neocons have shaped Washington’s Iran war plans · Jacobin Artificial intelligence is already making war more horrific · Jacobin Defend Cuba from US efforts to crush it

Left Voice

Left Voice published “Their violence and ours” by Andrea D’Atri (March 4), which states that revolutionary socialists are “neither pacifists nor neutral” and defends Iran against US-Israeli aggression without offering political support to the Iranian government. The article reflects the PTS/FT tendency’s framework of calling for the “military defeat” of imperialism — a formulation that, while more radical than Jacobin’s, substitutes a military outcome for the independent political mobilization of the working class.

The tendency also published a statement from the Current for Permanent Revolution – Fourth International (CPR-FI) arguing that “the working class and anti-imperialist movement must fight for the defeat of imperialist military aggression.” While this invokes working-class agency, the practical content remains oriented toward the existing anti-war protest movement rather than building independent organs of working-class power.

Left Voice’s coverage of the Columbia student ICE abduction case (Ellie Aghayeva) and the UAW Local 2325 strike authorization vote against ICE reflect the tendency’s strength in labor reporting, though the political conclusions remain limited to trade union activism within the existing AFL-CIO framework rather than a break with the union bureaucracy.

Sources Left Voice Their violence and ours

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL’s flagship statement “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” characterizes the conflict as “a war for empire” driven by US determination to dominate Middle East oil resources and strategic waterways. The statement correctly notes the war is “illegal under both the US Constitution and international law” and cites the Minab school bombing. It calls for nationwide protests through the ANSWER Coalition network.

The political limitations are characteristic: the PSL’s opposition is framed through constitutional legality and public opinion rather than class struggle. The statement notes the war is “immensely unpopular” and calls on “Congress to halt war funding” — directing anti-war energy toward the very institution that has consistently funded imperialist wars. There is no call for independent working-class political action, no critique of the trade union bureaucracy’s failure to mobilize against the war, and no internationalist perspective connecting American workers’ interests to those of Iranian workers. The PSL’s practical activity — organizing street protests — functions as a pressure campaign on the Democratic Party rather than a challenge to the capitalist system that produces war.

A separate article on military production argues that “switching to civilian production creates more jobs” — a reformist framework that treats the war economy as a policy choice rather than a structural feature of declining capitalism.

Sources Liberation News PSL statement: Stop Trump’s war on Iran!

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

Socialist Worker’s most significant piece is “Why you should oppose Labour’s ban on Al-Quds Day march,” covering Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood’s decision to ban the march. The article correctly identifies the ban as an attack on Palestine solidarity and democratic rights, noting police double standards in allowing far-right Tommy Robinson supporters to march while restricting Palestinian solidarity events. The Stop the War Coalition is quoted: “It is not necessary to agree with all the politics of Al-Quds Day to know that this is an attack on Palestine and the anti-war movement.”

“Where next after Your Party failure?” (March 12) examines the collapse of the left electoral project, while “Don’t use Cyprus as a launch pad for your war” (March 11) interviews a Cypriot activist opposing British military bases used in the Iran campaign. The SWP’s orientation remains characteristically focused on street mobilization and broad-front activism. The Cyprus interview correctly identifies the material infrastructure of imperialism but limits the solution to protest pressure rather than working-class political organization capable of challenging the state.

The SWP’s overall orientation for the period is dominated by building for the March 28 anti-far-right demonstration, channeling political energy into a broad alliance with trade union bureaucracies (TUC, Unite, UNISON) rather than building independent working-class organization. The “Your Party” post-mortem — examining why the left electoral project collapsed — is notable for what it omits: any serious analysis of why programmatic adaptation to Labourism and trade unionism inevitably produces organizational disintegration.

Sources Socialist Worker Why you should oppose Labour’s ban on Al-Quds Day march · Socialist Worker Where next after Your Party failure? · Socialist Worker Don’t use Cyprus as a launch pad for your war

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s US section (socialistalternative.org) has produced limited output in recent days. Its most notable recent piece, “Could the US invade Greenland?” (January 14), frames Trump’s Greenland threats within the context of “mass movements against corrupt and authoritarian regimes” in 2025, arguing that “in the struggle against imperialism and capitalism, the seeds of a socialist world are sprouting.” While the rhetoric invokes anti-imperialism, Socialist Alternative’s practical political orientation remains tied to building broad protest movements and, in the US, to the legacy of its work within and around the Democratic Party orbit through figures like Kshama Sawant.

The UK section (socialistalternative.info) is focused on building for the March 28 Together Alliance demonstration against the far right in London, calling for “a socialist Your Party bloc against the right.” The call for trade union organizations — TUC, Unite, UNISON — to endorse the march reflects the ISA tendency’s characteristic orientation toward existing labor bureaucracies rather than building rank-and-file organizations independent of them. The framing of the demonstration as an opportunity for “the working class to fight back against anti-migrant and racist propaganda” is correct in its diagnosis but limited in its political prescription: street marches organized through union bureaucracies do not constitute a strategy for socialist revolution.

Sources Socialist Alternative Could the US invade Greenland? · Socialist Alternative (UK) 28 March: For a socialist Your Party bloc against the right!

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The International Marxist Tendency’s rebranded outlet (now at communist.red following the 2024 name change to “Revolutionary Communist Party”) is promoting “A Communist History of the British General Strike” as a new Wellred Books publication. While the historical subject is significant — the 1926 General Strike remains a critical episode in the betrayal of the British working class by the trade union and Labour bureaucracy — the IMT’s treatment of such history typically serves to validate its perspective of “entryism” into mass reformist organizations, a strategy directly opposed to the Trotskyist program of building independent revolutionary parties.

The IMT’s relative silence on the Iran war in its English-language press, at a moment when the US is conducting the largest military operation since the Iraq invasion, is itself politically significant. The tendency’s orientation toward building organizational infrastructure (book launches, recruitment drives, the RCP rebrand) while a major imperialist war is underway reflects its longstanding pattern of organizational fetishism disconnected from the central political tasks facing the working class.

Sources The Communist (RCP) A Communist History of the British General Strike — new book out now
