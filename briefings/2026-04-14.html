1. US Navy blockade of Iran takes effect as UK leads 40+ nation counter-coalition

The US Navy blockade of all Iranian ports took effect at 10:00 AM EDT Monday, marking a dramatic escalation of the 45-day-old US-Israeli war against Iran. The blockade force includes the USS Abraham Lincoln carrier strike group, 11 destroyers, and the USS Tripoli amphibious ready group. Two oil tankers — the Rich Starry and Ostria — turned away from the Strait of Hormuz within 20 minutes of implementation. No deaths at sea were reported in initial operations.

Trump posted on Truth Social that any Iranian ship approaching the blockade “will be immediately ELIMINATED,” while also claiming Monday that Tehran had called and “wants to work a deal.” The blockade followed the collapse of weekend peace talks in Islamabad, where 21 hours of negotiations ended without agreement.

In a historic break with Washington, UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer explicitly stated Britain “will not take part” in Trump’s blockade. A senior NATO military official confirmed to CBS News that the UK is leading planning for a coalition of more than 40 nations “to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and protect freedom of navigation” — the sharpest transatlantic divide on military action since the 2003 Iraq War. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz offered support only contingent on ceasefire conditions, not blanket backing for the blockade.

Key facts: - Iran’s IRGC warned it would introduce “new forms of warfare” that opponents have limited ability to counter; thousands rallied in Tehran against the blockade - Oil prices surged above $100/bbl on the blockade news (see World Economy section for full market data) - Pakistan’s PM reported ceasefire violations “at few places” across the conflict zone - India, which depends heavily on Gulf oil, has not publicly aligned with either the US blockade or the counter-coalition - UN Secretary-General Guterres declared: “To the United States and Israel, it is high time to stop the war that is inflicting immense human suffering and already triggering devastating economic consequences”

2. Peter Magyar defeats Viktor Orbán in Hungarian landslide

Peter Magyar’s centre-right Tisza party won a crushing victory in Hungary’s parliamentary election, taking 53.6% of the vote and 138 of 199 seats — ending 16 years of Orbán’s Fidesz rule. Fidesz took 37.8% and 55 seats. Turnout exceeded 77%, a record for post-Communist Hungary.

Tisza’s supermajority enables constitutional amendments, reversing 16 years of Fidesz constitutional rewrites. The result unblocks a €90 billion EU loan to Ukraine that Orbán had vetoed and removes Putin’s primary ally inside the EU and MAGA’s key European partner.

6. At least 30 killed in stampede at Haiti’s Citadelle Laferrière

A stampede at the Citadelle Laferrière — an early-19th-century UNESCO World Heritage fortress in northern Haiti — killed at least 30 people and injured dozens. The event, branded “Citadel Vibe 3.0,” was heavily promoted on social media and drew a crowd that far exceeded the fortress’s capacity.

Witnesses described mass panic along narrow, steep mountain paths; multiple people were crushed or fell. Haiti’s Culture Minister Emmanuel Menard confirmed the death toll; rescue teams searched for additional missing persons. CARICOM expressed condolences. The disaster occurred against the backdrop of ongoing gang violence and the near-total collapse of state capacity across much of Haiti, which has left the country without functioning emergency services, safety regulation, or public infrastructure.

7. Peru presidential election in chaos as ballots fail to reach 63,000 voters

Peru’s April 12 general election — the country’s ninth presidential vote in less than a decade — was extended by a day after logistical failures left approximately 63,000 voters without ballots, ballot boxes, or voting materials. The election features the largest field of presidential candidates in Peruvian history, reflecting deep political fragmentation.

With 62% of ballots counted, right-wing candidate Keiko Fujimori led with 16.88%; far-right candidate Rafael López Aliaga was second at 13.88%. A June 7 runoff is virtually certain as no candidate is expected to clear the 50% threshold. The absence of any credible left-wing candidate — after the impeachment and imprisonment of Pedro Castillo — is itself a significant political fact. As the WSWS noted, the far right’s dominance reflects “the depth of social despair and the absence of any independent political alternative for the working class.”

Key facts: - Approximately 211 polling tables failed to open, disenfranchising 63,000 voters - Nine out of ten Peruvians disapprove of Congress - Final results remained unknown as of Monday night; the chaos drew criticism of Peru’s electoral authority (ONPE)

Russia’s Supreme Court declares Memorial an “extremist” organization

Russia’s Supreme Court ruled on April 9 that International Memorial — one of Russia’s oldest and most respected human rights organizations, winner of the 2022 Nobel Peace Prize — constitutes an “extremist” group. The designation results in the shutdown of Memorial’s database documenting over three million victims of Stalin’s Great Terror, including shooting lists signed personally by Stalin and other Politburo members.

The Justice Ministry filed the lawsuit on March 27. The case concluded in a single closed-door hearing, the case file was classified “top secret,” and Memorial’s attorneys were not allowed to participate. The designation entails a ban on all Memorial activities under threat of lengthy prison sentences; individuals suspected of involvement can be placed on Russia’s “list of extremists” with their bank accounts frozen.

Key facts: - Memorial was previously ordered liquidated in 2021 but continued operations; the “extremist” designation is a qualitative escalation carrying criminal penalties - Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and NBC News all reported on the ruling - Memorial denounced the verdict as “unlawful and unfounded” and pledged to continue its work

2,200 Nova Scotia long-term care workers strike

More than 2,200 long-term care workers at 22 facilities across Nova Scotia walked off the job on Monday after being without a contract since October 2023. The striking CUPE members — licensed practical nurses, physiotherapists, continuing-care assistants, and housekeeping staff — earn under $19/hour, compared to workers in comparable roles in Prince Edward Island who earn over $10/hour more.

The Nova Scotia government offered a four-year deal with raises of 12–24%, retroactive pay to 2023, and a 70% increase in shift and weekend premiums, but the union rejected it as inadequate. CUPE represents approximately 5,000 long-term care workers provincewide and expects additional facilities to join the strike in coming days.

LAUSD strike deadline arrives as SEIU Local 99 holds out

With the April 14 strike deadline arriving, Los Angeles Unified School District reached tentative contract agreements with the teachers’ union (UTLA) and school administrators’ union (AALA) on Sunday — but the largest and lowest-paid bargaining unit, SEIU Local 99, remains without a deal. The 30,000+ SEIU workers — custodians, cafeteria workers, bus drivers, special education assistants, and early care workers — earn an average of approximately $35,000/year, a poverty wage in Los Angeles.

If the strike proceeds, it would close all LAUSD schools to roughly 400,000 students.

California Supreme Court halts Riverside County ballot seizure

The California Supreme Court on April 8–9 halted Riverside County Sheriff Chad Bianco’s investigation into a November 2025 Proposition 50 redistricting vote, ordering the preservation of all seized materials. Approximately 650,000 ballots had been seized from polling locations and election offices.

The warrant applications relied on claims from the “Riverside Election Integrity Team,” characterised by elections experts as “the political equivalent of flat earthers.” The group alleged 45,000 missing ballots; the actual discrepancy was 0.016% — approximately 100 votes, typical for an election of that size. Attorney General Rob Bonta has until April 15 to refile his petition; Bianco has 30 days to respond.

US measles cases hit 1,714 across 33 states

As of April 9, the US has recorded 1,714 confirmed measles cases spanning 33 states, with 94% outbreak-associated. South Carolina (667 cases) and Utah (408) are the hardest-hit states. No deaths have been reported in 2026 after three in 2025, but 2026 is on track to be one of the two worst years for measles in the US since 2000.

The surge is driven by declining vaccination rates and the systematic dismantling of public health infrastructure. As vaccine policy expert Dorit Reiss detailed in a WSWS interview, RFK Jr. fired all 17 sitting members of the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) in June 2025 and replaced them with ideological loyalists, reducing recommended childhood vaccines from 17 to 11 disease categories. A completed CDC vaccine effectiveness report that cleared internal scientific review was blocked from publication by political leadership. A federal judge has placed a temporary hold on the restructured ACIP.

Key facts: - 2,255 measles cases in 2025 (highest since 1992); 28,783 pertussis cases with 13 deaths - An estimated 81,000–175,000 excess COVID deaths attributed to the policy environment - Reiss warned that the loss of “enormous numbers of personnel” destroys institutional memory that cannot be quickly restored

COVID-19 BA.3.2 “Cicada” variant spreads to 33 countries

The heavily mutated BA.3.2 variant — carrying 70+ spike mutations compared to the original Wuhan strain — has spread to at least 33 countries and 31 US states as of April 13. Children are roughly five times more likely to be infected by this variant than by currently co-circulating strains. Overall US COVID hospitalizations remain very low at approximately 1,900 per week, but BA.3.2’s immune-evasion profile and disproportionate impact on children warrant close surveillance.

Trump budget proposes elimination of NEA and NEH

The Trump administration’s proposed budget for fiscal year 2027, released April 3, would close the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the Institute of Museum and Library Services entirely. This represents the most aggressive federal assault on arts and cultural funding in American history, coming amid the administration’s simultaneous $1.5 trillion defense budget (a 44% increase) and $73 billion in cuts to education, health, housing, and domestic programmes.

The attack is already being implemented in practice. Portland’s Oregon Contemporary had its $30,000 NEA grant for the 2026 Artists’ Biennial cancelled after the agency learned the curator and most artists are members of marginalized communities. The gallery’s director stated the NEA denied the already-approved grant based solely on the names and biographies of the artists. An emergency local fund drive allowed the exhibition to open April 3 with works intentionally including perspectives suppressed for centuries, including those of Indigenous and Black artists.

A federal court ruled in April that the Trump Administration must withdraw its attempt to dismantle the Institute of Museum and Library Services, providing temporary reprieve for libraries and museums nationwide. But the broader campaign to defund cultural institutions represents a conscious effort to eliminate spaces for critical artistic expression — particularly work that engages with war, social inequality, and historical memory.

PEN America Literary Awards honour works engaging with Gaza, Indigenous futures, and migration

PEN America’s 62nd Annual Literary Awards ceremony on March 31 conferred nearly $350,000 in prizes, with several awards going to works that directly engage with the most urgent political questions of the moment. Peter Beinart won the PEN/John Kenneth Galbraith Award for Nonfiction for Being Jewish After the Destruction of Gaza: A Reckoning — a significant recognition given the political pressures on institutions to avoid the subject. Mandan/Hidatsa/Arikara/Lakota artist Cannupa Hanska Luger won the PEN/Jean Stein Book Award ($75,000) for SURVIVA: A Future Ancestral Field Guide, a work of Indigenous futurism. Haitian-American novelist Edwidge Danticat received the PEN/Nabokov Award for Achievement in International Literature, and Jamaica Kincaid won the essay prize for Putting Myself Together: Writing 1974–.

The awards arrive amid PEN America’s ongoing documentation of the book ban crisis: nearly 23,000 books have been removed from US public school classrooms and libraries since 2021, disproportionately targeting books about race and racism, titles by or about people of colour, and books featuring LGBTQ+ characters. The scale of censorship represents a systematic assault on intellectual freedom at precisely the moment when critical engagement with war, imperialism, and social inequality is most urgently needed.

WSWS coverage — last 24 hours

Perspective published: “The Iran war, oil and the crisis of American imperialism” by Gabriel Black — read in full on wsws.org

Black’s Perspective advances three arguments not fully captured in bourgeois press coverage. First, the scale of physical supply disruption: the net shortfall in global oil markets is approximately 13 million barrels per day — 13% of the world’s energy supply. IEA chief Fatih Birol told Le Figaro this crisis is “more serious than the ones in 1973, 1979 and 2022 together.” Physical “dated” Brent crude hit $141/bbl on April 2, with a $50 gap between spot and futures prices that signals panic, not optimism. For LNG, the situation is worse: Qatar’s Ras Laffan complex — the world’s largest LNG export facility — suffered missile strikes destroying two LNG trains, cutting 17% of export capacity with a $25 billion repair bill and up to five years’ recovery time. There are no strategic LNG reserves and no alternative pipeline routes.

Second, Black dismantles White House economic adviser Kevin Hassett’s claim that “energy prices are going down,” explaining that a declining futures curve (backwardation) under present conditions reflects panic buying of physical supply. The crunch will hit Asia hardest — destination of 85% of Gulf fossil fuels — and be most devastating in poorer importing nations (Cambodia, the Philippines, Thailand), threatening food insecurity for hundreds of millions.

Third, Black documents the bipartisan character of the war: the New York Times editorial board urged Trump to “involve Congress and seek help from America’s allies” — which Black interprets as a call for “bipartisan collaboration in the prosecution of the next stage of the war.” He quotes Trump at an April 1 Easter luncheon: “It is not possible for us to take care of daycare, Medicaid, Medicare — all these individual things. We have to take care of one thing: military protection.” The article closes with WSWS chairman David North’s response: “It is not enough to be appalled. Horror, left to itself, exhausts itself in impotent frustration… What is required is the development of a mass working class socialist movement.”

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The Iranian working class under 45 days of bombardment and naval blockade

Coverage of the Iran war has focused almost entirely on military assets, diplomatic manoeuvres, and oil markets. The material conditions of Iranian workers, civilians, and the poor — subject to ongoing bombardment, a naval blockade cutting off imports, and economic collapse — have been almost entirely absent from Western and WSWS reporting alike. Iran’s oil export revenue of approximately $45 billion/year (13% of GDP) is now being strangled. The impact on wages, food prices, medicine, and public services — and the political response of the Iranian working class to both the US assault and their own government — demands direct attention from a socialist publication.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war: What is happening on day 34 of US-Israel attacks?

2. Global energy price shock and its class impact — who pays for $100+ oil?

While oil prices and GDP projections dominate financial media, the distributional impact — on working-class energy bills, transport costs, food prices (from supply chain disruption), and public budgets in the Global South — has received virtually no coverage. The IMF’s own April 2026 WEO warns that “smaller, less diversified economies” face “revenue losses and fiscal strain” with “limited capacity to absorb higher costs.” The WSWS should lead on the class dimensions of the energy shock: who benefits and who pays.

Recommended reading UNCTAD Global Trade Update April 2026

3. Haiti’s Citadelle stampede as a product of state collapse

The deaths of at least 30 Haitians at the Citadelle Laferrière are being framed as a crowd-control failure. The deeper story is Haiti’s decade of deliberate state dismantlement — by the Haitian oligarchy, successive US-backed governments, and international “reconstruction” operations — that has left the country without functioning emergency services, safety regulation, or public infrastructure. The WSWS has covered Haiti’s political crisis extensively; this disaster is a direct product of those conditions.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera At least 30 dead in stampede at Haiti’s historic Citadelle Laferrière

4. Nova Scotia long-term care workers’ strike and the crisis of elder care

The strike of 2,200 long-term care workers — earning under $19/hour, without a contract for over two years, with potential expansion to 5,000 workers — exposes the crisis of elder care across Canada and internationally. The low wages and understaffing that drove the strike are inseparable from the for-profit model of long-term care that produced catastrophic death tolls during COVID-19. This connects directly to the WSWS’s pandemic coverage and its analysis of the commodification of health care.

Recommended reading Global News 2,200 long-term care workers go on strike in Nova Scotia

5. ProPublica strike: first US newsroom walkout over AI protections

Approximately 150 ProPublica journalists staged a 24-hour strike on April 9 — the first major US newsroom walkout driven by demands for AI protections. With 92% strike authorization, workers demanded restrictions on AI replacing jobs, standard seniority protections in layoffs, and wage increases. The strike raises fundamental questions about the ownership and control of AI technology and the defence of journalism as a profession — questions the WSWS is uniquely positioned to address from a socialist perspective.

Recommended reading Nieman Lab ProPublica journalists walk off the job in first U.S. newsroom strike over AI

6. The Trump administration’s assault on arts funding and cultural censorship

The proposed elimination of the NEA, NEH, and IMLS — alongside the cancellation of grants based on artists’ racial and ethnic identities and the acceleration of book bans to 23,000+ — constitutes the most aggressive federal attack on arts and culture in American history. This is happening simultaneously with a 44% increase in the defense budget. The WSWS’s arts criticism tradition provides the framework to analyse this as a deliberate effort to eliminate critical cultural production during wartime.

7. Peru’s election chaos and the bankruptcy of the political establishment

Peru is electing its ninth president in less than a decade, with the largest field of candidates in history and 63,000 people disenfranchised by logistical collapse. The leading candidates are Keiko Fujimori (whose father is a convicted authoritarian) and Rafael López Aliaga (far right). The absence of a credible left — after the impeachment and imprisonment of Pedro Castillo — is itself the central story, warranting deeper WSWS treatment than the initial dispatch provided.
