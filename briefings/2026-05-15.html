1. Russia launches record 1,600-weapon barrage on Kyiv, 24 dead

Russia launched 1,567 attack drones and 56 ballistic and aeroballistic missiles in a continuous 30-hour barrage against Ukraine on May 13–14, smashing the previous single-attack record of roughly 1,000 weapons. At least 24 people were killed in Kyiv, including three children; more than 48 were wounded and over 10 remained missing as of Thursday evening. A nine-story residential apartment building in the capital was partially destroyed, with a 12-year-old girl among the dead.

President Volodymyr Zelensky said he had “instructed the Defense Forces of Ukraine and the special services to propose possible formats for our response” and demanded that “sanctions must be more painful for Russia.” Ukrainian air defenses shot down 652 of 675 drones — a 94 percent interception rate — but stopped only 41 of 56 ballistic and aeroballistic missiles, a 73 percent rate that drops below 10 percent for the most sophisticated Iskander-M and Kinzhal weapons. Russia’s drone force has expanded from 86,000 personnel in January to 114,000 by May 1, reflecting the conversion of large parts of Russian industry to drone production.

Key facts: - 1,567 drones + 56 missiles over 30 hours — largest single attack of the war - 24 confirmed dead in Kyiv; nine-story apartment block partially destroyed - 94% drone interception rate vs. 7% on ballistic missiles - Russia drone personnel up 33% in four months (86,000 → 114,000)

2. Trump–Xi Beijing summit ends without breakthrough

Donald Trump and Xi Jinping concluded a two-day summit at Zhongnanhai in Beijing on May 15 with no formal agreements but a stage-managed list of commercial transactions: China pledged to buy US oil and 200 Boeing aircraft, plus increased agricultural imports. Trump, accompanied by Elon Musk, Tim Cook, and Jensen Huang, declared “fantastic trade deals.” Fortune summarized Wall Street’s response in a single headline: “Nothing of real substance.” Global equity markets sold off on the outcome.

Xi explicitly invoked the “Thucydides Trap,” warning the relationship was headed to “an extremely dangerous place” if Washington impeded China’s rise — above all over Taiwan, which Xi called the “most important” issue between the two countries. Secretary of State Marco Rubio reaffirmed afterward that the US position on Taiwan is “unchanged.” On Iran, Trump claimed Xi “assured him that China is not going to give military equipment to Iran,” a key US demand since China remains Iran’s largest oil purchaser. Neither side announced movement on the Strait of Hormuz blockade or the underlying trade architecture; analysts described the meeting as a floor, not a ceiling.

Key facts: - China to purchase 200 Boeing aircraft and additional US oil and agricultural goods - Xi invoked “Thucydides Trap” and called Taiwan the “most important” issue - Musk, Cook, Huang accompanied Trump; Hang Seng closed flat, Shanghai tech off 1.5% - No formal agreements; Fortune — “Nothing of real substance”

3. Iran war on “massive life support” entering day 76

The US-Israel war on Iran, launched February 28 with airstrikes killing senior Iranian officials, is now in its 76th day. Trump declared this week that the ceasefire framework is on “massive life support” after rejecting Tehran’s latest counter-proposal as “totally unacceptable.” Aides say the president is more seriously contemplating resumed combat operations than at any point since talks began.

Envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner are negotiating a 14-point memorandum of understanding that would declare an end to hostilities and open a 30-day formal negotiation period. The core sticking point is Iran’s nuclear program: the US-Israeli demand is that Iran halt uranium enrichment for 12 years and surrender approximately 440 kilograms of 60-percent-enriched uranium. Iran has not agreed. The US military has, under its Strait of Hormuz blockade, redirected 67 commercial ships and disabled four others. Hapag-Lloyd reported a €219 million ($256 million) Q1 loss directly tied to the blockade. UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned this week that the conflict has gone “out of control.”

Key facts: - War in 76th day; 14-point MOU stalled on uranium enrichment demand - US blockade has redirected 67 ships and disabled 4 others - Hapag-Lloyd posts €219M Q1 loss attributed to Hormuz disruption - Strategic Petroleum Reserve drawn down 8.6 million barrels in one week — largest since early 1980s

4. Gaza: 77 percent of population in acute food insecurity

Approximately 1.6 million people in Gaza — 77 percent of the population — face high levels of acute food insecurity, according to WFP and IPC data summarized in a UN inter-agency briefing released this week. Aid deliveries fell 37 percent between the first three months after the October 2025 ceasefire (167,600 metric tonnes) and April 2026 (under 105,000 metric tonnes). Only 10 percent of the required humanitarian funding for 2026 has been secured.

The crisis is sharpest among children. More than 100,000 children under five are projected to face acute malnutrition through April–May. Zero children aged 6–23 months are receiving the minimum required dietary diversity, according to FAO, UNICEF, WFP, and WHO. Bread prices in Gaza have tripled; community kitchens now operate only once or twice per week in many districts. The UN agencies issued a joint warning that “humanitarian gains achieved after the ceasefire remain fragile” and that funding shortfalls could push the strip back to famine. The collapse coincides with massive Western military expenditure on Israel and Ukraine, exposing what the WSWS has consistently identified as the deliberate political character of the starvation.

5. Kevin Warsh confirmed Fed chair in closest vote of modern era

The Senate confirmed Kevin Warsh as the 11th Federal Reserve chair on May 13 by a 54–45 vote — the narrowest margin for any Fed chair confirmation in the modern era. Only one Democrat, Pennsylvania Sen. John Fetterman, crossed party lines. Warsh, a former Morgan Stanley banker and Bush-era Fed governor, formally takes over from Jerome Powell on May 15. His first FOMC meeting as chair is scheduled for June 16–17.

Warsh inherits an inflation crisis driven by the Iran war and the Strait of Hormuz closure. April CPI hit 3.8 percent year-on-year — the highest annual reading since May 2023 — with energy prices up 17.9 percent. As Nick Beams noted on the WSWS, US 30-year Treasury yields have crossed 5 percent for the first time since 2007, signalling that bond markets are pricing in sustained inflation and fiscal strain. Warsh has publicly argued that there is room to lower rates, but pledged to “use his own judgment” — a direct response to Trump’s repeated attacks on Fed independence. Trump allies in Congress have already warned against cutting too quickly, and rate-cut probabilities for 2026 have collapsed to zero in futures markets. The FOMC is, paradoxically, drifting toward hikes rather than the cuts Trump demands.

Key facts: - 54–45 confirmation (closest in modern era); only Sen. Fetterman crossed - Warsh takes over with CPI at 3.8%, 30-year yields above 5% - First FOMC meeting June 16–17 - Rate-cut odds for 2026 fully priced out

6. UC strike by 42,000 workers cancelled overnight by AFSCME bureaucracy

AFSCME Local 3299 abruptly cancelled a planned strike by 42,000 University of California healthcare and service workers at 1:26 a.m. on May 14, announcing a tentative agreement reached in closed-door negotiations. The deal — 5 percent retroactive wage increases and a $1,500 lump-sum payment, with tiered increases of up to 4 percent annually through 2029 — abandons the core demand for $25,000 housing subsidies per worker. Members reacted with widespread fury.

The cancellation follows a pattern of union bureaucracies suppressing rank-and-file actions, including recent SEIU strike cancellations affecting tens of thousands of workers. The WSWS coverage by Max Jones, Kimie Saito, and Joshua Rodriguez calls for rank-and-file organizing to reject ratification and connects the cancellation to the broader assault on living standards documented in Australia’s $36 billion NDIS cuts, the New School layoffs, and the SAG-AFTRA tentative agreement — all unfolding in the same week.

7. House Democrats force vote on $9.3 billion Ukraine package

All 215 House Democrats, plus two Republicans and one independent, signed a discharge petition forcing a floor vote on the “Ukraine Support Act” — bypassing Speaker Mike Johnson, who had blocked the bill for nearly a year. The legislation authorizes $1.3 billion in direct military aid and up to $8 billion in loans, expands sanctions on Russia, and reaffirms US commitment to NATO.

The signatories include all three DSA members of Congress — Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, Rashida Tlaib, and Greg Casar — alongside the Democratic establishment. As Patrick Martin noted on the WSWS, this demonstrates that the nominal “socialist” left is fully integrated into the war coalition. The contrast with congressional resistance to the Iran war is stark: War Powers Act resolutions to constrain Trump’s Iran operations have been defeated seven consecutive times, including votes of 219–212 and 214–213 in the past month. Democrats reserve their procedural muscle for arming Ukraine — not for restraining a war that has already killed thousands and shoved global energy markets into crisis.

UK: Starmer government on the brink as Labour faces leadership challenge

Keir Starmer’s premiership is on the verge of collapse, with ministerial resignations, no-confidence letters from within the Parliamentary Labour Party (including arch-Blairite Health Secretary Wes Streeting), and a formal leadership challenge anticipated. The crisis is being driven less by domestic missteps than by external economic and military shocks — most directly, the US-Israel war on Iran and Trump’s evisceration of the UK-US “special relationship.”

Food costs are projected to end 2026 some 50–64 percent higher than in mid-2021. UK government borrowing costs are the highest of any G7 member; 30-year gilt yields are at their highest since 1998. Kathleen Brooks of XTB stated bluntly to the Financial Times: “Until a challenge from the left of the Labour Party is eradicated, or the government embarks on growth-positive economic policy, we do not see UK bond yields substantially falling from here.” That is, the bond market is dictating the terms of any leadership succession. As Thomas Scripps argues in today’s WSWS Perspective, the Labour “soft left” hopefuls Andy Burnham and Angela Rayner are already making pilgrimages to corporate headquarters, while John McDonnell calls for an “orderly transition” specifically to pre-empt independent working-class action.

Hungary: Magyar sworn in, ending Orbán’s 16-year rule

Péter Magyar of the center-right Tisza party was sworn in as Hungary’s prime minister on May 9, ending Viktor Orbán’s 16-year rule — the longest of any EU member state since the collapse of Stalinism. Tisza won 141 of 199 parliamentary seats (53.6 percent) versus Fidesz’s 55 seats (37.8 percent) in the April 12 election. Turnout reached 77 percent — a post-communist record. Magyar called the margin “visible from the moon and every window in Hungary.”

The new government has pledged to restore judicial independence, align Hungary with EU democratic norms, and shift away from Orbán’s pro-Kremlin orientation. The shift directly affects EU unanimity rules on Russia sanctions and Ukraine aid, where Budapest had been the most persistent blocker. Chatham House analysts warn that “Orbánism” may survive the man himself: Fidesz still commands roughly 38 percent of the electorate and retains deep institutional roots in the state apparatus, courts, and media.

Senate again rejects War Powers Resolution on Iran — 7th defeat this year

The US Senate this week voted down — for the seventh time in 2026 — a War Powers Resolution measure that would have required congressional authorization for further military action against Iran. The bipartisan measure cleared a simple majority but fell short of the 60-vote threshold to overcome procedural hurdles. The conflict is now in its 76th day with no congressional check.

The Wikipedia 2026 Iran war page, citing Congressional Research Service materials, characterizes the pattern as “the effective consolidation of war-making authority in the executive branch.” The same week the Senate rejected the Iran measure, all 215 House Democrats forced a vote on Ukraine aid — illustrating the bipartisan willingness to extend war powers when politically convenient and the bipartisan refusal to constrain them. UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned this week that the Iran war has gone “out of control” with regional escalation risks rising. Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu reportedly made a covert visit to the UAE during the conflict, though Abu Dhabi denied the visit — a signal of internal friction within the US-led coalition.

Germany: DGB congress backs Merz austerity

The four-day DGB (German Trade Union Confederation) congress in Berlin closed this week with chairwoman Yasmin Fahimi reelected with over 96 percent support. The result was secured despite a 250,000-member drop in DGB affiliates, real wages still below their 2019 level, and the loss of more than 10,000 industrial jobs per month over the past two years. As Peter Schwarz writes on the WSWS, the vote echoes Stalinist party elections of the former GDR.

Chancellor Friedrich Merz addressed the congress to secure DGB endorsement for sweeping social cuts: abolition of the Bürgergeld unemployment benefit, €16 billion in health insurance cuts, and pension restructuring to “basic security” levels. Merz pointedly avoided mentioning the government’s massive rearmament drive — sparing the bureaucracy the embarrassment of openly endorsing record war spending. The DGB endorsement is now being cited by Frankfurter Allgemeine and Welt as proof of “social peace” for the Merz coalition’s austerity agenda.

Australian Opposition Liberal leader Angus Taylor used his budget reply speech to call for the immediate deportation of 75,000 visa overstayers, exclusion of non-citizens from government benefits, and a more-than-40-percent reduction in immigration. US equities at record highs as CPI hits 3.8%

US equities closed at all-time highs on May 14 before pulling back into the close on May 15 ahead of the Trump–Xi outcome. The Dow Jones recaptured 50,000 for the first time since the Iran war began, closing at roughly 50,103 (+0.69%). The S&P 500 closed above 7,500 (+0.6%) and the Nasdaq Composite at 26,635 (+0.9%) — both record closes. May 15 morning futures opened down sharply (Nasdaq-100 -1.3%, S&P -0.9%) before recovering on Cisco earnings strength.

European indices closed higher: FTSE 100 at 10,372.93 (+0.46%) and DAX at 24,456.26 (+1.32%). Asian markets diverged on the Trump–Xi outcome: the Nikkei 225 fell 1.61% to 61,644.25, Shanghai Composite mixed near 4,183 with mainland tech off 1.5%, and the Hang Seng closed flat.

China new loans go negative for first time in years

China’s new bank loans fell to negative 10 billion yuan in April — the first negative reading in years and a dramatic miss against the 320 billion yuan consensus. Total social financing came in at 620 billion yuan against a 1,500 billion expectation, less than half what markets anticipated. The data signal severe domestic deflationary pressure even as the rest of the world faces producer-price surges from the Iran war. The Hormuz closure has cut off a major portion of China’s oil imports from Iran, adding supply-side pressure. The IMF’s April World Economic Outlook warned the Iran war “darkens the global economic outlook.”

Gold pulls back as India doubles import tariffs to 15%

Gold (spot) traded in a wide ~$4,564–$4,651 range, closing around $4,617/oz, weighed down by a stronger US dollar and by India doubling import tariffs on gold and silver to 15 percent from 6 percent (with a 100kg/license import cap). US measles cases hit 1,842 across 37 states

The American Academy of Pediatrics, summarizing CDC data through May 7, reports 1,842 confirmed measles cases in 37 states in 2026, with 93 percent outbreak-associated. South Carolina leads with 669 cases, followed by Utah (439), Texas (182), and Florida (135). About 72 percent of cases are in children and teens; 92 percent were unvaccinated or of unknown vaccination status. 2026 is now one of the two worst measles years since 2000. The CDC has warned that summer travel will drive further spread and that the US is at serious risk of losing its measles-elimination designation — held since 2000.

First documented human H5N5 avian flu infection published in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine published the first documented human case of highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). A 75-year-old immunocompromised woman with direct bird exposure developed fever and diarrhea; the virus carried a transient, low-frequency mammalian-adaptive mutation. Two findings are particularly significant: H5 strains beyond H5N1 are now demonstrated to be capable of zoonotic spillover into humans, and upper-respiratory testing alone missed the infection, raising serious questions about the adequacy of current surveillance.

WHO reports child H5N1 fatality in Bangladesh

The WHO, summarized by CIDRAP, reported 13 avian and swine influenza cases in humans in Q1 2026 — including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 infection in a child from Bangladesh, the first H5N1 fatality in South Asia in 2026. Three additional H5N1 cases were reported in Cambodia. US dairy-worker monitoring shows no unusual human influenza indicators as of May 1.

White House pivots on AI: pre-deployment testing for Google, Microsoft, xAI

The Commerce Department’s Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) announced pre-deployment testing agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI on May 5, expanding on earlier MOUs with OpenAI and Anthropic. Models will be evaluated in classified environments for capability and security risks before public release. The pivot reportedly was accelerated by internal concerns around Anthropic’s not-yet-public “Mythos” model, which demonstrated the ability to rapidly identify and exploit software security vulnerabilities. This is the first concrete model capability publicly cited as driving a US regulatory shift, setting precedent for the dual-use governance of frontier AI.

Arts and culture

The 79th Cannes Film Festival opened May 12 with a 21-film competition slate notable both for the geographic breadth of its auteurs and for the political weight of its subject matter. Among the most anticipated premieres are Hirokazu Kore-eda’s Sheep in the Box, Cristian Mungiu’s Fjord, and Ryusuke Hamaguchi’s All of a Sudden. Five women directors are in competition — fewer than the festival’s own publicists wish to acknowledge, but a marginal improvement on prior years. A preopening screening of Guillermo del Toro’s Pan’s Labyrinth marked the film’s 20th anniversary; the choice is itself a statement, given the film’s confrontation with fascism, child violence, and the moral inheritance of the Spanish Civil War — themes whose contemporary relevance the festival programmers cannot have missed.

The Metropolitan Opera staged the world premiere of Gabriela Lena Frank’s El Último Sueño de Frida y Diego on May 14, imagining a posthumous reunion of Frida Kahlo and Diego Rivera. The piece arrives in a US cultural moment marked by hostility to historical works engaging with revolutionary politics — Rivera’s communism, Kahlo’s Trotskyism, the broader Mexican modernist tradition — and the company’s commissioning of a Latina composer for its main stage represents at minimum a refusal of the broader retrenchment now underway. Whether the libretto and staging engage seriously with their subjects’ politics, or merely use them as aesthetic backdrop, will be the test.

The 2026 British Book Awards, the “Nibbies,” were held May 11 in London and were described in trade press as marked by a “spirit of defiance,” with top honors going to works examining the corruption of wealth and political power. The tilt confirms a broader literary moment — the bestseller lists of the last 18 months have been disproportionately weighted toward fiction and nonfiction engaging the questions of inequality, oligarchy, and political crisis that have been driven into public consciousness by the financial crisis, the pandemic, and the wars on Ukraine, Gaza, and Iran.

Two significant deaths. Clarence Carter, the blind soul and R&B singer best known for the 1970 hit “Patches,” died at 90 — one of the last surviving figures of the classic Southern soul era centered on Fame Studios in Muscle Shoals, where Black and white musicians produced an integrated body of work that anticipated and outlasted the political integrations the South would only later concede. Rex Reed, the razor-tongued film critic who rose to prominence alongside Pauline Kael in the 1960s and remained a byline at the New York Observer for decades, died May 12 at his Manhattan home. Reed was one of the last links to the golden age of personality-driven American film criticism — a tradition now displaced by algorithmic aggregation and studio-controlled influencer content.

The state assault on the arts continues. The NEA’s Challenge America grant program has been cancelled for FY2026, and dozens of institutions have received termination notices citing a policy shift to fund only projects “aligned with presidential priorities.” Washington National Opera severed ties with the Kennedy Center in January; the US has withdrawn from ICCROM and other international cultural bodies. Advocates describe the cumulative effect on small and mid-size arts organizations — particularly those serving Black, Latino, indigenous, and rural communities — as the most severe federal contraction in the NEA’s history. The censorship is the explicit policy of the Trump administration and has provoked no countervailing mobilization from the Democratic establishment, which continues to fund the war machine that crowds out cultural spending.

Perspective published: The crisis of the Starmer government, the Labour Party and British capitalism — Thomas Scripps. The full text is on wsws.org. Key facts have been integrated into the “UK: Starmer government on the brink” item above.

Bond markets send out a warning — Nick Beams

Beams’s analysis adds three points beyond the daily market reporting. First, he traces the specific industrial mechanism of inflation: freight transportation costs rose 8.1 percent in April alone, feeding directly into every commodity priced on US shelves. Second, he reports that the International Energy Agency now records global oil stockpiles being drawn down at nearly 4 million barrels per day in April — meaning no near-term price relief is structurally possible regardless of Fed policy. Third, he draws the explicit class linkage between the Australian budget’s $35 billion NDIS cut and its simultaneous military spending hike — making clear that central-bank policy and fiscal austerity are coordinated mechanisms of wage suppression, not technical responses to inflation.

Australia: Carer Julienne Verhagen on the NDIS attack — Richard Phillips

The interview contains specific data not in mainstream NDIS coverage. Verhagen documents that medical supplies cost 30 to 40 percent more when purchased through NDIS-accredited suppliers than identical items purchased privately — direct evidence that the “unsustainability” rhetoric covers institutionalized price-gouging. She reports that administrative tribunals overturn NDIA decisions in 78 percent of cases, meaning the bureaucratic denial machinery is rejected by its own appeal mechanisms more often than not. Her estimate that 300,000 people will be pushed off the scheme by 2030 is a number the Labor government has not publicly confirmed.

Germany: Bosch works council irregularities — Dietmar Gaisenkersting

A German labor court is hearing challenges to the March 11 works council election at Robert Bosch GmbH’s Schwäbisch Gmünd facility. The “Team RB GmbH” slate won 13 of 23 seats with about 55 percent of votes. Challengers — including excluded “Freie Metaller” candidate Mustafa Simsek — allege the largest slate was improperly excluded, company-funded campaign materials were distributed three days before the vote, employee pressure was applied, and officially confirmed vote counts (1,911) differ significantly from those initially reported (1,400). The case is part of the wider IG Metall-controlled works council apparatus functioning as management’s enforcer for job cuts planned through 2030.

Canada: 35-year Coca-Cola worker terminated after injury — Steve Hill

Shawne Hopkins, 57, a 35-year employee of Coke Canada Bottling (owned by billionaires Larry Tanenbaum and the Bridgeman estate; ~$3.3 billion annual revenue), was struck by a 900 kg malfunctioning overhead door in January 2024. He suffered a torn shoulder joint, underwent multiple surgeries, and was terminated by phone in February 2026 with a severance offer of $2,511.20 conditioned on signing a non-disclosure agreement. The termination was framed under “frustration of employment” doctrine despite Alberta Human Rights Act protections against disability-based termination. Hopkins rejected the offer. The company unveiled a $75 million new Calgary warehouse days before firing him. Canada’s 89 billionaires grew their wealth 20 percent in 2025 alone while approximately 25 percent of Canadians face food insecurity.

Ford Rouge plant: build rank-and-file committees — Martaz Crutchfield

A May Day Online Rally speech by a fifth-generation Ford Rouge plant worker. Crutchfield catalogues a history of workplace deaths and injuries — including the killing of Ronald Adams Sr. at a Stellantis facility — as evidence of corporate contempt for workers’ lives. He reports that rank-and-file committee members at the Dearborn Truck Plant are building independent structures to “take control of safety and our conditions away from the companies and the union bureaucracy.” He endorses Will Lehman’s UAW presidential campaign and urges affiliation with the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees.

Vivek Chibber’s lead essay Socialism Has a Future. Central Planning Doesn’t. argues that Soviet central planning failed on calculation and incentive grounds while insisting that “a different sort of socialism can still flourish.” The argument is textbook Jacobin social-democratic revisionism: it distances the project from the October Revolution, liquidates the lessons of the USSR into a market-compatible “socialism,” and prepares the ground for a Scandinavian-model reformism as the horizon of socialist politics. The framing collapses the question of working-class state power into a debate about mixed economies.

The longer report Philly Wants to Send a Socialist to Congress profiles DSA-backed candidate Chris Rabb running for Congress as a Democrat. Despite the candidate’s rhetorical acknowledgment that “both parties” are complicit, the article uncritically promotes running inside the Democratic Party ballot line — textbook subordination of socialist politics to the bourgeois electoral apparatus. The piece appears the same week that all three DSA members of Congress signed the discharge petition forcing the Ukraine military aid vote. Flag: Democratic Party entryism integrated with imperialist war.

1. Strategic Petroleum Reserve drawdown subsidizes Iran war while gasoline prices crush workers

The US Strategic Petroleum Reserve fell by 8.6 million barrels last week — the largest single-week drawdown since the early 1980s — as the Iran war keeps oil prices elevated. Rather than reducing prices at the pump, the drawdown is being used to keep markets functioning during a war whose direct beneficiaries are defense contractors and regional geopolitical positioning. The class character of the SPR — built up by working-class tax revenues, drawn down for imperial purposes — deserves direct WSWS treatment.

India’s wholesale price index surged to 8.30 percent year-on-year in April (consensus 4.40 percent), with fuel prices up 24.71 percent — a direct consequence of the Iran war and the Strait of Hormuz closure. This is devastating for hundreds of millions of Indian workers and the poor, who spend a disproportionate share of income on food and fuel. Western coverage focuses on Wall Street’s record highs; the South Asian working class’s wartime impoverishment is invisible.

German shipping giant Hapag-Lloyd reported losing €219 million in Q1 2026, directly attributing the figure to the US-Israel blockade of Iranian waters and Strait of Hormuz disruption. 4. China’s credit collapse — deflationary crisis threatens hundreds of millions of workers

China’s new bank loans turned negative in April for the first time in years (-10 billion yuan vs. +320 billion expected), and total social financing came in at less than half consensus expectations. The data signal a severe contraction in domestic credit demand reflecting the property sector crisis, Iran-war-related trade disruptions, and weak consumer confidence. Chinese workers — already squeezed by structural unemployment and wage suppression — face the consequences of a deflation international media frames as a macroeconomic curiosity.

The first documented human case of H5N5 avian influenza, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, demonstrates that H5 zoonotic spillover extends beyond H5N1 — and that the patient’s upper-respiratory tests were negative, meaning the current US surveillance architecture would miss many infections. With the Trump administration having gutted CDC capacity and HHS public-health staff, the implications for pandemic preparedness are systemic. This deserves a science-policy article from the WSWS’s pandemic team.
