1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day eight: death toll surpasses 1,332

The US-Israeli bombing campaign against Iran entered its eighth day on Saturday with no sign of de-escalation. The Iranian Red Crescent documented at least 1,332 killed, over 6,500 wounded, and more than 3,600 civilian sites damaged in Iran since strikes began on February 28. An additional 217 have been killed in Lebanon and 11 in Israel, with 6 US service members killed in the conflict.

US CENTCOM reported it has struck over 3,000 targets across Iran and destroyed 43 Iranian warships since the launch of “Operation Epic Fury.” Israeli Army Chief Eyal Zamir claimed Israel has destroyed “80% of Iran’s air defenses” and “at least 60%” of Iran’s missile launch capability. Trump declared on social media: “There will be no deal with Iran except UNCONDITIONAL SURRENDER!” Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi rejected ceasefire talks outright and, when asked about the prospect of a US ground invasion, responded: “No, we are waiting for them.”

Key facts: - Iran is operating under near-total internet blackout (1% connectivity); the rial has collapsed to 1,660,000 per dollar - An Israeli strike on a girls’ school in Minab killed 165+ children - Israel has simultaneously launched a major Lebanon offensive, ordering evacuation of entire Beirut districts, while Gaza remains under a total aid blockade since March 1 - Hezbollah entered the conflict on March 2; Iran-backed militias attacked a hotel in Irbil, Iraq - UAE intercepted 109 drones and 9 ballistic missiles; Qatar intercepted 9 of 10 Iranian drones on Friday - Trump met privately with executives from seven major defense contractors — Lockheed Martin, Boeing, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, BAE Systems, L3Harris, and Honeywell — with Lockheed agreeing to quadruple munitions production

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, the war’s cost has already reached approximately $3.7 billion in its opening days, while Trump simultaneously proposes a $1.5 trillion military budget — a 50% increase.

2. Khamenei assassination triggers succession crisis

The assassination of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in Israeli airstrikes on February 28 — confirmed by the Iranian Supreme National Security Council on March 1 — has opened a succession crisis at the apex of the Iranian state during wartime. An Interim Leadership Council took power under Article 111 of the Iranian constitution, comprising Chief Justice Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Eje’i, Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, and President Masoud Pezeshkian.

IRGC commanders are now “repeatedly pressuring” Assembly of Experts members to vote for Mojtaba Khamenei, the supreme leader’s son — a move that risks contradicting the Islamic Republic’s anti-monarchical founding ideology. Trump openly inserted himself into the process, stating: “We’re going to have to choose that person along with Iran,” and dismissed Khamenei’s son as “a lightweight.”

Key facts: - The Assembly of Experts, an 88-member clerical body, is constitutionally responsible for selecting the next Supreme Leader - IRGC pressure for a dynastic succession represents an unprecedented politicization of the selection process - The succession fight is unfolding while Iran is under active bombardment and near-total communications blackout - Washington Post analysis suggests the leadership vacuum has complicated Iran’s military command structure

Sources Washington Post Iran succession: Who comes after Khamenei? · Bloomberg Iran’s Assembly of Experts nears naming Khamenei’s successor · The Hill Iran interim leadership after Khamenei death

3. Russia sharing satellite intelligence on US military positions with Iran

Multiple US officials confirmed that Russia is providing Iran with satellite imagery showing the locations of US troops, warships, and aircraft across the Middle East — a qualitative escalation of Moscow’s proxy support for Tehran. One official described the effort as “pretty comprehensive,” encompassing locations of American warships and aircraft positions.

The intelligence sharing directly implicates Russia in potential US casualties and transforms the conflict from a US-Israel versus Iran war into an emerging great-power confrontation. It comes as the Trump administration has simultaneously sought to deepen US-Russia economic ties around Ukraine negotiations, exposing the contradictions at the heart of Washington’s geopolitical strategy.

Key facts: - Russia is sharing satellite imagery of US troop deployments, ship positions, and aircraft locations - The intelligence could enable more effective Iranian targeting of US forces - The development parallels Cold War-era proxy conflicts but with direct superpower intelligence support - US officials have not announced any retaliatory measures against Russia for the intelligence sharing

4. Qatar halts LNG production after Iranian drone strikes; oil surges above $90

Iranian drone strikes on QatarEnergy’s Ras Laffan and Mesaieed industrial facilities forced the world’s largest LNG exporter to halt production entirely, triggering a global energy shock. Brent crude surpassed $90 per barrel on March 7, up approximately 20% since February 28. US crude posted its biggest weekly gain on record.

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed to commercial shipping since Iran’s IRGC declared it “closed” on March 2. Over 150 tankers are now anchored in open waters as insurers withdraw coverage from the Persian Gulf corridor. Goldman Sachs has warned oil could reach $100 per barrel if the Hormuz disruptions persist five or more weeks, adding an estimated 0.8% to global inflation.

Key facts: - European natural gas futures jumped 45-50% following the Qatar production halt - Saudi Aramco’s Ras Tanura refinery was also struck and closed as a precaution - The US Treasury issued a 30-day waiver allowing India to continue buying Russian crude — a carve-out reflecting wartime strains on global energy supply chains - Oxford Economics and Bloomberg both warned the war threatens global economic recovery already damaged by Trump tariffs

5. February jobs report: US economy sheds 92,000 jobs

The US economy lost 92,000 jobs in February — dramatically worse than the consensus expectation of a 60,000 net gain — while unemployment rose to 4.4%. This marks the third monthly contraction in five months, with the US labor market having shed jobs in five of the past nine months and losing a net 19,000 jobs since Trump’s major tariff announcements in May 2025.

The report lands amid an accelerating wave of corporate layoffs. Hiring announcements collapsed 55% in a single month, reaching the lowest level since 2010. Wages rose modestly — average hourly earnings increased 0.4% month-over-month and 3.8% year-over-year — but these gains are being eroded by the war-driven energy price spike.

Key facts: - Deepest sectoral declines: healthcare (down 28,000), leisure and hospitality (down 27,000), construction (down 11,000) - 2025 saw 1.2 million announced job cuts — highest outside a formal recession since 1989 - Major layoffs: Oracle (30,000), Amazon (30,000 since October), UPS (tens of thousands after a prior 48,000-person reduction), Block (eliminated half its workforce in a single day) - The Trump administration has eliminated 317,000 federal workers - Microsoft’s AI chief has predicted “most, if not all, professional tasks” will be automated within 18 months; Amazon plans to automate 75% of warehouse operations, putting 600,000 jobs at risk

As Tom Hall argued in today’s WSWS Perspective, the war and the economic crisis are not separate events but “expressions of the same class offensive by the capitalist ruling elite.”

6. Senate defeats war powers resolution on Iran 47-53

The Senate rejected a war powers resolution sponsored by Sen. Tim Kaine that would have constrained Trump’s authority to prosecute the Iran war, failing 47-53 in a procedural vote. Sen. Rand Paul was the only Republican to vote with Democrats; Sen. John Fetterman (D-PA) was the lone Democrat to oppose the measure. A parallel House resolution also failed the previous day, 212-219.

Trump launched the war without a congressional declaration of war, invoking the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force — a framework designed for the post-9/11 Afghanistan campaign, now being stretched to cover a full-scale air war against a sovereign state. Republicans argued that withdrawing authorization while Iran was launching missiles at US bases would be “irresponsible.”

Key facts: - The bipartisan refusal to invoke war powers follows the same pattern from the Iraq and Libya wars - Congressional debate has been confined entirely to questions of military effectiveness, not the legitimacy of the aggression itself - Polling shows only 38% of Americans approve of the Iran war while 49% disapprove — yet both parties voted to sustain it

Sources Al Jazeera US Senate fails to advance war powers measure to rein in Trump’s war with Iran · The Hill Senate war powers vote on Iran · Axios Iran Senate war powers vote

7. Stand Up for Science rallies in 46 locations nationwide

The second national Stand Up for Science mobilization took place Saturday, March 7, with rallies in 26 primary locations and 20 pop-up events across the United States. Speakers included members of Congress, Nobel laureates, and leaders in science and health. The protests target the Trump administration’s systematic dismantling of federal science agencies, funding cuts to research, and immigration crackdowns that have driven researchers out of the country.

The rallies arrive at a critical moment: HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. this week canceled funding for Moderna’s candidate H5N1 avian flu vaccine, and newly appointed ACIP members include figures aligned with vaccine-skeptical positions. The movement, formed in February 2025 in response to executive orders undermining the scientific establishment, has expanded significantly in its second year.

Austin Sixth Street shooting exploited for anti-Muslim hysteria

The March 1 mass shooting at Buford’s Backyard Beer Garden on Austin’s Sixth Street — in which 53-year-old Ndiaga Diagne, a naturalized US citizen originally from Senegal, killed 3 and wounded 14 before being shot by police — is being weaponized by Republican senators and representatives to demand anti-Muslim crackdowns and restored DHS funding.

Sens. Lindsey Graham and Chuck Grassley, along with Rep. Chip Roy, have used the shooting to press for expanded surveillance and immigration enforcement powers. The FBI has identified only a “potential nexus to terrorism” with no confirmed motive or organizational affiliation. Notably, Senegal is predominantly Sunni while Iran is Shia — the conflation of all Muslim populations with the Iran war enemy reflects the cynical manufacture of domestic fear to justify wartime authoritarianism.

Nashville journalist seized by ICE

Nashville journalist Estefany Rodriguez Florez was arrested by ICE agents in unmarked vehicles on March 4 while sitting in a marked Nashville Noticias press vehicle outside a gym — arrested without warrant despite five years of lawful residence and an active asylum claim. She was transferred to an Alabama detention facility and separated from her 8-year-old daughter.

Rodriguez, a Colombian journalist who fled to the US in 2021 after receiving threats for her coverage of armed groups, had been actively reporting stories critical of ICE practices, including coverage of immigration raids the day before her detention. Her attorneys filed an emergency lawsuit claiming her arrest constitutes First Amendment retaliation, and that ICE agents “implicitly admitted” making a warrantless arrest. A hearing is scheduled for March 21.

The case fits a broader pattern: a Texas photojournalist has been detained for over five months after refusing to become an FBI informant; an Atlanta reporter was arrested covering protests and subsequently deported; former CNN anchor Don Lemon faces charges for reporting on anti-ICE protests.

Philadelphia school closures: 18 schools targeted

Philadelphia’s $2.8 billion Facilities Master Plan proposes closing 18 schools (reduced from an initial 20), disproportionately in working-class neighborhoods. Over 1,000 residents attended the public board meeting in protest. The plan calls for modernization of 159 buildings and maintenance of 124 facilities over 10 years, funded by $1 billion in district resources and $1.8 billion in public and philanthropic support. Previous school closures in the early 2010s produced measurable harm: increased absenteeism, higher suspension rates, and declines in academic performance among displaced students. A student testified: “Don’t sell us out to pay your debts.” Changes would not take effect until the 2027-28 school year.

US measles cases reach 1,281

The CDC reports 1,281 confirmed measles cases in 2026 as of March 5, with 58 hospitalizations nationwide. The Spartanburg County, South Carolina outbreak has neared 1,000 cases, with almost all infections occurring in unvaccinated individuals. Eighty-nine percent of national cases (1,137) are outbreak-associated, and 12 new outbreaks have been declared in 2026. The CDC has deployed $8.5 million to seven affected jurisdictions.

The crisis is being accelerated by the Trump administration’s vaccine-hostile posture. HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s appointment of vaccine-skeptical figures to the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices — including Sean G. Downing of Florida and Angelina Farella of Texas — signals further erosion of public health infrastructure. The US is now on track for its worst measles year in over three decades.

Sources CDC Measles data and research · CIDRAP US measles cases soar; South Carolina confirms 58 new infections · Axios Measles cases and outbreaks amid vaccine skepticism

HHS cancels Moderna H5N1 vaccine funding

Kennedy’s HHS terminated funding for Moderna’s candidate H5N1 avian flu and pandemic vaccines — eliminating a critical preparedness measure while bird flu remains widespread in US dairy cows and poultry flocks. H5N1 has demonstrated the capacity for mammalian transmission, and the cancellation leaves the US without a next-generation mRNA-based pandemic flu vaccine in development at a time when the WHO continues to classify H5N1 as a pandemic threat.

Sources CIDRAP HHS cancels funding for Moderna’s candidate H5 avian flu and pandemic vaccines

Nasal spray vaccines show promise

Two separate studies report promising results for nasal spray vaccines against respiratory diseases. A new nasal vaccine outperformed traditional flu shots against H5N1 in animal tests by targeting the nose and lungs — the primary sites of infection. A separate universal nasal spray demonstrated protection against COVID-19, influenza, and pneumonia in preclinical trials. These breakthroughs arrive at the worst possible political moment, with federal vaccine programs facing deep cuts under the Kennedy-led HHS.

Sources ScienceDaily H5N1 nasal vaccine shows promise · ScienceDaily Universal nasal spray protects against COVID, flu, and pneumonia

New protein identified as key to diabetic blindness

Researchers have identified the protein LRG1 as the trigger for the earliest damage in diabetic retinopathy — the leading cause of blindness among working-age adults. LRG1 constricts retinal blood vessels and reduces oxygen supply, initiating the cascade of damage that leads to vision loss. The discovery opens a potential target for preventive treatments that could intervene before irreversible damage occurs.

Arts and culture

The dominant cultural developments of the past 24 hours are inseparable from the broader political crises — war, labor conflict, and state censorship are reshaping cultural institutions in real time.

WGA staff strike forces cancellation of Writers Guild Awards ceremony

The Writers Guild of America West canceled its March 8 Los Angeles awards ceremony rather than cross the picket line of its own striking staff. The 115 employees of the WGA West, organized under the Pacific Northwest Newspaper Guild since April 2025, have been on strike since February 17 over wages, just-cause protections in the discipline process, and AI surveillance protections — the last of these reflecting the growing recognition among cultural workers that artificial intelligence threatens not merely employment but workplace autonomy itself.

The WGA East will proceed with its New York ceremony. Dozens of WGA member captains joined the staff picket line in solidarity, an unusual display of intra-union class consciousness. The stakes extend beyond the internal dispute: AMPTP contract negotiations begin March 16 — the first round of talks since the contentious 2023 bargaining cycle that produced historically long, dual writers and actors strikes. The staff strike’s outcome will shape the political atmosphere heading into those negotiations.

Sources Variety WGA cancels L.A. award show due to staff strike · Deadline WGA Awards West Coast ceremony canceled amid staff union strike · Hollywood Reporter Writers Guild Awards Los Angeles ceremony canceled amid internal labor strike

Berlinale Golden Bear goes to Yellow Letters amid government censorship controversy

Ilker Catak’s Yellow Letters won the Golden Bear at the 76th Berlin International Film Festival. The film follows a Turkish actress and academic couple persecuted by the state after signing a peace petition — a direct reference to the real-world prosecution of approximately 2,000 Turkish artists and academics between 2016 and 2019. Shot in Berlin and Hamburg but deliberately set in “Ankara” and “Istanbul,” the film signals that its warning against authoritarianism applies to Germany itself.

The award carries additional weight given the censorship controversy surrounding this year’s Berlinale. German government officials pressured festival director Tricia Tuttle to accept “recommendations” including an “advisory forum representing diverse social groups, including Jewish voices” — which critics interpret as granting Zionist organizations influence over film selection. Director Catak publicly condemned this as “blatant state interference in the autonomous exercise of art… We would have to call it what it is: censorship.” The collision between a film about political repression of artists and the real-world political pressure on the festival where it won is a striking cultural moment.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — channels antiwar sentiment into Democratic Party proceduralism and AIPAC-focused lobbying; avoids class analysis of war’s origins in capitalist crisis
  • Left Voice — takes more radical anti-imperialist positions than Jacobin but still lacks a Trotskyist program for working-class power; notable polemic against Eric Blanc’s reformism on military spending
  • PSL / Liberation News — Stalinist-oriented war opposition that defends the Iranian regime as anti-imperialist without class analysis
  • Socialist Alternative — reformist framework channels opposition through abstract “movement” building rather than revolutionary party construction

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s war coverage reveals the fundamental limitations of Democratic Socialist politics when confronted with imperialist war. Four significant pieces published in the past 24 hours:

“US, Israel Target Civilians as Iran Escalates War to Survive” covers civilian casualties but frames the conflict through the lens of Iranian “survival” — as though the war were a conflict between two belligerents rather than an imperialist assault on a semi-colonial nation. The article avoids any class analysis of the war’s origins in the crisis of American capitalism.

“Trump’s War on Iran Makes a Mockery of American Democracy” channels opposition through concern for democratic procedure — noting that only 21% of the public supports the war — but proposes no path beyond Congressional mechanisms. The implied solution is better Congressional oversight, not independent working-class action.

Most revealing is “The Iran War Shows Why It’s Time for Chuck Schumer to Go,” which channels genuine antiwar sentiment into an internal Democratic Party leadership fight. The argument that replacing Schumer with a more “progressive” Senate leader would change the trajectory of US imperialism exposes Jacobin’s essential function: keeping working-class opposition trapped within the two-party system.

“AIPAC Is Influencing Trump’s War in Iran” focuses on the Israel lobby’s influence — a framework that implies removing AIPAC’s money from politics would alter the war’s trajectory, obscuring the fact that the war serves fundamental US imperialist interests regardless of any individual lobby’s influence.

Overall orientation: Jacobin is performing its characteristic function during wartime — expressing limited opposition while channeling it into safe Democratic Party proceduralism and single-issue lobbying frameworks. The class character of the war and the need for independent working-class action are entirely absent.

Sources Jacobin US, Israel target civilians as Iran escalates war to survive · Jacobin Trump’s war on Iran makes a mockery of American democracy · Jacobin The Iran war shows why it’s time for Chuck Schumer to go · Jacobin AIPAC is influencing Trump’s war in Iran

Left Voice

Left Voice published three pieces, reflecting its more radical posture relative to Jacobin while exposing its own political limitations.

“We Need a Mass Movement to Defeat Trump’s Imperialist War on Iran” correctly identifies the war as imperialist but proposes a movementist framework — a “mass movement” — rather than the construction of a revolutionary working-class party. The article lacks any programmatic perspective for how such a movement would wield working-class power to actually stop the war.

“Feminists Must Fight Imperialism in Iran Without Supporting the Theocratic Regime” adopts an identity-politics framing of anti-imperialism and a third-camp position on the Iranian regime — opposing both the US attack and the Iranian state without developing the class-independent perspective that the ICFI advances.

Most notable is “As Trump Bombs Iran, Eric Blanc Thinks Socialists Should Fight for a ‘Less Expensive War Machine’” — a polemic against Jacobin contributor Blanc’s argument that socialists should advocate for a smaller, cheaper military rather than opposing imperialist war as such. Left Voice correctly identifies Blanc’s reformism but cannot offer a genuine alternative because it lacks the Trotskyist program for permanent revolution.

Overall orientation: More radical than Jacobin on the war, but the absence of a revolutionary program means Left Voice’s opposition remains at the level of protest politics rather than a strategy for working-class power.
