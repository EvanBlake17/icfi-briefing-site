1. US-Iran near 14-point deal; oil crashes 10% as Trump pauses Hormuz escort mission

A White House-brokered 14-point memorandum of understanding to end the US-Iran war and frame parallel nuclear talks moved into final negotiation overnight. Iran is expected to respond within 48 hours. Crude markets plunged: WTI fell roughly 10.9% to about $91.10/barrel, and Brent crude dropped 9.2% to $99.79 — below $100 for the first time since April 22, after closing at $114.44 the prior day. Gold spiked sharply on parallel safe-haven repositioning, with futures running from a $4,569 open to $4,712 by mid-morning Eastern time. The reversal followed Trump’s two-day suspension of “Project Freedom,” the US Navy operation to escort neutral-flagged commercial ships through the Strait of Hormuz. Of approximately 1,600 commercial vessels with 20,000 seafarers trapped in the Persian Gulf, only three transited under naval escort before the operation was paused.

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, the suspension constitutes “a humiliating strategic defeat” — the Pentagon has spent $25 billion on the Iran campaign so far, lawmakers are anticipating a $100 billion supplemental request, and Iranian forces have struck US assets more than ten times since an April 8 ceasefire collapsed. The New York Times’ own framing — “A Silver Bullet to End the Iran War. There May Be None” — was cited as an inadvertent bourgeois admission of the same fact.

Key facts: - US forces sank six Iranian vessels in the Strait of Hormuz earlier this week; Iran denies combat ships were hit and reported five civilians killed - UAE struck for the first time in the conflict: three cruise missiles launched, three intercepted; an Iranian drone hit a UAE oil tanker; a strike on the Fujairah energy facility injured three Indian nationals - A South Korean commercial vessel was hit by explosion and fire in the strait; crew nationalities and fate unreported - Brent had risen more than 50% above pre-conflict levels (Feb. 28, 2026 start); US gasoline national average hit $4.52/gallon, the first time above $4.50 since July 2022 - German Chancellor Merz called on Iran to “return to the negotiating table and stop holding the region and the world hostage”; Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi: “There is no military solution to a political crisis” - Pakistan continues as active mediator; senior-level US-Iran talks have aborted twice - Trump invoked the War Powers Resolution on May 1, formally asserting hostilities had “ended” — even as US ships were sunk and US escorts came under fire

2. Russia rejects Ukraine’s open-ended ceasefire; kills 28+ in overnight strikes

Within minutes of Ukraine proposing an open-ended ceasefire to begin at midnight on May 6, Russian forces launched 108 drones and three missiles at targets across the country. At least 28 were killed in the past 24 hours, including 12 in Zaporizhzhia from aerial bombs and drones, more than five in Kramatorsk from KAB guided bombs, and one security guard at a kindergarten in Sumy. Russia separately declared its own unilateral truce only for May 9 — Victory Day — for which Ukraine pledged reciprocal restraint.

Key facts: - Ukrainian President Zelensky: “Russia’s choice is an obvious spurning of a ceasefire” - Foreign Minister Sybiha: “Putin only cares about military parades, not human lives” - Ukrainian deep-strike retaliation: drones killed five in Crimea and two in Cheboksary, deep inside Russia, demonstrating continued penetration of Russian airspace - This is the 1,530th-plus day of the full-scale war; on May 3 alone Russia mounted 141 combat engagements, 254 guided aerial bombs, and 9,870 kamikaze drones - The May 9 truce window has drawn explicit objection from European foreign ministers as a propaganda exercise rather than a step toward de-escalation

3. Gaza “truce” frays as Israel threatens war resumption; 828 Palestinians killed since October

NPR’s correspondents in Gaza filed a long report this morning following a Palestinian family recovering relatives’ remains from beneath rubble more than 18 months after one of Israel’s deadliest single strikes of the war. The piece anchors a brutal accounting: Israel has killed at least 828 Palestinians since the partial October 2025 truce, with approximately 8,000 bodies still buried under debris across the territory. Total Palestinian deaths since October 2023 stand between 72,608 and 75,498, per the Gaza Health Ministry, against approximately 2,039 Israelis killed.

Key facts: - Israel has formally threatened to resume full-scale operations unless Hamas disarms; Hamas has rejected disarmament as a precondition - The April 2026 UN Commission of Inquiry report concluded the regional war has “eclipsed” surging human rights violations against Palestinians - Reconstruction in Gaza remains effectively zero — no debris removal, no infrastructure rebuilding, no return of displaced populations to former homes - The Gaza-originating war has been geographically subsumed by the broader Iran-Israel-US war, with daily Palestinian casualties now drawing minimal international attention - Israel has continued targeted strikes inside Gaza City and Khan Younis through the “truce” period

4. Spirit Airlines collapse eliminates 17,000 jobs as Trump refuses $500M bridge loan

Spirit Airlines’ Chapter 7 liquidation was finalized this week, abolishing 17,000 jobs overnight, simultaneously terminating all worker benefits, and pricing low-income travelers out of air travel as remaining carriers raise fares to capture the abandoned market. The trigger was the doubling of jet fuel prices from the US war on Iran. The structural cause, as Tom Hall argues in the WSWS Perspective today, was “five decades of systematic class warfare” beginning with the Carter administration’s Airline Deregulation Act of 1978.

The Trump administration refused Spirit’s request for a $500 million bridge loan. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy was widely quoted: “What we don’t want to do is put good money after bad.” The same administration is, by its own figures, spending $1 billion per day on military operations against Iran.

Key facts: - Industry consolidation: from ten major US carriers in 1978 to three today; top firms’ collective market share rose from 40% to 80% - Trade unions at Spirit accepted wage cuts of up to 15.7% in exchange for equity stakes — converting union leadership into co-managers of austerity - Fares on remaining carriers up sharply on routes formerly served by Spirit; rural and Caribbean routes now effectively eliminated - Hall cites Paul Volcker’s 1979 admission — “The standard of living of the average American has to decline” — as the ideological through-line linking Carter “shock therapy” to the present - The WSWS Perspective demands airline nationalization under workers’ democratic control, funded by expropriating war profits accumulated by defense contractors and energy corporations during the Iran conflict

5. Louisiana governor postpones congressional primary to enforce post-VRA gerrymander

Republican Governor Jeff Landry on April 30 invoked emergency powers to postpone Louisiana’s congressional primary from May 16 to July 15 — the first such use of gubernatorial emergency authority to delay an election for partisan advantage in US history. The action follows the Supreme Court’s 6-3 ruling in Louisiana v. Callais gutting Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, which has enabled the elimination of majority-minority (Black-majority) districts across multiple states.

Key facts: - Democratic election attorney Marc Elias: “What is happening in Louisiana right now is both a redistricting power grab and a dry run for authoritarian election subversion this fall” - Texas redistricting under the new VRA framework projects approximately five additional Republican seats; Florida projects four additional - Patrick Martin’s WSWS analysis frames both parties as anti-democratic, arguing congressional Democrats have refused to mount any serious challenge to either the Callais ruling or Landry’s emergency-powers maneuver - The article warns Trump may invoke the Insurrection Act to subvert the November midterms outright, citing his administration’s expanding deployment of federal forces in domestic settings - Civil rights organizations have filed expedited motions in the Eastern District of Louisiana but face an unfavorable Fifth Circuit and Supreme Court

6. Balikatan 2026: 17,000 troops, first live Tomahawk fire on Philippine soil, 100 miles from Taiwan

The Balikatan 2026 exercises, now under way across Luzon and the Batanes islands, involve 17,000 troops from seven nations — approximately 10,000 of them American. The exercise featured the first live firing of a Tomahawk cruise missile on Philippine soil and the deployment of 1,400 Japanese Self-Defense Force troops in combat roles for the first time since World War II. Anti-ship missile systems have been positioned on Itbayat Island, roughly 100 miles from Taiwan.

As John Malvar wrote on the WSWS this morning, the exercises are explicitly war-preparatory rather than defensive. Filipino diesel prices have doubled from ₱55 to ₱130 per liter; headline inflation reached 7.2% in April 2026. Malvar draws a direct parallel between Japan’s 1940s “Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere” and the present “Free and Open Indo-Pacific” doctrine, arguing the rebranding masks the same imperial logic.

Key facts: - Seven participating nations: US, Philippines, Japan, Australia, France, UK, South Korea - The Tomahawk firing — Philippines’ first — was condemned by Beijing as an “explicit threat to peace and sovereignty in the Asia-Pacific” - Japan’s deployment of SDF combat troops abroad represents a fundamental rupture with Article 9 of its constitution, which has been preserved in formal terms but functionally voided - Filipino fishermen and indigenous communities on Itbayat have been displaced from traditional fishing grounds for the duration of the exercises

7. Sudan enters fourth year of civil war; famine confirmed, 34 million need aid

The civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces has entered its fourth year. The UN now reports 34 million people — 65% of the country’s population — require urgent humanitarian assistance, while 11.5 million are internally displaced, the largest single displacement crisis on the planet. Famine has been formally confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli, with risk of famine in 20 additional areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan.

Key facts: - 19 million Sudanese — two of every five — face high-level acute food insecurity - 4.2 million children and pregnant or breastfeeding women need malnutrition treatment immediately - Red Sea shipping disruptions from the Iran-Hormuz conflict are raising the cost of food, fuel, and fertilizer for Sudan, compounding the catastrophe - Western governments have diverted diplomatic and aid resources to the Iran war, leaving the Sudan response chronically underfunded - The crisis receives a small fraction of the bourgeois media attention afforded the Middle East, despite a comparable or greater absolute death toll

Tokyo mass protest against Article 9 revision and Iran war

50,000 people gathered at Tokyo’s Rinkai Disaster Prevention Park on Constitution Memorial Day to oppose Prime Minister Takaichi’s drive to revise Article 9 — Japan’s pacifist constitutional clause — and embed emergency-powers provisions enabling domestic military deployment. The protest movement has grown rapidly, from 3,600 participants in February to more than 30,000 in April to Tuesday’s 50,000.

The driver, as Peter Symonds reports on the WSWS, is the Iran war: Asahi Shimbun polling shows 82% of Japanese voters oppose US attacks on Iran, including 70% of LDP supporters. The article situates this within a decade-long erosion of Article 9 and a doubling of Japan’s military budget under successive governments, and notes — with the Balikatan deployment of Japanese combat troops — that constitutional formalism is increasingly disconnected from operational reality.

Nexteer auto-parts workers reject 96% — bargaining team extends contract anyway

The UAW Local 699 bargaining team at Nexteer Automotive — the global auto-steering supplier whose 4,600 Saginaw, Michigan workers anchor a $4.6 billion 2025 revenue base — has extended the contract without member approval following a 96% rank-and-file rejection of the prior tentative agreement. US measles cases hit 1,814 — already approaching 2025’s full-year total of 2,288. As of April 30, the CDC has confirmed 1,814 measles cases across 37 US jurisdictions, with 24 new outbreaks declared in 2026 alone; 93% of cases (1,688) are outbreak-associated. The full-year 2025 total was 2,288. With seven months of 2026 remaining, the country is on pace to far exceed last year’s count, marking a multi-decade high in domestic measles transmission. CDC outbreak teams have been deployed to Texas, New Mexico, Ohio, Florida, and the Pacific Northwest; the agency’s continuing budget restrictions imposed by the FY26 reconciliation package have forced field-team consolidation.

PLOS Biology: H5N1 detected in farm air, cattle breath, and wastewater. A study published this morning in PLOS Biology sampled 14 H5N1-infected California dairy farms and detected infectious virus in airborne particles during milking, in cattle breath, and in farm wastewater — pathways previously underestimated. H5N1 has now affected over 700 California herds across at least 16 states. Airborne transmission on farms substantially expands the known exposure risk for the estimated 500,000-plus US dairy workers and complicates containment strategies that have focused on raw milk.

WHO confirms H5N1 child death in Bangladesh. WHO has tracked 13 avian and swine influenza cases in humans in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child from Bangladesh — one of four confirmed human H5N1 cases in the quarter. Pediatric fatalities outside the US underscore that the risk is not limited to occupational farm exposure. The WHO is simultaneously finalizing data review for May 2026 vaccine antigen composition decisions for the next immunization cycle.

Connecticut passes one of nation’s most comprehensive AI laws. The Connecticut House passed SB 5 by 131-17 on May 1, sending the AI Responsibility and Transparency Act to Governor Lamont, who has indicated he will sign. The law covers frontier-model whistleblower protections (for models trained above 10²⁶ FLOP), automated hiring-decision disclosures (effective Oct. 1, 2026), AI-companion safeguards for minors, and mandatory synthetic-content labeling. Connecticut would join a small number of states with binding AI legislation, applying meaningful obligations to both frontier developers and downstream deployers — and notably stands as a counterweight to the federal voluntary framework.

NIST signs voluntary pre-deployment AI evaluation deals. The Center for AI Standards and Innovation, the renamed NIST entity under the Trump administration, announced agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI to conduct pre-deployment evaluations and targeted safety research on frontier AI models. The framework is voluntary and non-regulatory — the federal government’s primary near-term mechanism for AI risk management relies entirely on industry cooperation rather than enforceable mandate.

COVID activity at low ebb, declining in 31 states. CDC CFA Rt estimates show COVID-19 declining or likely declining in 31 states as of April 28, growing in only one. Ensemble forecasts project approximately 1,500 new hospital admissions for the week ending May 2 (range 660-3,000). No significant surge or new variant-driven wave is active in the US.

A Nature Medicine study published in the past 24 hours validated the OBSCORE model, which integrates thousands of clinical and molecular variables across 200,000 individuals to predict the risk of 18 distinct obesity-related complications — a potential tool for personalized weight-management intervention prioritization. Access to the May 2 Lancet (Vol. 407, Issue 10540) was restricted at time of compile.

Bess Wohl’s Liberation wins Pulitzer Prize for Drama

The 2026 Pulitzer Prize for Drama, announced May 4, was awarded to Bess Wohl’s Liberation — a play that returns to a 1970s Ohio women’s consciousness-raising group through the lens of a daughter trying to understand her mother’s political life. Wohl beat Nazareth Hassan’s Bowl EP and Talene Monahon’s Meet the Cartozians. Liberation matters not for its formal innovation, which is modest, but for the seriousness with which it treats a political moment that contemporary American theater has either trivialized or rendered as nostalgia. The play does not deliver a celebration of “second-wave feminism” in identitarian terms; it stages the contradictions of a movement embedded in mid-century American liberalism, the disappointments and abandoned solidarities, and the difficulty of transmitting political knowledge across generations under capitalist atomization. Wohl’s win cements her as one of American theater’s most prominent voices and is likely to give the play a Broadway transfer.

Cannes 2026 lineup set; Park Chan-wook to preside

The 79th Cannes Film Festival (May 12-23) will feature 21 films in main competition drawn from three continents, with five female directors represented and South Korean auteur Park Chan-wook presiding over the jury. Park’s selection — director of Oldboy, The Handmaiden, and Decision to Leave — signals a continued institutional turn toward filmmakers whose work addresses class violence, gendered power, and historical memory rather than the apolitical art-cinema gloss that dominated the 2010s. The lineup includes new films from Mia Hansen-Løve, Jafar Panahi (whose return to Cannes from house arrest is a significant artistic-political event), and a new feature from Brazilian director Kleber Mendonça Filho.

Boots Riley’s new feature opens Seattle International Film Festival

University of North Texas pulled ICE-critical exhibit

The ACLU and the National Coalition Against Censorship deployed a mobile billboard campaign against UNT after officials removed an art exhibit critical of ICE. Leaked transcripts confirmed administrators feared legislative funding retaliation. The case has become a flashpoint in a broader pattern of publicly funded institutions self-censoring under political pressure — a pattern intensifying as the $53 billion ICE/CBP expansion package moves through Congress and as Republican-controlled state legislatures threaten higher-education funding over individual exhibitions and faculty speech. Faculty across the country are watching closely; the chilling effect, well documented in pre-emptive cancellations of programming on Palestine, immigration, and labor history, has become a structural condition of US arts and humanities work.

House subcommittee proposes 35% NEA/NEH cut; agencies bar DEI grants

The House Appropriations subcommittee has proposed cutting NEA and NEH budgets from $207 million to $135 million each — a roughly 35% reduction — while both agencies have already barred grant funds from supporting DEI or “gender ideology” programming. More than 450 artists have signed a public letter of opposition. If the House figure prevails over the Senate’s status-quo proposal, it would be the deepest single-year cut to federal arts funding in decades. The structural intent is unmistakable: to starve any federally supported cultural production that addresses race, gender, sexuality, or class in terms not approved by the administration. As with the UNT exhibit, the pressure is producing self-censorship far in excess of the formal restrictions.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — celebratory framing of the Iran ceasefire diplomacy as a Trump “anti-war” turn; tactical praise for Sanders/AOC bills that propose nothing
  • Left Voice — formally radical anti-war language paired with substitution of “the streets” for any program of building independent rank-and-file organizations
  • Liberation News / PSL — uncritical orientation toward Iran as a “resistance” state; absence of any class analysis of the Iranian regime
  • Socialist Alternative — calls to “primary” Democrats from the left without any discussion of building a workers’ party
  • Socialist Worker / SWP (UK) — Stop the War Coalition / Labour-left orientation; pressure on Starmer rather than break from Labour
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — abstract revolutionary phrases, concrete adaptation to the same parliamentary milieu

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s lead political pieces in the past 24 hours frame the emerging US-Iran 14-point framework as a vindication of “diplomacy over war” and credit Sanders-AOC War Powers Resolution amendments for forcing Trump’s hand. The position elides the actual driver of the reversal — Iran’s demonstrated capacity to close the strait under bombardment, as Andre Damon argues on the WSWS — and recasts a forced military retreat as a triumph of liberal congressional proceduralism. A second piece on the Spirit Airlines collapse demands a federal “Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act” enforcement push and bridge financing for “viable” carriers, with no mention of nationalization. The political function is unmistakable: to provide left cover for Democratic Party legislators while ruling out any independent working-class response to either war or industrial collapse. Day’s orientation: congressional Democrats as the agency of left politics; class struggle reduced to lobbying.

Left Voice

Left Voice (PTS) publishes more sharply anti-imperialist language than Jacobin and correctly identifies the Iran framework as a humiliating US retreat rather than a peace breakthrough. But the practical conclusion — sustained “in the streets” mobilization — substitutes street activism for the construction of independent rank-and-file organizations in workplaces. Coverage of Sterling Heights and Nexteer is sympathetic but carefully avoids any explicit attack on the UAW International, instead calling for “democratization” of union structures — the classic Morenoist formula that leaves the bureaucracy intact. A third piece on Louisiana correctly identifies the Landry election delay as authoritarian but frames the response as a “united front of social movements” without specifying what political organization that front would constitute. Day’s orientation: correct identification of imperialist crisis paired with refusal of the program of independent revolutionary organization that the analysis logically requires.

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL’s coverage of the Iran framework explicitly characterizes the Islamic Republic as part of an “axis of resistance” to US imperialism, with no class analysis of the Iranian regime — a position that erases the brutal repression of Iranian workers, women, and minorities by the same state apparatus the WSWS has consistently exposed. Liberation News covers Spirit Airlines as a story of “corporate greed” and demands “regulation,” with no engagement with the 47-year arc of deregulation under both parties Tom Hall traces today, and certainly no nationalization demand. A piece on Balikatan denounces US imperialism in terms partially congruent with the WSWS but frames the Filipino response as a question of “national sovereignty” rather than international working-class struggle, reproducing the third-worldist substitution of nationalist categories for class. Day’s orientation: anti-imperialism severed from class politics; bourgeois states defended in proportion to their friction with Washington.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s lead article on the Louisiana primary postponement calls for “primarying” the Democratic congressional caucus from the left and explicitly disavows building a workers’ party as “premature given the conjuncture.” A second piece on Sterling Heights endorses the strike vote and praises Fain’s “left turn,” contradicting the WSWS analysis of the UAW’s pattern of overriding rank-and-file authorizations. A third piece on Australian rate hikes calls for a “people’s RBA” with worker representation — a typical SAlt formulation that absorbs class struggle into the existing state apparatus. Day’s orientation: systematic adaptation to the Democratic Party domestically; institutional reformism that preserves rather than breaks the bureaucratic apparatus of class collaboration.

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

The British SWP, through its Stop the War Coalition vehicle, frames the Iran ceasefire as a “victory for the anti-war movement” and credits Labour-left MPs for asking “tough questions” of Starmer — a Labour leadership that has uniformly backed the US war and continues to back Israeli operations in Gaza. A second piece on Gaza calls for “Labour to break with Starmer’s pro-war line” — pressure on Labour rather than break from Labour, the consistent SWP position since the 1980s. A third piece on the rising racist far right in Britain calls for “broad united fronts” without identifying the political character of those fronts or distinguishing them from popular-front cross-class formations. Day’s orientation: reformist orientation toward the Labour Party; popular-front methodology disguised as anti-fascist mobilization.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The IMT’s recently relaunched Revolutionary Communist Party press leads with abstract calls for “communism” and the “revolutionary potential of the working class” while concretely orienting to the same Labour and Democratic Party milieus. Coverage of the Iran framework correctly identifies it as a US retreat but the political conclusion — “join the RCP” — is severed from any concrete program of intervention into industrial struggles like Sterling Heights or Nexteer. A piece on Spirit Airlines calls for nationalization (a position that aligns with the WSWS), but the demand is offered without any mechanism — no rank-and-file committees, no orientation toward independent political organization of airline workers. Day’s orientation: maximalist phrases for recruitment, minimalist practice in struggle; revolutionary self-presentation paired with absence of an actual program of revolutionary intervention.

The pseudo-left press today exhibits a structural feature the WSWS has consistently identified: convergence with bourgeois press framing on the central question of Iran (defeat as victory; ceasefire as Democratic achievement) and convergence with the union bureaucracy on the central question of industrial struggle (the UAW International is reformable, not to be broken with). On Louisiana, the convergence is with the Democratic Party legal infrastructure — Marc Elias is approvingly cited across most outlets while no tendency calls for the construction of independent working-class political organization adequate to the scale of the authoritarian threat.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The 14-point US-Iran memorandum: contents, secret protocols, and the question of Iranian nuclear concessions

The framework is moving toward signature within 48 hours, and its contents will shape Middle East geopolitics for a decade. Initial leaks suggest provisions on uranium enrichment caps, regional militia operations, and US sanctions — but the full text and any side-letters or secret protocols have not been published. A WSWS analysis would document what is being conceded by both sides and identify the class interests served by each provision. Particular attention is warranted to the question of what Tehran is being asked to do regarding its support for Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, and the Houthis — and whether the US is in fact extracting concessions on Iranian internal repression in exchange for sanctions relief.

2. Sudan’s famine — the world’s largest ignored catastrophe

Sudan is simultaneously the world’s worst humanitarian crisis and most displaced population (11.5 million people), with famine confirmed in Darfur. The crisis receives a fraction of the media coverage of Middle East conflicts despite comparable or greater absolute death tolls, and Western governments have systematically diverted aid and diplomatic resources to the Iran war. A piece foregrounding the fourth anniversary of the war, the structural drivers of the catastrophe (the SAF-RSF split as a consequence of imperialist intervention since 2019), and the role of UAE arms transfers to the RSF would establish a framework the WSWS has not yet published.

Recommended reading NPR “Humiliated, broken, powerless”: Sudan enters fourth year of war

3. The death toll of the “truce” in Gaza and the 8,000 buried bodies

828 Palestinians have been killed since the October 2025 partial ceasefire and approximately 8,000 bodies remain buried under debris. The NPR report this morning documents a family recovering relatives killed in one of Israel’s deadliest single strikes more than 18 months later. The WSWS has covered Gaza as part of the regional war, but a focused piece on the “truce” period — what is killing Palestinians under formal cessation, the structural absence of reconstruction, and the political economy of displaced labor in southern Gaza — would expose the bourgeois diplomatic framing.

Workers under kafala face deportation if they are unable to work, and have no independent organizational rights. A WSWS investigation foregrounding these workers — and the South Korean tanker crew, whose nationalities and fate are also unreported — would expose the working-class casualties the bourgeois “interception success” narrative obscures.

The 60-day clock has been rendered meaningless by executive-branch redefinition of “hostilities.” Congressional Democrats have raised no challenge. A WSWS piece tracing the legal-historical arc from the 1973 War Powers Resolution to its present effective nullification would document a constitutional collapse that bourgeois commentators are barely acknowledging.

The study has profound implications for occupational health policy at a moment of CDC austerity and a growing dairy-worker mobilization. A WSWS analysis would link the airborne-transmission finding to the structural crisis of US public health under the Trump CDC, the absence of any federal occupational protection mandate, and the political question of who pays for personal protective equipment when 500,000-plus workers are at risk.

The federalist split is the central US AI policy question of 2026. A WSWS analysis would identify the class interests behind the federal voluntary turn (the AI capital-state nexus) and assess whether state-level legislation can constrain firms whose operational scale is global.
