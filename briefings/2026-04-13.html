1. US-Iran war: Trump orders Strait of Hormuz naval blockade after peace talks collapse

Twenty-one hours of US-Iran peace talks in Islamabad collapsed on April 12 after Iran rejected American demands for full dismantlement of its nuclear enrichment program, surrender of 1,000 pounds of enriched uranium, and withdrawal of support for Hezbollah and Hamas. Trump posted that “most points were agreed to, but the only point that really mattered, NUCLEAR, was not,” and announced the US Navy would immediately blockade the Strait of Hormuz — interdicting vessels that paid Iran transit tolls and clearing mines Iran allegedly placed there. He warned Iran would be “BLOWN TO HELL.”

The strait carries approximately one-fifth of global oil and LNG shipments. Iran’s IRGC said civilian vessels may cross under “specific regulations” but military vessels approaching would violate the ceasefire and “be dealt with severely.” The WSWS’s Andre Damon reported that guided-missile destroyers USS Michael Murphy and USS Frank E. Petersen Jr. have been deployed into the strait, while over 600 vessels remain trapped in the Gulf.

Key facts: - Brent crude surged 7.3% to $102.15/barrel; WTI rose 8.6% to $104.89 - US average gas price stands at $4.12/gallon, up 38% since the war began February 28 - Goldman Sachs called it “the largest oil supply shock in recorded history” - Democratic Sen. Mark Warner questioned the strategy: “How does blockading the strait pressure Iran to open it?” - China, India, and Europe condemned the blockade

2. Hungary’s Orbán defeated after 16 years as Magyar wins supermajority

Peter Magyar’s Tisza party won 138 of 199 parliamentary seats on 53.6% of the vote, ending Viktor Orbán’s 16-year grip on Hungarian politics. Orbán’s Fidesz won only 55 seats on 37.8%. Voter turnout reached 77.8% — a record for post-Communist Hungary — and Magyar claimed 3.3 million votes, the most any Hungarian party has ever received.

The two-thirds supermajority allows Tisza to amend Hungary’s constitution, reversing Orbán’s governance model that had concentrated power in the executive and eroded judicial independence. The result unlocks approximately €90 billion in EU funds that Brussels had suspended over democratic standards violations. Putin loses his primary EU ally within the bloc.

Key facts: - VP JD Vance had rallied for Orbán days before the vote - Barack Obama wrote: “This is a victory for democracy not just in Europe but around the world” - Orbán conceded: “We are going to serve the Hungarian nation from opposition as well” - Magyar and Tisza are a centre-right, pro-EU formation — not a left or socialist alternative to Fidesz

3. Russia-Ukraine Easter ceasefire collapses with thousands of violations

Putin declared a 32-hour ceasefire from 4 p.m. Saturday through end of Sunday for Orthodox Easter (April 12). Both sides accused each other of violations within hours. By 7 a.m. Sunday, Ukraine’s General Staff had recorded 2,299 Russian violations: 28 assault actions, 479 shellings, 747 attack drone strikes, and 1,045 FPV drone strikes. Russia’s Defense Ministry claimed 1,971 Ukrainian violations: 258 artillery/tank firings, 1,329 FPV drone strikes, and 375 munition drops.

Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov said Russia would not extend the truce unless Zelenskyy accepts Russian terms. A similar Easter truce last year also collapsed immediately. The war is now in its fourth year with no path to negotiated settlement.

Key facts: - Combined claimed violations exceed 4,200 in under 24 hours - The scale of drone warfare is notable: nearly 2,000 FPV drone strikes claimed by both sides combined - No international monitoring mechanism was established for the ceasefire

4. Israel-Lebanon war: IDF nears full control of Bint Jbeil amid white phosphorus allegations

The war in Lebanon, which began March 2, has entered a decisive phase. Israel’s 98th Division has nearly fully seized Bint Jbeil — the main site of ground combat — cutting all roads into the town with artillery, airstrikes, and alleged white phosphorus bombs. More than 2,000 militants and civilians have been killed, and over 1 million people — 20% of Lebanon’s population — have been displaced.

April 8 “Black Wednesday” saw Israel’s largest single-day strikes across Lebanon, killing at least 357 people. Lebanon’s government called it a massacre. IDF chief of staff Eyal Zamir said the IDF remains in “a state of war” in Lebanon despite the US-Iran ceasefire, reflecting ambiguity over whether that truce included Lebanon. Israel has announced plans to occupy southern Lebanon indefinitely. Pope Leo XIV called for an immediate ceasefire.

Key facts: - White phosphorus use in densely populated areas constitutes a violation of international humanitarian law - Israel has announced a plan to permanently occupy southern Lebanon - The scope of displacement — 20% of the national population — exceeds Lebanon’s 2006 war displacement

6. Sudan civil war approaches third anniversary: 400,000 dead, 14 million displaced

As Sudan’s civil war nears its April 15 three-year anniversary, the humanitarian catastrophe continues to deepen. Fourteen million people — one-quarter of Sudan’s population — have been forced to flee: 9 million remain internally displaced and 4.4 million have crossed borders. Twenty-one million Sudanese face acute food insecurity, with 6.3 million in the most dire emergency category.

Former US envoy for Sudan estimates up to 400,000 have been killed since fighting began April 15, 2023. The UN says RSF actions in El Fasher (Darfur) amount to war crimes and potential crimes against humanity. Yale University researchers identified satellite evidence of Ethiopian military supplying RSF forces via the Asosa base in western Ethiopia.

Key facts: - The SAF-aligned government has announced its return to Khartoum, having operated from Port Sudan since 2023 - Heavy fighting continues in Kordofans, Darfur, and Blue Nile State - The conflict receives a fraction of the media and diplomatic attention given to Ukraine or Iran despite comparable or greater humanitarian impact - No Western government has imposed punitive measures in response to UN war crimes findings

EU adopts far-right agenda in migration policy

The European Parliament adopted the new Return Regulation on March 26 by 389–206, in a coalition led by the European People’s Party with explicit support from far-right groups including the AfD and Rassemblement National. The regulation permits detention of migrants for up to 24 months (previously 6 months) based on arbitrary “security risk” designation without criminal conviction, establishes offshore “Return Hubs” to deport rejected asylum seekers to African countries with no personal ties, and — after amendment — now permits deportation of children and families in violation of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child.

Amnesty International condemned the measures as “punitive detention and deportation plans” rushed through “without adequate scrutiny or meaningful human rights assessments.” Greece, Germany, the Netherlands, Austria, and Denmark have already entered negotiations with African governments to host detention sites. The bloc aims to increase its effective return rate from below 20% to over 40%.

Lufthansa faces its third major strike in a month. Internal documents cited by the WSWS document increased pilot errors from “high emotional stress.”

Harvard graduate workers set April 21 strike deadline

Harvard’s 4,000 graduate workers (HGSU-UAW) have set an April 21 strike deadline after voting 96% to walk out, with 79% member participation. The dispute centers on wages of $18–21/hour — so low that many qualify for state food assistance — against a university endowment of $53.2 billion. Harvard offered only 2.5% annual raises against workers’ documented need for a 74% increase to achieve pay parity between Teaching Fellows and Research Assistants.

Key issues include wages, protections for non-citizen workers, and access to third-party arbitration in harassment and discrimination cases. This would be the third strike in HGSU-UAW’s history. The 2021 strike resulted in a real wage cut of 1.2% after inflation.

US measles cases surpass 1,714

The CDC confirmed 1,714 measles cases across 33 US jurisdictions as of April 9, making 2026 one of the two worst years for measles since the disease was declared eliminated in 2000. Ninety-four percent of cases (1,609) are linked to outbreaks. Utah’s outbreak — the largest still active — approaches 600 cases alone. The surge is driven in part by kindergarten vaccination coverage falling from 95.2% (2019–20) to 92.5% (2024–25), dropping below the 95% threshold needed for herd immunity.

In a rare positive note, Florida’s case count held at 144 for the week ending April 12 — the first week-over-week plateau in months. The Collier County outbreak centered on Ave Maria University has been stable at 106 cases since March 28, suggesting containment there may be succeeding.

H5N1 bird flu: entrenched in wildlife, low immediate human risk

The US has recorded 71 total human A(H5) bird flu cases since February 2024, with 94% classified as mild. November 2025 brought the first globally confirmed human infection with H5N5 — a distinct sub-variant. No person-to-person transmission has been detected. However, H5N1 is now entrenched in US dairy cattle, wild birds across six continents, and an expanding range of mammalian hosts, sustaining long-term pandemic concern.

Alzheimer’s early detection via smell loss mechanism

Researchers have identified the cellular mechanism explaining why smell loss may precede other Alzheimer’s symptoms by years. The study found that immune cells in the brain actively destroy olfactory (smell) nerve fibers after detecting abnormal surface signals. This opens a potential pathway for earlier biomarker detection through olfactory testing before cognitive decline is apparent — a significant development given Alzheimer’s affects an estimated 6.9 million Americans.

AI developments: cybersecurity, policy, and regulation

Anthropic’s unreleased Claude Mythos model, tested under Project Glasswing with Amazon, Microsoft, Apple, Google, and Nvidia, identified tens of thousands of vulnerabilities across operating systems and browsers, reproducing working exploits in over 80% of cases. Anthropic restricted access and committed $100 million in usage credits for defensive cybersecurity testing — marking the industry’s first large-scale coordinated defensive deployment of an AI model.

Separately, OpenAI released a 13-page policy paper arguing superintelligence is imminent and proposing a public wealth fund partly financed by AI companies, taxes on automated labor, and a four-day workweek incentive. Critics dismissed the proposals as “regulatory nihilism” designed to preempt tougher government oversight.

The pace of US AI legislation is accelerating: since mid-March, the number of enacted AI laws has grown from 6 to 25, with another 27 bills having cleared both chambers. The House Foreign Affairs Committee unanimously passed a bill requiring location-verification chips on AI hardware eligible for export.

Asha Bhosle, legendary Indian playback singer, dies at 92

Asha Bhosle, one of the most prolific recording artists in world history with more than 12,000 songs across eight decades, died on April 12 in Mumbai from multiple organ failure. She was 92. Bhosle’s career spanned the full arc of Indian cinema from the 1940s onward, and her extraordinary range — from classical and ghazal to pop, disco, and rock — defined the sound of Bollywood across successive generations. She received two National Film Awards, two Grammy nominations, and in 2011 was recognized by the Guinness Book of World Records for the most studio recordings in history.

Her last rites are scheduled with full state honors at Shivaji Park on April 13. Bhosle’s significance extends beyond entertainment: her voice was the soundtrack to post-independence India’s self-image, and her artistic evolution tracked the country’s social transformations over seven decades. In a period of Hindu nationalist cultural consolidation under the BJP, it is worth noting that Bhosle’s repertoire crossed linguistic, religious, and regional boundaries in ways that nationalist cultural projects seek to foreclose.

Olivier Awards: Paddington sweeps, Rosamund Pike and Rachel Zegler win

The 2026 Laurence Olivier Awards, marking the ceremony’s 50th anniversary, were held at the Royal Albert Hall on April 12. Paddington the Musical dominated with seven wins including Best New Musical — a commercially polished adaptation whose success reflects London’s West End leaning ever more heavily on presold intellectual property. Punch won Best New Play. Rosamund Pike took Best Actress for Inter Alia at the National Theatre. Rachel Zegler won Best Actress in a Musical for her portrayal of Eva Perón in Evita — a revival that gains political resonance against the backdrop of rising global authoritarianism. Paapa Essiedu won Best Supporting Actor for All My Sons, Arthur Miller’s indictment of wartime profiteering, whose themes carry obvious contemporary weight. Julie Hesmondhalgh won Best Supporting Actress for Punch.

Coachella 2026: spectacle, controversy, and cultural commodity

Coachella’s first weekend (April 10–12) featured headliners Justin Bieber, Sabrina Carpenter, and Karol G, with passes for both weekends selling out in two days. The festival was marked by controversy when Sabrina Carpenter mistook a fan’s zaghrouta — a traditional Arabic celebratory vocal expression — for yodelling and called it “weird” on stage, prompting backlash over cultural insensitivity. Carpenter later apologized on social media. Dance artist Anyma’s closing set was canceled due to strong winds — one of several weather-related disruptions. Karol G warned fans about ICE enforcement activity in the region ahead of her performance, a reminder that the festival’s curated escapism unfolds amid mass deportation campaigns. Bieber’s minimalist, almost desultory set drew mixed reviews.

The reviewers note that the Davos elite met at a luxury resort four hours from Verbier — the structural irony is built into the geography.

This week in history: April 13–19

25 years ago — April 18, 2001: Ethiopian Special Forces massacre 41 university students. Ethiopian security forces opened fire on students at Addis Ababa University during a crackdown on pro-democracy demonstrations. The students had organized protests demanding democratic reforms, academic freedom, and an end to state repression. In addition to the 41 killed, approximately 15,000 political opponents were arrested across the country in the ensuing crackdown. Ethiopia at the time ranked 171 out of 174 countries on the UN Human Development Index. The massacre exemplified the willingness of African ruling elites, backed by Western governments, to use extreme violence against democratic movements.

50 years ago — April 15, 1976: India resumes full diplomatic relations with China. After a 15-year rupture following the 1962 Sino-Indian border war, India and China restored full ambassadorial relations. The rapprochement reflected shifts in the Cold War balance — both nations sought to counterbalance Soviet influence in the region. The border dispute that triggered the 1962 conflict remained unresolved and continues to generate periodic military tensions to the present day, including deadly clashes in Ladakh in 2020.

75 years ago — April 18, 1951: Treaty of Paris establishes the European Coal and Steel Community. Six nations — France, West Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg — signed the treaty creating the ECSC, the institutional precursor to the European Union. Proposed by French Foreign Minister Robert Schuman, the community placed coal and steel production under supranational authority, ostensibly to make war between France and Germany “not merely unthinkable, but materially impossible.” The project served to integrate European capitalism under American hegemony during the Cold War and laid the foundation for the EU’s current structures.

100 years ago — April 18, 1926: Martha Graham premieres first independent dances. Martha Graham presented her first independent concert at the 48th Street Theater in New York, marking the beginning of modern dance as a distinct American art form. Graham rejected the conventions of classical ballet in favor of movement rooted in breathing, contraction, and emotional expression. Over the next seven decades, she created more than 180 works and trained generations of dancers who transformed global choreographic practice. Her work demonstrated that genuine artistic innovation emerges from engagement with human experience rather than formal tradition.
