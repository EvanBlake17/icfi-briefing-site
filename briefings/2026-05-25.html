1. Russia launches largest-ever assault on Kyiv with nuclear-capable Oreshnik missile

Russia carried out the largest barrage against Kyiv of the entire full-scale war overnight into May 24, firing roughly 600 drones and 90 missiles at the Ukrainian capital. Ukrainian air defenses reported intercepting 549 drones and 55 missiles, but at least four people were killed and between 60 and 83 wounded as some 40 separate locations were hit across every district of the city. Russia’s Defense Ministry framed the attack as retaliation for Ukrainian strikes on occupied Luhansk that killed at least 18 people.

The most significant escalation was the use of an Oreshnik hypersonic ballistic missile — nuclear-capable and impossible to intercept with current Ukrainian systems — which struck Bila Tserkva, about 50 miles south of Kyiv. It was only the third combat use of the Oreshnik in the war, and analysts read it as a deliberate signal of red lines rather than a battlefield decision.

Key facts: - The barrage also included Iskander, Kinzhal, and Tsirkon missiles; the Museum of Chernobyl was struck and more than 40 percent of its collection “irretrievably lost” - President Zelenskyy said “Russia tried to destroy not only life, but also memory,” while EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas condemned “deliberate strikes on city centres” - The Oreshnik’s role as nuclear intimidation underscores the unrestrained character of an inter-imperialist proxy conflict in which working people on both sides have no shelters and no voice

2. US-Iran ceasefire deal largely negotiated but key disputes remain unresolved

A proposed framework to end the 2026 Iran war is “largely negotiated” but remains stalled over central disputes, according to an Axios exclusive. The deal is structured as a 60-day memorandum of understanding: Iran would clear mines from the Strait of Hormuz, and in exchange the United States would lift its naval blockade and issue sanctions waivers allowing Iran to sell oil freely. Tehran would commit never to pursue nuclear weapons, negotiate the suspension of uranium enrichment, and remove its highly enriched uranium stockpile. Reporting indicates the framework also envisions the release of roughly $25 billion in frozen Iranian assets.

The arrangement remains fragile. Iran’s state media (Fars) contradicted Trump’s claim that Iran would “no longer control access” to the strait, calling it “inconsistent with reality.” Trump posted Sunday that “the Blockade will remain in full force and effect until an agreement is reached, certified, and signed,” and Secretary of State Rubio called the deal “a work in progress.” Netanyahu raised objections to a Hezbollah ceasefire condition in a call with Trump, while Tehran insists nuclear matters are a question of sovereignty.

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, the deal is unfolding against a backdrop of thousands of Iranian deaths, more than $300 billion in Iranian infrastructure damage, $29 billion in direct US military expenditures, and US gasoline at a four-year high of $4.51 per gallon. Democratic senators Cory Booker and Chris Van Hollen have joined Republican hawks Roger Wicker, Ted Cruz, and Lindsey Graham in attacking the framework, with Booker charging that Trump was “being played as a fool” and Graham warning it would be “a nightmare for Israel” — bipartisan evidence, Damon argues, that the dominant faction of the ruling class remains committed to military confrontation.

3. Israeli airstrike kills 36 at Gaza school as ceasefire exists in name only

An Israeli airstrike on May 25 struck the Fahmi al-Jawjawi school in the Daraj neighborhood of Gaza City, killing at least 36 people and wounding more than 55. The strike is the latest in a sustained pattern of attacks that has continued throughout a ceasefire that exists only on paper.

Since the October 10, 2025 truce came into effect, the Gaza government media office has documented at least 2,400 Israeli violations, and Israeli forces have killed at least 856 Palestinians. Israel now controls more than 50 percent of the Gaza Strip despite the supposed cessation of hostilities. On May 15, Israeli strikes killed Izz al-Din al-Haddad, identified as the leader of Hamas in Gaza.

Key facts: - International legal experts increasingly question whether any legally meaningful ceasefire still exists given the scale and consistency of the violations - Hamas blames Israel for systematic breaches; the diplomatic framework intended to consolidate the truce has been paralyzed (see International developments) - US arms transfers continue to underwrite the operations, making Washington a direct party to the ongoing killing under diplomatic cover

4. Turkish police storm opposition CHP headquarters to enforce court ouster

Turkish riot police stormed the Ankara headquarters of the main opposition Republican People’s Party (CHP) on May 24, firing tear gas and rubber bullets to evict party leader Özgür Özel and supporters who had occupied the building for three days. The raid enforced a Turkish appeals court ruling that annulled the 2023 CHP congress at which Özel was elected, citing unspecified irregularities, and reinstated former chairman Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu — the candidate who lost the 2023 presidential election to Recep Tayyip Erdoğan.

Özel denounced the ruling as a “judicial coup” and vowed to reclaim the building “in such a way that no one will be able to meddle again,” before leading supporters on an eight-kilometer march to Parliament. As Ulaş Sevinç reported on the WSWS, the operation was timed for a holiday weekend to suppress protest and circumvented the constitutional jurisdiction of the Supreme Electoral Council.

Key facts: - The court action bypassed the body constitutionally empowered to rule on party congresses, marking a sharp escalation of Erdoğan’s authoritarian consolidation - The CHP crisis is widely covered internationally (Washington Post, NBC, PBS, Reuters); the SEP argues both major Turkish parties serve the capitalist oligarchy and that only independent working-class mobilization can reverse the democratic erosion

5. Coal mine explosion kills 82 in China’s deadliest disaster since 2009

A gas explosion at the Liushenyu Coal Mine in Qinyuan County, Shanxi province, killed at least 82 miners on the evening of May 22, the deadliest Chinese mining disaster in over a decade. Of the 247 workers underground at the time of the blast, 123 were injured — four critically — and rescuers continued searching for those still missing. By the following morning, more than 200 people had been evacuated, and executives of the operating company, Shanxi Tongzhou Group Liushenyu Coal Industry, had been detained.

As Peter Symonds reported on the WSWS, the facility had been flagged by China’s National Mine Safety Administration in 2024 for “high gas levels” creating explosion risk, and more than half the workers that night had descended without proper registration, in violation of mandatory facial-recognition and tracking procedures.

Key facts: - An underground carbon monoxide sensor had triggered an alarm before the explosion; rescue was hampered by inaccurate blueprints, extreme depth, and rising water - Annual Chinese mine fatalities have fallen sharply over decades (162 in 2023), but Symonds argues profit-over-safety priorities make such disasters systemic rather than aberrational

6. WHO declares Ebola a global health emergency as outbreak spreads in DRC and Uganda

The World Health Organization has declared the Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) — its highest alert level. As of May 23, there were 968 suspected cases and at least 216 confirmed deaths, with the outbreak spreading from Ituri Province to North Kivu and South Kivu, plus five cases confirmed in the Ugandan capital, Kampala.

The causative agent is the Bundibugyo virus (Orthoebolavirus bundibugyoense), for which there are no approved vaccines or treatments. At least four healthcare workers have died, and the WHO warns that high positivity rates among initial samples and rising syndromic reporting suggest the true outbreak is much larger than detected.

Key facts: - The PHEIC was declared May 17; the outbreak does not yet meet pandemic-emergency criteria - Response is severely hampered by ongoing armed conflict and displacement in eastern DRC, collapsed health infrastructure, and high urban mobility — conditions rooted in decades of imperialist resource competition over cobalt, coltan, and gold

7. Sudan’s hunger catastrophe deepens as famine spreads across Darfur and Kordofan

Nearly 19.5 million people — two in five Sudanese — now face crisis-level acute food insecurity, the UN reported, as the world’s worst humanitarian crisis continues to deteriorate. Five million people face emergency levels and 135,000 face catastrophic starvation. Famine has been confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli, with the risk of famine in 20 additional areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan.

An estimated 825,000 children under five are expected to suffer severe acute malnutrition in 2026 — a 25 percent increase above pre-conflict levels. Nine million people are internally displaced, 40 percent of health facilities are non-functional, and 17 million lack access to safe drinking water.

Key facts: - Only 20 percent of Sudan’s 2026 Humanitarian Needs and Response Plan has been funded; just 3.13 million people received aid in February against a 4.8 million monthly target - The civil war that began in April 2023 has made Sudan the world’s hungriest country; the famine is the product of armed actors deliberately blocking aid, not a natural disaster

Hajj 2026 begins as Saudi Arabia deploys air defenses over Mecca

The 2026 Hajj officially began on May 25 with more than 1.6 million pilgrims arriving internationally and nearly 2 million expected in total. For the first time, Saudi Arabia has deployed advanced air-defense batteries on the outskirts of Mecca; the Defense Ministry posted video of the systems, describing them as “responsible for protecting the skies over the holy sites and dealing with all aerial threats.” The unprecedented militarization of the pilgrimage is directly tied to the ongoing Iran war and the fragile state of the Strait of Hormuz. Pilgrims also face extreme heat, with volunteers distributing water and misting fans at the holy sites.

One year after their nuclear-armed war, India and Pakistan remain locked in hostility

A year after the four-day war that nearly tipped two nuclear-armed states into catastrophe, India and Pakistan remain locked in hostility with no path to dialogue. The May 2025 conflict — triggered by the April 22, 2025 Pahalgam terrorist attack that killed 26 civilians, followed by India’s Operation Sindoor — saw both sides deploy cruise missiles, short-range ballistic missiles, and drones in the most sophisticated exchange between the two powers in history. The ceasefire technically holds, but Pakistan has staged government-organized victory concerts celebrating “The Day of the Battle of Truth,” and trust between the governments is described as “alarmingly low.” Both nations maintain their nuclear arsenals on hair-trigger readiness amid an ongoing risk of renewed escalation.

Republican gerrymandering offensive sweeps the South after Louisiana v. Callais ruling

A sweeping Republican campaign to redraw congressional maps across the American South is underway following the Supreme Court’s April 29 ruling in Louisiana v. Callais. By a 6–3 vote, the Court struck down Louisiana’s congressional map containing a second majority-Black district as an unconstitutional racial gerrymander — a decision that, in practice, has gutted what remained of the 1965 Voting Rights Act’s protections for minority-majority districts. Within weeks, several Southern states moved to redraw maps, claiming their own VRA-mandated majority-minority districts were now unconstitutional.

In his Perspective on the WSWS, Patrick Martin catalogs the scope: Tennessee Republicans have already eliminated the heavily Black Memphis district, carving it into three rural-anchored constituencies; Louisiana and Alabama are moving to eliminate second majority-Black districts; and the veteran districts of South Carolina’s Jim Clyburn and Mississippi’s Bennie Thompson face erasure. Martin calculates the number of majority-Black seats across the Deep South could fall to as few as seven, even though African Americans make up over 30 percent of the region’s population. He frames the drive as “an attack on the democratic rights of the working class as a whole,” and notes the Democrats pursue their own gerrymanders in California and Virginia.

USCIS reclassifies green card process to force applicants out of the country

A USCIS policy memorandum (PM-602-0199) issued May 21 reclassifies adjustment of status — the standard process by which roughly 500,000 people annually obtain green cards without leaving the US — as a “discretionary and extraordinary” form of relief rather than a presumptive right. The memo directs officers to weigh all factors case by case and emphasizes consular processing abroad as the default pathway, potentially forcing applicants to attend interviews in their home countries, where overstays can trigger three- to ten-year reentry bans.

As Jacob Crosse reported on the WSWS, immigrant spouses of US citizens face the greatest impact — potentially becoming stranded in countries where the Trump administration restricts entry or where US consular services are suspended. Former Biden-era USCIS official Doug Rand warned that hundreds of thousands of cases could be affected. Immigration analysts note the memo’s harsher framing came from an agency press statement rather than the operative guidance, but the chilling effect on family-based applicants is immediate.

Chemical disaster at California aerospace plant displaces 50,000

A leaking tank of methyl methacrylate (MMA) at the GKN Aerospace Transparency Systems plant in Garden Grove, California, forced the evacuation of as many as 50,000 residents over the Memorial Day weekend after the tank began overheating on May 21. Governor Gavin Newsom declared a state of emergency in Orange County, and an Orange County Fire Authority chief called it “the worst-case scenario I’ve ever faced in my career,” warning the tank could fail and spill thousands of gallons or go into thermal runaway and explode. A class-action lawsuit has been filed and the district attorney has launched a probe.

As Marc Wells reported on the WSWS, the failure traces to clogged relief valves and inadequate maintenance: GKN had received 2018 citations for improper cooling systems, a 2019 air-quality violation settled for $1 million, and March 2025 notices for identical record-keeping failures — yet faced only a $2,898 fine. Wells attributes the disaster to the deliberate underfunding of Cal/OSHA, which operates with a 32 percent staff vacancy rate.

Measles surges toward elimination threshold. The US has recorded 1,952 confirmed measles cases in 2026 as of May 21, across 29 outbreaks, with 93 percent of cases outbreak-associated. That total already equals 85 percent of all 2,288 cases recorded in 2025 — reached in under five months — putting the country at serious risk of losing the measles elimination status it has held since 2000.

Avian influenza spillover widens. The New England Journal of Medicine reported the first documented human infection with avian influenza A(H5N5): a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman whose nasal swab was negative but whose bronchoalveolar-lavage specimens confirmed the subtype, with higher viral loads in the lower respiratory tract. Separately, the WHO tracked 13 zoonotic flu cases in humans in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 infection in a child in Bangladesh and three additional H5N1 cases in Cambodia. No sustained human-to-human transmission has been detected, and the CDC continues to rate general public risk as low.

Arts and culture

The 79th Cannes Film Festival closed May 23, awarding the Palme d’Or to Romanian director Cristian Mungiu’s Fjord, a culture-war drama set in Norway starring Sebastian Stan and Renate Reinsve. Mungiu becomes only the tenth director to win the Palme twice, having previously taken the prize for 4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days (2007), a harrowing account of clandestine abortion under Ceaușescu’s police state. The recognition of Mungiu — one of the foremost living practitioners of social realism, whose work dissects the moral wreckage left by authoritarianism and the market — stands as an implicit rebuke to the spectacle-driven priorities of the commercial film industry. The Grand Prix went to Russian director Andrei Zvyagintsev’s Minotaur; Zvyagintsev’s earlier Leviathan offered one of the sharpest cinematic indictments of corruption and despair in post-Soviet Russia, and his presence in competition is notable given the cultural boycotts that have accompanied the Ukraine war. The jury was chaired by South Korean director Park Chan-wook.

That a festival can honor both a Romanian chronicler of dictatorship and a Russian critic of his own society’s decay points to a current in serious filmmaking that resists the nationalist and militarist pressures bearing down on cultural life. The challenge for these works is reaching audiences through a distribution apparatus increasingly geared toward franchise product and algorithmic streaming.

Governments escalate the global war on online anonymity — Stephen Parker

Parker documents a coordinated legislative offensive by the US, EU, UK, Canada, and Australia to eliminate online anonymity through mandatory digital identification and age-verification systems marketed as child protection. The US GUARD Act would require government ID or facial scans before accessing AI chatbots, with criminal penalties up to 20 years; the EU threatens Meta with over $12 billion in fines absent identity verification; France proposes “ghost participant” technology inserting invisible state agents into encrypted conversations; and Canada’s Bill C-22 would require ISPs to retain metadata for up to a year. Parker argues the true objective is suppressing independent working-class political organization, and calls for expropriating the technology monopolies under democratic working-class control.

Unique conclusion: Crosse frames the reclassification of adjustment of status as an assault on workers’ fundamental right to form families and live across borders, part of a broader drive to render the entire immigrant working population deportable at administrative discretion.

Rising unemployment in Australia points to Iran war’s deepening impact — Mike Head

Australia’s official unemployment rate hit a five-year high of 4.5 percent in April 2026, with youth joblessness at 11.1 percent and 692,500 total unemployed — though independent Roy Morgan surveys put the true figure at 10.1 percent when underemployment is counted. Rising fuel and food prices linked to the Middle East war are suppressing spending and production; the Reserve Bank has raised rates 0.75 percent this year, costing the average mortgage holder about $4,000 annually. Roughly 350,000 Australians now seek monthly food assistance. Head links the deterioration directly to the Albanese Labor government’s backing of US-Israeli operations against Iran.

New Zealand budget attacks workers and students, boosts the military — Tom Peters

New Zealand’s 2026 budget cuts 8,700 government jobs (14 percent of the public sector) over three years, eliminates the third-year fee-free tertiary policy (potentially adding $12,000 to degree costs), raises public housing rent from 25 to 30 percent of income, and cuts youth welfare despite 15 percent youth unemployment — while allocating $1.6 billion for naval upgrades and drones as part of a plan to double military spending to 2 percent of GDP. Prime Minister Christopher Luxon framed the international situation as “similar” to the two World Wars. Peters notes Labour leader Chris Hipkins offered no commitment to reverse the cuts.

Jailed 37 days for a Charlie Kirk meme — Jane Wise

Larry Bushart, 61, a retired law-enforcement officer, won an $835,000 settlement from Perry County, Tennessee after being jailed 37 days on felony “threatening mass violence” charges for sharing a Facebook meme. The arresting officer admitted he had “really no idea” what the post meant, and the sheriff conceded charges would have been dropped had Bushart simply deleted it. Wise contrasts the case with Trump’s $1.776 billion “Anti-Weaponization Fund,” which compensates January 6 defendants and Republican operatives rather than genuine victims of state persecution — exposing the fund as a partisan slush fund while protected speech is criminalized.

Virginia Governor Spanberger vetoes collective bargaining and immigrant protection bills — Ed Hightower

Governor Abigail Spanberger vetoed HB 1263/SB 378, which would have extended collective bargaining rights to over 500,000 public-sector workers, on May 14; six days later she vetoed HB 650/SB 351, which would have required judicial warrants before federal agents could arrest immigrants at courthouses, schools, health facilities, and polling places. Hightower frames Spanberger — a former CIA operative elected amid a wave of “CIA Democrat” recruitment — as a representative of corporate and national-security interests who wields veto power against even modest protections, noting the Washington Post ran three editorials against the bargaining bill.

West Lafayette fast-tracks SK Hynix memory chip factory — George Kirby, Kevin Reed

Tippecanoe County officials are accelerating infrastructure for SK Hynix’s $4 billion AI memory-chip facility despite a resident lawsuit challenging the rezoning. Residents cite undisclosed end-use information, property-value impacts, and proximity to toxic semiconductor chemicals linked to cancer and reproductive harm. The plant will receive $2 billion in federal CHIPS Act funds plus ~$700 million in state incentives and will consume nearly 4 million gallons of water daily — about 1.5 times current area use. The authors link the project, through the Purdue Research Foundation, to the military-industrial-academic complex.

Texas cuts education jobs, shutters dozens of schools — Chase Lawrence

At least 34 Texas campuses face closure or consolidation through 2029, with over 800 positions eliminated across Judson, Fort Worth, Crystal City, and Austin districts — driven by flat per-pupil funding since 2019, a 75,000-student enrollment decline, and $1 billion siphoned to the Education Savings Account voucher program. Lawrence contrasts these cuts with a $250 million state grant to Samsung and over $1 billion in data-center sales-tax exemptions, arguing the defunding of public education is a deliberate political choice. Federal cuts threaten a further $1.76 billion.

Another gruesome week on America’s death row — Kate Randall

Two executions proceeded the week of May 20–21 — Leroy McGill in Arizona and Richard Knight in Florida — while Tennessee’s attempted execution of Tony Carruthers was halted when personnel failed to establish IV access, the seventh such failure in the modern era. The US execution count since 1976 now stands at 1,668; 2025’s 47 executions were the highest in over 16 years. Randall emphasizes the shared pattern: defendants from severe poverty, untreated mental illness and trauma never presented to juries, and contested execution methods. Carruthers, forced to represent himself at trial, received a one-year reprieve; his attorneys allege untested DNA evidence pointing to possible innocence.

This week in history: May 25–31

The column marks four anniversaries. 25 years ago: the May 2001 federal conviction of four men for the 1998 US embassy bombings in Nairobi and Dar es Salaam, with confessions obtained during incommunicado detention and evidence drawn from warrantless wiretaps — a prosecution asserting US imperialism’s claim to global juridical reach. 50 years ago: Syria’s May 31, 1976 military intervention in Lebanon’s civil war, in which Assad deployed 40,000 troops not to protect Palestinians but to crush the PLO-Lebanese National Movement coalition, enabling the August 1976 Tel al-Zaatar massacre of some 2,000 Palestinian refugees. 75 years ago: Britain’s May 1951 ICJ appeal against Iran’s nationalization of the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company under Mossadegh — a failed legal maneuver that preceded the 1953 CIA/MI6 coup. 100 years ago, today’s date: the May 25, 1926 assassination in Paris of Ukrainian People’s Republic leader Symon Petliura — whose forces killed between 30,000 and 50,000 Jews — by Samuel Schwarzbard, a Jewish anarchist acquitted in 1927 on self-defense grounds. The column notes Ukraine has since officially rehabilitated Petliura as a national hero.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — channels anti-war and anti-corporate sentiment into demands for Congressional oversight and DSA electoral campaigns, leaving the capitalist state intact
  • Left Voice — opposes the imperialist assault on Iran but counterposes abstract “self-organization” to the fight for a revolutionary international party
  • PSL / Liberation News — campist “hands off” defense of bourgeois-nationalist and Stalinist states subordinates the working class to the “anti-imperialist” camp
  • Socialist Worker (SWP) — dissolves class politics into identity-based movementism and pressure on the Labour left
  • Communist (RCP/IMT) — pairs uncritical defense of Cuba with electoral and propagandist orientations under a “revolutionary communist” brand

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s lead piece, “Congress Must Investigate War Profiteers Once Again,” responds to the Iran war by demanding renewed Congressional scrutiny of defense contractors. The framing treats militarism as a problem of corporate “profiteering” to be checked by the institutions of the capitalist state, rather than as an organic expression of imperialism — and thereby fosters illusions that the very Congress that funds the wars can be made to police them. “Is Ann Arbor Ready for Democratic Socialism?” profiles a DSA-backed local candidate, reaffirming the magazine’s orientation to electoral work conducted on the ballot line of the Democratic Party. “Is British Steel the Next Green Betrayal?” usefully reports on industrial policy but frames the question as one of better state management rather than workers’ control. The day’s output reflects Jacobin’s consistent project: redirecting radicalized sentiment back into the Democratic Party and the administrative apparatus of capitalism.

Left Voice

Left Voice continues its coverage of the Iran war with analyses of the Strait of Hormuz confrontation and “Between Social Unrest and the Threat of Imperialist Military Attack: Who Will Decide Iran’s Future?” The publication correctly identifies the US blockade as imperialist coercion and opposes military attack on Iran, and it points to the December protests against the Iranian regime. Its weakness is programmatic: the PTS counterposes the “self-organization” of the masses in the abstract to the central task of building a section of a world party of socialist revolution, tailing spontaneous movements rather than fighting for the political independence of the working class through the reconstruction of the Fourth International. The orientation remains centrist — revolutionary in phrase, adaptive in practice.

PSL / Liberation News

Liberation News maintains its “hands off” framing of the conflicts with Iran and Cuba. The PSL’s defense of oppressed nations against imperialist attack is formally correct but is conducted on a campist basis: it presents the bourgeois-nationalist regime in Tehran and the Stalinist bureaucracy in Havana as “anti-imperialist” forces to be politically supported, rather than insisting on the independent mobilization of the Iranian and Cuban working classes against both imperialism and their own ruling elites. This subordination of the working class to a “progressive” camp of bourgeois states is the hallmark of the politics the PSL inherits from Stalinism.

Socialist Worker (SWP)

The British SWP’s front page leads with trans rights (“Build resistance in workplaces as government ramps up attacks on trans+ rights”) and the Nottingham university jobs fight, alongside attacks on Labour’s Andy Burnham over migrants. The strike and anti-austerity coverage is the paper’s stronger side, but its overall method dissolves the class struggle into a coalition of identity-based “resistance” campaigns and pressure on the Labour left. The SWP offers no perspective for breaking the working class from the Labour Party and the trade union bureaucracy, instead orienting workers back toward the very apparatuses presiding over the attacks.

Communist (RCP / IMT)

The IMT’s Communist site pairs “US indictment of Raúl Castro — we say: US imperialism, hands off Cuba!” with electoral commentary (“The Green Party’s foreign policy”) and reporting on Farage and the Nakba march. The “hands off Cuba” line again rests on an uncritical defense of the Stalinist bureaucracy rather than a call for political revolution by the Cuban working class. Combined with the IMT’s recent “Revolutionary Communist Party” rebranding and recruitment-driven propagandism, the output reflects a tendency that substitutes radical labels and moral denunciation for a worked-out revolutionary program and an analysis of the betrayals of Stalinism.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Ebola in the DRC: healthcare collapse as the product of conflict capitalism

The WHO’s PHEIC declaration covers a Bundibugyo-strain outbreak with no approved vaccine or treatment, spreading through a region wracked by three decades of war driven by competition over cobalt, coltan, and gold. The absence of any therapy reflects pharmaceutical companies’ refusal to invest in diseases of the poor. A socialist analysis should connect the outbreak to the political economy of the Congo and the imperialist plunder behind its collapsed health system.

Recommended reading WHO Ebola in DRC and Uganda determined a PHEIC

2. Sudan’s famine-by-design: who arms the warring factions?

While Western governments cite funding shortfalls, the UAE has been documented arming the RSF while the Sudanese Armed Forces receive weapons from Egypt and Russia. The famine afflicting 19.5 million people is not a natural disaster but the product of armed actors deliberately blocking aid. A socialist account should expose the geopolitical and class interests driving the war and name the powers fueling it.

Recommended reading UN News Sudan hunger crisis deepens as famine spreads

3. Air defenses over Mecca: the Iran war reaches the holy cities

For the first time, Saudi Arabia has deployed advanced air-defense batteries over Mecca as 1.6 million pilgrims gather for the Hajj — an extraordinary militarization of the pilgrimage that dramatizes how far the US-Iran war has destabilized the entire region. The WSWS should examine the geopolitical fault lines exposed by this step and the dangers of a wider conflagration.

Recommended reading NPR Muslims begin the annual Hajj

4. Avian influenza’s widening spillover and the bankruptcy of pandemic preparedness

The first-ever documented human H5N5 infection, a fatal pediatric H5N1 case in Bangladesh, and 13 zoonotic flu cases in a single quarter underscore an accelerating threat even as public-health agencies are being defunded. The WSWS should connect the spillover events to factory farming, the gutting of surveillance capacity, and the profit-driven neglect of pandemic preparedness.

Recommended reading NEJM First documented human infection with avian influenza A(H5N5)

5. Cannes 2026: Mungiu’s “Fjord” and the persistence of social realism

Cristian Mungiu’s second Palme d’Or and Andrei Zvyagintsev’s Grand Prix represent a current in world cinema that confronts authoritarianism, social decay, and historical memory against the pressures of nationalism and the commercial entertainment industry. A WSWS review could assess these works in their social and historical context and defend serious art against the degradation of cultural life.

Recommended reading Variety Cristian Mungiu wins his second Palme d’Or for ‘Fjord’

6. GLP-1 drug safety: profit, post-market surveillance, and the “eye stroke” signal

A new analysis links semaglutide (Wegovy/Ozempic) to the highest GLP-1 risk for a rare cause of sudden vision loss, as these drugs reach tens of millions of users. The WSWS should examine how the rush to market blockbuster drugs, the weakness of post-market surveillance, and the profit motive in pharmaceuticals expose patients to inadequately studied risks.
