1. B-2 bombers devastate Iranian missile caves; House votes down war powers resolution 219-212

Day 7 of Operation Epic Fury marked a significant escalation in US firepower as B-2 Spirit stealth bombers from Whiteman Air Force Base dropped GBU-31 2,000-pound penetrating munitions on deeply buried Iranian ballistic missile launchers. Admiral Brad Cooper of CENTCOM reported that Iran’s ballistic missile attacks are down 90% and drone attacks down 83% compared to Day 1. Israel claims 2,500 strikes carried out and 80% of Iran’s air defense systems destroyed, asserting “near-complete air superiority.”

The death toll in Iran has surpassed 1,230 killed, with approximately 200 targets struck in the past 72 hours. Defense Secretary Hegseth declared “we are accelerating, not decelerating,” while Trump boasted that Iran has been “demolished” and has “no air force, no air defense.” Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi responded that there is “no reason why we should negotiate with the US.” The IRGC announced a combined drone-missile attack on Tel Aviv. Over 20,000 Americans have been evacuated from the Middle East.

The House voted 219-212 against a war powers resolution that would have required the president to withdraw forces within 30 days. Four Democrats broke ranks to vote against the resolution (Cuellar, Golden, Landsman, Vargas), while only two Republicans voted for it (Massie, Davidson). The Senate had voted 47-53 the previous day. As Patrick Martin noted on the WSWS, the Democrats’ opposition is hollow given that they voted for the military budgets enabling the war, and Senator Fetterman was the sole Democrat to vote against in the Senate.

The CSIS estimates the war has cost $3.7 billion in its first 100 hours, averaging $891 million per day, with $3.5 billion unbudgeted. This pace of spending, if sustained, would exceed the Iraq War’s most expensive periods.

2. Iran war engulfs the region: Bahrain refinery hit, Iraq blackout enters fourth day, 300,000 displaced in Lebanon

The war has spread far beyond Iran’s borders into a multi-front regional conflagration spanning 13 countries.

Gulf states under fire. An Iranian ballistic missile struck Bahrain’s primary BAPCO oil refinery on the island of Sitra (processing capacity of 380,000-400,000 barrels/day), triggering large fires that were subsequently contained with no casualties reported. A hotel and two residential buildings in Bahrain were also hit. The UAE intercepted multiple missiles and more than 120 drones in a single barrage — an unprecedented scale of asymmetric warfare. Qatar and Saudi Arabia intercepted additional drone and missile attacks. Kuwait’s US embassy has suspended operations.

Iraq’s humanitarian catastrophe. Iraq’s power grid remains down nationwide for a fourth consecutive day, leaving 43 million people without electricity. The blackout was triggered by a sudden drop in gas supplies to the Rumaila gas power plant in Basra province; Iraq relies on Iran for 40% of its gas imports. The US Embassy in Baghdad issued an urgent directive for Americans to leave the country. Hospitals, water treatment facilities, and food storage systems are operating on backup generators where available, but the extended duration of the outage is creating a humanitarian crisis that has received almost no dedicated international coverage.

Lebanon’s mass displacement. Israel has issued evacuation orders covering Beirut’s southern suburbs (Dahiyeh) for the first time — an area home to approximately 400,000 people. This is the largest Israeli evacuation order targeting a civilian population center in Lebanon’s history. More than 300,000 Lebanese have been displaced since Monday, including 38,000 who have fled to Syria. The Lebanese Health Ministry reported 102 killed and 638 wounded since Monday. Israel’s Finance Minister Smotrich threatened further strikes on Beirut’s suburbs. Human Rights Watch warned the evacuation orders “raise serious risks of violations of the laws of war.”

5. Kennedy drops broad recommendations for six childhood vaccines as measles surges past 1,100 US cases

Health Secretary RFK Jr.’s overhaul of the childhood vaccine schedule, which removed universal recommendations for vaccines against rotavirus, RSV, meningococcal disease, hepatitis A, hepatitis B, influenza, and COVID-19 (cutting the schedule from 17 diseases to 11), is compounding an accelerating measles crisis. The policy was imposed by secretarial decree in January 2026 without scientific review or a vote by the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices, whose 17 members Kennedy had dismissed in June 2025 and replaced with vaccine skeptics.

The South Carolina outbreak centered on Spartanburg County has reached 990 cases, the largest measles outbreak in the US since 1992. Total US cases in 2026 have surpassed 1,136, already nearing half of 2025’s full-year total by early March. Ninety-four percent of patients were unvaccinated or of unknown vaccination status. The CDC is belatedly deploying a team to South Carolina for the first time, five months after the outbreak began in October 2025.

The Pan American Health Organization (PAHO) will review the US measles elimination status in April, and the country is at serious risk of losing the designation it earned in 2000. The American Academy of Pediatrics and allied medical organizations have a federal lawsuit pending, arguing the vaccine schedule changes bypassed established scientific processes and pose a grave public health threat.

6. Germany: tens of thousands of students strike against conscription

Tens of thousands of students across Germany walked out of schools on March 5 in a nationwide “school strike against conscription,” protesting the Military Service Modernization Act that took effect January 1, 2026. Approximately 3,000 students gathered at Berlin’s Potsdamer Platz, with rallies held in dozens of cities across the country. Organizers deliberately scheduled protests during school hours to maximize impact.

The law, passed by the Bundestag on December 5, 2025, requires males born in 2008 to fill out mandatory online military questionnaires this year or face fines. While the questionnaires are currently voluntary for women, the law establishes the legal infrastructure for full conscription. Student organizer Leon Reinemann told NTV: “Reintroducing conscription is nothing other than rearmament.” The strikes follow a larger wave of protests in December 2025 that brought tens of thousands onto the streets.

The IYSSE (International Youth and Students for Social Equality) intervened in the protests with a socialist antiwar perspective, connecting opposition to conscription with the need to oppose the imperialist war on Iran. The IYSSE statement calls for emergency campus meetings and argues that opposition to conscription must be based on an independent working-class program, not pacifist appeals to a bourgeois state that is itself driving the rearmament.

Australia and Canada endorse Iran war while claiming to seek “de-escalation”

Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney and Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese, meeting in Canberra on Thursday, both endorsed the US-Israeli war on Iran while calling for “broader de-escalation.” Carney stated he backed the strikes “with some regret” as they represented “an extreme example of a rupturing world order,” but critically refused to rule out Canadian military participation, saying “one can never categorically rule out participation” and “we will stand by our allies.” He acknowledged that the actions are “prima facie… inconsistent with international law” — a remarkable admission that has produced no practical consequence.

The posture of both governments exposes the fraud of “independent foreign policy” claims. As Oscar Grenfell noted on the WSWS, both align with the imperialist war while managing domestic public opinion with rhetorical appeals to de-escalation and hand-wringing about international law.

Tennessee bill to track immigrant students’ status advances amid protests

Tennessee House Bill 793, sponsored by House Majority Leader William Lamberth (R-Portland) with Senator Bo Watson (R-Hixson) as co-sponsor, advanced from subcommittee Wednesday amid large protests and Democratic opposition. The bill would require public schools to collect and report the immigration status of enrolling students to the Tennessee Department of Education. While the current version collects data in aggregate without identifying information, Democrats warned the data could ultimately be shared with federal immigration enforcement agencies.

Educators argued the bill would cost an estimated $55 million to implement (unfunded by the state), deter students from attending school, and create a chilling effect in immigrant communities. The original version, which stalled last year amid concerns it could jeopardize more than $1.1 billion in federal education funding, has been revised but retains the core tracking provision. The bill represents a direct challenge to Plyler v. Doe, the 1982 Supreme Court ruling guaranteeing public education regardless of immigration status — a ruling the current Supreme Court majority may be willing to revisit.

Measles: the epidemiological picture

Beyond the political dimensions covered above, the measles outbreak has significant epidemiological implications. The 990 cases in Spartanburg County represent sustained community transmission — not a contained cluster — indicating that vaccination coverage has fallen below the herd immunity threshold of approximately 95% in that region. The outbreak’s five-month duration without effective containment suggests that the public health infrastructure in South Carolina, already weakened by years of underfunding, has been overwhelmed.

The national picture is equally alarming. At 1,136+ cases by early March, the 2026 trajectory would produce more than 4,500 cases for the year if the current rate continues — far exceeding the 2019 peak of 1,274 that nearly cost the US its elimination status. The PAHO review in April will assess whether sustained endemic transmission has resumed, which would reverse the elimination designation the US has held since 2000.

The broader context is the degradation of the US childhood vaccination infrastructure. Kennedy’s dismissal of all 17 ACIP members, the imposition of a reduced vaccine schedule by executive fiat, and the CDC’s institutional paralysis under hostile political leadership have created conditions for the return of preventable diseases that the US had largely eliminated. The measles crisis is a leading indicator of what is coming for hepatitis, rotavirus, and other diseases whose vaccine recommendations were stripped.

Landmark study reveals tropical insects far less resilient to warming than assumed

A sweeping new study of more than 2,000 insect species, published in Nature, reveals that many insects may be far less capable of coping with rising temperatures than previously assumed. The survey of tropical insect populations and thermal tolerance limits indicates that species from lowland areas have particularly low capacity to survive increased temperatures, with thermal tolerance constrained by fundamental properties of protein architecture — suggesting a hard biological limit rather than a gap that evolution can bridge on relevant timescales.

The findings have major implications for biodiversity projections. Tropical lowlands host the majority of the planet’s insect species and are the engine of terrestrial ecosystem function. If lowland species cannot survive even modest warming, the cascading effects on pollination, decomposition, and food webs could be catastrophic. The study adds to growing evidence that climate change projections have been systematically underestimating biological impacts.

Arts and culture

The war on Iran dominates the cultural landscape this week, as the arts world grapples with the largest US military operation since the 2003 invasion of Iraq. The most significant cultural developments center on labor struggles within the entertainment industry, the politicization of cultural institutions, and the plight of Iranian artists caught between a repressive regime and imperialist bombardment.

WGA staff walkout continues; LA awards ceremony canceled

The Writers Guild of America staff walkout, now entering its third week, has forced the cancellation of the WGA West’s Los Angeles awards ceremony originally scheduled for March 8. The 115 staff members of the Pacific Northwest Staff Union are demanding fair wages and just-cause protections in the employee discipline process amid rising living costs and significant pay disparities. Dozens of WGA member captains joined the picket line outside WGA West headquarters in Los Angeles in a show of solidarity.

The timing is significant: the writers guild is set to enter negotiations with the Alliance of Motion Picture and Television Producers (AMPTP) on March 16, the first round of talks between above-the-line unions and studios since the contentious 2023 bargaining cycle that produced historically long dual writers’ and actors’ strikes. The staff walkout — a labor struggle within a labor organization — reflects the broader crisis in an industry that has undergone massive consolidation and cost-cutting while executive compensation continues to soar. The East Coast ceremony will proceed as scheduled.

The contradiction is revealing: the guild exists to protect writers’ labor rights, yet its own employees must strike for basic protections. This mirrors the broader pattern across organized labor, where union bureaucracies preside over declining wages and conditions for workers in their own organizations while negotiating concessionary contracts with management.

Iranian artists caught between repression and bombardment

A feature in The National documented how Iranian artists, already navigating decades of censorship, imprisonment, and exile, now face an entirely new crisis as their country is bombed by the US and Israel. Iranian cinema has been one of the most influential artistic movements to emerge from the Middle East in the past half century, with films regularly premiering at Cannes, Berlin, and Venice. Directors like the late Abbas Kiarostami, Asghar Farhadi, and Jafar Panahi (who was imprisoned for his art) established a tradition of deeply humanist filmmaking that has influenced global cinema.

Now these artists are doubly imperiled — targets of the Islamic Republic’s repression and of American bombs. The cultural sector had increasingly aligned with the 2025-2026 protest movement against the regime, making artists politically suspect to both sides. A companion piece in The National surveys Iranian art as resistance: films, books, and podcasts that challenge the regime’s repression, documenting a cultural tradition that refuses to be silenced by either authoritarian censorship or imperialist violence. Artists are working to sustain cross-cultural dialogue even as military escalation threatens to sever those connections entirely.

The situation of Iranian artists crystallizes a broader truth: the working class and the intellectuals of oppressed nations suffer at the hands of both their own ruling classes and the imperialist powers.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s war coverage continues to frame opposition to the Iran war within the parameters acceptable to the Democratic Party. Three articles from the past 24 hours are notable:

“US, Israel Target Civilians as Iran Escalates War to Survive” frames Iran’s retaliatory strikes as “escalation to survive” — presenting the war as a conflict between states without class analysis of either the Iranian regime or the working class of the region. The framing implicitly accepts the legitimacy of US strategic interests while criticizing the methods.

“Trump Will Not March Quickly to Victory Over Iran” provides strategic assessment implying the war could be prosecuted more effectively. The criticism is from the standpoint of US imperial management — the war is bad because it may fail, not because it is a criminal assault on an oppressed nation. This converges directly with the bourgeois press line from the liberal establishment (New York Times, Washington Post editorial boards) that has criticized the war’s execution while accepting its premises.

“AIPAC Is Influencing Trump’s War in Iran” focuses on the Israel lobby’s influence on Democrats, channeling antiwar sentiment into Democratic Party reform. This represents the DSA’s fundamental orientation: identifying AIPAC rather than the capitalist system and its two parties as the obstacle to peace. The article promotes the illusion that a reformed Democratic Party could be an instrument of antiwar politics.

The overall orientation: Jacobin seeks to contain the emerging antiwar movement within the Democratic Party’s orbit, offering strategic criticism that never challenges the fundamental legitimacy of American imperial power. Not a single article calls for independent working-class action against the war.

Left Voice

The gap between the correct formal position (defend Iran against imperialism, no political support for the theocracy) and the absence of a working-class strategy to implement it is characteristic of Left Voice’s approach.
