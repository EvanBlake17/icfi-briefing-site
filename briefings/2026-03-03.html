1. Day 4 of US-Israel war on Iran: US Embassy in Riyadh hit, ground troops “not ruled out”

The US-Israeli military assault on Iran entered its fourth day with significant escalation on multiple fronts. CENTCOM disclosed that over 1,250 targets have been struck across Iran in the first 48 hours of Operation Epic Fury, hitting 24 of Iran’s 31 provinces. The death toll inside Iran has risen past 600, including an estimated 180 children killed in the bombing of a girls’ school in Minab.

An Iranian missile struck the US Embassy compound in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, drawing the Gulf kingdom directly into the conflict. Six US service members have been killed and 18 seriously wounded since operations began. In an interview with the New York Times, Trump said he expects “quite a bit higher” US casualties and that the operation could take “four to five weeks.” Pentagon spokesperson asked about ground forces: “The option of ground forces has not been taken off the table.”

As the WSWS Perspective argued, Operation Epic Fury represents “the latest chapter in a continuous 35-year trajectory of imperialist intervention” in the Middle East, driven by control of energy supplies and strategic chokepoints. The article noted critical differences from the 2003 Iraq model: targeted assassination combined with massive bombardment, hybrid warfare integrating “precision strikes, cyberwar and economic siege,” and the absence of congressional authorization reflecting “growing resort to executive unilateralism.”

2. IAEA warns of “Chernobyl-scale” evacuations as Iran nuclear regulatory goes silent

The IAEA Board of Governors held its second emergency session since the war began, with Director General Rafael Grossi delivering an alarming assessment. While there is currently “no indication” that nuclear installations have been directly hit or damaged, and radiation readings in neighboring countries remain at normal background levels, Grossi warned that continued strikes create the risk of a radiological release that “cannot be excluded.” He said evacuations could cover areas “as large or larger than major cities” — a reference to Chernobyl-scale displacement.

Iran’s nuclear regulatory authorities have been unreachable since the strikes began, leaving the IAEA unable to verify conditions at major facilities. The IAEA Incident and Emergency Centre has been activated. Iran’s ambassador to the IAEA, Mohsen Naziri Asl, stated that the Natanz enrichment facility was struck and characterized attacks on safeguarded nuclear facilities as war crimes under international law.

Key facts: - Bushehr nuclear power plant, Tehran Research Reactor, and fuel storage facilities are all within potential strike range - If uranium hexafluoride (UF6) is released, it produces hydrofluoric acid — a highly toxic substance requiring mass evacuation - IAEA Emergency Centre activated; Iran regulatory contacts unreachable - Iran ambassador: attacks on safeguarded facilities constitute war crimes - No confirmed radiation increase detected so far in neighboring countries

Nuclear safety crisis: the technical dimension

Beyond the diplomatic implications covered above, the nuclear safety crisis has a technical dimension that warrants closer attention. Iran operates multiple nuclear facilities that process or store hazardous materials: the Natanz enrichment plant (confirmed struck by Iran’s IAEA ambassador), the Bushehr nuclear power plant on the Persian Gulf coast, the Tehran Research Reactor in the capital, and several uranium fuel storage sites.

The critical substance at risk is uranium hexafluoride (UF6), used in the enrichment process. If released by an explosion or fire, UF6 reacts with moisture in the air to produce hydrofluoric acid — an extremely corrosive and toxic compound that can cause severe burns, organ damage, and death at relatively low concentrations. A significant release at Natanz would require evacuation of surrounding population centers. At Bushehr, a reactor breach would release fission products with contamination patterns depending on wind and weather — analogous to the scenarios modeled after Fukushima.

The silence from Iran’s nuclear regulatory authority is itself a major concern. It could indicate communications infrastructure damage, personnel casualties, deliberate information suppression, or some combination. Without independent monitoring, the international community is effectively blind to conditions at these facilities.

US measles cases reach 1,136 through February — highest since 1994

The CDC reported 1,136 confirmed measles cases in the United States through February 26, 2026 — already half the total for all of 2025 (2,281 cases) and the highest year-to-date figure since 1994. Cases have been reported across 28 jurisdictions. South Carolina remains the epicenter with 632 confirmed cases, followed by Utah (117), Florida (64), Arizona (36), and Washington state (24).

Ninety percent of confirmed cases (1,023 of 1,136) are outbreak-associated, concentrated in under-vaccinated communities. Robert F. Kennedy Jr., now HHS Secretary, has not implemented mandatory reporting requirements despite the escalating outbreak. No measles deaths have been reported in 2026, but the trajectory suggests the US may exceed 2025’s full-year total before summer.

Golestan Palace damaged: UNESCO sounds alarm on cultural heritage in Middle East

Tehran’s Golestan Palace, the city’s only UNESCO World Heritage Site and a masterpiece of Qajar-era architecture spanning the 16th through 19th centuries, was damaged on March 1 when a US-Israeli airstrike hit the Arag Square area within the site’s buffer zone. The palace’s windows, doors, and ornate mirrors were destroyed by debris and shockwave. Iran’s minister of cultural heritage, Reza Salehi-Amiri, visited the site on March 2 and described the damage as “a clear violation of international rules and cultural commitments,” pledging to submit a formal restoration report to UNESCO.

UNESCO issued a broader statement expressing concern over the protection of cultural heritage sites across the Middle East. Iran is home to 29 UNESCO World Heritage Sites and more than 800 museums, representing thousands of years of Persian civilization. The destruction recalls the pattern of cultural devastation in previous imperialist wars — the looting of the Iraq National Museum in 2003, the destruction of Palmyra by ISIS, the bombing of Yemen’s Old City of Sana’a.

The 1954 Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict, ratified by 133 states, explicitly prohibits attacks on cultural heritage. The US ratified it in 2009. The cultural destruction adds another dimension to the war crimes case already being built around the targeting of nuclear facilities and civilian infrastructure.

Iranian artists under siege between regime repression and imperialist bombardment

Iranian artists face a dual threat: ongoing regime repression and imperialist bombardment. The Iranian Independent Filmmakers Association (IIFMA) reported at the Berlin Film Festival in February that over 50 filmmakers and artists have been transferred to detention sites located near strategic infrastructure and military bases, placing them at direct risk of harm from strikes. Other artists are participating in cultural resistance through encrypted networks, circulating anonymous visual work and music.

The situation illuminates a recurring pattern in imperialist wars: the creative intellectuals who document and resist the domestic regime become collateral damage of the foreign intervention that claims to liberate them. Iranian cinema — from Abbas Kiarostami to Asghar Farhadi to Jafar Panahi — has been among the world’s most artistically significant national cinemas precisely because it grappled honestly with social contradictions the regime sought to suppress.

Whether the biennial’s largely academic and identity-based framing can rise to the moment of a shooting war remains to be seen.

This week in history: March 2-8

March 2, 1919 — Founding of the Communist International (Comintern). The First Congress of the Communist International opened in Moscow, attended by 52 delegates from 34 parties and organizations across 22 countries. The founding represented the organizational culmination of Lenin and the Bolsheviks’ struggle against the betrayal of the Second International, which had collapsed into national chauvinism at the outbreak of World War I in 1914. The Comintern’s founding manifesto, drafted by Trotsky, declared: “We who have assembled in Moscow, Communists, representatives of the revolutionary proletariat of Europe, America and Asia, feel and consider ourselves the followers and fulfillers of the programme whose banner was unfurled 72 years ago.” The congress established the organizational framework for world revolution — a framework that would later be destroyed by the Stalinist bureaucracy’s nationalist degeneration.

March 5, 1953 — Death of Joseph Stalin. Stalin’s death at age 74 triggered a succession crisis in the Soviet bureaucracy and opened a period of political instability that culminated in Khrushchev’s “secret speech” three years later. The ICFI has analyzed Stalin’s rule as the political expression of the bureaucratic caste that usurped power from the Soviet working class, betraying the program of world socialist revolution in favor of the nationalist doctrine of “socialism in one country.”

March 7, 1965 — “Bloody Sunday” in Selma, Alabama. Alabama state troopers attacked 600 civil rights marchers attempting to cross the Edmund Pettus Bridge. The televised brutality — troopers on horseback beating unarmed marchers with clubs and firing tear gas — galvanized national opposition and accelerated passage of the Voting Rights Act of 1965. The event demonstrated both the courage of the civil rights movement and the violent resistance of the Southern ruling class to even minimal democratic reform.

March 8, 1917 — International Women’s Day triggers February Revolution. Women textile workers in Petrograd walked off the job on International Women’s Day, demanding bread and an end to the war. Within five days, the strike had spread to 200,000 workers and the tsarist autocracy — which had ruled Russia for over 300 years — was overthrown. The February Revolution confirmed the Marxist understanding that wars create revolutionary crises, as the mass suffering inflicted by World War I found expression in working-class action that no amount of repression could contain.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — focuses on war powers constitutionality and public opinion; frames opposition through Democratic Party congressional mechanisms; DSA Councilmember Mamdani collaborated directly with the Trump administration
  • Left Voice — published international declaration calling for “defeat” of US and Israel; militant rhetoric but channels opposition through street protest and union bureaucracies rather than independent revolutionary party
  • PSL / Liberation News — organizes ANSWER Coalition protests in 33+ cities; characterizes war as “war for empire” but proposes no program beyond street demonstration and congressional pressure
  • Socialist Alternative / CWI — calls for working-class mass struggle and Iranian workers’ revolution; avoids confrontation with union bureaucracies and does not call for a revolutionary party in opposition to pseudo-left organizations
  • SWP UK — condemns US-Israeli “murderous assault”; criticizes Starmer for enabling strikes from British bases; orients toward building “mass movement” within reformist framework

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s Iran war coverage is dominated by two analytical frames: the war’s constitutionality and its unpopularity. Ben Burgis’s Trump’s War on Iran Makes a Mockery of American Democracy argues that only 21% support the war and Trump launched it without congressional approval, but proposes no solution beyond implied congressional action — orienting opposition entirely through the Democratic Party’s institutional channels. A second piece, The Supreme Court May Legalize Donald Trump’s War on Iran, warns that conservative justices could use the war to strike down the War Powers Resolution, expanding presidential war-making power. The article identifies real dangers but its framework assumes the Constitution and courts are the terrain on which wars are stopped — a fundamentally reformist orientation.

The DSA’s most notable action was Councilmember Shahid Mamdani’s meeting with the Trump administration to secure a student’s release. This direct collaboration with a government waging an unauthorized war — while the DSA nominally opposes that war — illustrates the organization’s thoroughgoing integration into bourgeois politics. The DSA participated in anti-war protests but its political perspective remains bound to pressuring Democratic legislators.

Sources Jacobin Trump’s war on Iran makes a mockery of American democracy · Jacobin The Supreme Court may legalize Donald Trump’s war on Iran

Left Voice

Left Voice published an international declaration on March 2 titled For the Defeat of the United States and Israel! which characterizes the war as “imperialist aggression” and calls for the “strongest repudiation in the streets by the working class and young people.” The declaration demands trade unions take action to block war logistics — a formally correct demand — but Left Voice’s Trotskyist Fraction (FT) has no perspective for actually challenging the trade union bureaucracies that would need to be overthrown to implement such action. The declaration’s militant anti-imperialist rhetoric coexists with an organizational practice of adapting to existing protest movements and their petty-bourgeois leaderships rather than fighting for an independent revolutionary party of the working class.

Left Voice’s broader Iran coverage frames the conflict as an opportunity to build “the anti-war movement” without specifying the class character or political program such a movement requires, defaulting to the protest-movement politics that leave the fundamental questions of state power and capitalist war-making unaddressed.

Sources Left Voice International declaration: For the defeat of the United States and Israel!

PSL / Liberation News

The PSL’s statement Stop Trump’s War on Iran anchors the ANSWER Coalition’s national day of action in 33+ US cities. The statement correctly identifies the war as illegal under both the US Constitution and international law and characterizes it as “a war for empire.” However, the PSL’s program amounts to street protest plus congressional pressure: demanding Congress “halt this illegal war of aggression.” There is no call to break with the Democratic Party, no program for independent working-class political action, and no analysis of the capitalist system as the root cause of imperialist war. The PSL channels genuine anti-war sentiment into a protest-movement framework that poses no threat to the political establishment.

Sources Liberation News PSL statement: Stop Trump’s war on Iran!

Socialist Alternative / CWI

Socialist Alternative’s Trump & Netanyahu’s War On Iran calls for “the working class must step in and build mass struggle to end the bloodshed” and states “the revolutionary overthrow of the dictatorship is the task of the Iranian multi-ethnic working class.” The CWI’s international site (socialistworld.net) published a companion piece arguing for “a workers’ government prepared to fight a revolutionary war if necessary, while at the same time starting a socialist transformation within Iran.” The rhetoric is more left than Jacobin or the PSL, but the CWI’s characteristic evasion is present: it calls for working-class action without confronting the trade union bureaucracies that prevent such action, and it does not call for building a revolutionary party in explicit opposition to the pseudo-left organizations that politically disarm the working class. The CWI’s approach is to adapt to protest movements while using socialist vocabulary.

Sources Socialist Alternative Trump & Netanyahu’s war on Iran · Socialist World Another war on Iran — Trump moves to consolidate US power

SWP UK

The SWP’s US-Israeli Aggression in Iran Plunges the Region Into Chaos characterizes the strikes as a “murderous assault” driven by “imperialist ambitions.” The article criticizes Starmer’s Labour government for allowing British bases in Cyprus to be used for strikes and for British warplanes participating despite claims of non-involvement. The SWP argues “it is up to Iranians to determine their future, not Western bombs.” Branch meetings are being organized across Britain under the title “Terror in Iran: Resisting imperialist war in the Middle East.”

The SWP’s orientation remains building a “mass movement” that pressures the Labour government — fundamentally a reformist perspective that assumes the existing state and parliamentary framework can be made to serve anti-war ends. There is no call for a revolutionary break with Labour or for independent working-class political organization outside the trade union and Labour structures.

Sources Socialist Worker US-Israeli aggression in Iran plunges the region into chaos

RCI / IMT (Socialist Appeal)

Alan Woods published The War on Iran: Where Do Communists Stand? on communist.red (March 2). The article correctly identifies US imperialism as the primary aggressor and condemns the humanitarian pretext. However, Woods’s treatment of the Iranian regime is characteristic of the IMT’s method: he acknowledges its “crimes” but immediately relativizes them, writing they “shrink into insignificance” compared to US aggression. This formulation evades the independent political task of opposing both imperialism and the theocratic regime from the standpoint of the working class.

The article’s central slogan — “Hands Off Iran — Down with western imperialism” — is entirely defensive. There are no demands for independent working-class action, no program for Iranian workers, no call to break with trade union bureaucracies or bourgeois parties. The IMT’s “revolutionary communist” branding coexists with a political perspective that does not go beyond left anti-imperialism — the very orientation that historically has led to support for bourgeois-nationalist regimes in the name of opposing imperialism.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Strait of Hormuz shipping crisis and global supply chain catastrophe

The complete halt of tanker transits through the Strait of Hormuz is the most immediate threat to working-class living standards globally. Energy price inflation, supply chain disruption, and potential food crisis will follow. Nick Beams’s economic article covers the financial markets dimension, but a dedicated piece on the real-economy impact — how the Hormuz closure translates into price increases at the grocery store, factory shutdowns from petrochemical shortages, and heating costs in Europe — is urgently needed.

Recommended reading CNBC Strait of Hormuz crisis: US-Iran-Israel war, shipping, trade, oil

2. IAEA nuclear safety crisis: radiological release risk from ongoing strikes on Iran

IAEA Director General Grossi has warned of evacuations covering “major city”-scale areas. This is the most serious nuclear security crisis since Chernobyl and demands a dedicated WSWS science/nuclear analysis — examining the technical risks at each facility, the potential contamination scenarios, and the criminal recklessness of the US-Israeli targeting decisions. Not just a mention within broader war coverage, but a standalone piece.

Recommended reading IAEA Director General’s introductory statement to the Board of Governors

3. Class character of Iranian protests versus imperialist regime change

The pre-existing Iranian protest movement (7,000+ killed since December 2025) is now overlapping with US-Israeli bombardment. Monarchist Reza Pahlavi is positioning himself as a US-backed successor. The WSWS must distinguish workers’ legitimate grievances against the theocratic regime from imperialist regime-change operations that would install a pliant bourgeois government. This is a critical political question that has not yet been addressed in a dedicated article.

Recommended reading Britannica 2026 Iranian protests

4. Hezbollah-Israel front: Lebanon under attack

Hezbollah launched its first offensive since the November 2024 ceasefire. Israel is bombing Beirut and has issued evacuation orders for 50+ villages. Naim Qassem has been declared a “marked target.” Lebanon could become a second major theater of the war. No dedicated WSWS article exists on this front — it is currently mentioned only within broader Iran war coverage. The Lebanese working class and the hundreds of thousands of refugees face catastrophic consequences.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera US-Israel attacks on Iran: death toll and injuries live tracker

5. Anti-war movement: class dynamics, state repression, and political perspective

Anti-war protests are taking place in 33+ US cities, with 9 killed in Pakistan and an attempted storming of the US Embassy in Baghdad. Trump warns of “aggressive enforcement.” The WSWS should analyze the class composition of the movement, the political limitations of ANSWER/PSL channeling opposition into protest-ism, and the need for independent working-class political action through rank-and-file workplace committees — distinct from the workers’ voices interviews already published.

Recommended reading ANSWER Coalition Stop Trump’s war on Iran

6. UNESCO and cultural heritage destruction in the Iran war

Golestan Palace (UNESCO World Heritage Site) has been damaged. The Louvre Abu Dhabi is threatened. Over 50 Iranian filmmakers and artists are detained near military targets. The pattern of cultural destruction in imperialist wars is repeating. A WSWS arts/culture analysis would connect this to the historical record of Iraq 2003, Syria, and Yemen — and to the broader relationship between imperialist war and the destruction of civilization.

Recommended reading The Art Newspaper US-Israeli strikes damage UNESCO-listed Golestan Palace, Tehran

7. US Embassy in Riyadh struck: Saudi Arabia drawn into the conflict

An Iranian missile hit the US Embassy compound in Riyadh, the first direct military strike on Saudi Arabian soil by a state actor in the current conflict. The Gulf states are being drawn into a war they sought to avoid. A dedicated analysis of the Gulf states’ position — caught between US alliance obligations and their own economic interests in stability — would address the strategic implications for OPEC, US-Saudi relations, and regional power dynamics.
