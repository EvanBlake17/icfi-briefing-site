1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 18: schoolgirls massacred in Minab, missiles hit Tel Aviv suburbs

The US-Israel bombing campaign against Iran entered its 18th day with no indication of de-escalation. A US airstrike on a primary school in Minab killed more than 170 people, over 160 of them schoolgirls — an atrocity on the scale of the most notorious war crimes of the past two decades. Separately, a three-day-old infant, a two-year-old, their mother and grandmother were killed in an airstrike in Arak. Total Iranian casualties since the campaign began on February 28 have reached at least 1,444 killed and 18,551 injured, according to Al Jazeera.

Israel assassinated Ali Larijani, Iran’s top security official, along with his son Morteza and office director Alireza Bayat. Basij commander Gholamreza Soleimani was also confirmed killed. Iran’s newly elected Supreme Leader, Mojtaba Khamenei — son of the killed Ali Khamenei — issued his first public message vowing to “avenge the blood of our martyrs” and called on neighboring states to close American military bases. Iranian President Pezeshkian declared Iran “will not surrender to bullies,” while Trump dismissed Iran as “a paper tiger.”

Key facts: - The Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant was struck; the UN watchdog reports no radiation damage - Iranian missiles killed 2 people near Tel Aviv (Ramat Gan); Israel struck Beirut, killing at least 6 and wounding 24 - The USS Tripoli carrying the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit is transiting to the Persian Gulf — the first ground combat-capable US force to enter the theater, where roughly 50,000 American service members already operate - Republican lawmakers are openly advocating seizing Kharg Island, through which 90% of Iranian crude exports pass - Iran officially withdrew from the 2026 FIFA World Cup, citing security concerns - Trump’s approval stands at approximately 40%, with 74% of registered voters opposing ground troops

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, despite the assassination of the Supreme Leader and destruction of 7,000+ bombing targets, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with daily transits collapsing from 125 to approximately 5.

2. Strait of Hormuz crisis drives oil above $100 as Iran expands strikes to Gulf states

Iran expanded its retaliatory strikes beyond Israel to target Gulf states that host US military infrastructure. A drone struck near Dubai International Airport, briefly suspending flights; a fire erupted at the Fujairah oil industrial zone; one Palestinian resident was killed in Abu Dhabi by a missile strike. The IMF’s Kristalina Georgieva warned of “prolonged inflationary risk,” and the UN World Food Programme flagged global food security threats from disrupted fertilizer supplies.

6. JBS meatpacking strike enters day 2 in Greeley, Colorado

Some 3,800 meatpacking workers at the JBS plant in Greeley, Colorado entered the second day of their strike, with picket lines stretching nearly half a mile around the facility. Workers are demanding wages rise from $23 to $33/hour after the company offered a 60-cent increase. Between 80 and 90 percent of the workforce are immigrants, with more than 50 languages spoken inside the facility.

Workers describe near-daily injuries — “cut, stabbed, poked” — $100 charges for replacement safety gloves, and Greeley rents of $1,800–$2,000/month. Unmarked vans were reportedly stationed near the strike vote location, and ICE is operating at least nine secret detention sites in Colorado. As the WSWS Perspective by Tom Hall documented, the UFCW bureaucracy has already declared the strike will last “only two weeks” — a pre-planned signal to management that a retreat is being prepared.

Key facts: - JBS is a Brazilian multinational employing 270,000–280,000 workers globally; BlackRock and Vanguard hold significant ownership stakes - The UFCW reached a national JBS agreement covering 26,000 workers last year but deliberately excluded Greeley - JBS has preemptively transferred cattle to its Canyon, Texas plant - At least six workers died during the first year of the pandemic; in 2021 a worker fell into a chemical vat and died

Lung cancer combination therapy extends survival by 10 months

A study published in the New England Journal of Medicine (Vol. 394:27-38) found that in EGFR-mutated non-small-cell lung cancer, first-line osimertinib plus platinum-pemetrexed chemotherapy extended median overall survival to 47.5 months, compared with 37.6 months with osimertinib alone — an approximately 10-month gain. The EGFR-mutated subgroup accounts for roughly 15% of NSCLC in Western populations. The combination carried increased adverse-event risk.

Sources NEJM Original articles, Vol. 394

CAR T-cell therapy achieves complete remission in autoimmune disease

Published in the NEJM (Vol. 394:253-267), a study of 11 patients with multirefractory autoimmune hemolytic anemia found that CD19-targeted CAR T-cell therapy produced a complete response in all patients, with drug-free remission lasting a median of 11.5 months and manageable toxicity. This extends the emerging use of CAR T beyond cancer into severe autoimmune disease, with implications for other refractory autoimmune conditions.

98th Academy Awards: One Battle After Another wins best picture

Paul Thomas Anderson’s One Battle After Another — a political drama loosely adapted from Thomas Pynchon’s Vineland, following a washed-up revolutionary and his daughter against a backdrop of border immigration, white supremacy, and political polarization — won six Oscars including Best Picture, Best Director, and Best Adapted Screenplay. The film opens with an anarchist group raiding a military-controlled ICE-like detention facility, and its victory marks the rare commercial success of a film engaging directly with contemporary American political violence. The ceremony introduced a new Best Casting category, and a rare tie occurred between two live-action shorts: The Singers and Two People Exchanging Saliva. Best Documentary Short went to Mr. Nobody Against Putin, a notable selection given the current geopolitical climate. Autumn Durald Arkapaw became the first woman of color to win Best Cinematography (for Sinners). Guillermo del Toro’s Frankenstein won three awards including Best Production Design.

From a WSWS analytical standpoint, the Best Picture winner is significant for engaging — however imperfectly — with themes of political repression, immigration enforcement, and the legacy of radical movements in America. Anderson told Deadline backstage about the film’s racial politics, acknowledging ongoing debate about his depiction of Black women. The broader Oscar slate reflects a tension between genuinely artistically ambitious work (Anderson, Buckley’s performance, del Toro’s Frankenstein) and the industry’s continued absorption of social content into commercial spectacle.

Jürgen Habermas (1929–2026): death of the last Frankfurt School philosopher

Jürgen Habermas died on March 14 in Starnberg, Germany at the age of 96. The most cited humanities scholar of the postwar era and the last surviving representative of the Frankfurt School, Habermas is best known for his two-volume Theory of Communicative Action and his theories of the public sphere and deliberative democracy. Born in Düsseldorf in 1929, he was drafted into the German military in 1944 and later recalled the Nazi atrocities as a formative moment that guided him toward philosophy.

Habermas’s relationship to Marxism was complex: the Frankfurt School was critical of capitalism but also of orthodox Marxism, and Habermas’s later work on communicative rationality explicitly departed from the class-struggle framework of historical materialism. His theory of the public sphere, while influential, idealized bourgeois democratic discourse in a manner that obscured the class character of the institutions he studied. Jacobin published a sympathetic obituary praising his “profound critique of irrationality” — a framing that elides his distance from revolutionary politics. His ideas shaped constitutional law scholarship and debates over European integration, but the fundamental contradictions of bourgeois democracy that he sought to resolve rationally remain unresolvable within the capitalist framework.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s most politically significant article of the past 24 hours is “A Post-Order World” by Juliano Fiori, which argues that US hegemony has collapsed not because a new hegemon has emerged but because consent for American empire has broken down, producing “enduring hegemonic crisis” and “zonal geopolitics.” The article employs Gramscian theory and criticizes US military interventions in Venezuela and Iran, but its political conclusion — that regional blocs might constrain great-power ambitions — amounts to a prescription for a multipolar imperialist order rather than the revolutionary overthrow of imperialism by the international working class. This is the characteristic Jacobin method: sophisticated academic analysis that stops precisely where Marxist politics begins.

“Thousands of Colorado Meatpacking Workers Are on Strike” by Alex N. Press covers the JBS Greeley walkout sympathetically, emphasizing worker militancy and conditions. Notably absent is any examination of the UFCW’s pre-announced two-week time limit on the strike or the union’s deliberate exclusion of Greeley from the national contract — the very mechanisms by which the bureaucracy is positioning to strangle the strike. This silence on the role of the union apparatus is consistent with the DSA’s structural relationship to the trade union bureaucracy.

“Jürgen Habermas Showed What Philosophy Could Be” offers a sympathetic obituary praising Habermas’s “profound critique of irrationality” as “an indispensable guide in the struggle against oppression” — a framing that erases the fundamental class divide between Habermas’s communicative-rationalist project and the Marxist-Trotskyist understanding of the state, class struggle, and revolution.

The day’s overall orientation: Jacobin functions as a left-academic organ that absorbs genuine working-class struggles into a reformist framework, combining sophisticated international analysis with systematic evasion of the trade union question.
