1. Iran war hits 60-day War Powers deadline as Democrats threaten lawsuit

The U.S.-Israeli war on Iran, launched February 28, hits the statutory 60-day War Powers Act deadline on Friday, May 1 — requiring President Trump to obtain congressional authorization or terminate hostilities. The Senate voted 52–47 on April 15 to block the fourth consecutive bipartisan war powers resolution, exposing the collapse of any institutional check on executive war-making. Multiple Democrats told Time they are “exploring suing Trump” if the war continues past Friday’s deadline, calling it an illegal war.

Iran’s latest proposal offers to reopen the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for the U.S. lifting its blockade of Iranian ports, deferring the nuclear program question entirely. An unnamed U.S. official told CBS News that Trump “doesn’t love the proposal” because it excludes nuclear provisions. Trump claimed Tuesday that Iran “informed us that they are in a ‘State of Collapse’” and posted to social media warning of “no more Mr. Nice Guy” if Tehran refuses a deal. The Iranian foreign minister has flown to Moscow to seek political backing as U.S.-Iran talks stall.

Key facts: - Day 61 of the war as of April 28; first new U.S. war in the Middle East since the 2003 invasion of Iraq. - Senate Appropriations Chair Susan Collins: “If there are military hostilities beyond the 60 days, Congress has to authorize them, or Congress can block them, but Congress has to act.” - The April 8 ceasefire has been extended open-endedly; the U.S. port blockade remains in effect.

The May 1 deadline is the defining test of whether constitutional war-making oversight remains functional in the United States. The bipartisan vote against war powers enforcement reveals not a Trump-Democrat conflict but a ruling-class consensus that the executive may wage war at will.

2. UAE exits OPEC effective May 1 as World Bank forecasts 24% energy price surge

The United Arab Emirates announced April 28 that it will leave OPEC and OPEC+ effective May 1, citing “national interests” — a historic blow to the cohesion of the 60-year-old cartel. The exit gives the UAE freedom to sell without quota constraints once the Strait of Hormuz reopens, potentially adding 1 million barrels per day to global supply.

The World Bank’s April 28 Commodity Markets Outlook forecasts a 24% surge in energy prices in 2026 — the largest since Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine. Strait of Hormuz traffic has collapsed to roughly 3.8 million barrels per day, down from over 20 million b/d before the war began — a record supply shock of approximately 10 million bpd. Brent crude averaged $103/barrel in March (up $32 from February) and briefly touched $128/barrel on April 2.

Key facts: - World Bank projection: Brent could reach $115/barrel if energy infrastructure sustains further damage. - UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned of a “global food emergency” cascading from fertilizer and commodity price shocks. - The UAE’s departure is the most serious challenge to OPEC cohesion since Qatar’s 2019 exit.

5. Israeli strikes kill eight in southern Lebanon including three paramedics

6. MSF: Israel has destroyed 90% of Gaza’s water infrastructure

Médecins Sans Frontières on April 28 published a major report accusing Israel of “systematically depriving” Gaza of water and destroying or damaging nearly 90% of Gaza’s water and sanitation infrastructure — including desalination plants, boreholes, pipelines, and sewage systems. Tens of thousands of cubic meters of raw sewage have seeped into Gaza’s groundwater aquifer, contaminating the territory’s principal long-term freshwater source.

MSF disclosed it has become the largest single water producer and main distributor in Gaza outside local authorities, supplying 5.3 million liters per day — meeting the minimum needs of 407,000 people, roughly one-fifth of Gaza’s population. A third of MSF’s supply requests for pumps, desalination units, water tanks, and chlorine have been “rejected or left unanswered” by Israeli authorities. Palestinians have been “injured and killed while trying to access water,” the report states.

Israel’s COGAT rejected the findings as “factually incorrect,” claiming it facilitates “well over 70,000 cubic meters of water daily.” The MSF documentation, combined with the systematic rejection of basic repair materials, fits the legal definition of using civilian infrastructure as a weapon of war and provides direct evidence for collective punishment under international humanitarian law.

7. IMF cuts global growth forecast to 3.1%, warns of near-recession scenario

The IMF’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook cuts the 2026 global growth forecast to 3.1%, down from a pre-war projection of 3.4%, and raises the global inflation forecast to 4.4% — up 0.6 percentage points from January. Middle East and Central Asia growth is downgraded to 1.9%, a two-percentage-point cut. In a “severe scenario” of prolonged conflict, the IMF projects global growth could fall to 2% in 2026–27 — technically a near-recession.

Developing economies face an average inflation rate of 5.1% in 2026, one full percentage point higher than pre-war projections. UN Secretary-General Guterres described the cascading shocks as “the worst supply chain disruption since COVID-19 and the war in Ukraine.” The IMF’s downgrade matches the World Bank’s commodity outlook and reflects the structural impact of removing roughly 20% of global seaborne oil through the Hormuz blockade.

The Fund’s analysis treats the inflation surge primarily as a supply-side shock from energy and food, but the deeper class dimension — the transfer of war costs onto working-class real wages globally — receives little treatment in the multilateral analysis. As Nick Beams noted on the WSWS, in Australia alone the war has driven inflation to 4.6%, ASX 300 profit downgrades to 20%, and the largest one-month consumer-confidence drop since the pandemic.

The report builds on Trump’s January 20, 2025 executive order “Restoring the Death Penalty and Protecting Public Safety.” During Trump’s first term, 13 federal prisoners were executed in six months (July 2020–January 2021) — more than three decades of prior federal executions combined. South Carolina executed three by firing squad in 2025; Idaho designates the firing squad as its primary method effective July 1, 2026; Florida conducted 19 executions in 2025, double its previous state record. Public support for capital punishment is at five-decade lows, with only 41% support among Americans aged 18–34. As Kate Randall noted on the WSWS, the methodological expansion is the assertion of “unlimited killing authority” by the state at precisely the moment public consent is collapsing.

US measles cases hit 1,792 in 2026 across 22 active outbreaks

The CDC reports 1,792 confirmed measles cases across 37 jurisdictions as of April 23 — a pace that far exceeds any year since the United States declared measles eliminated in 2000. 93% of cases (1,668) are outbreak-associated, spanning 22 active outbreaks, including carryover from outbreaks that began in 2025. The continued rise reflects the collapse of vaccination uptake under the RFK Jr.-led HHS and the dismantling of state-level public-health response capacity.

H5N1 spreads to dairy herds in 9 states; Cambodia confirms fourth 2026 case

Cambodia confirmed its fourth human H5N1 case of 2026 on April 21. In the United States, FDA reports document spread to dairy herds in nine states, with one-in-five commercial milk samples testing positive for viral traces. Researchers at the University of Nebraska–Lincoln published a new H5N1 vaccine showing strong efficacy in mice and cattle, with human trials commencing this month. The “pink eye” (conjunctivitis-only) presentation in farm-linked human cases is complicating clinical surveillance, while the dairy-cattle reservoir remains the principal driver of human exposure risk in the United States.

Lilly’s oral GLP-1 orforglipron produces 11.2% weight loss in NEJM phase 3

In the 72-week ATTAIN-1 phase 3 randomized controlled trial published in the New England Journal of Medicine, Eli Lilly’s oral GLP-1 agonist orforglipron at 36 mg produced a mean −11.2% body weight change versus −2.1% for placebo (p<0.001); 54.6% of participants on the top dose lost ≥10% of body weight. A companion Lancet trial (ATTAIN-2) showed similar efficacy in people with type 2 diabetes. As the first oral small-molecule GLP-1 agonist with phase 3 data, orforglipron could dramatically expand access to weight-loss treatment by eliminating injections and cold-chain storage. The class implications are significant: a pill formulation could shift the therapy from a high-margin specialty drug toward primary-care prescribing, with implications for global pricing.

COVID-19 activity remains low; XFG variants dominant

CDC respiratory surveillance shows COVID-19 accounting for just 0.18% of ER visits nationally as of the week ending April 18; 37 states show declining R(t). The dominant variant is XFG.1.1 at 32% prevalence, with projected ~1,500 new hospital admissions for the week ending May 2. No emergent high-severity variant has been identified, though XFG sublineages continue displacing prior strains and warrant monitoring.

In the United States, Florida’s legislature opened a four-day special session on April 28 to consider an “AI Bill of Rights”; California bills SB 903, AB 2575, and AB 1979 advanced through committee. Critical RCE flaw in Hugging Face robotics platform

Security researchers disclosed CVE-2026-25874, a critical vulnerability in Hugging Face’s open-source LeRobot platform enabling unauthenticated remote code execution via unsafe deserialization — affecting deployments in robotics research labs worldwide. As open-source robotics platforms proliferate in academic and industrial settings, supply-chain vulnerabilities at this layer represent a new and largely unmonitored attack surface.

The WSWS framework here is decisive: Marxists do not endorse boycotts based on national origin, which ratify imperialist sorting of art into “permitted” and “prohibited” categories, but defend the artistic freedom of Iranian, Russian, Israeli, and Palestinian artists alike against state pressure on all sides.

The film industry remains in protracted contraction following the long aftermath of the 2023 strikes, the consolidation of streaming, and the AI-driven attack on creative labor. The performing arts face parallel crises: regional theater closures and orchestra contract disputes are accelerating.

In music and publishing, the suppression of pro-Palestinian artists by major labels and venues continues; several university press editors have publicly resigned over the censorship of Gaza-related scholarship. The cultural-political question posed by the moment — whether the artistic and intellectual life of the country can speak truthfully about the wars being waged in its name — finds its answer in mass independent organization, not in the curatorial gatekeeping of institutions integrated with the war machine.

RBA deputy governor Andrew Hauser called the situation a “central banker’s nightmare.” Consumer confidence plunged 12.5% to 80.1 in April — the largest monthly fall since the pandemic. Cochlear’s share price fell 41% after slashing profit guidance from $435–460 million to $290–330 million. National Australia Bank raised impairment provisions from $485 million to $706 million in anticipation of defaults from fuel-dependent agriculture and transport businesses. Beams’s distinctive contribution is the political linkage: simultaneously, the Albanese Labor government is cutting the National Disability Insurance Scheme by $35 billion, transferring the costs of global capitalist crisis directly onto disabled workers and their families. The political conclusion is that Labor’s role in administering austerity-and-rearmament under wartime conditions exposes the impossibility of any reformist accommodation with Australian capital.

German government plans massive cuts to health, pensions and social benefits — Peter Schwarz

Germany’s defense budget reached €97 billion last year (up 24%), making it the world’s fourth-largest military spender, with plans to raise expenditure from 2.3% to 5% of GDP — potentially exceeding €200 billion annually. The cost is being borne by workers: healthcare cuts of €20 billion are planned for next year; Chancellor Merz declared statutory pensions “will at best still be the basic security for old age”; and a leaked 108-page government document outlines €8.6 billion in annual social cuts targeting the disabled, children, and families. The Equality Welfare Association said the proposals treat “social benefits like budget items.” Despite coalition tensions, both CDU/CSU and SPD support the rearmament-and-austerity axis; the ruling parties poll only 37% and 12–14% respectively — a collapse of governing legitimacy that Schwarz traces directly to the class character of the rearmament program.

Australian Education Union divides teachers to prepare sellout in Victoria — Sue Phillips

Phillips reports that the March 24, 2026 statewide strike of over 40,000 Victorian educators — the largest in 13 years — was immediately neutralized by the AEU leadership, which replaced unified strikes with fragmented regional half-day stoppages after the Labor government offered a 17–18% pay increase over four years tied to increased workloads. The AEU simultaneously accepted a Tasmanian deal of only 3% annually for two years and 2.75% in the third — below inflation — separating Tasmanian educators from their Victorian counterparts. The article draws parallels to 2022, when union leadership used “misinformation and censorship” to ratify a sellout contract after a 97% strike vote. The conclusion: educators must build democratically elected rank-and-file committees independent of the AEU apparatus, link their struggles internationally, and oppose the government’s $53 billion military spending increase alongside NDIS cuts. The article is a model rank-and-file polemic and a key reference for the SEP-Australia’s industrial work.

Australian pseudo-left Socialist Alternative hysterically denounces workers who voted for One Nation — Oscar Grenfell

Grenfell’s polemic targets Socialist Alternative leader Mick Armstrong, who characterized working-class One Nation voters in South Australian state elections as “lumpen” and “racist scum” requiring “physical crushing.” One Nation received 33% in Elizabeth, 20% in Port Adelaide, and 25% in Mawson — all traditionally safe Labor seats experiencing historic swings. Grenfell argues these results reflect “seething social discontent” rather than inherent racism, and that Armstrong’s exclusion of tradies and precarious workers from legitimate working-class status is “an open display of middle-class prejudice.” The piece is a sharp demonstration of how pseudo-left tendencies adapt to bourgeois sociology — equating white working-class protest votes with the far right while refusing to address the cost-of-living crisis or the Iran war that drove the swing.
