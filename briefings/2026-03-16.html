1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 16 with Isfahan strikes

2. Strait of Hormuz closure triggers largest oil disruption in decades

Iran’s de facto blockade of the Strait of Hormuz has produced the most severe oil supply disruption in decades. Brent crude hit $106/barrel early Monday before settling around $104, having swung between a peak near $126 and a low of $80 within a single week — the most volatile oil trading since the 1970s. Crude flows through Hormuz have collapsed from approximately 20 million barrels/day to what analysts describe as “a trickle,” with Gulf producers cutting output by at least 10 million barrels per day.

Key facts: - At least 16 commercial vessels attacked in the region since February 28, per UKMTO - Trump called on China, France, Japan, South Korea, and the UK to send warships to escort tankers; Germany, China, Japan, and the UK have all declined - The IEA authorized a release of 400 million barrels — the largest emergency release in its history - EIA raised its 2026 Brent forecast from $58/barrel (pre-war) to $79/barrel average; Goldman Sachs models $98/barrel average for March–April - Goldman Sachs warns: if disruption persists, “substantial stagflationary drag” on Europe and East Asia; prolonged disruption risks global recession in major oil-importing nations

US measles cases reach 1,362 across 31 jurisdictions

As of March 12, the CDC has confirmed 1,362 measles cases across 31 jurisdictions. Ninety-four percent of cases (1,281) are outbreak-associated — 1,025 from outbreaks that began in 2025 and 256 from 14 new outbreaks in 2026. States affected include Texas, Ohio, New York, California, Florida, and 26 others. The scale reflects sustained transmission driven by vaccination gaps, marking one of the worst measles years in decades — a direct consequence of the anti-vaccination movement that has been politically weaponized by the Trump administration and its allies.

Sources CDC Measles data and research

H5N1 A3 genotype reaches marine mammals in California

An H5N1 outbreak among elephant seals at Año Nuevo State Park has spread to a sea otter and a sea lion. USDA lab tests confirmed the A3 genotype — known for mammalian adaptation — making this the first detection of A3 in California. The broad cross-species spread underscores why virologists warn the pandemic risk trajectory is worsening even as human cases remain rare. PAHO/WHO reports 75 human H5N1 infections and 2 deaths across the Americas since April 2022, with the virus now detected in 37 mammalian species and 94 bird species across the region.

Key facts: - Pennsylvania H5N1 has killed more than 7 million birds in 2026, concentrated in Lancaster County with spread to Philadelphia and Bucks Counties - More than 195 million birds affected nationally since the 2022 outbreak began, threatening Pennsylvania’s $7.1 billion poultry industry

Medical research breakthroughs

Two significant studies published in the past week merit attention:

Dravet syndrome: An experimental drug, zorevunersen, reduced seizures by up to 91% in children with Dravet syndrome, a severe genetic epilepsy with few effective treatments. Published in the New England Journal of Medicine (Vol. 394), this is one of the most striking efficacy results in pediatric epilepsy in years and could redefine standard of care.

Cancer immunotherapy: Three simultaneous trials published in Nature Medicine confirmed that fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) significantly enhances immunotherapy efficacy in patients with advanced solid tumors. The convergent evidence from three independent trials elevates FMT from experimental to a credible adjunct cancer treatment strategy.

Sources NEJM Issue index — March 2026 · Nature Medicine 2026 articles

Arts and culture

The 98th Academy Awards ceremony on Sunday night offered a revealing snapshot of the cultural contradictions of the present moment — a celebration of filmmaking artistry taking place against the backdrop of a deepening war in the Middle East, accelerating attacks on cultural institutions, and the death of one of the last major figures of European critical philosophy.

One Battle After Another wins best picture at the 98th Oscars

Paul Thomas Anderson’s One Battle After Another, adapted from Thomas Pynchon’s 1990 novel Vineland, swept the 98th Academy Awards with six wins including Best Picture, Best Director, and Best Adapted Screenplay. The film — which follows an ex-revolutionary pursued by a corrupt military officer, tackling themes of immigration raids and white supremacy — resonated unmistakably with the current political climate. Anderson, accepting the screenplay award, said: “I wrote this movie for my kids to say sorry for the housekeeping mess that we left in this world.” Sean Penn won Best Supporting Actor for playing a brutal military officer, entering the elite club of three-time acting Oscar winners.

Ryan Coogler’s Sinners made history with 16 nominations and took four awards including Best Actor for Michael B. Jordan and Best Cinematography for Autumn Durald Arkapaw — the first woman to win that award. Best Documentary went to Mr. Nobody Against Putin, David Borenstein and Pavel Talankin’s film about a Russian schoolteacher who documented Kremlin propaganda and youth indoctrination during the Ukraine war. “In the name of our future, in the name of all of our children, stop all of these wars now,” Talankin said in Russian from the stage. Notably, three films about the Gaza war were nominated in various categories but fell short — a fact that drew commentary given the ongoing Israeli military operations.

The ceremony, hosted by Conan O’Brien for the second consecutive year, took place as the Trump administration continues its systematic assault on cultural institutions. A federal judge had to intervene to restore IMLS grants cut by the administration, while the president’s 2026 budget proposal advances the complete defunding of the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the Corporation for Public Broadcasting. One-third of US museums report being affected by Trump-related grant cuts.

Boston Philharmonic announces closure after 48 years

The Boston Philharmonic Orchestra announced that its 2026–27 season will be its last, along with its tuition-free Youth Orchestra of 120 musicians aged 12–21. Founded by Benjamin Zander in 1979, the orchestra will close with a performance of Mahler’s Symphony No. 9 — the same work that opened its first concert. The closure reflects the broader financial precarity of cultural institutions outside the corporate-sponsored elite tier, exacerbated by federal defunding and pandemic-era audience erosion. The BPYO’s loss is particularly significant — a tuition-free program that had performed internationally across four continents.

Death of Jürgen Habermas

Jürgen Habermas, the German philosopher and last surviving member of the Frankfurt School, died on March 14 in Starnberg at age 96. Associated with Adorno and Horkheimer’s critical theory tradition, Habermas was best known for his concept of the “public sphere” and his two-volume Theory of Communicative Action. His death comes at a moment when the conditions he spent his career analyzing — the erosion of democratic discourse, the irrationality of capitalist crisis, the manipulation of public opinion for war — are manifesting with particular acuteness. Jacobin published a tribute calling him “an indispensable guide in the struggle against oppression” — though the WSWS has historically assessed his later work as a retreat from the revolutionary implications of the Frankfurt School tradition toward bourgeois-democratic reformism.

This week in history: March 16–22

The WSWS “This Week in History” column covers four anniversaries with particular relevance to the present moment:

Harold Wilson’s resignation (March 16, 1976): Wilson resigned as UK Labour Prime Minister, framed not as a personal decision but as a ruling-class response to Britain’s deepening economic crisis — the worst since the 1930s. MI5 and military conspiracies against Wilson’s government were later revealed, including a plot by Lord Mountbatten to stage a coup. Wilson’s successor James Callaghan imposed IMF-dictated austerity, setting the stage for Thatcher.

Anglo-Iranian Oil Company strike (March 22, 1951): Fifteen thousand workers struck the refinery in Abadan for wages under $1/day, coinciding with Iran’s nationalization of its oil industry under Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh. The strike foreshadowed the 1953 CIA-MI6 coup that overthrew Mossadegh and installed the Shah — establishing the pattern of imperialist intervention in Iran that continues to the present war.

Circuit City Stores v. Adams (March 21, 2001): The US Supreme Court ruled 5–4 that employers could force workers to waive their right to sue for discrimination through mandatory arbitration clauses, a decision that has since stripped millions of workers of legal recourse.

Chiang Kai-shek’s 1926 coup: On March 20, 1926, Chiang purged Communists from the Kuomintang. Stalin suppressed news of the coup from the Comintern, enabling the catastrophic 1927 Shanghai massacre — precisely the outcome Trotsky and the Left Opposition had warned against.

Sources WSWS This week in history: March 16–22

Jacobin / DSA

Eric Blanc’s “Why Is There No Antiwar Movement in the US?” (March 9) is the most politically significant Jacobin article this cycle. Blanc, a Rutgers professor and DSA Bread and Roses caucus member, acknowledges the war’s historic unpopularity but identifies the main obstacle as “sectarianism” — meaning activists who use “alienating, excessively radical rhetoric and slogans” and tie antiwar demands to “romanticization of ‘anti-imperialist’ forces.” His solution is to build toward the March 28 and May 1 protests “not as one-off protests but as mechanisms to recruit, onboard, and train as many people as possible into ongoing campaigns.” As the WSWS responded on March 12, this is not an explanation for the absence of an antiwar movement — it is propaganda aimed at blocking one. Blanc’s framework channels opposition away from independent working-class action and toward the Democratic Party orbit, with “sectarianism” serving as a codeword for any politics that breaks with bourgeois respectability.

Jacobin’s “A Post-Order World” (March 2026) examines declining US global hegemony without identifying it as a crisis of the capitalist system. The analysis is presented as a geopolitical observation rather than the expression of the terminal crisis of the nation-state system. A piece on Hasan Piker describes “imperial end times” and a “quagmire in Iran” — adopting a left-populist vocabulary that substitutes media commentary for political program. The overall orientation: channeling antiwar sentiment into pressure politics within the existing system, with the Democratic Party as the unspoken vehicle.

Sources Jacobin Why is there no antiwar movement in the US? · Jacobin A post-order world · WSWS Jacobin proposes to subordinate opposition to war to the interests of imperialism

Left Voice

Left Voice published a statement from the Current for Permanent Revolution – Fourth International (CPR-FI) on March 2 calling for a “mass movement” against the Iran war, bringing together “all sectors organizing against Trump” — from the “No Kings” protests to the Palestine movement to NGOs organizing against ICE. The political line is a broad anti-Trump front rather than an independent working-class movement against imperialist war. The call for “labor playing a leading role” is decorative, as it is subordinated to a cross-class coalition with NGOs and Democratic-aligned protest networks.

On International Women’s Day (March 8), Left Voice connected women’s oppression to imperialist aggression, citing the US invasion of Venezuela and Trump’s threats against Mexico, Colombia, and Cuba. The analysis is structurally correct but the programmatic conclusions remain within the framework of the Morenoite tradition’s adaptation to popular-front politics. Coverage of Chile’s Kast government and Mexico’s 40-hour workweek reform is the strongest material — noting that Sheinbaum’s “concessions to business owners show which sector the government prioritizes” — but stops short of drawing the conclusion that bourgeois-nationalist governments are incapable of defending workers’ interests.

Sources Left Voice We need a mass movement to defeat Trump’s imperialist war on Iran · Left Voice On this International Women’s Day, down with imperialist war

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL’s “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” statement (February 28) and subsequent coverage oppose the war from a consistent campist perspective — identifying US imperialism as the primary enemy while evading the class character of the Iranian bourgeois-clerical regime. The statement declares that Trump and Israel “launched a massive war against Iran that has already brought death, destruction and chaos all across the Middle East” but makes no critique of the Iranian government’s own repression of the working class. A separate article argues “military production is parasitic” and that “switching to civilian production creates more jobs” — a reformist economic argument that accepts the framework of the capitalist economy while proposing to redirect spending within it. The PSL’s demand to “Free Booker Ngesa Omole” — the kidnapped secretary general of the Communist Party Marxist–Kenya — is a legitimate solidarity action, though it is not connected to any broader analysis of the crisis of bourgeois rule in Africa.

Sources Liberation News PSL statement: Stop Trump’s war on Iran! · Liberation News Free Booker Ngesa Omole!

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

The SWP’s primary focus is building the March 28 Together alliance demonstration against the far right in London, with trade union endorsements from the TUC, Unite, and UNISON. Their “Hands Off Iran” coverage (March 7) opposes British complicity in the war, but the political framework subordinates anti-war politics to the construction of a broad anti-far-right popular front — the Together alliance is cross-class by design and politically led by forces to the right of the SWP. Coverage of a French tyre factory occupation (920 workers, four weeks) and below-inflation pay offers to British workers reflects the SWP’s orientation toward trade union militancy, but without any critique of the union bureaucracies’ role in containing struggle. The overall orientation: building the March 28 rally as the central organizing task, with anti-war politics folded into a generic “resist the right” framework.

Sources Socialist Worker Workers unite for Together demo · Socialist Worker Homepage — March 2026

RCP / IMT

The RCP’s “The trade unions and the war on Iran” is the most notable piece, arguing that the war is producing “massive radicalisation” but that “Britain’s trade union leaders are lagging far behind this mood.” The article singles out Unite for not even posting a statement and mocks the GMB’s five-line response. The RCP’s “Hands Off Iran!” statement opposes the war with militant rhetoric: “2026 has already been marked by titanic events that are shaking the world.” But the political conclusion — pressure the union leaders to act — remains within the framework of reforming the existing bureaucratic structures rather than building independent rank-and-file organizations to replace them. The RCP warns of “a depression like that of the 1930s” from the Hormuz crisis, but its organizational perspective remains national (building toward the RCP’s National Congress, targeting 1,400 members) rather than internationalist.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Working-class impact of the Hormuz oil shock — fuel poverty, inflation, and austerity

The Hormuz closure is driving fuel prices to levels not seen since 2022, with Brent at $104–106/barrel. For workers in Europe, Asia, and the Global South — already squeezed by years of post-pandemic inflation — this represents a direct cut in real wages. No major outlet is connecting the oil disruption to the lived experience of working-class households or to the political economy of who benefits (oil majors, arms manufacturers) versus who pays. The WSWS could produce a data-driven analysis of the class impact across several countries.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Oil prices keep rising as Trump seeks coalition to reopen Strait of Hormuz

2. Russia–Iran military cooperation — a new axis formed in the war

Reporting has emerged that Russia is supplying Iran with targeting intelligence on US troops, warships, and aircraft, while helping Tehran improve drone tactics drawing on Russian experience in Ukraine. This amounts to a de facto military alliance between Moscow and Tehran operating against US forces — a major strategic development with profound implications for the trajectory of both wars and the broader disintegration of the post-Cold War order. A WSWS analysis could connect this to the Marxist understanding of inter-imperialist conflict.

Recommended reading FDD Russia helps Iran attack US and its allies; Ukraine helps defend them

3. The 98th Academy Awards — art, war, and political complicity

Paul Thomas Anderson’s One Battle After Another, dealing with immigration raids and white supremacy, won Best Picture as the US wages war on Iran. Three Gaza films were nominated but failed to win. The Best Documentary, Mr. Nobody Against Putin, featured an anti-war speech from its Russian co-director. Meanwhile, Trump’s administration is systematically defunding the NEA, NEH, and IMLS. A WSWS arts review could analyze the ceremony’s political contradictions — the film industry’s gestures toward social conscience alongside its structural dependence on the same ruling class prosecuting the war.

Recommended reading Times of Israel One Battle After Another wins best picture; 3 films about Gaza war fall short

4. European rearmament — €800 billion war budget and the assault on social spending

The EU’s ReArm Europe Plan is mobilizing €800 billion for military spending while member states simultaneously pursue austerity under EU fiscal rules — the same rules being loosened only for defense. Germany’s 2026 defense budget of €82.69 billion (15% of the total national budget) dwarfs social spending increases. The WSWS could produce a country-by-country analysis showing what social programs are being cut or frozen to fund this unprecedented militarization.

Recommended reading Atlas Institute Germany’s path to Kriegstüchtigkeit: The 2026 defence budget

5. Gaza full blockade — 46% of medicines out of stock, 391 UN workers killed

All Gaza crossings remain closed as of March 16, with 46% of essential medicines and 66% of medical consumables depleted, per WHO. UNRWA has recorded 391 of its own staff killed — an unprecedented toll for a UN agency. With the US-Iran war dominating headlines, this ongoing humanitarian catastrophe is being systematically pushed off front pages. The WSWS should ensure it does not replicate this pattern.

Recommended reading UNRWA Situation report #212: Humanitarian crisis in Gaza Strip and occupied West Bank

6. Death of Jürgen Habermas and the legacy of the Frankfurt School

Habermas, the last surviving member of the Frankfurt School, died March 14 at 96. His work on the public sphere, communicative rationality, and discourse ethics shaped decades of left-liberal thought. A WSWS obituary could engage with both his genuine contributions and the trajectory from the revolutionary implications of Adorno and Horkheimer’s critical theory toward Habermas’s accommodation to bourgeois democratic institutions — a political evolution that parallels the broader degeneration of postwar left-liberal academia.

Recommended reading Washington Post Jürgen Habermas, influential German philosopher, dies at 96

7. Boston Philharmonic closure and the crisis of cultural institutions

The Boston Philharmonic’s announced shutdown after 48 years, including its tuition-free youth orchestra, reflects the broader crisis of cultural institutions under conditions of austerity and federal defunding. One-third of US museums have been affected by Trump-era grant cuts. A WSWS article could situate this within the systematic degradation of cultural life under capitalism, noting the contradiction between the $1.17 trillion arts economy and the elimination of public funding.
