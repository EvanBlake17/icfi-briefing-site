1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 12 with “most intense” strikes yet

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its twelfth day on March 11 with no indication of a ceasefire. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared Tuesday “our most intense day of strikes inside Iran,” deploying maximum bomber and fighter capacity. US CENTCOM confirmed over 5,000 targets struck since Operation Epic Fury launched February 28. Iran reports over 1,300 civilians killed and nearly 10,000 civilian sites hit, including 77 healthcare facilities and 69 schools damaged within a single 24-hour period. The Iranian Red Crescent documented 19,734 civilian structures damaged in that same timeframe.

US confirmed casualties stand at 7 soldiers killed and approximately 140 wounded. In Lebanon, 570 people have been killed — including 19 on Wednesday alone — with over 667,000 displaced. Four Iranian diplomats were killed in a strike on Beirut.

Iran’s IRGC launched its 37th wave of retaliatory attacks using “super-heavy Khoramshahr missiles” targeting Iraq, Bahrain, and Israel, with a reported hit on Tel Aviv. Saudi Arabia intercepted Iranian drones over its oil-rich eastern province. The UAE intercepted 26 drones, though 9 fell inside its territory. Qatar issued an elevated threat alert. Over 43,000 American citizens have been evacuated from the region.

Key facts: - Trump called for Iran’s “unconditional surrender” and told CBS he wanted “nobody around to cry uncle” — language the WSWS characterized as genocidal rhetoric - IRGC spokesperson Ali Mohammad Naini: “Iran will determine when the war ends” - NPR/PBS News/Marist poll: a majority of Americans oppose US involvement in the war - Only 38% of Americans support the war; 49% oppose it

2. US Tomahawk missile identified in strike that killed 165 girls at Iranian school

Multiple investigations have confirmed that a US Tomahawk cruise missile destroyed the Shajareh Tayyebeh girls’ elementary school in Minab, Hormozgan province, on February 28 — the first day of the war — killing 165 people, most of them girls aged 7–12. Missile fragment markings were confirmed as American by multiple weapons experts. 3. Strait of Hormuz closure triggers worst energy shock since 1973

The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which one-fifth of global oil supply transits — has produced the most severe energy supply disruption since the 1973 oil embargo. Brent crude spiked to nearly $120/barrel, up from under $70 in February, before swinging violently to the current $85–90 range. Claudio Galimberti of Rystad Energy stated: “We have not seen anything like this in pretty much the history of the Strait of Hormuz.”

Qatar declared force majeure on LNG exports after Iranian drone attacks. Qatar supplies 20% of global LNG and may need a month to restore production. Saudi Aramco’s Ras Tanura refinery and major crude export terminal shut down. Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, and Iraq have all reduced production due to shipping disruption. The IEA is considering the largest emergency oil reserve release in history.

Key facts: - Japan’s Nikkei 225 plunged 5%+ on Monday before recovering - South Korea, Thailand, Bangladesh, and Pakistan introduced fuel price caps and rationing - IMF formula: every 10% oil price rise = 0.4% inflation increase + 0.15% GDP reduction - Up to 30% of global fertilizer exports pass through Hormuz, threatening food production worldwide - Djibouti’s finance minister warned of “severe economic consequences for developing countries”

Baden-Württemberg election: SPD collapses to 5.5% as AfD doubles its vote

Sunday’s Baden-Württemberg state election produced a historic collapse of German social democracy. The SPD fell to 5.5% — barely clearing the parliamentary threshold — from 11% in the prior state election and 14.2% in the state’s share of the previous federal election. The Left Party received 4.4% and failed to enter parliament. Combined, the two nominally left parties received under 10%.

The AfD surpassed one million votes for the first time in the state, reaching 18.8% — nearly double its previous result. Infratest dimap showed the AfD capturing 37% of the working-class vote versus 5% for the SPD and 4% for the Left Party. The Greens narrowly won with 30.2% over CDU at 29.7%, with Green candidate Cem Özdemir likely to succeed Kretschmann as state premier. The CDU’s Manuel Hagel was damaged by an eight-year-old video showing him making sexist remarks about a minor.

Polling shows 58% of Germans oppose the Iran war but none of the establishment parties offer an outlet for this opposition.

Two major Trump executive order deliverables were due on March 11. Sixth anniversary of the COVID-19 pandemic

March 11 marks the sixth anniversary of the WHO’s pandemic declaration. The WSWS’s Benjamin Mateus documented over 30 million COVID deaths and 400 million Long COVID cases worldwide, while major bourgeois media have deliberately ignored the anniversary. The Pandemic Mitigation Collaborative estimates 565,000 new daily US infections — roughly 1 in 87 Americans actively infectious at any time — with 54 million US infections in the first three months of 2026 alone, generating 205,000–820,000 new Long COVID cases weekly and 1,200–1,900 excess deaths weekly.

Actuarial data for 2025 suggests 109,000–175,000 excess US deaths — up to 73% higher than directly reported COVID deaths, eclipsing deaths from colon, pancreatic, breast, and prostate cancer combined. The Lancet published a scathing editorial on RFK Jr.’s first year at HHS — “1 year of failure” — documenting the dismissal of experts, elimination of critical datasets, and promotion of fringe beliefs.

US measles cases near 1,300 across 31 states

As of March 5, the US has recorded 1,281 confirmed measles cases across 31 states, with South Carolina alone reporting 991 — the highest per-state count in US history since elimination was declared. 94% of cases involve unvaccinated or unknown-vaccination-status individuals. This is one of the three worst years for measles since 2000 and raises serious questions about whether the US can maintain its measles elimination status.

Lessons of the 2026 New York City nurses strike — Robert Milkowski

A detailed postmortem of the 41-day NYSNA strike by 15,000 NYC nurses (January–February 2026). The new contracts contain below-inflation wages, unaffordable health benefits, weak AI provisions, and toothless workplace violence language. NYSNA leadership canceled strike notices at 11 of 15 hospitals before bargaining concluded. President Nancy Hagans went behind the back of the last holdout unit (NewYork-Presbyterian) to reach a management-friendly deal, provoking 1,500 nurses to sign a petition demanding her resignation. Governor Hochul declared a state of emergency and imported scab nurses. Mayor Zohran Mamdani appeared on picket lines for optics but worked behind the scenes with federal mediators to broker a settlement acceptable to hospital management, with his deputy mayor Julie Su (former Biden Labor Secretary who suppressed railroad, dock, and Boeing strikes) reportedly playing a key role. The article calls for independent rank-and-file committees organized through the IWA-RFC.

Henry Ford Genesys walkout enters 6th month, as Corewell Health nurses vote on strike action — Kevin Reed

750 nurses at Henry Ford Genesys Hospital in Grand Blanc, Michigan have been on strike since September 1, 2025. Management has offered a pay raise (up to 13%, with claims some nurses will top $100,000) but refuses to guarantee strikers’ original positions, insisting on the right to reassign after filling roles with permanent replacements. The Teamsters apparatus is signaling readiness to wrap up the strike on management’s terms. Simultaneously, 10,000 Corewell Health nurses across Michigan are voting on strike authorization over identical staffing issues. The article calls for rank-and-file committees to unify Genesys and Corewell nurses, and potentially the 6,000 nurses at Michigan Medicine, into a single confrontation with the for-profit healthcare system.

US memo exposes Sri Lankan “humanitarian” posturing over Iranian sailors’ rescue — Wasantha Rupasinghe

On March 4, a US submarine torpedoed and sank the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena in international waters near Sri Lanka, killing 108 sailors. The Sri Lankan Navy rescued 32 survivors. The cable required Sri Lanka to “minimize Iranian attempts to use the detainees for propaganda” and ordered the IRIS Bushehr to remain “in Sri Lankan custody for the duration of the conflict.” The Israeli envoy inquired about encouraging crew “defection.” The SEP (Sri Lanka) and IYSSE have scheduled a public meeting for March 17 at the Colombo Public Library Auditorium.

US and Ecuadorian militaries burn homes and torture workers in “Operation Total Extermination” — Andrea Lobo

  • Jacobin / DSA — extensive Iran war coverage focused on Democratic Party failures and defense industry profits, but channeling opposition back into reforming the Democrats and building a “mass movement” without class content or programmatic conclusions
  • Left Voice — calls for “mass movement” against imperialism and appeals to educators and feminists, but politically subordinates opposition to identity-based frameworks and avoids the question of revolutionary party-building
  • PSL / Liberation News — organized major antiwar protests through ANSWER Coalition, but its Stalinist political framework treats anti-imperialism as a cross-class popular front rather than a working-class strategy
  • Socialist Alternative / ISA — characterizes the war as a “nightmare” and calls for mass protests, but its political orientation remains toward pressure on existing institutions rather than independent working-class mobilization

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin has published prolifically on the Iran war over the past week, making it the tendency’s dominant theme. Three articles stand out:

  • “Defense Contractors Stand to Profit Off the Iran War” documents that Lockheed Martin jumped 3.4%, RTX rose 4.7%, and Northrop Grumman posted a 6% increase following the strikes. The article notes defense contractors have prioritized shareholder enrichment over production expansion — the US has “burned through” precision-guided missile reserves in less than a week. The analysis correctly identifies the war economy’s profit dimension but draws no conclusions about the capitalist system as such.

  • “The Iran War Shows Why It’s Time for Chuck Schumer to Go” correctly documents that Schumer simultaneously postures against the war and refuses to block its funding. But the article’s political conclusion — that better Democratic leadership could effectively oppose the war — expresses the fundamental Jacobin/DSA orientation: reforming the Democratic Party rather than building an independent working-class party. The article notes 89% of Democratic voters oppose the war but does not ask why the party structurally cannot represent this opposition.

  • “Trump’s War on Iran Makes a Mockery of American Democracy” frames the issue as a democratic deficit rather than a class question. The article asks “Why Is There No Antiwar Movement in the US?” but answers by pointing to institutional failures rather than the political role of organizations like the DSA itself in channeling opposition into Democratic Party dead ends.

The overall orientation is to provide detailed reportage on Democratic complicity while proposing no alternative outside the existing party system. The DSA’s own position on the war remains an appeal to Democratic officeholders to “do better.”

Sources Jacobin Defense contractors stand to profit off the Iran war · Jacobin The Iran war shows why it’s time for Chuck Schumer to go · Jacobin Trump’s war on Iran makes a mockery of American democracy

Left Voice

Left Voice has published three notable articles on the Iran war:

  • “We Need a Mass Movement to Defeat Trump’s Imperialist War on Iran” calls for an anti-war movement from workplaces and schools but defines the political agent as “the labor movement” abstracted from any concrete class program. The article correctly characterizes the war as imperialist but avoids the question of which party or international organization should lead the opposition.

  • “Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran” appeals to teachers and education workers to mobilize against the war, connected to recent strike activity. The political line is that the labor movement must “decisively oppose imperialism” — but the concrete strategy amounts to pressure within existing union structures rather than a break with the bureaucracy.

  • “Feminists Must Fight Imperialism in Iran Without Supporting the Theocratic Regime” attempts to navigate the dual contradiction of opposing the war while criticizing Iran’s government. The article’s identity-based framing — centering “feminists” as the political subject rather than the working class — reflects Left Voice’s characteristic adaptation to identity politics, treating gender oppression as an autonomous axis rather than integrating it into a class analysis of imperialism.

The overall orientation is verbally militant but politically eclectic, combining correct anti-imperialist rhetoric with an avoidance of the central question: the need for a revolutionary party and international organization.

Sources Left Voice We need a mass movement to defeat Trump’s imperialist war on Iran · Left Voice Educators must take the lead in opposing the imperialist war on Iran · Left Voice Feminists must fight imperialism in Iran without supporting the theocratic regime

PSL / Liberation News

The PSL’s statement “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” characterizes the conflict as “another war based on lies” and “a war for empire.” The PSL and its ANSWER Coalition organized protests in over 150 cities, making it the most organizationally active tendency in street mobilization. However, the political framework remains that of Stalinist popular-frontism: the protests unite “progressive” forces across class lines, with demands limited to “stop the war” and “bring the troops home” — avoiding any challenge to the capitalist system that produces imperialist wars. The PSL’s historical adaptation to bourgeois nationalism (support for various “anti-imperialist” capitalist governments) means it cannot explain why the Democratic Party, which it correctly criticizes, serves the same imperialist interests. The ANSWER protests channel genuine mass opposition into politically harmless demonstrations that make no organizational demands on the working class.

Sources Liberation News PSL statement: Stop Trump’s war on Iran!

Socialist Alternative / ISA

Socialist Alternative published “11 Days of War on Iran — Trump Has Unleashed a Nightmare” (March 10), documenting the escalation and characterizing the war as a product of US imperialism. The article calls for “an immediate end to the war in Iran, the occupation in Palestine, and all imperialist wars across the globe.” However, the concrete political strategy amounts to building “mass protests” and pressure campaigns without any orientation toward independent working-class political action or a break with the trade union bureaucracies and social-democratic parties that are enabling the war. The ISA’s international sections continue to adapt to local reformist movements rather than building a unified international revolutionary organization. A separate article warned of Trump’s desire for ground troops and the implications for Kurdish populations — useful reportage, but framed through the ISA’s characteristic national-reformist lens.

The SWP’s orientation, as always, is to build the Stop the War Coalition — a popular-front formation that historically channels anti-war sentiment into appeals to the Labour Party and trade union leaders. With the Starmer government actively participating in the war (British military assets are deployed), the SWP’s strategy of pressuring Labour amounts to appealing to a war party to oppose war. The article does not address Starmer’s complicity or call for a political break with Labour.

2. Iranian civilian life under US “precision” bombing — the invisible working-class toll

US and Israeli strikes have hit nearly 10,000 civilian sites per Iranian officials. Beyond the aggregate casualty numbers, the material conditions of Iranian workers — disrupted healthcare, food supply, fuel, communications, and housing — are almost entirely absent from Western reporting, which frames the war through strategic and geopolitical lenses. The Minab school massacre is the most visible atrocity, but the systemic destruction of civilian infrastructure deserves sustained attention. The WSWS has covered the war’s military dimensions but a dedicated article on the social catastrophe facing Iranian workers would fill a critical gap.

Bangladesh and Pakistan have already imposed rationing. The connection between Western military adventurism and energy poverty across the developing world is almost entirely missing from mainstream coverage. This connects to the WSWS’s ongoing analysis of imperialism’s impact on the working class internationally and the class character of the “energy transition.”
