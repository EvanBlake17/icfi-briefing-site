1. Trump weighs 60-day Iran ceasefire as Hormuz crisis enters fourth month

US and Iranian negotiators have reached a tentative 60-day memorandum of understanding to extend the ceasefire and open formal nuclear negotiations, but President Trump had not signed as of Sunday morning. “The president relayed to the mediators that he wants a couple of days to think about it,” a US official told reporters. The deal would end a war that began February 28, 2026, when the US-Israel air campaign opened, Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz, and Supreme Leader Khamenei was assassinated.

The standoff has now dragged into its fourth month, with the global economy absorbing a sustained energy shock and the US maintaining a naval blockade that has repeatedly skirted direct, wider war — including a May 28 Iranian retaliatory strike on a US base in Kuwait.

Key facts: - Terms include unrestricted Strait of Hormuz passage, lifting of the US port blockade, Iranian removal of sea mines within 30 days, and an Iranian commitment not to pursue nuclear weapons; roughly 440 kg of highly enriched uranium at 60% purity must be disposed of. - Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth: “They’re coming in our direction. The talks have been productive” — while warning the US military is “more strongly placed to resume combat than on day one.” - The US has redirected 116 vessels and disabled six ships attempting to breach the blockade over three months; the Treasury seized roughly $1 billion in Iranian cryptocurrency under the “Economic Fury” campaign. - Iran’s Strait of Hormuz authority, itself sanctioned, vowed operations would continue “without interruption”; a floating object believed to be a naval mine was sighted in the strait.

Sources CBS News Iran war live updates: US-Iran ceasefire deal close · Axios Iran peace deal awaits Trump approval · NPR US-Iran negotiations updates

2. Israel seizes Beaufort Castle in southern Lebanon

Israeli troops captured Beaufort Castle, the 900-year-old strategic ridge fortress in southern Lebanon, on May 31 — the deepest advance of the offensive, roughly 7 miles past the Litani River, despite a ceasefire nominally declared April 16. The seizure confirms that the truce has collapsed in all but name as Israel exploits the cover of the Iran war to press its campaign against Hezbollah.

Key facts: - 3,324 people killed and over 10,000 wounded since the conflict began March 2; more than 1 million displaced; an average of 11 children killed or wounded daily last week. - Saturday saw one of the heaviest days of Hezbollah rocket and drone fire toward northern Israel since the April ceasefire; Israel closed schools in border communities, capped outdoor gatherings at 50 people, and moved one hospital underground. - On May 19, Israeli airstrikes killed at least 22 people, including women and children, in Deir Qanoun an-Naher, Nabatieh and Kfar Sir; on May 22, six Lebanese paramedics were killed in two separate strikes. - Secretary of State Rubio held a call with Lebanese President Aoun blaming Hezbollah as “entirely responsible for the ongoing fighting.” Hezbollah was never a formal signatory to the truce, which was extended for 45 days on May 15.

Sources US News / AP Israel troops capture Beaufort Castle in southern Lebanon · CFR Israel-Lebanon ceasefire extended

3. Colombia votes in presidential first round; Cepeda leads

An estimated 41.4 million Colombians were eligible to vote May 31 in the first round of a presidential election widely framed as a referendum on outgoing leftist President Gustavo Petro’s four-year term — the first left-wing presidency in Colombian history. With 14 candidates contesting, a runoff between the top two is scheduled for June 21 if no one wins a majority. The new president takes office August 7; Petro is constitutionally barred from re-election.

Key facts: - Iván Cepeda of Petro’s Historic Pact polled at 44.6%; independent celebrity lawyer Abelardo de la Espriella at 31.6%; conservative Paloma Valencia of the Democratic Center at 14% (Invamer/Noticias Caracol, mid-May). - US Senator Bernie Moreno traveled to Colombia ahead of the vote; the US Embassy issued an election-day security alert. - The campaign has unfolded against the backdrop of failed peace negotiations with FARC dissidents and persistent inequality — the material record of Petro’s “leftism.”

Sources Americas Quarterly Colombia: Meet the candidates · CEPR Colombia’s presidential election: What you need to know

4. Russian drone hits Romanian apartment block — first civilian casualties on NATO soil

A Russian drone targeting Ukrainian positions crashed into a 10-story apartment building in Galați, eastern Romania — about 10 miles from the Ukrainian border — injuring two people and igniting a roof fire. It is the 28th confirmed drone breach of Romanian airspace since Russia’s 2022 invasion, but the first to injure civilians inside an allied state.

Key facts: - Romania’s president expelled Russia’s consul and ordered the closure of the consulate in Constanța; the Foreign Ministry called the crash “a serious violation of international law.” - Romania did not shoot the drone down, exposing NATO’s unresolved rules of engagement: downing Russian drones risks Article 5 escalation, but inaction exposes civilians. - European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen: “Russia’s war of aggression has crossed yet another line.” NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte called it “a danger to us all.”

Sources CNN Russian drone hits Romania near Ukraine border · Washington Post Russian drone hits residential building in Romania, posing test for NATO · WSWS European warmongers seize on Russian drone crash in Romania to escalate war

5. Gaza aid collapses ~80% under cover of the Iran war

Aid to Gaza has fallen by roughly 80% since Israel closed all aid corridors when the Iran war began in late February 2026, citing security. OCHA recorded an overall 37% decline in aid in the first three months of 2026. The October 2025 ceasefire nominally holds, but Human Rights Watch documented Israel curbing aid and killing civilians in May.

Key facts: - Six months into the ceasefire, UN partners had collected ~293,600 metric tons of aid — a 72% increase over the pre-ceasefire period — but the corridor closures are reversing those gains. - Food aid now reaches just over 1.1 million people monthly, far below need; the UN warns famine risk is re-emerging after being averted. - The UN Security Council held an open debate on consolidating the ceasefire and halting West Bank settlement expansion.

Sources Human Rights Watch Gaza: Israel curbs aid, kills civilians during ceasefire · OCHA Gaza humanitarian response situation report no. 66

6. Ukraine strikes Russian port and airbase amid record drone barrage

Ukraine struck Russia’s Taganrog seaport and a southern airbase on May 30, setting a tanker, a fuel-storage tank and an administrative building ablaze; no casualties were reported. The strikes came as the air war reached one of its highest single-day intensities of the conflict.

Key facts: - Russia launched 8,549 kamikaze drones and 277 guided aerial bombs in the 24 hours ending May 30, alongside 7 missiles and 91 airstrikes; 291 combat engagements were recorded in a single day. - Earlier in May, Ukraine struck a Russian drone training camp, killing 65 cadets — a notable blow to Russia’s drone program. - The Romania incident (Story 4) underscores how the escalating air war increasingly threatens NATO territory.

Sources Bloomberg Tanker catches fire in drone attack on Russia’s Taganrog port · EMPR Russia-Ukraine war updates, May 30, 2026

Myanmar’s coup architect assumes “civilian” presidency

Min Aung Hlaing, who overthrew Aung San Suu Kyi in the February 2021 coup, removed his military uniform and was sworn in as Myanmar’s “civilian” president on April 10, following a sham election held December 2025–January 2026. Open sources documented 408 military air attacks killing at least 170 civilians during the vote. The “civilian veneer” is aimed at winning regional legitimacy and easing sanctions, and several ASEAN states are engaging the new government.

  • The junta now controls only 21% of Myanmar’s territory; resistance forces and ethnic armies hold 42%, with the rest contested.
  • A new opposition umbrella, the Steering Council for the Emergence of a Federal Democratic Union (SCEF), unites the largest armed groups around a federal-democracy platform.
  • Crisis Group and analysts warn Myanmar has “slipped down the global crisis hierarchy” as attention shifts to Iran, Ukraine and Lebanon.

China-Taiwan coast guard standoff at Pratas Island

A China Coast Guard vessel entered the restricted waters of Pratas/Dongsha Island in late May, forcing a Taiwan Coast Guard response and an “intense verbal confrontation over sovereignty via radio” — the sixth such intrusion this year across four CCG vessels. Beijing has escalated grey-zone operations since Trump’s May 13–15 summit with Xi Jinping, probing the limits of US commitments.

  • The PLA conducted exercises east of the Luzon Strait with a Type 055 destroyer, a Type 052D destroyer, a Type 054A frigate and an auxiliary vessel.
  • Hegseth struck a “more moderate tone” on US-China relations at a regional security forum and notably declined to raise Taiwan directly.
  • Analysts at the Global Taiwan Institute warn Pratas may be the next target after China’s 2026 South China Sea consolidation.

DOJ launches criminal probe into E. Jean Carroll

The Justice Department has opened a criminal investigation into E. Jean Carroll, 82, examining whether she committed perjury by testifying in 2022 that she received no outside funding for her suit against Trump — when Reid Hoffman’s nonprofit later paid some legal fees. Investigators are pursuing possible money laundering, obstruction and conspiracy charges tied to a Hoffman-founded trust; the probe is run out of the US Attorney’s Office for the Northern District of Illinois.

  • Carroll won $5 million in 2023 (sexual abuse liability) and $83 million in 2024 (defamation), totaling $88 million in civil judgments against Trump.
  • Acting AG Todd Blanche — who previously represented Trump in appeals tied to Carroll’s lawsuits — has recused himself, exposing the conflict at the heart of the case.
  • The same day, a federal judge declined to block Trump’s executive order tightening mail-in voting rules.

Labor share of income hits record low

New data show workers’ share of US economic output fell to 51% in Q1 2026 — the lowest since tracking began in 1947 — while corporate profits reached 12.1% of gross domestic income, the highest since 1950. As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, “Both major political parties have enabled this redistribution upward.”

  • Adjusted for inflation, hourly wages rose 3% since end-2019 while corporate profits surged 50%; quarterly wage growth (0.8%) trailed profit growth (2.7%).
  • S&P 500 earnings jumped 28% year-over-year; the “Magnificent 7” grew profits 63%.
  • Salesforce CEO Marc Benioff, on cutting staff from 9,000 to 5,000: “I need less heads.”

Science, technology, and public health

  • Measles tops 1,983 cases — worst US year in decades; kindergarten vaccination coverage has slipped to 92.5%
  • First severe human H5N5 infection reported in NEJM — a previously uncharacterized avian-flu subtype causes critical illness in a 75-year-old
  • Trump pulls AI security executive order — pre-deployment cyber-review gate dropped hours before signing as CAISI signs voluntary deals

Measles continues its worst US year in decades: as of May 28, the CDC reported 1,983 confirmed cases across 40 jurisdictions, with 93% (1,847) outbreak-associated across 30 discrete outbreaks; no deaths so far in 2026. Kindergarten vaccination coverage has slipped to 92.5% (from 95.2% in 2019–20), leaving an estimated 286,000 children unprotected and sustaining transmission chains — a direct product of the anti-vaccine policy now entrenched in the federal public health apparatus.

In avian influenza, the NEJM published the first documented severe human H5N5 infection: a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman developed acute hypoxemic respiratory failure after an initially negative influenza A nasal swab. H5N5 was a previously uncharacterized subtype in humans, broadening the roster of avian-flu variants capable of critical illness. Separately, the CDC’s H5N1 human case count reached 71 since 2024 across 13 states (2 deaths, 3 of unknown exposure); conjunctivitis remains the predominant presentation, with no human-to-human transmission detected.

Roundup: - COVID-19 is at its lowest level since spring 2021: national test positivity 0.83%, 0.2 hospitalizations per 100,000, and record-low wastewater readings. A new variant, BA.3.2 (“Cicada”), shows slow growth in the Northeast, with upticks in Georgia, Florida, Louisiana and Hawaii. (The Sick Times) - Long COVID: A large neuroimaging study found no evidence of widespread brain inflammation in patients; instead, the most severe cases showed heightened hippocampus and amygdala activity, redirecting therapy toward stress/emotional regulation rather than anti-inflammatories. (ScienceDaily) - Brain aging: Stanford researchers, using the turquoise killifish, found ribosomes collide and stall during translation elongation in aging brains, promoting aggregates linked to Alzheimer’s, ALS and Parkinson’s — identifying a druggable bottleneck more precise than amyloid or tau. (ScienceDaily) - AI policy: A planned White House executive order requiring frontier developers to submit models for federal cybersecurity review before release was pulled hours before the May 21 signing, with Trump citing competitiveness. Meanwhile the government’s AI safety institute (CAISI) signed voluntary pre-deployment evaluation deals with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI — replacing a mandatory gate with softer oversight. - AI and labor: OpenAI released a 13-page “Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age” proposing a public AI wealth fund, a 32-hour/4-day workweek pilot, and broad low-cost AI access — even as Altman and Amodei walked back earlier “jobs apocalypse” predictions ahead of blockbuster IPOs, drawing criticism that it is regulatory positioning, not governance.

Arts and culture

The 79th Festival de Cannes closed with the Palme d’Or awarded to Fjord, a Norway-set drama by Cristian Mungiu (a previous Palme winner for 4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days), starring Sebastian Stan and Renate Reinsve. The choice of a director whose work has consistently dramatized the corrosive pressures of bureaucracy, poverty and moral compromise on ordinary people is significant at a moment of war and deepening social crisis — a counterweight to the festival’s celebrity spectacle, which this year included honorary Palmes for Peter Jackson, John Travolta and Barbra Streisand.

The jury, presided over by Park Chan-wook and including Demi Moore, Ruth Negga, Chloé Zhao and Stellan Skarsgård, distributed its major acting prizes in ways worth noting: - Best Actor went jointly to Valentin Campagne and Emmanuel Macchia in Lukas Dhont’s Coward, continuing Dhont’s preoccupation with masculinity, shame and adolescent vulnerability. - Best Actress was shared by Virginie Efira and Tao Okamoto for Ryusuke Hamaguchi’s All of a Sudden — Hamaguchi again probing chance, intimacy and the fragility of bourgeois domestic life. - Best Screenplay went to Emmanuel Marre’s A Man of His Time.

The festival unfolds against the backdrop of an industry reshaped by streaming consolidation, AI anxieties and the political pressures of the present conjuncture. Cannes has historically been a venue where serious filmmaking — engaging war, class and historical memory — competes for attention against the marketing machinery of the culture industry. The honorary tributes to aging Hollywood and blockbuster figures reflect that tension: genuine artistic achievement (Mungiu, Hamaguchi, Dhont) sharing a stage with the nostalgia economy.

Broader cultural notes: - The selection of European auteurs working in a humanist, socially attentive register — rather than the nationalist or militarist themes increasingly promoted in state-backed cultural production — is a welcome sign, though the festival remains an elite marketplace insulated from the conditions of working-class audiences priced out of cinema. - The continued prominence of films interrogating private life and moral failure (Hamaguchi, Dhont) points to a cinema reaching for psychological truth in the absence of a broader political vocabulary — a limitation, not merely a virtue, of much contemporary art-house work.

The task for a Marxist criticism is to assess these works not as festival trophies or lifestyle content but in their social and historical context: what they reveal about consciousness under capitalism in crisis, and where they retreat from the great questions of war, inequality and historical reckoning that define the moment.

The Nexteer rebellion: The working class vs the trade union apparatus — Perspective

This Perspective frames the rejection by Nexteer auto-parts workers of a pro-company contract — for the third time in two months — as a concentrated expression of the wider rebellion of the working class against the trade union apparatus. The key political conclusion, useful for editorial discussion, is the contrast drawn between the rank and file’s repeated, conscious votes and the UAW bureaucracy’s collusion with management: the apparatus functions not as an organ of struggle but as an instrument of labor discipline. The piece connects the Nexteer fight to the WSWS/IWA-RFC campaign for rank-and-file committees independent of the bureaucracy and to the international wave of opposition to the union-management partnership. It situates the local revolt within the broader political economy of declining labor share and AI-driven layoffs (see the Damon analysis cited in the US section).

After third contract rejection, Nexteer workers denounce UAW-management collusion — WSWS reporters

A companion report providing on-the-ground worker testimony absent from the Perspective. Unique content for the editorial board: direct quotations from Nexteer workers expressing distrust of UAW leadership after three rejected contracts, frustration at being kept in the dark on contract terms, and concern over tiered wages and forced overtime. The report documents the role of rank-and-file organizing in sustaining the “no” votes against the apparatus’s pressure to ratify, and ties the dispute to parallel struggles across the auto-parts sector.

European warmongers seize on Russian drone crash in Romania to escalate war — Jordan Shilton

The factual core of this article is covered in Top Story 4. Labor share of income hits record low as corporate profits soar — Andre Damon

The headline data and the Benioff quote are integrated into the US section. Kenyan court blocks US offshore Ebola camp as epidemic accelerates across central Africa — Evan Blake

Kenya’s High Court on May 29 froze a US plan to open a 50-bed offshore Ebola facility at Laikipia Air Base — built by the US military and staffed by US Public Health Service officers — with Judge Patricia Nyaundi barring any such facility pending a June 2 hearing. The article frames the camp as an imperialist response prioritizing border control over medical aid: the US has invoked Title 42 to bar travelers from affected regions and flew an infected American doctor to Germany rather than treating him domestically. Key data: suspected/confirmed Ebola cases exceeded 1,200 with ~250 deaths as of May 27 — roughly doubling in one week from 551 — making this the third-largest outbreak in history, even as donor pledges collapsed from $500 million to $290 million in four days and the US cut 90% of USAID funding over 15 months. Georgetown’s Lawrence Gostin called the scheme “reckless, unethical & possibly unlawful”; a WHO official warned, “When you cannot trace the contacts…you cannot stop the transmission chain.” The article’s political conclusion: the dismantling of global public-health infrastructure under both parties has manufactured the conditions for catastrophe, and only an internationally coordinated, working-class response can contain emerging epidemics.

1. Oil company windfall profits as working families face fuel poverty

European governments are drafting “fuel shortage” protocols with no mention of price controls or windfall taxes — a clear class line worth exposing.

A Cepeda victory would extend that record; the WSWS should interrogate the class content behind the “Historic Pact” label.

Reid Hoffman’s philanthropy is being recast as “money laundering,” and acting AG Blanche’s recusal exposes that the prosecuting office is run by Trump’s former personal attorney.
