1. US-Iran ceasefire extension stalls as Trump withholds approval

US and Iranian negotiators reached a tentative agreement to extend the three-month-old ceasefire by 60 days and open fresh nuclear talks, but President Trump’s Situation Room meeting on May 29 ended without a decision. Under the draft terms, Iran would clear all mines from the Strait of Hormuz within 30 days; in return, Washington would gradually lift its naval blockade on Iranian ports and relax sanctions to permit increased Iranian oil exports.

The central obstacle remains Iran’s stockpile of 440.9 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60 percent. Trump opposes transferring the material to either China or Russia, and no third-party custodian has been agreed. Tehran further demands that any deal halt Israeli military operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon — a condition Washington has not accepted.

Key facts:

  • Vice President JD Vance: “It’s hard to say exactly when or if the president’s going to sign.”
  • Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, speaking at the Shangri-La Dialogue in Singapore on May 30, said the US is “more than capable” of resuming strikes.
  • An adviser to Iran’s supreme leader accused Trump of “betraying diplomacy for the third time” by maintaining the blockade while negotiating.
  • Iranian forces fired ballistic missiles at Kuwait and launched drones toward the Strait on May 29, even as talks continued.

The WSWS has tracked the US-Israel war on Iran since February as a calculated imperialist war drive, not a misadventure — the negotiation posture is a tactic within that drive, not a departure from it.

2. Israel pushes Lebanon war north of the Litani; one million displaced

For the first time, the Israeli military issued an evacuation order on May 26 for Nabatieh, a city north of the Litani River, alongside a second order for Mashgara in the Bekaa Valley — a sharp geographic escalation of the 2026 Lebanon war. UNIFIL recorded 91 Israeli airspace violations on May 26 alone, the highest single-day total since a ceasefire nominally took effect April 17, with 399 total firing incidents logged against 11 Hezbollah projectile trajectories.

Key facts:

  • Nine attacks on healthcare facilities occurred May 21–24, killing eight health workers and injuring 45; 25 medical staff were wounded in a single strike on Hiram Hospital on May 23. A Civil Defense Center in Nabatieh was destroyed the same day.
  • Netanyahu declared: “Our forces crossed the Litani… We are operating in Beirut as well, in the Beqaa as well, across the entire front, and striking Hezbollah hard.”
  • Approximately 1 million people remain displaced across Lebanon since hostilities escalated March 2; families spent the night in vehicles in Beirut as shelters filled.
  • More than 2,000 civilians and combatants have been killed since March.

The repeated targeting of protected medical infrastructure constitutes a documented pattern of war crimes under the Geneva Conventions, enabled by uninterrupted US arms transfers to Israel.

Sources UN News Middle East update · Al Jazeera Israel-Lebanon coverage · Times of Israel May 27 liveblog

3. Russia mounts largest aerial assault on Ukraine since 2022

On May 24, Russia launched 367 drones and missiles in a single day against multiple Ukrainian cities — the largest aerial barrage since the full-scale invasion began in February 2022 — killing at least 12 civilians, including three children. On May 20, strikes on Dnipro killed at least two civilians and destroyed a UNHCR-contracted warehouse holding roughly 900 pallets of humanitarian aid valued at over $1 million.

Key facts:

  • A Russian drone that deviated from its path crashed into an apartment building in eastern Romania, injuring two — an incident on NATO-member territory.
  • Ukraine lost its foothold in Russia’s Kursk region earlier this spring after a major Russian counteroffensive; the front has stabilized with Ukrainian forces on the defensive.
  • UNHCR “strongly condemned” the Dnipro strikes as deliberate targeting of civilian infrastructure.

The intensification comes as Washington’s attention and resources are absorbed by the Iran and Lebanon fronts, underscoring the simultaneous, interlocking character of the imperialist war drive the ICFI has analyzed as a single global eruption.

4. Ebola epidemic accelerates across central Africa; Kenyan court blocks US offshore camp

A rare-strain Ebola epidemic is accelerating across central Africa, with the WHO Director-General traveling to Kinshasa on May 30 to oversee the response. The outbreak, caused by the Bundibugyo strain — for which existing vaccines are ineffective — has reached over 1,200 suspected and confirmed cases and roughly 250 deaths as of late May, already the third-largest Ebola outbreak in history.

In a major development, Kenya’s High Court froze a Trump administration plan to establish a 50-bed offshore Ebola facility at Laikipia Air Base (~125 miles north of Nairobi), barring the government from “establishing, operationalizing, facilitating, approving or permitting” any such arrangement pending a June 2 hearing.

Key facts:

  • International pledges collapsed from $500 million to $290 million within four days.
  • The Trump administration has cut USAID funding by 90 percent over 15 months and withdrawn from the WHO.
  • The DRC confirmed its FIFA World Cup delegation is in compliance with US Ebola protocols as the US co-hosts the 2026 tournament.

As Evan Blake wrote on the WSWS, the offshore facility plan is driven by xenophobic “keep them out” logic rather than public health, and the funding collapse is a direct consequence of the imperialist dismantling of global health infrastructure.

Sources Democracy Now! May 28 headlines · Daily Current Affairs WHO director visits Kinshasa · WSWS Kenyan court blocks US offshore Ebola camp

5. US labor share of income hits record low as profits soar

New Commerce Department data shows workers’ compensation fell to 51 percent of US gross domestic income in Q1 2026 — the lowest since tracking began in 1947 — while corporate profits hit 12.1 percent, their highest since 1950. The figures were reported in the Wall Street Journal and analyzed on the WSWS by Andre Damon as confirmation that capitalism systematically transfers wealth from labor to capital, now accelerated by AI-driven displacement.

Key facts:

  • In Q1 alone, worker pay rose 0.8 percent while corporate profits rose 2.7 percent.
  • Since 2019, inflation-adjusted hourly wages grew 3 percent while profits surged 50 percent.
  • The “Magnificent 7” tech giants grew profits 63 percent; Nvidia became the first company in history to reach a $5 trillion valuation.
  • Companies cited AI directly for 49,135 job cuts in the first four months of 2026.
  • Billionaires now number 3,428 holding $20.1 trillion; the bottom half of Americans hold 2.5 percent of wealth against the top 1 percent’s 31.7 percent.

Sources BEA Q1 2026 GDP and corporate profits · WSWS Labor share of income hits record low

6. Sudan’s military accuses UAE and Ethiopia of drone strike on Khartoum airport

Sudan’s armed forces publicly accused the United Arab Emirates and Ethiopia of carrying out a drone strike on Khartoum International Airport and vowed retaliation — a major escalation threatening to widen a war that has already displaced more than 10 million people, the largest internal displacement crisis on Earth. In the Kordofan region, near-daily drone strikes are inflicting substantial civilian casualties, with markets, health facilities and residential areas repeatedly hit.

Key facts:

  • Ethiopia and Eritrea, having “traded barbs for months,” may be edging toward direct war, which would compound instability across the Horn of Africa.
  • The UN has flagged South Sudan, Sudan and northern Ethiopia as facing greater than 60 percent probability of below-normal rainfall — a famine-risk multiplier atop active conflict.
  • Russian/Wagner-backed RSF forces remain active; Ukraine has reportedly conducted counterinfluence operations across Africa, adding a further geopolitical layer.

7. State Department purge leaves diplomatic vacuum amid multiple wars

The State Department finalized the firing of nearly 250 foreign service officers this month, with scores of senior diplomats saying they were “forced out” amid simultaneous active crises in Iran, Lebanon, Gaza, Ukraine and Sudan. Multiple assistant secretary posts remain vacant or filled by unconfirmed acting officials, raising concerns about the institutional capacity to conduct the very negotiations required to end the Iran war.

Key facts:

  • Roughly 60 US diplomats have signed public statements warning the purge damages national security; several resigned in protest.
  • Trump, Hegseth and Vance are simultaneously redirecting focus to 2026 midterm campaigning, with the Iran war as a backdrop to domestic messaging.
  • The gutting eliminates consular staff who process visas and assist working-class Americans in distress abroad — a class dimension obscured in establishment coverage.

India and US sign critical minerals framework

India and the United States signed a framework cooperation agreement on critical minerals and rare earths in New Delhi, covering mining, processing and recycling of materials vital to defense and green-technology supply chains. The partnership explicitly aims to diversify supply chains away from China, which dominates global rare-earth processing at roughly 85–90 percent of capacity.

The deal follows the May 14–15 Trump-Xi Beijing summit, where China agreed to expand purchases of US goods including at least $17 billion per year in US agricultural products across 2026–2028. The minerals — used heavily in weapons systems, drones and military electronics — make the agreement a militarized supply-chain realignment that locks India deeper into the US-China rivalry.

EU clears hurdle toward US trade pact

The European Union cleared a key legislative hurdle toward a US-EU trade pact reducing import duties on American goods. The framework includes a safeguard mechanism allowing the EU to suspend tariff cuts if US imports threaten European industry — a signal of persistent transatlantic trade tension even amid cooperation. The pact unfolds against a backdrop of UNCTAD projections of decelerating global trade volumes in 2026.

US measles cases approach 2,000, elimination status at risk

As of May 28, the CDC reported 1,983 confirmed measles cases across 40 jurisdictions, with 30 active outbreaks; 93 percent of cases were outbreak-associated and roughly 92 percent occurred in unvaccinated individuals or those of unknown status. The US risks losing its official measles elimination status, to be reviewed in November 2026, as MMR coverage in children has slipped from 95.2 percent (2019–20) to 92.5 percent (2024–25). The crisis is mirrored internationally: Peru declared a 90-day emergency after 301 confirmed and 1,403 suspected cases, with Puno region vaccination collapsing from 70 percent to just 16 percent — a failure the WSWS attributes to IMF/World Bank austerity gutting public health.

Phase 3 trial: bepirovirsen “functionally cures” 1-in-5 hepatitis B patients

In the global B-Well Phase 3 trial (1,834 participants, 29 countries) published in the NEJM, 19–20 percent of patients achieved a functional cure (sustained HBsAg loss plus undetectable viral DNA) after 24 weeks of the RNA-targeting drug bepirovirsen, versus 0 percent on placebo and ~3 percent with the current decade-long standard of care. It is the first Phase 3 evidence that fixed-duration therapy can durably suppress hepatitis B, which chronically infects ~300 million people worldwide. An FDA priority-review decision is expected in Q3 2026.

H5N1 fatal child case in Bangladesh; 71 US human cases

The WHO tracked 13 avian/swine influenza human cases in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh. The US has logged 71 total A(H5) human cases since February 2024, concentrated among dairy and poultry workers, with no sustained human-to-human transmission detected. Scientists warn the virus is circulating in more species and continents than at any prior point, raising pandemic risk if it adapts to efficient human spread.

COVID wastewater at lowest since spring 2021

SARS-CoV-2 wastewater concentration is at its lowest recorded level since spring 2021, with national test positivity at 0.83 percent and hospitalizations near 0.2 per 100,000. However, cases are growing in Georgia, Florida and Louisiana as heat pushes people indoors; the dominant variant is XFG.1.1 (32 percent of sequences), while the emerging BA.3.2 (“Cicada”) sub-variant shows slow growth in the Northeast.

EU AI Act amended; Trump pivots toward AI oversight

The EU’s “Digital Omnibus” package — the first amendments to the AI Act since June 2024 — pushes high-risk AI system compliance deadlines from August 2026 to December 2027 (a 16-month deferral) and adds two new prohibited practices: AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) and child sexual abuse material (CSAM). Separately, the Trump administration is now considering formal oversight of frontier AI models — a policy it previously rejected — driven by national-security concerns over Anthropic’s new “Mythos” model.

Arts and culture

The most politically significant cultural development of the day is a federal judge’s ruling blocking the Kennedy Center’s board from renaming the venue after Donald Trump and from shuttering the complex for a two-year “renovation.” The case is a direct front in the administration’s drive to subordinate public cultural institutions to the cult of the leader — the same impulse behind the purge of the Kennedy Center board earlier in Trump’s second term. The ruling preserves, at least temporarily, the institutional independence and identity of one of the country’s premier arts venues, but the underlying assault on publicly funded culture continues.

The day’s most substantial WSWS cultural contribution is John Andrews’s centenary retrospective on Miles Davis (born May 26, 1926), examined below in the WSWS section. The Metropolitan Opera’s filmed production of El Último Sueño de Frida y Diego — exploring the relationship between Frida Kahlo and Diego Rivera, both communists whose art was inseparable from revolutionary politics — opened in cinemas nationwide on May 29, extending the Met’s effort to widen opera’s audience while spotlighting two figures central to twentieth-century Latin American art and Marxist cultural history.

Roundup:

  • Film: Lionsgate’s musical comedy Power Ballad (Paul Rudd, Nick Jonas; 88% on Rotten Tomatoes) opened against A24’s surrealist horror Backrooms over Memorial Day weekend — a dual-genre summer launch reflecting Hollywood’s continued reliance on broad four-quadrant product and franchise-adjacent horror amid an industry still contracting after the strikes.
  • Television: HBO’s Emmy-winning Hacks aired its series finale, closing the arc of Deborah Vance and Ava — one of the more genuinely observed comedies of prestige TV’s late phase, attentive to class and generational tension in the culture industry.
  • Streaming: An A24 mockumentary starring Charli XCX as a fictionalized version of herself, set during “Brat Summer” and co-starring Alexander Skarsgård, began streaming on HBO Max on May 29 — a self-referential extension of pop branding into scripted narrative that exemplifies the blurring of celebrity persona and commodity.
  • Music: Wyclef Jean released the single “Mr. October” ahead of his June 26 album Clef Notes, the first of an ambitious seven-album series, Quantum Leap — a rare large-scale long-form statement from a major artist.

Across these releases, the dominant tendency remains the commodification of personality and nostalgia over works engaging seriously with war, inequality or historical memory. The exceptions — the Kahlo-Rivera opera and the Davis retrospective — point backward to periods when art was bound up with mass social struggle, a connection the WSWS argues contemporary cultural life has largely lost.

WSWS coverage — last 24 hours

Perspective published: The Nexteer rebellion: The working class vs the trade union apparatus by the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees (URL confirms today’s date, /2026/05/30/) — read in full on wsws.org.

The Perspective treats the contract rebellion at Nexteer Automotive in Saginaw, Michigan as a signal event in the broader conflict between the working class and the trade union apparatus. Workers rejected three successive tentative agreements — by 96.2 percent (April 2), 73 percent (May 14), and 55–45 percent (May 28–29, production workers at 59 percent) — and authorized a strike by 86 percent on May 20–21, yet the UAW has refused to call any work stoppage. The IWA-RFC argues the apparatus functions as “labor police,” citing UAW officials collecting $3.2 million in 2024, with president Shawn Fain receiving $274,407, and a court-appointed monitor’s documentation of officials’ threats of violence. At a May 18 meeting, International servicing representative Jason Tuck allegedly “cursed workers and threatened plant closure if they struck,” then walked out. The piece draws parallels to a 41-day New York nurses’ strike and a three-week Colorado meatpacking strike, both shut down without contracts workers could review. It sharply criticizes the DSA and Jacobin for promoting union leadership despite this record, and calls for rank-and-file committees independent of the apparatus, coordination with American Axle workers, and transparent access to contract language.

After third contract rejection, Nexteer workers denounce UAW-management collusion — By our reporter

On-the-ground interviews add worker testimony to the Perspective’s analysis. A 16-year veteran called the five-year deal’s $3.00 raise “a slap in the face.” A younger worker reported intensified management harassment after each rejection and asked, “What are we paying the union for, if they let management” violate contract language? A 20-year veteran called for solidarity with American Axle workers, noting a strike “would shut down GM and Stellantis for sure.” UAW Local 699 warned members to keep working and threatened arbitration against unauthorized action — concrete confirmation of the bureaucracy’s role as enforcer against its own members.

Sri Lankan court case continues over violent attack on SEP members — Our reporters

Moratuwa Magistrate’s Court continued proceedings over a November 2023 assault on SEP members Dehin Wasantha and Lakshman Fernando, attacked with wooden poles while distributing leaflets for meetings commemorating the centenary of Trotskyism. The defense sought to portray the SEP as a foreign-funded NGO; Fernando testified the party is legally registered, contests elections, and is funded by “collections and donations made by party members, supporters, political contacts, and through open political campaigns.” The accused held positions in a trade union affiliated with the former ruling party. The WSWS frames the attack and the NGO smear as political repression aimed at delegitimizing independent socialist organizing — a method the editorial board will recognize across multiple national contexts.

Australian unions endorse Labor’s war and austerity budget — Martin Scott

The ACTU praised the Labor government’s federal budget as “fairer for workers,” citing property tax and capital gains tweaks the WSWS shows are cosmetic: the impact is only 7,500 additional home purchases annually (0.026% of the population), and $250 in promised tax relief won’t arrive until mid-2028. Meanwhile the budget contains $63.8 billion in social spending cuts over four years, over half targeting the NDIS, with 300,000 people facing removal from disability support and 28,000 public sector jobs eliminated — against a $60 billion annual military budget. Individual unions including the Health Services Union and Australian Services Union are accused of celebrating cuts affecting their own members, confirming the union apparatus as the government’s instrument for suppressing opposition to austerity and militarism.

Australia: Victoria’s Labor government oversees police-state raids against anti-war protesters — Eric Ludlow

Victoria’s Labor government under Premier Jacinta Allan deployed counter-terrorism police in three waves of raids against anti-genocide protesters: a May 12 single-home raid; April 17 raids involving ~50 officers against a theatrical US consulate protest; and the March 27 “Zelda 8” simultaneous raids of eight women, including elderly women handcuffed in nightclothes and denied medication. November 2025 legislation expanded police powers to unmask protesters, and a new federal Combatting Antisemitism, Hate and Extremism Act lowers the prosecution threshold from inciting violence to “promoting hatred.” The article connects the repression to Labor’s simultaneous NDIS cuts — the same state apparatus silencing dissent and disciplining workers.

Students protest against New Zealand budget cuts — Tom Peters

Thousands of students protested across New Zealand on May 28 against a coalition budget eliminating “fees-free” third-year tuition (worth up to $12,000) and authorizing a 6 percent fee increase. Total student debt stands at $16.1 billion. The budget also raises social housing rent 20 percent, bars most 18–19-year-olds from unemployment benefits despite 15 percent youth unemployment, cuts ~8,700 government jobs, and raises the retirement age to 67 — while military spending doubles from 1 to 2 percent of GDP. A midwifery student, Evangelia, said: “We’re constantly being told… the road to success is through getting degrees, then they do absolutely nothing to support us.” Labor and Green representatives addressed the rally but offered no genuine alternative.

Miles Davis at 100, a complex figure reflecting a complex time — John Andrews

Marking the centenary of Davis’s birth, Andrews traces the trumpeter from bebop with Charlie Parker through the “Birth of the Cool” Capitol sessions with Gil Evans, the modal revolution of Kind of Blue (1959, the best-selling jazz album in history), and the electrified fusion of the late 1960s–70s. Davis’s dictum — “Don’t play what’s there, play what’s not there” — captures his emphasis on attitude over technical execution. The piece does not evade his contradictions: his physical abuse of first wife Frances Taylor, his aloof persona while colleagues struggled financially, and his political conventionality (performing at a 1961 benefit for an imperialist-aligned African relief foundation that Max Roach protested). Andrews argues Davis’s artistic peak coincided with postwar labor militancy and the civil rights movement, and that his later retreat mirrored broader cultural decline through the Reagan era — closing with the suggestion that a renewed leftward movement could again generate conditions for transformative art.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s “We Need a Left-Labor Presidential Candidate” uses Zohran Mamdani’s NYC mayoral win to argue for running a “left-labor” candidate in the 2028 Democratic presidential primary — classic DSA electoralism that funnels working-class energy back into the bourgeois Democratic Party rather than toward independent political organization. “The Surveillance Economy Is Here. This Is How We Fight Back,” written by NYC’s Department of Consumer and Worker Protection commissioner, offers a pure regulation-and-reform framing with no challenge to the capitalist state — a national-reformist program presented as resistance. “For Iranians, Despair Is Not Strategy” reasonably critiques pro-Pahlavi monarchist diaspora politics but offers no socialist alternative. The day’s orientation confirms Jacobin’s role as the literary organ of Democratic Party “realignment,” precisely the perspective the IWA-RFC Perspective named today as disorienting workers at Nexteer.

Left Voice

Left Voice published the most consistent critiques of left-electoralism among US tendencies. “Promises, Promises: AOC, Israel, and the DSA” sharply attacks AOC’s capitulations on Palestine funding votes and the DSA’s refusal to discipline her. “Inside a Self-Organized Blockade Committee to Oust Rodrigo Paz — Bolivia” covers the Bolivian uprising through the lens of self-organized rank-and-file committees, rejecting subordination to MAS or any bourgeois alternative. The May magazine highlights Minneapolis anti-ICE resistance as a model of working-class self-organization. The orientation toward direct action over parliamentary lobbying is genuine, but the PTS/FT tendency stops short of the Trotskyist conclusion — building a revolutionary party and a section of an international — leaving its “self-organization” as a method without a programmatic destination.

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL’s output combines real anti-imperialist reporting with campist political cover. “Democrats Back Anti-Immigration Policies in Attempt to Secure Funding for U.S. Imperialism” correctly exposes Democratic complicity in anti-immigrant repression, breaking with lesser-evilism. But “U.S. Government… Must Respect Venezuela’s Democracy” defends Maduro’s bourgeois-nationalist government as “democratic,” and the Raúl Castro statement offers uncritical solidarity with the Cuban state apparatus. These conflate opposition to imperialism with defense of capitalist and Stalinist-derived states that jail leftists and independent labor organizers — the hallmark of campism, which subordinates the working class to one bourgeois camp against another.

Socialist Alternative

SA’s “How My Non-Union Store Sicked Out” is a useful agitational report on direct worker action outside official union structures. But “Chicago’s Rush Hospital Nurses Win Union Vote” reports a 77 percent unionization vote straight as a class victory with no critique of the AFL-CIO apparatus — the uncritical trade unionism that defines the tendency. “Only Rotten Choices In Their System — We Need a Planned Economy” argues for socialist planning but frames it around what “a socialist government” would do, implying state management via electoral victory rather than workers’ control — a reformist-statist conception.

SWP (UK)

“Fight Reform UK with Class Struggle Not Lesser Evil Politics” is editorially sound, refusing the logic of voting Labour to stop the far right. But the SWP’s Stand Up To Racism front simultaneously campaigns alongside Labour officials, exposing the contradiction between line and practice. “SWP Conference 2026: Resisting the Right, Building the Left” frames strategy around “broad left” coalitions and anti-fascist united fronts — the broad-front methodology that persistently risks subordinating socialist politics to lowest-common-denominator alliances with reformist NGOs and Labour-aligned figures.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

Now publishing as The Communist via communist.red, the British IMT section passed “British Perspectives 2026” at its Third Congress, framing Starmer’s Labour as discrediting social democracy and opening space for revolutionary ideas. Notably it treats Zack Polanski’s Green surge as a potential left opportunity rather than a petty-bourgeois diversion. “Britain: May Elections 2026 — A Funeral for the Two-Party System” correctly diagnoses the collapse of Labour and Tory vote shares but admits the RCP ran no candidates and offered no independent working-class pole — strong analysis, absent intervention. The Militant legacy of decades inside Labour still shadows the turn to open party-building, substituting recruitment arithmetic for a concrete strategy rooted in workplace struggle.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Sudan’s drone war and the UAE-Ethiopia escalation threaten regional war

Sudan’s military has publicly accused the UAE and Ethiopia of striking Khartoum airport in a conflict that has displaced over 10 million — the world’s largest displacement crisis — yet receives a fraction of the coverage given Ukraine or the Middle East. A socialist analysis should expose the imperialist and regional powers fueling both the SAF and RSF, the role of Russian/Wagner forces, and the famine conditions spreading through Kordofan and Darfur.

Recommended reading Wilson Center Conflict in Sudan: regional and international actors

2. India-US critical minerals deal as a militarized supply-chain realignment

The New Delhi framework agreement is explicitly aimed at countering Chinese dominance of rare-earth processing — minerals used primarily in weapons systems, drones and military electronics. The WSWS should analyze what “supply-chain diversification” means for workers in all three countries and how it locks India deeper into the US war drive against China.

Recommended reading WEF Blockade diplomacy and geopolitical stories

3. Global oil-inventory drawdown is a class question

The Hormuz disruption has driven record depletion of global oil inventories. While the IMF and WEF frame this as a systemic risk, the direct consequence is rising energy and food costs falling hardest on the working class globally — even as energy corporations book windfall profits. The WSWS should examine the inventory drawdown alongside oil-major earnings.

Recommended reading IMF War darkens global economic outlook

4. DOJ criminalizes litigation financing in escalation against political opponents

The Justice Department’s criminal probe into the financing of E. Jean Carroll’s lawsuits — targeting a trust tied to donor Reid Hoffman — represents a qualitative escalation in the weaponization of prosecutorial power. The WSWS should situate this within the administration’s broader assault on democratic rights, the courts, and electoral access ahead of the 2026 midterms.

Recommended reading NBC News DOJ opens probe into Carroll lawsuit financing

5. Hepatitis B functional-cure trial and the question of access under capitalism

The NEJM Phase 3 bepirovirsen result — a functional cure for ~20 percent of patients with a disease infecting 300 million people — is a major scientific advance. The WSWS should examine the contradiction between this achievement of socialized scientific labor and the pricing, patent, and access barriers that will ration it under the profit system, especially in the Global South where hepatitis B is most prevalent.

Recommended reading Science New drug functionally cures many hepatitis B infections

6. UN climate report warns of five more years at record heat

The new UN report’s “nearly certain” projection of five more years at or near record temperatures, combined with 60-percent-plus below-normal rainfall probabilities across the Horn of Africa, points to compounding climate-and-conflict famine conditions. The WSWS should connect the war-driven disruption of energy markets to the stalled transition from fossil fuels and the incapacity of the capitalist nation-state system to address a global crisis.

Recommended reading WEF Global Risks Report 2026
