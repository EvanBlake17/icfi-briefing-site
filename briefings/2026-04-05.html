1. US-Iran war enters week 6: downed F-15 crew member rescued in “heavy firefight”

The US-Israeli war on Iran, now in its 37th day since surprise strikes killed Supreme Leader Khamenei on February 28, saw its worst single day of American air losses this week. Iranian air defenses shot down a US Air Force F-15E Strike Eagle and an A-10 Thunderbolt, with two rescue helicopters damaged during the subsequent recovery operation. US special operations forces conducted a ground rescue inside Iranian territory, recovering one weapons systems officer alive in what Trump described as a “heavy firefight involving dozens of aircraft.” The second crew member remains missing.

The escalation directly falsified Trump’s claim two days prior that Iran had “no antiaircraft equipment” and Defense Secretary Hegseth’s assertion that there was “almost nothing” Iran could do militarily. Trump responded by posting: “OPEN THE HORMUZ STRAIT, TAKE THE OIL, & MAKE A FORTUNE.”

Key facts: - US losses to date: 15 soldiers killed, 520+ wounded; 7 manned aircraft destroyed; Iran claims additional kills of a C-130 and 2 Black Hawks during the rescue - Iranian casualties (Iranian Health Ministry): at least 2,076 civilians killed, 26,500 injured; US/Israeli sources claim 6,000+ Iranian military killed - Civilian infrastructure: 30+ Iranian universities struck by US-Israeli air raids, including Shahid Beheshti University in Tehran on April 4; 85,000 civilian structures damaged; 3–4 million internally displaced - Regional escalation: Iran fired on Kuwait oil complexes; Spain closed its airspace to US military aircraft; Jordan has intercepted 261 Iranian missiles and drones in five weeks; Houthis formally entered the conflict March 28 - War spending: Estimated $2.1 billion per day in the first two weeks, totaling over $25 billion so far

2. Ukraine war: drone kills 5 at Nikopol market as Russia claims full Luhansk control

A Russian drone targeted a civilian market in Nikopol, Dnipropetrovsk Oblast on April 4, killing five civilians and injuring 27. Overnight exchanges of strikes between Russia and Ukraine killed 10 people total. Russia launched 286 drones against Ukraine over April 3–4, with Ukraine’s air force repelling the bulk of the attack.

In a major strategic development, Russia announced on April 1 that it has taken full control of Luhansk Oblast — one of four Ukrainian regions it illegally annexed in 2022. The Institute for the Study of War assessment from April 4 indicates Ukrainian forces remain active in Kharkiv Oblast, advancing in Kupyansk and Oleksandrivka directions, suggesting the situation is more contested than Moscow’s claims imply.

Key facts: - Ukrainian-claimed Russian losses since February 2022: approximately 1,301,260 personnel - Ukraine struck a Lukoil refinery; allies have asked Kyiv to pause refinery drone strikes as the Iran war drives up global fuel prices - US envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner expected to visit Kyiv this month for resumed peace talks - Ukraine struck at least one chemical plant in Samara Oblast, Russia

3. Trump’s $1.5 trillion military budget funds war through social counter-revolution

The Trump administration’s FY2027 budget request — the largest military appropriation in American history — proposes $1.5 trillion in Pentagon and war-related spending, a 44 percent increase over already record-breaking prior budgets. The proposal simultaneously calls for $73 billion in immediate cuts to domestic agencies covering housing, education, and climate programs, with deeper structural targeting of Social Security (serving ~70 million people), Medicare (68 million), and Medicaid (75 million).

The budget arrives as Trump’s tariff regime compounds the economic burden on working-class households. The Tax Foundation calculates the tariffs represent the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, costing an average of $1,500 per US household in 2026. By mid-year, consumers are expected to bear two-thirds of tariff costs through higher prices.

As Joseph Kishore argued in the WSWS Perspective, the budget represents “a budget for World War III” — not merely a spending shift but a class program financing imperialist war by dismantling social infrastructure. Trump himself stated in leaked remarks: “We can’t take care of day care, Medicaid, Medicare… We have to take care of one thing: military protection.”

Key facts: - Budget includes line items for advanced weapons systems, a new naval expansion program (“Trump-class battleship”), and preparation for confrontation with China - Democrats have provided crucial votes for military and intelligence budgets throughout Trump’s second term - Oil prices surging due to Strait of Hormuz disruption; Ukraine asked to pause refinery strikes to avoid compounding the energy price shock - Spain’s closure of airspace to US military aircraft signals deepening NATO-US friction over the Iran war

4. Cuba releases 2,010 prisoners under US economic pressure

Cuba’s government pardoned 2,010 prisoners during Easter Holy Week — one of the largest releases in nearly a decade and the second mass release this year amid ongoing Trump-Cuba negotiations. The releases came under intense US economic pressure: Washington has blocked oil imports, severed Cuba-Venezuela trade links, and threatened tariffs against Cuba’s trading partners, triggering an energy crisis that has disrupted hospitals and suspended flights.

The framing as a diplomatic win for Trump obscures the underlying humanitarian crisis. Cuba denies holding political prisoners and denies acting under foreign pressure. A Trump spokesperson demanded the release of “hundreds of other brave Cuban patriots,” while Cuba excluded from release anyone convicted of “crimes against authority” — a category human rights groups say encompasses political detainees.

Key facts: - Exclusions cover murder, sexual assault, drug crimes, theft, and “crimes against authority” - US had severed Cuba-Venezuela oil trade, imposed secondary tariff threats on Cuba’s trading partners - Hospital disruptions and flight suspensions reported due to energy shortages - The underlying economic warfare context is largely absent from mainstream US coverage

Sources Al Jazeera Cuba to pardon more than 2,000 prisoners amid US pressure · NPR Cuba releasing 2,010 prisoners as the US pressures the island’s government · Washington Post Cuba prisoner release amid Trump blockade

5. Artemis II crew passes midpoint en route to first crewed lunar flyby since 1972

The Artemis II crew — Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch (NASA), and Jeremy Hansen (CSA) — has passed the midpoint of humanity’s first crewed mission beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in December 1972. The SLS/Orion spacecraft launched April 1 from Kennedy Space Center and is on track for a lunar flyby on Monday, April 6, when the crew will photograph areas of the Moon’s far side never directly observed by human eyes.

Hansen will become the first Canadian — and first non-American — to leave Earth orbit. The 10-day mission is a test of Orion’s deep-space systems rather than a landing attempt. NASA released the crew’s first high-resolution Earth images from mid-transit on April 4.

Key facts: - Launch: April 1 at 6:35 p.m. EDT from Kennedy Space Center - Lunar flyby: Monday, April 6 - No landing — this tests Orion spacecraft deep-space life support, navigation, and communications - First crewed mission beyond low Earth orbit in 54 years

Sources NASA Artemis II flight day 3: crew prepares cabin for lunar flyby · Space.com Artemis 2 NASA Moon mission updates · NPR Artemis II: 10-day mission preview

6. Kosovo parliament approves troop deployment to US-led Gaza stabilization force

Kosovo’s parliament voted to deploy Kosovo Security Force (FSK) troops to Gaza under Trump’s “Board of Peace” stabilization framework. Five countries have now pledged troops: Indonesia, Morocco, Albania, Kosovo, and Kazakhstan. The mission mandate includes security provision, “demilitarization of Hamas fighters,” and guarding aid and reconstruction deliveries.

Kosovo PM Albin Kurti justified the deployment by citing NATO’s KFOR presence since 1999: “We ourselves have been and are beneficiaries of international forces.” Troop numbers have not been disclosed. The formation of a US-designed multinational force with a mandate to “demilitarize” the indigenous population raises fundamental questions about whether this constitutes a permanent military occupation of Palestinian territory under an international legitimacy veneer.

7. Pope Leo XIV leads first Easter vigil, calls for peace amid global wars

Pope Leo XIV presided over his first Easter Vigil at St. Peter’s Basilica on April 4, baptizing 10 new Catholics. His homily cited “mistrust, fear, selfishness, and resentment” as internal forces producing their outer consequences: “war, injustice, and the closing off of peoples and nations.” He called for “the Easter gifts of harmony and peace to grow and flourish everywhere” — widely interpreted as a reference to the Iran war and Ukraine.

Leo became the first pope in decades to personally carry the cross for all 14 Stations of the Cross on Good Friday. On Holy Thursday, he washed the feet of 12 priests, reversing Pope Francis’s practice of including laypeople — a signal to Catholic traditionalists. The new pope’s peace language remains notably general, avoiding direct naming of the US-Israeli war or the parties responsible.

South Korean factory fire investigation exposes serial safety failures

An investigation into the March 20 fire at Anjun Industrial in Daejeon that killed 14 workers and injured 60 has exposed systemic negligence: at least seven prior fires over 15 years, malfunctioning alarms and sprinklers, an illegal gym expansion blocking emergency exits (where nine victims died), and 101 kg of unlicensed sodium stored on site. Workers reported supervisors discouraged calling emergency services because it halted production. Hyundai Motors, whose components Anjun manufactured, responded by seeking a replacement supplier rather than addressing safety. Anjun’s revenue had nearly quadrupled to 135 billion won through the cost-cutting that traded worker safety for competitive pricing.

US March jobs report

The March nonfarm payrolls report, released Friday April 3, showed the US economy added 178,000 jobs — well above the consensus estimate of 59,000 and reversing February’s -133,000 decline. The unemployment rate edged down to 4.3 percent. However, the headline number masks deteriorating wage conditions: average hourly earnings rose just 0.2 percent month-over-month and 3.5 percent year-over-year — the lowest annual gain since May 2021. Long-term unemployed rose to 1.8 million, up 322,000 year-over-year. Healthcare led hiring at +76,000, followed by construction and transportation. Traders pared Fed rate-cut expectations; the 10-year Treasury yield rose 5 basis points to 4.368 percent.

Armed ICE officers remain permanently deployed at airports

Nexteer Automotive workers vote 96% against UAW-backed contract

Workers at Nexteer Automotive in Saginaw, Michigan voted 96 percent against a UAW-backed contract that would expand wage tiers and raise healthcare costs for the plant’s 1,300 workers, who manufacture steering components for GM, Ford, and Stellantis. US measles cases near 1,700

The US has recorded 1,671 measles cases across 33 jurisdictions as of April 2 — a 6.1 percent week-over-week increase with 96 new cases. Ninety-one patients (5%) have been hospitalized, and 94 percent of cases are outbreak-associated. The year is on pace to be one of the two worst for measles in the US since elimination was declared in 2000, driven by multi-state outbreaks disproportionately affecting children under 5.

The crisis unfolds against the backdrop of national vaccination coverage collapsing to 92.5 percent, with 39 states below the 95 percent herd immunity threshold. The Trump-Kennedy administration has purged the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices, replacing members with anti-vaccine ideologues. As virologist Stanley Plotkin warned: “To tell people that they don’t need to be vaccinated is simply promoting disease. Not only is it stupid, but it’s immoral.”

H5N1 bird flu update

H5N1 continues circulating widely in wild birds, US dairy cows, and poultry with sporadic worker infections. CDC influenza surveillance shows no unusual human activity signals as of early April. However, the virus has caused its first confirmed wildlife die-off in Antarctica, killing 50+ skuas — extending its unprecedented geographic range. Virologists continue to flag the species spread as a growing pandemic risk even as immediate human-to-human transmission risk remains low.

Sources CDC Bird flu situation summary · STAT News H5N1 bird flu updates

Oral GLP-1 drug orforglipron achieves 11% weight loss in phase 3 trial

In the 72-week ATTAIN-1 phase 3 trial published in the New England Journal of Medicine, Eli Lilly’s once-daily oral orforglipron (36 mg) produced −11.2 percent mean body weight reduction vs. −2.1 percent for placebo. More than half of participants (54.6%) lost ≥10 percent of body weight. A companion Lancet trial (ATTAIN-2) reported similar results in people with type 2 diabetes. A once-daily pill achieving ~11 percent weight loss would be a major accessibility advance over injectable GLP-1 drugs like semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy). Lilly is targeting global regulatory submission in 2026.

Sources NEJM Orforglipron ATTAIN-1 trial · Lancet Orforglipron ATTAIN-2 trial

The Drama opens to polarized reviews

Kristoffer Borgli’s The Drama, released by A24 on April 3, stars Zendaya and Robert Pattinson as a wealthy Back Bay couple whose wedding plans collapse after a drunken confession: Emma (Zendaya) reveals she once self-identified as a would-be school shooter. The film walks a tonal tightrope between dark comedy and psychological horror, with critics divided on whether its provocation amounts to genuine social commentary or calculated shock.

At 78 percent on Rotten Tomatoes from 161 critics, the consensus praises the lead performances — particularly Pattinson’s “incredibly witty and precise” disintegration — while noting the film’s frustrating restraint in developing Zendaya’s character. The more interesting cultural question is what the film’s premise reveals about American social life: that mass violence has become so endemic that its potential exists as a dormant psychological feature even among the privileged classes. That the film locates this confession within the rituals of bourgeois marriage — the most private and financially consequential institution of middle-class life — gives it a sharper social edge than most critics acknowledge.

Sources RogerEbert.com The Drama review · NPR ‘The Drama’ review: Zendaya and Robert Pattinson star in a provocative wedding comedy · Variety ‘The Drama’ review

The Super Mario Galaxy Movie dominates box office

Nintendo and Illumination’s The Super Mario Galaxy Movie opened to $188 million+ domestically over five days and $370 million globally — the biggest opening of 2026 by a wide margin. The sequel earned $34.5 million on opening day alone from 3,821 theaters. The numbers fall slightly short of the 2023 original’s $204 million five-day domestic haul but represent the biggest opening since Moana 2.

The gap between critics (44% on Rotten Tomatoes) and audiences (91%) is striking and reflects a broader pattern in franchise entertainment: critical assessments of artistic merit increasingly diverge from audience enthusiasm for familiar IP. The film’s massive commercial success amid an active shooting war, economic turbulence, and rising social anxiety illustrates the entertainment industry’s function as what Adorno and Horkheimer called “the culture industry” — providing managed distraction precisely when critical consciousness is most needed.

Sources Deadline Box office: ‘Super Mario Galaxy Movie’ $188M+ · Variety Box office: ‘The Super Mario Galaxy Movie’ powers up with $48 million

Berlinale retrospective examines 1990s cinema

The 76th Berlin International Film Festival’s retrospective, “Lost in the 90s,” surveyed 1990s cinema across films by Richard Linklater, Jean-Luc Godard, Werner Herzog, Andreas Dresen, and Yugoslav directors. The WSWS review argues the retrospective documented the gap between capitalism’s post-Cold War triumphalism and actual social deterioration — capturing “existential anxiety, skepticism and pessimism about the future” rather than promised prosperity.

The review criticizes the Berlinale for failing to interrogate the East German “civil rights movement” whose illusions many artists shared, and identifies a reactionary cultural shift in the 1990s whereby socialist ideas and class solidarity were replaced by individualism. The disillusionment captured in those films now appears prescient given “incessant wars, the return of fascism and the destruction of the welfare state.”

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output over the past several days is dominated by the Iran war’s economic dimensions. The lead article, “Trump Is Robbing You to Pay for His Dumb War” by Branko Marcetic, frames the $1.5 trillion military budget as a direct transfer from domestic programs to war spending, calling the war “dumb” — a word choice that trivializes the catastrophe as a policy error rather than an expression of imperialism’s structural logic. The companion piece, “The War on Iran Is More Expensive Than You Think,” cites $2.1 billion per day in war costs, framing the issue primarily in terms of domestic fiscal impact rather than the devastation inflicted on Iran.

Other notable pieces include “Stop Asking If Israel Has a Right to Exist” by Rawan Abhari, which correctly identifies the question as a rhetorical trap but approaches Palestinian self-determination through a liberal rights framework divorced from class analysis, and “Crypto Is Flailing” by Hadas Thier, which documents the sector’s decline under Trump but treats it as a market curiosity rather than analyzing the class interests driving crypto deregulation.

The overall orientation remains consistent: opposition to war framed in populist, taxpayer terms (“robbing you”) designed to pressure Congress rather than mobilize the working class independently. The Democratic Party is absent from Jacobin’s analysis as a co-architect of the war state — a silence that reflects the DSA’s continued orientation toward pressuring Democrats from within.

Sources Jacobin Trump is robbing you to pay for his dumb war · Jacobin The war on Iran is more expensive than you think · Jacobin Crypto is flailing

Left Voice

Left Voice published the sharpest material among the pseudo-left tendencies this cycle. “Why Is Shawn Fain and the UAW Leadership Supporting Trump’s Trade Policies?” explicitly names the mechanism by which union bureaucracies tie workers to national capital against international solidarity: Fain’s embrace of tariffs subordinates workers’ internationalist interests to labor chauvinism and makes the UAW complicit in a protectionist program that will accelerate inflation and layoffs. A companion piece, “Internationalism Has to Be Anti-imperialist: A Dialogue with Labor Notes,” extends the critique to Labor Notes itself for insufficient challenge to the UAW’s nationalist orientation.

“All Roads Lead to Stagflation” analyzes Trump’s tariff escalation as driving the US economy toward structural crisis, framing it as a feature of capitalism rather than a policy error. This is consistent with Left Voice’s Trotskyist method of using economic conjuncture to argue for working-class political independence.

Left Voice remains the most analytically rigorous of the pseudo-left tendencies on the trade question. However, its organizational orientation toward the Fraction of Trotskyists (FT/PTS) means these critiques serve an electoral and propaganda function in Latin America rather than building toward independent workers’ organizations in the US. The absence of any engagement with the ICFI’s perspective on the war or the Fourth International’s analysis limits the political conclusions to a general call for “class independence” without the programmatic specificity to give it content.

Sources Left Voice Why is Shawn Fain and the UAW leadership supporting Trump’s trade policies? · Left Voice All roads lead to stagflation · Left Voice Internationalism has to be anti-imperialist: a dialogue with Labor Notes

PSL / Liberation News

The PSL’s coverage centers on its “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” statement, which opposes the war on populist grounds — invoking “the majority of people in the country” — while citing the bombing of an elementary school for girls in Minab that killed nearly 100. There is no engagement with the question of working-class political independence or the need for an international socialist program.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s April output focuses on two themes: anti-ICE organizing and May Day mobilization. “How Seattle Educators Are Organizing Against ICE” reports on protests after rumors of ICE moving into a Tukwila facility, culminating in the city council passing a six-month ban on ICE facilities. “Striking On May Day: An FAQ For Workers” promotes the nationwide “No Work, No School, No Shopping” call for May 1 against Trump, ICE, and imperialist war. “Britain: Half A Million March Against The Far Right!” covers the March 28 London march of 500,000.

The orientation is toward broad movement-building that carefully avoids the question of independent working-class political organization. The May Day call channels opposition into a single-day action format that, by design, does not challenge the trade union bureaucracies’ control over the labor movement or raise the question of a new political party. The ICE piece celebrates a six-month municipal moratorium — a temporary, local measure — as a model of resistance, reflecting SAlt’s adaptation to municipal reformism.

Sources Socialist Alternative Striking on May Day: an FAQ for workers · Socialist Alternative How Seattle educators are organizing against ICE · Socialist Alternative Britain: Half a million march against the far right!

SWP (UK)

The SWP’s Socialist Worker covered the NEU union conference (April 1), where education workers passed an emergency motion titled “No to Trump and Netanyahu’s wars” condemning the US attacks on Venezuela, Israel’s aggression in Lebanon, and Trump’s threats to Cuba. Delegates called out the Labour government for allowing the US to use British bases. Other recent pieces include “Trump’s war on Iran is desperate bid to prop up US power” and solidarity calls for convicted Palestine activists Ben Jamal and Chris Nineham.

The SWP’s orientation toward trade union resolutions is characteristic: framing resistance through the institutional channels of the union bureaucracy while avoiding the question of why Labour — the party the SWP’s broader milieu has historically supported — is enabling the war. The NEU motion “condemns” but proposes no mechanism for enforcement. The Palestine solidarity work remains within the framework of legal defense rather than political mobilization of the working class.

Sources Socialist Worker NEU union conference: motion condemns ‘barbarous’ imperial war · Socialist Worker Trump’s war on Iran is desperate bid to prop up US power

RCP / IMT

The Revolutionary Communist Party (formerly IMT’s British section) is focused on preparations for its Third Congress, scheduled for May 1–4 to coincide with the centenary of the British General Strike. Editor Adam Booth published an April 1 analysis of the Iran war emphasizing the “challenges” the US faces — a framing that treats the war as a strategic problem for imperialism rather than a crime against the Iranian people. A congress contribution, “A very British catastrophe,” analyzes the Starmer government’s crisis.

The IMT/RCP’s characteristic approach — emphasizing capitalism’s “contradictions” while building a mass revolutionary organization through patient propaganda — avoids concrete engagement with the immediate demands of the antiwar movement. The congress timing (May 1–4) is symbolically potent but the organizational focus on internal perspectives documents and resolutions reflects an inward orientation at a moment when the war demands outward-facing political intervention.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Iran civilian death toll and the systematic targeting of universities — war crimes accountability

US-Israeli strikes have now hit more than 30 Iranian universities, including Shahid Beheshti University in Tehran on April 4. With at least 2,076 civilians confirmed killed by Iran’s Health Ministry and estimates ranging far higher, there is virtually no Western coverage framing this as systematic targeting of civilian and academic infrastructure — a potential war crime under international humanitarian law. The WSWS should document the pattern of university strikes and place it in the context of deliberate destruction of a nation’s intellectual infrastructure, as was done in Iraq.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war live: Tehran rejects Trump’s ultimatum; fire at Kuwait oil complex

2. Cuba’s energy crisis and humanitarian collapse behind the prisoner-release headline

The prisoner release story is being framed across Western media as a diplomatic win for Trump. The underlying story — a US-induced humanitarian crisis of blocked oil imports, severed Venezuelan trade, hospital closures, and suspended flights — is largely absent from coverage. The WSWS should analyze the economic warfare dimension and its impact on the Cuban working class, rather than accepting the framing of “prisoner diplomacy.”

Recommended reading CBS News Cuba releases prisoners as Trump pressures the island

3. The Gaza “stabilization force” as permanent military occupation — five countries now sending troops

Five countries sending troops to Gaza under a US-designed framework with a mandate to “demilitarize Hamas” raises fundamental questions about whether this constitutes a permanent US-aligned military occupation of Palestinian territory. Kosovo PM Kurti’s justification — citing NATO’s KFOR as a model — is itself revealing: KFOR has been in Kosovo for 27 years. Coverage treats this as routine diplomacy rather than analyzing the occupation structure being assembled.

Recommended reading Balkan Insight Kosovo govt backs sending troops to join Gaza stabilisation force

4. Suspicious trading ahead of Iran war announcements — the ruling class profiting from war

Axios reported on March 25 suspicious trading patterns in oil and defense stocks preceding Trump’s Iran war announcements. This has received virtually no follow-up investigation. The WSWS should pursue this as a potential case of insider trading by government officials or their associates — a concrete illustration of the class that profits from the war it is directing.

Recommended reading CNBC Trump tariffs trade war impact

5. Ukraine’s Luhansk loss and the stalled peace framework

Russia’s claimed consolidation of full control over Luhansk Oblast — one of four illegally annexed regions — is a major strategic development that effectively forecloses a simple territorial return to pre-2022 borders in any negotiated settlement. With Witkoff and Kushner expected in Kyiv this month for peace talks, the WSWS should analyze what Russia’s Luhansk consolidation means for the terms being discussed and the class interests driving each side’s negotiating position.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Russia claims to take full control of Ukraine’s Luhansk region

6. The Iran war’s suppressed labor and protest context

The conflict was partly triggered by Iran’s January 2026 crackdown on massive pro-democracy protests. The social and labor character of those protests — and whether the US-Israeli war has suppressed or co-opted the movement — has received almost no independent coverage. The WSWS should investigate what happened to the Iranian workers’ movement that was active before the bombs started falling, and how the war has affected independent organizing inside Iran.

Recommended reading Wikipedia 2026 Iran war

7. Pope Leo XIV’s peace rhetoric and the Vatican’s real-world role in wartime

The new pope’s Easter homily called for “harmony and peace” while carefully avoiding any direct naming of the US-Israeli war or the parties responsible. The WSWS should analyze the Vatican’s historical role in providing moral cover for imperialist powers during wartime through generalized peace rhetoric that never translates into concrete opposition — a pattern stretching from World War II through the present.
