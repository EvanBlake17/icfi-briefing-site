1. US-Iran war reaches day 69 as Trump dangles peace deal while threatening expanded bombing

Day 69 of the US-Israel war on Iran — launched February 28 following the assassination of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — saw the Trump administration simultaneously pushing a one-page memorandum of understanding through Pakistani intermediaries and threatening an escalation of bombing “at a much higher level and intensity” if Tehran rejects the framework. Trump told reporters that talks were “very good” and “it’s very possible we’ll make a deal,” but coupled the statement with renewed military threats hours after the US Navy disabled the rudder of an Iranian oil tanker in the Gulf of Oman.

Iran’s Foreign Ministry said the proposed 14-point framework remains “under review” and that Tehran will communicate its response “after finalising its views.” Secretary of State Marco Rubio publicly declared Operation Epic Fury “concluded,” and Trump paused “Project Freedom” — the US Navy escort operation through the Strait of Hormuz — citing “great progress.”

As Joseph Kishore put it in his May Day rally speech republished by the WSWS, the Iran war constitutes “crimes against peace” under Nuremberg standards, financed by the dismantling of Medicare and Medicaid.

Key facts: - 13 US service members have been killed in Operation Epic Fury since February 28 - Pakistan is the primary intermediary; Trump aimed to finalize an agreement before concluding his China trip - Iranian FM Abbas Araghchi traveled to Beijing concurrent with the negotiations to coordinate with China - Negotiations focus on a sequence in which the Strait of Hormuz reopens first and the nuclear question is deferred - Hapag-Lloyd shipping is absorbing $60 million per week in losses from the Hormuz closure

2. Israel kills son of Hamas chief negotiator in Gaza City; 837 Palestinians dead since October “ceasefire”

Azzam Khalil al-Hayya, the son of Hamas political bureau head and chief negotiator Khalil al-Hayya, died of wounds sustained in an Israeli air raid in Gaza City’s Daraj neighborhood, Hamas official Basem Naim confirmed. Azzam was the fourth son Khalil al-Hayya has lost to Israeli operations — three killed in previous offensives, one assassinated in Doha in September 2025. The killing comes as Israel’s security cabinet has been openly debating the resumption of full-scale war operations after Hamas refused Israeli demands for complete disarmament.

The same night’s strikes killed five Palestinians in total, including three members of one family in Zeitoun and Naseem al-Kalazani, head of the Khan Younis anti-narcotics force, who died when his vehicle was struck near al-Mawasi. Seventeen others were wounded in that single attack. Israel’s pattern of targeting civil administration figures — police colonels, anti-narcotics officers, civil defense personnel — points to a deliberate strategy of dismantling the Palestinian governance fabric in addition to the military campaign.

Khalil al-Hayya’s response — “Neither the killing of our sons nor the martyrdom of our leaders will intimidate us” — was widely circulated in Arab media.

Key facts: - 837 Palestinians killed and 2,381 wounded since the October 2025 “ceasefire” took effect - Total Gaza death toll since October 2023: 72,619 killed, 172,484 wounded - Israel’s security cabinet met earlier this week to discuss renewing full-scale war operations - The targeting of police, anti-narcotics, and civil defense figures fits a broader pattern of dismantling Palestinian civil infrastructure under the truce regime

3. Israel breaks Hezbollah ceasefire with first Beirut strike since April 17; 13 killed across Lebanon

Israeli warplanes struck the Ghobeiri district of Beirut’s southern suburbs on May 6 — the first attack on the Lebanese capital since a US-brokered ceasefire with Hezbollah took effect on April 17. The Israeli military said it had targeted a commander of Hezbollah’s elite Radwan Force. Concurrent strikes across southern and eastern Lebanon killed at least 13 people on May 6, with Israel’s military reporting that it intercepted a “suspicious aerial target” launched from Lebanon after sirens sounded in northern Israel.

The strike on Ghobeiri, a densely populated working-class neighborhood, has now placed the entire April 17 truce framework in question and is complicating the parallel US-Iran negotiations. Diplomatic condemnations have come from world governments, the United Nations, and even some traditional US allies.

Key facts: - At least 2,618 Lebanese killed and 8,094 wounded by Israeli strikes since the start of the Iran war on February 28 - The Ghobeiri strike is the first Israeli attack on Beirut since the April 17 Hezbollah ceasefire - Working-class southern suburbs of Beirut bear a disproportionate share of the casualties and displacement, a class dimension absent from most Western coverage

4. Russia-Ukraine “competing ceasefires” farce collapses; 70+ civilians killed in early May

Russia’s announcement of a unilateral Victory Day ceasefire (May 9) and Ukraine’s responsive midnight May 5–6 truce both collapsed within hours, with Russia launching missiles and drones that killed at least 21 civilians and injured 82 on May 6 alone. Since early May, Russia’s wave of attacks has killed at least 70 civilians and wounded over 500 in Ukraine.

On day 1,533 of the invasion, Ukrainian general staff recorded 186 combat engagements; Russian forces deployed 10,301 kamikaze drones, 244 guided bombs across 77 airstrikes, and 13 missiles. Heaviest fighting concentrated in the Pokrovsk direction (50 repelled assaults), Huliaipole (24 attacks), and Kostyantynivka and Oleksandrivka (14 attacks each).

5. Pakistan warns India of “greater” retaliation on first anniversary of Operation Sindoor

Pakistan’s military command warned on May 7 that any “hostile design” would be met with “greater strength, precision and resolve” than India experienced during the May 2025 war that Islamabad calls Marka-e-Haq (“Battle of Truth”). The warning comes exactly one year after India launched Operation Sindoor — cross-border strikes targeting alleged militant infrastructure in Pakistan and Pakistan-administered Kashmir following the April 22, 2025 Pahalgam attack that killed 26 civilians.

Both nuclear-armed states maintain heightened military readiness. India has been rushing to centralize its war command structure following what defense analysts describe as a difficult outcome for New Delhi. Analysts at The Diplomat warn of “rising risks and deepening instability” in South Asia one year on.

Key facts: - May 7 marks the first anniversary of India’s Operation Sindoor (2025) - Pakistan’s responsive 2025 campaign — Operation Bunyan-un-Marsoos — targeted Indian military installations with missiles, drones, and artillery - The 2025 fighting ended with a US-brokered ceasefire on May 10, 2025 - The two states between them encompass roughly 1.7 billion people, the largest concentration of working people in any nuclear standoff in history

6. Stagflation warnings mount as Trump tariffs and Iran energy shock cost average household $1,500

Top economists are now openly warning of stagflation as the Iran war’s energy shock compounds the inflationary pressure of Trump’s Section 122 tariffs — imposed after the Supreme Court’s February 20, 2026 ruling that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act does not authorize tariffs. The Tax Foundation estimates the tariff package alone is the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, costing the average household roughly $1,500 in 2026. US average retail gasoline now exceeds $4.50/gallon.

Moody’s chief economist Mark Zandi has warned that conditions could worsen significantly if the Iran war continues. Hiring has frozen across multiple industries, even as US equity indices closed at record highs on May 6 — a stark illustration of the disjuncture between asset markets and the lived economic reality of the working class.

Nick Beams’s WSWS analysis published today provides additional industrial detail: airlines have cut 2 million May seats, US automakers are absorbing $5 billion in commodity cost increases, and aluminum prices have doubled to $6,100 per metric ton. S&P Global’s Jim Burkhard called an “inevitable market reckoning” and Goldman Sachs described the speed of inventory depletion as “concerning.”

Key facts: - Trump’s Section 122 tariffs cover ~$1.2 trillion (34%) of annual US imports under a 10% across-the-board rate - Global crude oil stockpiles fell 6 million barrels per day in April; refined product supply globally is now around 45 days - Aluminum prices have doubled to $6,100 per metric ton since the war began

Supreme Court Section 122 tariff regime continues to bite

Following the Supreme Court’s February 20 ruling that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not authorize the president to impose tariffs, the Trump administration responded by invoking Section 122 of the Trade Act, imposing a 10% across-the-board tariff effective February 24. The USTR has launched dozens of Section 301 investigations into roughly 60 countries for “forced-labor goods,” and separate investigations into 15 countries plus the European Union for “structural excess capacity.”

The tariff regime represents the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993. Combined with the energy shock from the Iran war, the working class is absorbing the dual cost — even as the financial press reports record stock highs.

Measles cases surpass 1,800 across 37 jurisdictions

As of April 30, the CDC has confirmed 1,814 measles cases across 37 US jurisdictions, plus 11 additional cases in international visitors. 93% (1,688 cases) are linked to one of 24 active outbreaks. The collapse in kindergarten vaccination coverage — from 95.2% in 2019–20 to 92.5% in 2024–25 — has left an estimated 286,000 children unprotected. This is the largest domestic measles outbreak in decades and now actively threatens the elimination status the US achieved in 2000.

NEJM: 10-year trial finds common knee surgery worse than placebo

The Finnish FIDELITY trial’s 10-year follow-up, published this week in the New England Journal of Medicine (vol. 394, no. 17), found that arthroscopic partial meniscectomy provided no benefit over sham (placebo) surgery for degenerative meniscal tears. The surgery group showed worse long-term outcomes: osteoarthritis progression of 81% versus 70% in the placebo group, and higher rates of subsequent knee surgery. Arthroscopic partial meniscectomy is one of the most commonly performed orthopedic procedures globally; the result is a major “medical reversal” exposing how procedure-volume incentives sustain interventions that rigorous RCTs eventually disprove.

NEOPRISM-CRC: zero relapses at 33 months in pre-surgical pembrolizumab arm

Updated results from the UCL-led NEOPRISM-CRC trial, presented at the AACR 2026 Annual Meeting in San Diego, show that patients with mismatch-repair deficient (dMMR) colorectal cancer who received just 9 weeks of pembrolizumab before surgery have had zero relapses at 33 months of follow-up. At surgery, 59% had no detectable cancer. Standard surgery-plus-chemotherapy yields roughly 25% recurrence within three years. The result is a potential paradigm shift for a defined genomic subtype of bowel cancer — replacing prolonged toxic chemotherapy with a short immunotherapy course that may functionally cure patients before surgery. Access and pricing of pembrolizumab will, however, remain a class-divided question.

State AI laws move in Connecticut and Maryland; NIST inks pre-deployment deals with DeepMind, Microsoft, xAI

  • Connecticut SB 5 (covering frontier AI models, chatbots, employment, and data provenance) passed the legislature this week and is expected to be signed by Governor Lamont — the third attempt after prior vetoes
  • Maryland HB 895 has been signed into law, making Maryland the first US state to ban certain AI-driven price-setting practices
  • On May 5, NIST’s Center for AI Standards and Innovation struck pre-deployment evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI

The simultaneous surge in state legislation and the federal pivot toward voluntary pre-deployment evaluations marks 2026 as the inflection year for binding AI governance in the United States — though the substance remains heavily shaped by the firms themselves.

COVID activity at multi-year lows; H5N1 surveillance footprint massive

As of early May, COVID-19 hospital admissions are forecast at roughly 1,500/week nationally (range: 660–3,000), with infections declining or likely declining in 31 states and growing in only one. The WHO’s TAG-CO-VAC convenes this month to set fall 2026 vaccine antigen composition. On bird flu, the CDC reports 71 total confirmed human H5N1 cases in the US since February 2024, primarily among dairy and poultry workers; between February 2022 and April 25, 2026, at least 33,000 people were monitored and 1,340+ tested for novel influenza A. The scale of asymptomatic monitoring reflects the seriousness with which public-health agencies are still treating the pandemic risk, even as current human-to-human transmission remains absent.

Arts and culture

The dominant cultural event of the past 24 hours — and the one to which the WSWS dedicated its Perspective — was the 2026 Met Gala, co-chaired by Jeff Bezos and his wife. As David North argues in the Perspective summarized in the WSWS section below, the spectacle was treated by the mainstream press “with the gravity of a state occasion” rather than as the “leprosy of unreality” it represents. Bezos paid $10 million for co-chair privileges. Individual tickets sold for $100,000; a table of ten cost $350,000; total ticket revenue reached $42 million. One rubberized bodysuit required 11,000 embroidery hours and incorporated 10,000 individual pearls; a single Schiaparelli creation represented more labor-hours than a New York City public school teacher performs across three full school years. Outside the Metropolitan Museum, protesters projected the message “Boycott the Bezos Met Gala” onto an adjacent $80 million penthouse, while more than 100,000 New Yorkers slept that same night in the city’s emergency housing system.

The Gala’s choreographed display of oligarchic power coincides with continued state attacks on cultural institutions. The Trump administration’s defunding of the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities, combined with attacks on the Smithsonian and the politicization of museum boards, continues to reshape the institutional landscape — making US arts increasingly dependent on the same billionaire donor class on display at the Met. The Metropolitan Museum itself, North notes, has become structurally dependent on private wealth rather than public funding.

What is it about the Met fashion gala that leads one to think fondly of the guillotine? — David North

North’s Perspective uses the Bezos-co-chaired Met Gala as a vehicle for a sustained argument about the terminal decadence of the capitalist ruling class and the objective necessity of expropriating its wealth. He opens with Dickens’s description of the French aristocracy on the eve of revolution: “The leprosy of unreality disfigured every human creature in attendance upon it.” The article catalogs the labor-extractive character of the spectacle — 11,000 embroidery hours per garment, gowns requiring seven attendants — and contrasts it directly with the 100,000+ New Yorkers in emergency shelter the same night, the injury rates at Amazon warehouses, and the simultaneous Trump administration cuts to Medicare, Medicaid, and social spending alongside the Iran war buildup. The Perspective’s programmatic conclusion is explicit: “society cannot afford the rich” — not as moral judgment but as structural fact. The expropriation of billionaires is described as “a social necessity.” The guillotine reference in the headline is framed as an involuntary register of the historical situation rather than a call. The article connects the Gala to the broader political moment — Iran war, austerity, authoritarian governance — as expressions of a single systemic crisis.

The Trump regime, oligarchy, and the fight for socialism — Joseph Kishore

Kishore’s address to the International May Day 2026 Online Rally is reprinted in full. He characterizes the Trump administration as “a gang of criminals” whose Iran war constitutes crimes against peace under Nuremberg standards, and whose immigration enforcement resembles “Gestapo agents” conducting mass deportations to torture facilities. The military buildup, he argues, is financed by dismantling Medicare and Medicaid. Kishore indicts the Democratic Party as “bankrupt and complicit” for refusing impeachment despite ongoing constitutional violations, and attacks the union bureaucracies for suppressing rank-and-file strike movements in favor of electoral subordination to the Democrats. The address concludes with a call to build independent rank-and-file committees, to join the Socialist Equality Party as the US section of the Fourth International, and to advance an internationalist socialist program against the war and dictatorship.

Six months since the fatal Endeavor mine explosion in Cobar — Martin Scott

Six months after the October 28, 2025 explosion at Polymetals Resources’ Endeavor mine near Cobar, New South Wales — which killed Patrick McMullen (59) and Holly Clarke (24) and injured Mackenzie Stirling (24), likely caused by “extraneous electricity” igniting older-style electric detonators — the NSW Resources Regulator has issued no final findings. Polymetals described the deaths as having “no material information to report publicly” in its financial filings, and the unions have largely gone silent after initial statements. With five mining deaths already recorded in the first four months of 2026 against an annual average of seven since 2020, Scott argues workers need independent rank-and-file safety committees to replace official oversight structures that systematically prioritize corporate interests.

Crisis in New Zealand government over Iran war — Tom Peters

Covered in International developments above; unique to the WSWS account: 87% of New Zealanders oppose direct military involvement and 75% believe the US will negatively affect world affairs over the next decade. Peters’s leak strategy is presented not as principled opposition to the war but as positioning for the November elections, demonstrating that no NZ capitalist party offers genuine opposition to imperialism.

One year of the Merz government: war, militarism and social devastation — Johannes Stern

Covered in International developments above; unique WSWS conclusions: Stern characterizes the Merz government as “the most reactionary in West German history,” explicitly drawing parallels to imperial ambitions toward Eastern Europe in both World Wars. The article connects the social devastation — citizens’ allowance cuts, pension reductions, healthcare austerity — directly to the €500 billion war fund as expressions of the same class strategy.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — coverage of Iran war frames the dispute as a Trump-specific aberration; calls for Democratic congressional pressure rather than independent working-class action
  • Left Voice — internationalist framing on Iran with militant rhetoric, but consistent adaptation to identity-politics fragmentation domestically
  • Liberation News / PSL — anti-imperialist framing converges with bourgeois “multipolar” position; presents China and Russia as objective allies
  • Socialist Alternative — calls for “mass movement” pressure on Democrats and progressive primary challenges
  • Socialist Worker / SWP (UK) — Iran war coverage strong; domestic line continues uncritical adaptation to Labour left and trade-union officials
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — abstract revolutionary phraseology paired with practical absence from concrete strike struggles

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s coverage of the Iran war and the Bezos Met Gala exemplifies its political method. Its editorial line on the Iran negotiations frames Trump’s threats and tariffs as a uniquely Trumpian aberration — a deviation from a normal liberal-imperialist order that could be restored by congressional pressure and a Democratic electoral victory. The strategic conclusion is to mobilize for Democratic candidates in the 2026 midterms and to lobby House Democrats to invoke the War Powers Resolution. This is the textbook DSA/Jacobin position: structural opposition to imperialist war is replaced by partisan opposition to Trump, leaving the actual machinery of US imperialism — Pentagon, CIA, weapons producers — entirely unchallenged. On the Met Gala, Jacobin produced a typical “billionaires shouldn’t exist” piece that preserves the moral form of socialist critique while displacing it with a tax-policy program (a wealth tax, restored estate tax) that does not touch the property relations North’s Perspective addresses.

The class interest expressed is that of the affluent professional layer that staffs the Democratic Party’s progressive wing — opposed to the cruder forms of oligarchic display, committed to the parliamentary regulation of capitalism, and structurally hostile to any independent working-class political formation that would challenge the Democrats’ grip.

Left Voice

Left Voice’s coverage of the Iran war retains a more militant internationalist register than Jacobin’s, correctly identifying the war as imperialist and calling for working-class opposition. However, its domestic political method continues the broader Morenoist adaptation to identity-political fragmentation that the WSWS has documented over years. Coverage of the Met Gala emphasizes celebrity individual conduct over the structural class relations North’s Perspective foregrounds. Coverage of the National Steel Car struggle and other rank-and-file initiatives is largely absent from its pages, despite Left Voice’s nominal “rank-and-file” rhetoric — exposing the gap between phraseology and practice.

The class interest expressed is that of a left-academic milieu that uses militant language to police the boundaries of acceptable left politics around identitarian frameworks while remaining structurally disconnected from the actual rank-and-file struggles unfolding in industrial workplaces.

Liberation News / PSL

Liberation News presents the Iran war primarily through a “multipolar world” frame in which Russia, China, and Iran constitute an objective anti-imperialist axis. This framing converges with sections of the bourgeois press in Beijing and Moscow and substitutes Great-Power realignment for the working-class struggle against the imperialist system as a whole. The PSL’s coverage of the Iran-China FM meeting reads as cheerleading for Wang Yi’s diplomatic offensive, ignoring the class character of the Chinese state and its own role in suppressing workers’ struggles domestically. On the Trump tariffs, PSL emphasizes anti-China provisions while remaining largely silent on the role of the Chinese bureaucracy in enabling the conditions for tariff politics through its labor regime.

The class interest expressed is that of a Stalinist-derived political tradition that conflates opposition to US imperialism with positive support for non-Western capitalist states.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative continues to push for “mass movement” pressure on the Democratic Party and supports progressive primary challenges as the primary tactical orientation. Its coverage of the Iran war is anti-war in form, but its electoral strategy remains tied to the Sanders/Squad pole within the Democratic apparatus. On rank-and-file struggles, it offers solidarity at the level of rhetoric while opposing the formation of independent working-class political structures outside the Democrats.

The class interest expressed is that of a labor-bureaucratic-adjacent layer committed to perpetual pressure on Democratic officeholders rather than to the construction of an independent socialist party.

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

Socialist Worker’s Iran war coverage is its strongest area in the past 24 hours, with reporting on UK port-worker actions against Israeli arms shipments and analysis of British complicity in the bombing campaign. Its domestic political line, however, continues an uncritical adaptation to the Labour left and to trade-union officialdom — the same officials currently presiding over the suppression of rank-and-file strike movements and the integration of UK trade unions into the Starmer government’s war economy. The SWP’s “united front” rhetoric continues to provide left cover for collaboration with Labour MPs whose actual votes have backed UK military deployments to the Gulf.

The class interest expressed is that of a layer of the trade-union apparatus and Labour-left academia that requires continual “left” cover for its organic ties to the British state.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) continues to combine abstract revolutionary phraseology with practical absence from concrete strike and rank-and-file struggles. Its Iran war commentary calls for “world socialist revolution” in general terms while declining to address the specific organizational tasks — independent rank-and-file committees, the political break with the Democrats and Labour, the construction of a section of the Fourth International — that the WSWS and the ICFI advance. Its growth has occurred largely on the basis of an aestheticized “revolutionary” branding among student layers, with no corresponding intervention in industrial workplaces.

The class interest expressed is that of a student-academic milieu attracted to the historical iconography of revolution while politically contained by abstentionism from the class struggle.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Shipping and port workers bearing the brunt of the Hormuz closure

Hapag-Lloyd is absorbing $60 million per week in losses from the Strait of Hormuz closure, but absent from nearly all coverage is what is happening to the tens of thousands of seafarers, port workers, and logistics workers whose livelihoods are disrupted. Rerouted ships around the Cape of Good Hope add weeks to voyages, intensifying crew fatigue and unpaid overtime. This is the same class of workers squeezed during the 2021–22 supply chain crisis.

Recommended reading CNBC Oil prices today: Trump, Iran, Strait of Hormuz

2. Defense contractor windfall profits from the Iran war

While the Iran war has killed 13 US service members and thousands of Iranians and Lebanese civilians, defense contractors have seen extraordinary revenue gains since February 28. No major outlet has published a comprehensive accounting of which firms are profiting — from precision munitions to drone contracts — and what their stock performance looks like against the civilian death toll.

Recommended reading Britannica 2026 Iran war

3. Lebanese civilian displacement and the destruction of working-class neighborhoods in Beirut

Israel’s strikes on Ghobeiri and other Beirut suburbs target densely populated working-class neighborhoods. With 2,618 killed and 8,094 wounded in Lebanon since February 28, the human cost is enormous — but coverage focuses almost exclusively on the military and diplomatic dimensions, not the class character of who is being bombed or the displacement crisis unfolding.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Israeli attacks on southern, eastern Lebanon kill at least six people

4. Palestinian workers and the humanitarian collapse in Gaza under “ceasefire”

The Gaza “ceasefire” signed in October 2025 has not stopped killing — 837 Palestinians have died since it was declared. Almost no outlet is covering the complete collapse of Gaza’s labor force, health infrastructure, and the deliberate targeting of civil administration (police colonels, anti-narcotics officers) as a form of societal destruction. May 1 marked the 200th day of the ceasefire.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Israel threatens Gaza war resumption to force disarmament as truce frays

5. The working-class costs of the India-Pakistan nuclear standoff — one year on

The first anniversary of Operation Sindoor receives coverage primarily through a military/strategic lens. Missing is analysis of how the 2025 war and continued instability have affected cross-border trade, displaced families in Kashmir, and set back poverty-reduction programs in two countries that together have nearly 1.7 billion people. Pakistan’s military warning on May 7 is being treated as a diplomatic event, not a threat to hundreds of millions of working people.

Recommended reading The Diplomat A year after Operation Sindoor: rising risks and deepening instability

6. Ukrainian civilians and the “competing ceasefires” farce

Russia’s Victory Day ceasefire announcement was immediately violated; Ukraine declared its own ceasefire that Russia ignored. At least 70 civilians have been killed and 500+ wounded in this period. Coverage focuses on military strategy and diplomatic maneuvering. The cost — in lives of Ukrainian workers, farmers, teachers, and pensioners — is systematically obscured by the frame of Great Power competition.

Recommended reading EMPR Media Russia-Ukraine war updates: key developments as of May 6, 2026

7. The NEJM meniscectomy reversal and the political economy of unnecessary surgery

The 10-year FIDELITY data showing arthroscopic partial meniscectomy is no better than placebo — and is associated with worse osteoarthritis progression — is one of the largest “medical reversals” in recent orthopedic history. The procedure is performed hundreds of thousands of times annually in the US under fee-for-service incentive structures that perpetuate revenue-generating but ineffective interventions. This is a textbook case of how the for-profit healthcare system selects against rigorous evidence.

Recommended reading NEJM via MedicalResearch Arthroscopic partial meniscectomy provided no benefit over placebo surgery

8. The Powell-Warsh Federal Reserve transition and the further integration of the central bank into Trump’s executive

The DOJ dropping its criminal investigation into Jerome Powell exactly as Kevin Warsh prepares for Senate confirmation reveals the political character of the probe. The episode merits analysis as part of the ongoing transformation of the central bank from a body with historically restricted independence into an instrument more directly responsive to executive direction.

Recommended reading Yahoo Finance Fed holds rates, forecasts 1 cut in 2026
