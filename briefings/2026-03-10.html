1. Iran war enters day 10 as 40 killed in Tehran overnight and Mojtaba Khamenei named supreme leader

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its tenth day on March 10 with no diplomatic off-ramp in sight. Overnight US-Israeli bombardment killed at least 40 people near Tehran’s Rialsat Square, bringing the total confirmed death toll past 1,400, including over 200 children and 12,000 wounded across more than 200 Iranian cities. Iran’s Assembly of Experts named Mojtaba Khamenei, 56, son of the assassinated Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, as the new supreme leader on March 9 under IRGC pressure.

Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi told PBS News that “talking or negotiating with the US is no longer on the agenda.” The IRGC rejected claims its missile program was destroyed, announcing deployment of projectiles “in greater numbers, with warheads exceeding 1 tonne.” Iranian counterstrikes have now targeted Bahrain, Kuwait, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar, killing civilians in each country. In Bahrain, at least 32 were injured in a strike on the island of Sitra; in Qatar, two civilians were killed in a strike on a residential facility.

Key facts: - US casualties: 8 service members killed, including Sgt. Benjamin N. As the WSWS emergency webinar detailed on March 8, this war represents the culmination of 35 years of post-Cold War US strategy aimed at “abolishing the 20th century — to wipe out all the consequences of the national democratic and socialist struggles of the 20th century,” as David North stated.

2. Strait of Hormuz closure triggers worst oil supply disruption in history

Iran confirmed closure of the Strait of Hormuz on March 2, and tanker traffic has dropped approximately 70%, approaching zero. Over 150 ships are anchored outside the strait to avoid risk. The strait normally handles roughly 20% of global daily oil supply and major LNG volumes. Iraq, the UAE, and Kuwait have all cut production due to the waterway closure.

Brent crude peaked at $119/barrel — the first time oil crossed $100 since Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine. US gasoline jumped from approximately $2.98 to $3.45/gallon in one week, with a $4 national average projected this week. In Michigan, gas has already hit $3.58/gallon; in California, prices exceed $5.

Key facts: - QatarEnergy, the world’s largest LNG exporter, declared force majeure on March 4 after Iranian drone attacks on its facilities; all LNG production halted March 2 - Qatar’s Energy Minister Saad al-Kaabi warned that all Gulf producers could soon halt exports and that prices could hit $150/barrel - Top energy analysts describe this as the biggest oil output disruption in history, surpassing the 1973 Arab oil embargo - The IMF calculates that every sustained 10% oil price rise produces a 0.4% inflation increase and a 0.15% reduction in global economic growth - Economists warn of stagflation — simultaneous inflation spike and growth slowdown — hitting the global South hardest, where countries face dollar-denominated debts, oil import dependency, and no welfare buffers - Oil seesawed between $119 and sub-$90 in a single 24-hour session on March 9–10 after Trump signaled the conflict “could be over soon”

6. Europe scrambles with historic rearmament as NATO fractures widen

The Iran war has exposed deep European disunity and accelerated a historic rearmament drive. The UK and France deployed naval and air-defense assets to the eastern Mediterranean, while Germany, Ireland, Belgium, and the Netherlands focused on diplomacy and made no combat deployments.

Germany’s coalition has agreed to raise defense spending from €86 billion (2025) to €108.2 billion (2026) and will borrow up to €400 billion over five years for rearmament — a historic constitutional shift that overturns decades of post-war restraint. European military budgets surged 13% in real terms in 2025, and Europe’s share of global defense spending rose from 17% (2022) to 21% (2025).

Pittsburgh Post-Gazette to close May 3 after 240 years of publication

The Pittsburgh Post-Gazette, one of Pennsylvania’s largest newspapers, formally notified the state it will close May 3 and lay off its entire staff of 171 employees. The paper has published since 1786 — 240 years of continuous publication ending amid years of labor disputes, ownership conflicts, and print industry decline. Staff had been working without a contract for several years.

The closure represents the accelerating destruction of regional journalism across the US, driven by hedge fund ownership and digital advertising revenue collapse. It eliminates a major institution of public information in a key swing state.

US measles outbreak reaches 1,281 cases across 31 jurisdictions

The US measles outbreak continued to accelerate, reaching 1,281 confirmed cases across 31 jurisdictions as of March 5. South Carolina accounts for 653 cases, followed by Utah (149) and Florida (107). Of all cases, 89% are outbreak-associated; there have been 58 hospitalizations and no deaths. MMR kindergarten vaccination coverage has dropped to 92.5%, leaving approximately 286,000 children unprotected.

The 2026 total is on pace to rival 2025’s 2,283 cases — a 34-year high — with cases still accelerating through multiple simultaneous outbreaks. This represents the ongoing public health consequences of vaccine hesitancy campaigns and the erosion of state-level immunization mandates.

H5N1 confirmed in Antarctica for the first time

H5N1 bird flu has killed 50+ skuas in Antarctica — the first confirmed wildlife die-off from the virus on the continent, marking a significant geographic expansion. The US human case total stands at 71 (2 deaths), with no new cases reported since February 2025. The virus remains active in dairy cattle and poultry across the United States.

While no sustained human-to-human transmission has been detected, surveillance gaps persist, and the arrival of H5N1 in Antarctica underscores the virus’s relentless global spread through wild bird populations. The combination of an active bird flu season with a resurgent measles outbreak and ongoing COVID-19 transmission represents a compounding public health threat at a time when federal health agencies face budget cuts and political interference.

Federal AI policy deadline: FTC and Commerce Department reports due March 11

Two major federal AI policy reports are due March 11. The FTC must publish a statement defining how existing consumer protection law (Section 5, COPPA, FCRA) applies to AI, with enforcement beginning immediately after. Simultaneously, the Commerce Secretary must identify state AI laws that conflict with federal preemption policy.

This dual deadline, driven by Trump’s December 2025 AI executive order, could effectively freeze or override dozens of state-level AI regulations and reshape AI enforcement nationwide — centralizing regulatory authority in federal agencies aligned with the administration’s deregulatory agenda.

Trump administration demands “any lawful use” terms for civilian AI contracts

The administration has drafted new rules requiring AI vendors on civilian government contracts to permit “any lawful” use of their models, directly challenging AI companies’ internal usage restrictions and acceptable-use policies. This creates a direct confrontation with developers like OpenAI and Anthropic over who controls deployment terms for models used by the US government — part of the broader drive to subordinate AI safety measures to state and military objectives.

Once-monthly obesity drug MariTide shows 12–16% weight loss

A Phase 2 trial published in the New England Journal of Medicine found that Amgen’s maridebart cafraglutide (MariTide) — a once-monthly GLP-1/GIP dual-mechanism injectable — produced 12.3% to 16.2% body weight reduction at 52 weeks versus 2.5% for placebo in a 592-person trial. Participants with type 2 diabetes saw 8.4% to 12.3% weight loss; HbA1c dropped 1.2–1.6 percentage points.

The monthly dosing interval — compared to weekly for existing GLP-1 drugs like Ozempic and Mounjaro — could significantly improve patient adherence and disrupt the rapidly growing obesity treatment market.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — promoting the line that “the US is fighting Israel’s war,” channeling antiwar sentiment into pressure on Democratic Party leaders rather than independent working-class action
  • Left Voice — correctly opposing imperialism but orienting toward educators’ union structures and maintaining the framework of “defense of Iran” without a clear program for independent working-class intervention
  • Socialist Alternative — describing a “regional war spiraling out of control” with emphasis on the humanitarian catastrophe but no strategic perspective for how the working class can stop it
  • Liberation News / PSL — focused on anti-imperialist denunciation of US aggression while adapting to campist frameworks that treat the Iranian regime as a legitimate resistance force

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s Iran war coverage has been prolific, with at least eight articles published in the past week, but the political line is consistent: channeling antiwar sentiment back into the Democratic Party framework.

  • “The US Is Fighting Israel’s War on Iran” (Branko Marcetic) argues that the US was forced into the war by Israel, citing administration officials who acknowledged Israel “was determined to act with or without the US.” This is precisely the line David North rebutted at the WSWS webinar: “The tail may wag very vigorously, but it’s the dog that’s running the show. Israel does not drive American policy.” By absolving US imperialism of primary agency, Marcetic’s framing obscures the 35-year bipartisan drive for regime change in Iran — from the 1953 coup to Trump’s withdrawal from the nuclear accord — and redirects criticism toward the Israel lobby rather than the capitalist system.

  • “The Iran War Shows Why It’s Time for Chuck Schumer to Go” (Liza Featherstone) notes that 89% of Democratic voters oppose the war while Schumer offered only weak procedural complaints. The article’s political conclusion — replace Schumer with a more credible antiwar Democrat — epitomizes Jacobin’s role: identifying real grievances only to channel them back into the Democratic Party. The demand is for better leadership within an imperialist party, not independent working-class political action.

  • “Defense Contractors Stand to Profit Off the Iran War” documents how Lockheed Martin’s stock jumped 3.4% on the war’s opening — useful reporting, but the political framework remains one of corporate influence on policy rather than the systemic character of imperialist war.

The DSA’s role was directly exposed when NYC Mayor Zohran Mamdani visited the White House to shake Trump’s hand on the eve of the bombing — an event the WSWS webinar identified as “exposing the absolute political bankruptcy” of the DSA current. Jacobin has not addressed Mamdani’s visit.

Overall orientation: Jacobin functions as a pressure valve, acknowledging mass antiwar sentiment while systematically redirecting it toward Democratic Party reform — the opposite of the independent working-class political mobilization that the crisis demands.

Sources Jacobin The US is fighting Israel’s war on Iran · Jacobin The Iran war shows why it’s time for Chuck Schumer to go · Jacobin Defense contractors stand to profit off the Iran war

Left Voice

Left Voice has published extensively on the Iran war, with the Trotskyist Fraction (FT/PTS) international network producing statements from multiple countries.

  • “Defend Iran, but Don’t Support the Theocracy” articulates the FT’s characteristic “third camp” position: opposing US imperialism while distancing from the Iranian government. While the anti-imperialist instinct is correct, the formulation obscures the central question: who will stop the war? Left Voice offers no concrete strategy for mobilizing working-class social power — the actual lever that could halt arms production and military logistics.

  • “Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran” calls on teachers’ unions to pass antiwar resolutions and organize workplace actions. This orientation toward existing union structures — controlled by bureaucracies that have supported every US war — substitutes institutional pressure for independent working-class political organization. The article does not address the AFL-CIO’s endorsement of the “No Kings” protests, which channel worker anger into the framework of Democratic Party-aligned coalition politics.

  • “Down With U.S. Imperialist Aggression Against Iran!” and the South Korean socialist statement both correctly identify the war’s imperialist character but remain at the level of denunciation without a program for building the international working-class movement necessary to stop it.

Overall orientation: Left Voice produces more radical-sounding rhetoric than Jacobin but shares the fundamental limitation: no strategy for building an independent political movement of the working class against war, substituting union resolutions and campus protests for the revolutionary mobilization that the situation demands.

Sources Left Voice Defend Iran, but don’t support the theocracy · Left Voice Educators must take the lead in opposing the imperialist war on Iran · Left Voice Down with U.S. imperialist aggression against Iran!

  • “Middle East: A Regional War Spiraling Out of Control” (Danny Byrne, March 4) provides a factual overview of the war’s regional spread — the Minab massacre, 1,000+ civilian deaths, expansion into Lebanon and the Gulf states — but the analysis remains at the level of describing catastrophe without a revolutionary program. The article identifies the scale of the conflict (2,000 sites hit, roughly double the “shock and awe” bombings of 2003) but frames the crisis as one requiring “a new international anti-war movement” without specifying its political character or class basis.

The CWI’s longstanding adaptation to reformist politics — including its support for various “broad left” parties internationally — leaves it unable to pose the question of revolutionary leadership.

Overall orientation: Socialist Alternative describes the horrors accurately but offers no strategic perspective beyond calling for protests and a vaguely defined “anti-war movement” — a formula that has proven impotent in every imperialist war since Iraq.
