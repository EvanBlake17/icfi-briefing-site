2. Iran’s Hormuz closure sends oil past $111/barrel and paralyzes global shipping

The Iranian closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil and gas normally transits daily — has brought international shipping to a near standstill and triggered severe commodity dislocations. WTI crude stood at approximately $111/barrel on April 6, with Brent at $109, both roughly 70% above pre-war levels of ~$65.

Gulf Arab regional oil exports have fallen 60%, from 25 million to approximately 10 million barrels per day. Iraqi output has collapsed 70% (from 4.3 to 1.3 million bbl/day), while Saudi Arabia has cut production 20% (from 10 to 8 million bbl/day). The IRGC Naval Command declared the strait “will never return to its former state, especially for America and Israel.”

Key facts: - 150+ ships anchored outside the strait; at least 21 confirmed Iranian attacks on merchant vessels; 16 damaged, 7 abandoned, 1 tug sunk - 12 seafarers killed or missing; 15,000 cruise ship passengers stranded on 6+ vessels - Urea/fertilizer prices up ~50% since the war began — up to 30% of globally traded fertilizers normally transit the strait - European natural gas peaked above €60/MWh, double the pre-war level of €30/MWh - Two Qatar LNG vessels retreated after approaching the strait

8. Artemis II crew completes historic lunar flyby

NASA’s Artemis II crew — Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen (Canadian Space Agency) — reached a maximum distance of 252,760 miles (406,972 km) from Earth on April 6, surpassing the Apollo 13 record of 248,655 miles. The crew passed within 4,070 miles of the lunar surface at 7:02 PM EDT, with a 40-minute communications blackout during the flyby.

Launched April 1, 2026, this is the first crewed lunar mission since Apollo 17 in December 1972 — a 54-year gap. All outbound trajectory correction burns were cancelled as Orion maintained precise trajectory without adjustment. The crew received 30 assigned lunar surface observation targets including the Orientale Basin (600 miles wide, 3.8 billion years old) and the Hertzsprung Basin on the far side.

The US measles outbreak continues its alarming trajectory. As of April 2, the CDC reports 1,671 confirmed cases across 33 states in 2026, with 96 new cases in the most recent week (+6.1%). Utah alone added 73 infections. Of the cases, 94% are outbreak-associated and 92% involve unvaccinated individuals. While no deaths have been reported in 2026 (vs. 3 in 2025) and hospitalizations are running lower at 5% (vs. 11% last year), the year is on track to be one of the two worst for measles since the disease was declared eliminated in the US in 2000. Bangladesh’s parallel emergency — 130 deaths in six weeks — underscores how the global collapse of routine immunization is producing preventable mass casualties.

H5N1 bird flu remains entrenched in dairy cattle, wild birds across every continent, and has caused the first confirmed wildlife die-off in Antarctica (50+ skuas). CDC surveillance shows no unusual influenza activity in humans, but the virus has spilled into mammals at an unprecedented scale. Virologists describe the global trajectory as “completely out of control,” with the window for pandemic emergence considered wider than at any prior point.

  • COVID-19: Activity is declining or likely declining in 32 states and growing in none as of March 31. A new variant, BA.3.2 (“cicada”), is circulating but represents only ~0.55% of sampled viruses. Florida and Massachusetts show slight upticks. No surge signals at this time.

Cardiometabolic markers including blood pressure and triglycerides also improved significantly. A companion study (ATTAIN-2) was published in The Lancet. A proven oral small-molecule GLP-1 agonist could dramatically expand access to weight-loss treatment beyond injectable semaglutide/tirzepatide.
  • AI governance conflict: The Department of Defense designated Anthropic a “supply chain risk” after the company refused contract clauses allowing autonomous weapons and domestic mass surveillance use of its AI.   • State AI legislation: Six weeks into the 2026 state legislative season, 78 chatbot-related bills are active in 27 states. Vermont has enacted synthetic-media election legislation. The Commerce Department began accepting proposals April 1 for “full-stack AI technology export packages” under Trump’s AI Action Plan.

Arts and culture

The most significant cultural development this week is the Writers Guild of America’s surprise four-year deal with studios, announced April 4 without a strike authorization vote — a move that isolates screenwriters from coordinated industry action with SAG-AFTRA and suppresses rank-and-file militancy. The WGA leadership’s simultaneous crushing of its own staff strike (ongoing since February 17) reveals the class dynamics within the entertainment industry’s union bureaucracy, where executive director compensation of $682,692 stands in sharp contrast to the below-subsistence wages faced by working writers in Los Angeles.

The Super Mario Galaxy Movie opened to $190 million domestically in its first five days and $372.5 million globally — the biggest opening of 2026 — despite weak critical reviews. The film received an A CinemaScore from audiences. The gap between critical reception and mass audience enthusiasm reflects the franchise’s function as a reliable escapist commodity during a period of acute social anxiety. That families are flooding theaters during an active Middle Eastern war and oil crisis speaks to the deep need for distraction amid conditions that cultural production itself rarely addresses.

NEA funding crisis enters its second year. The Trump administration’s assault on the National Endowment for the Arts has now terminated over 50% of open NEA awards since May 2025. The Challenge America grant program — which funded 272 projects totaling $2.72 million, primarily serving youth, emerging artists, and rural communities — has been cancelled entirely for FY 2026. New grant requirements demand a “five-year history of arts programming” and that proposals “celebrate and honor the 250th anniversary of the signing of the Declaration of Independence” — effectively weaponizing public arts funding as nationalist propaganda. Over 150 cultural institutions signed a statement in August 2025 warning that “intellectual freedom in cultural institutions is under serious threat.” The proposed FY 2026 budget calls for the complete elimination of the NEA, the NEH, and the Institute of Museum and Library Services.

Notable exhibitions opening this month reflect the contradictions of art under capitalism. MIT’s List Visual Arts Center opens “Performing Conditions: Artistic Labor and Dependency as Form” on April 11, an institutional critique examining “art and art workers put to work under racial capitalism.” The V&A East in London opens “The Music is Black: A British Story” on April 18, tracing Black musicians’ impact on British culture from the 1900s to the present — encompassing jazz, reggae, drum and bass, grime, and trip-hop. In Berlin, the Georg Kolbe Museum’s retrospective of constructivist Marlow Moss (1889–1958) approaches the geometric abstractionist without the condescending framing of “rediscovery.” The Hammer Museum’s “Several Eternities in a Day,” featuring 22 artists working with living materials, opens April 5 in Los Angeles.

This week in history: April 6–12

25 years ago (2001): South Korean autoworkers assaulted by police. On April 10, 2001, police violently attacked 350 laid-off Daewoo autoworkers at the Pupyong assembly plant in Incheon, beating them with batons, shields, fists, and boots. Over 90 workers were injured, with 43 hospitalized — injuries included vision loss, broken ribs, and a fractured pelvis. The attack sparked nationwide outrage after video circulated widely, exposing the brutality of the South Korean state’s response to workers resisting mass layoffs during the Asian financial crisis aftermath.

50 years ago (1976): Chinese Communist Party strips Deng Xiaoping of all positions. On April 7, 1976, the CCP Political Bureau removed Deng Xiaoping from his roles as Vice Chairman and Chief of General Staff, simultaneously appointing Hua Guofeng as First Vice Chairman and Premier. The move followed the “Tiananmen Incident” protests against the “Gang of Four” faction and reflected the terminal crisis of Maoism — Deng would return to power within two years and launch the capitalist restoration program that transformed China into a bastion of super-exploitation.

75 years ago (1951): Truman fires MacArthur. On April 11, 1951, President Truman removed General Douglas MacArthur from command of UN forces in Korea over disagreements about war strategy. MacArthur advocated direct attacks on China, while Truman pursued limited war policies to avoid direct confrontation with the Soviet Union. The episode exposed the tensions within the US ruling class over how to prosecute imperialist war without triggering a nuclear conflict.

100 years ago (1926): Violet Gibson attempts to assassinate Mussolini. On April 7, 1926, the Irish-born Gibson fired at the fascist dictator, grazing his nose. The attack paradoxically strengthened Mussolini’s position, providing the pretext for a wave of repressive laws that consolidated his dictatorship. Gibson was committed to a mental institution in England, where she remained until her death in 1956 — her act of individual resistance ultimately serving the very regime it sought to destroy.
