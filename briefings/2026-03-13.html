1. US-Israel war on Iran enters Day 13: Strait of Hormuz closed, 1,350+ civilians killed

The US-Israel military campaign against Iran entered its 13th day on March 12 with no sign of de-escalation. Iran’s Red Crescent reported 1,348 Iranian civilians killed in 12 days of airstrikes. The Pentagon acknowledged that its February 28 strike on an Iranian girls’ school killed 182 people. In Lebanon, the death toll has exceeded 630, with more than 816,000 displaced.

Iran’s new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei — son of the late Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, killed early in the war — issued his first public address vowing to keep the Strait of Hormuz closed as “a tool to pressure the enemy.” The IRGC launched its 37th wave of attacks, targeting US bases in Kuwait, Israeli air bases, and Gulf oil infrastructure. Iraq and Oman shut down oil terminals after Iranian drone and missile attacks.

Key facts: - The US military destroyed 16 Iranian minelayers near the strait; Iran has been planting naval mines since March 10 - Tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has dropped to near zero - A US official admitted the military is “not ready” to escort oil tankers through the strait - 3.2 million Iranians have been displaced; the first six days of war cost the US more than $11.3 billion - 46 US senators have demanded accountability for civilian casualties - A new NPR/PBS/Marist poll shows a majority of Americans oppose the war with Iran

2. Oil breaches $100/barrel as IEA declares largest supply disruption in history

Brent crude settled at $100.46/barrel on March 12, its first close above $100 since August 2022. WTI surged 9.7% to $95.73. The International Energy Agency called it the “largest oil supply disruption in the history of the global oil market,” with flows through Hormuz set to plunge by 8 million barrels per day this month.

IEA member countries agreed to release a record 400 million barrels of emergency oil reserves. Trump separately authorized release of 172 million barrels from the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve. Iranian officials warned oil could reach $200/barrel if attacks on energy infrastructure continue. Treasury Secretary Bessent issued a temporary authorization for stranded Russian oil tankers to continue delivery through April 11 — a workaround under sanctions pressure.

Key facts: - Saudi Arabia ramped up Red Sea pipeline operations to compensate for lost Hormuz capacity - Russia has earned an additional €6 billion ($6.9 billion) in two weeks of the Iran conflict, per CREA analysis - Airlines are raising fares sharply on trans-Atlantic, Caribbean, and domestic routes due to jet fuel costs

3. Michigan synagogue attack linked to family killed in Israeli airstrike

A Lebanese-born naturalized US citizen identified as Ayman Mohamad Ghazali, 41, rammed a truck through Temple Israel in West Bloomfield Township, Michigan, at 12:19 p.m. ET on March 12, armed with a rifle. Ghazali was shot and killed by synagogue security. One security guard was knocked unconscious by the truck and is expected to recover. Thirty law enforcement officers were treated for smoke inhalation.

A source in Michigan’s Lebanese-American community said Ghazali’s two brothers and two nephews were killed in an Israeli airstrike on his family’s village in Lebanon approximately 10 days prior; a sister-in-law was severely hospitalized. The FBI declared it a “targeted act of violence against the Jewish community” and mobilized over 100 agents. 140 children were in the attached preschool and school — none were harmed.

Key facts: - Michigan Gov. Gretchen Whitmer called it “every community’s worst nightmare” - The attack represents a direct, traceable instance of blowback from the US-Israel war in the Middle East - The corporate media has largely framed this as a hate crime while avoiding the causal chain to US and Israeli military policy

4. Sudan: drone strikes kill 200+ civilians in 9 days, including schoolgirls

An RSF drone struck a secondary school and health center in the village of Shukeiri, White Nile province on March 11, killing 17 people — mostly schoolgirls — and wounding at least 10. The Sudan Doctors Network confirmed no military presence in the village. On March 12, a separate drone struck a fuel market in West Darfur near the Chad border, killing 4 and wounding 23, including 7 children. MSF blamed the Sudanese army for that strike.

UN Human Rights Chief Volker Türk reported more than 200 civilians killed by drones since March 4 alone in Kordofan and White Nile state. At least 152 civilians were killed in West Kordofan by Sudanese army drone strikes, including 50 when a market and hospital were hit. Sudan’s civil war, which began in April 2023, has killed more than 40,000 people by UN estimates, with the true toll potentially far higher.

5. Ukraine war: Russia-Iran intelligence sharing exposed as Russia earns billions from oil shock

US intelligence reports that Russia has been supplying Iran with targeting intelligence on US troops, warships, and aircraft, and sharing drone deployment tactics. Russia has gained €6 billion from war-elevated oil prices in two weeks, per CREA analysis — profiting massively from a war it helped enable.

Russian forces lost 57 square miles of Ukrainian territory over the past four weeks (February 10–March 10), a sharp reversal from the 182 square miles gained in the prior period. Ukraine struck a Russian plant producing microchips and missile electronics using Storm Shadow missiles. Peace talks remain “stuck” over Putin’s demand for full Donbas cession; 67% of Russians now support peace negotiations, but only 25% of Ukrainians believe talks will yield lasting peace. At least two-thirds of Ukraine’s energy production capacity has been destroyed, damaged, or occupied since fall 2024.

7. Supreme Court tariff fallout: $130 billion in refunds ordered

The February 20 Supreme Court ruling that IEEPA does not authorize presidential tariff powers continues to reshape US trade policy. A federal judge ordered the government to issue $130+ billion in refunds to more than 2,000 companies including Costco and FedEx that paid illegal tariffs. Trump pivoted to a 10% global tariff under Section 122, effective February 24. Treasury Secretary Bessent told CNBC the Section 122 tariff would rise to 15% “likely this week.”

The administration launched new Section 301 trade investigations covering 60 countries. Singapore publicly disputed US trade surplus data used to justify its inclusion. Twenty-four states filed suit to block the Section 122 tariffs. The Trump tariffs represent the largest US tax increase as a percentage of GDP since 1993, averaging a $1,500 per household burden in 2026.

Belgium national strike: 100,000 march against NATO-driven austerity

Over the past year there have been 13 national demonstrations and 5 national strike days — one of the largest sustained social mobilizations in Belgium since the 1960s.

Volkswagen raises job cuts to 50,000 as profits collapse by 44%

Volkswagen Group announced it will cut 50,000 jobs in Germany by 2030, up from the 35,000 previously agreed with trade unions. Europe’s largest carmaker reported earnings after tax fell 44% to €6.9 billion — the lowest since 2016. Revenue stagnated at around €322 billion while operating profit nearly halved to €8.9 billion. VW Group CEO Oliver Blume cited Trump’s tariffs, intensifying Chinese competition, and costly Porsche restructuring. The cuts span all German-based brands including Audi, Porsche, and the Cariad software unit, and would reduce VW’s German workforce from roughly 293,000 to 250,000.

US measles cases approach 1,300 as South Carolina epidemic sets records

The CDC reports 1,281 confirmed measles cases in 2026 across 31 jurisdictions as of March 5. South Carolina alone accounts for 993 cases centered in Spartanburg County — the highest single-state count since measles elimination was declared in 2000. Eighty-nine percent of cases are outbreak-associated, and 94% involve unvaccinated individuals. The US is at serious risk of losing its measles elimination status. CDC Epidemic Intelligence Service officers are now deployed on the ground in South Carolina.

H5N1 detected in 37 mammal species across the Americas

PAHO reported that between January 2025 and March 9, 2026, H5N1 was detected in 37 mammalian species across 2 countries and 94 bird species across 11 countries/territories in the Americas. US dairy cattle infections now span 18 states with 1,000+ herds affected. Total regional human cases since 2022: 75, with 2 deaths. The virus’s expanding mammalian host range — including swine — raises concerns about reassortment potential and pandemic risk. First human trial of PCSK9 base editing cuts LDL by 52%

A phase 1 trial published in Nature Medicine demonstrated that YOLT-101, a lipid nanoparticle–delivered base editor targeting PCSK9 in liver cells, produced a 74.4% reduction in circulating PCSK9 and a 52.3% reduction in LDL cholesterol at 24 weeks (0.6 mg/kg dose, n=6), with no serious adverse events and no detectable off-target editing. This is the first-in-human demonstration that a single-dose base editing therapy can durably lower cholesterol, offering a potential permanent alternative to daily statins or bi-weekly injections for familial hypercholesterolemia patients.

Sources Nature Medicine PCSK9 base editing phase 1 trial results

Trump Commerce Department targets state AI laws for preemption

Per Executive Order 14365 (signed December 2025), the Department of Commerce was required by March 11 to publish a report identifying state AI laws deemed to conflict with federal policy. The DOJ’s AI Litigation Task Force will use the report as a roadmap to sue states including California, Colorado, and New York. The EO also conditions $42 billion in BEAD broadband funding on state compliance. This marks the most aggressive federal move to consolidate AI oversight at the national level and could invalidate dozens of recently enacted state AI consumer-protection laws.

Meanwhile, the European Commission published the second draft of its AI-generated content labeling Code of Practice. Full AI Act applicability is set for August 2, 2026. The EU and US are now diverging sharply on AI governance — Brussels moving toward mandatory labeling and risk rules while Washington moves to preempt state-level guardrails.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s March output is dominated by the Iran war. “The Iran War Will Probably Increase Nuclear Weapons Globally” by Emma Claire Foley argues that Trump’s preemptive attack on Iran — ostensibly to prevent nuclear proliferation — has set a dangerous precedent, noting Macron’s announcement of an expanded French nuclear arsenal. The analysis correctly identifies the proliferation dynamic but frames it as a failure of American “leadership” rather than an inherent product of imperialism.

“The US Is Fighting Israel’s War on Iran” reports that three high-ranking US officials close to Trump have publicly said the US was “forced into” the war by Israel, citing an extensive New York Times report on Netanyahu’s behind-the-scenes maneuvering. The framing isolates the Israel lobby as the causal mechanism, obscuring the independent imperialist interests driving US policy in the Middle East.

“Workers Are Leaving the Trump Coalition” by Jared Abbott and Joan C. Williams analyzes Trump’s declining support among working-class voters, oriented entirely toward the question of whether the Democratic Party can recapture these voters — the standard Jacobin project of rehabilitating the Democrats as a vehicle for progressive reform.

The day’s overall orientation: substantive reporting on the war’s consequences combined with a political framework that remains trapped within bourgeois institutions. No article proposes independent working-class political action; the implicit solution is always better Democratic Party politics.

Sources Jacobin The Iran war will probably increase nuclear weapons globally · Jacobin The US is fighting Israel’s war on Iran · Jacobin Workers are leaving the Trump coalition

Left Voice

Left Voice has produced extensive coverage of the Iran war through the lens of its parent tendency, the PTS/Fracción Trotskista. “We Need a Mass Movement to Defeat Trump’s Imperialist War on Iran” (March 2), a CPR-FI statement, calls for the strongest repudiation of the war “in the streets by the working class and young people.” “Defend Iran, but Don’t Support the Theocracy” argues for a nuanced position — defending Iran against imperialism while opposing the Islamic Republic — and explicitly criticizes the PSL and Germany’s Kommunistische Organisation for giving political support to the Iranian regime.

“Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran” references the funeral of 160 girls killed by Israeli strikes on a school in Minab, arguing that educators have a special role in opposing the war.

The overall orientation is more explicitly anti-capitalist than Jacobin’s, with a clear position against both imperialism and the Iranian regime. However, the strategic perspective centers on building “mass movements” and pressuring the “labor movement” — categories that remain undefined in terms of political program. The absence of any concrete analysis of the trade union bureaucracies’ role in containing anti-war opposition is a significant gap.

Sources Left Voice We need a mass movement to defeat Trump’s imperialist war on Iran · Left Voice Defend Iran, but don’t support the theocracy · Left Voice Educators must take the lead in opposing the imperialist war on Iran

PSL / Liberation News

The PSL’s “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” (February 28) denounces the US-Israel attack and calls for an end to the war. The statement correctly opposes the war but slides from defending Iran against imperialism to offering implicit political support for the Islamic Republic. The formulation avoids any criticism of the Iranian regime’s suppression of workers, women, and political dissidents.

“Free Booker Ngesa Omole!” demands the release of the secretary general of the Communist Party Marxist–Kenya, reportedly kidnapped and brutalized by Kenyan police. This represents the PSL’s ongoing solidarity work with international left organizations.

The overall PSL orientation combines legitimate anti-war sentiment with an uncritical defense of bourgeois-nationalist regimes that opposes US imperialism from the standpoint of supporting its geopolitical rivals — a consistent pattern in the Marcyite tradition.

Sources Liberation News PSL statement: Stop Trump’s war on Iran!

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s most significant recent piece is “5 Steps To Get Strike Ready for May 1” (February 26), organizing for a national day of “No Work, No School, No Shopping.” The article proposes using the March 28 “No Kings Day” protest as an intermediate action. The strategic orientation is toward a national general strike, but the programmatic content remains limited to demands within the existing system — no call for a break with the Democrats, no socialist program for power.

“Iran: Thousands Killed In Bloody Crackdown” (January 21) covered the pre-war Iranian protest movement, correctly noting the role of 70% food inflation in triggering social unrest. The piece calls for support for Iranian workers and youth “fighting against dictatorship and capitalism” — a more balanced formulation than the PSL’s.

Sources Socialist Alternative 5 steps to get strike ready for May 1 · Socialist Alternative Iran: thousands killed in bloody crackdown

SWP (UK)

The SWP’s Socialist Worker is overwhelmingly focused on building the March 28 “Together” anti-far-right demonstration in London. Multiple articles in the past week — on students mobilizing, education workers backing the demo, and workers booking time off — are essentially organizational mobilization pieces. “National Housing Demo will unite the fightback” promotes an April 18 housing demonstration.

The near-total absence of Iran war coverage is striking. The SWP’s orientation toward broad-front “anti-far-right” coalition politics — the “Together” alliance brings together over 300 organizations — reflects the tendency’s longstanding adaptation to liberal protest movements. The housing and anti-fascist campaigns, while addressing real issues, avoid any challenge to the Labour government or the trade union bureaucracy.

Sources Socialist Worker Workers unite for Together demo · Socialist Worker National housing demo will unite the fightback

RCP / IMT

The Revolutionary Communist Party (formerly Socialist Appeal) is focused on party-building ahead of its National Congress, with “Seven weeks until the RCP’s National Congress — Forward to 1,400 members!” noting that “every single day of 2026 has illustrated the horror and bankruptcy of the entire system.” Alan Woods’ “Hands Off Iran” (March 2) argues that the US-Israel war “threatens to spiral out of control,” placing blame “entirely upon American imperialism and its allies.”

“Sexism and abuse comes from the top — Overthrow their toxic system!” by Fiona Lali uses the Epstein scandal to argue that “capitalism breeds violence, sexism, and abuse.” The IMT’s overall orientation combines revolutionary rhetoric with organizational recruitment drives. The Iran coverage opposes the war without engaging the question of what political program is needed to mobilize the working class against it.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Belgium’s national strike wave against NATO-driven austerity

Belgium’s third national strike in a week brought 100,000 into the streets on March 12, shutting down Brussels Airport, the Port of Antwerp, schools, and transit systems. Workers are fighting a €10 billion austerity package that cuts pensions and public services while the government ramps up military spending with a €33.8 billion NATO program. This is one of the largest sustained working-class mobilizations in Western Europe in decades — 13 national demonstrations and 5 strike days in the past year — and directly connects the WSWS’s core themes of war, austerity, and the role of trade unions.

Recommended reading Peoples Dispatch 100,000 join national strike against austerity in Belgium · Strike Tracker Belgium national strike March 12, 2026

2. Russia earns €6 billion from the war it helped provoke while US workers face $1,500 tariff burden

Russia is profiting massively from the oil shock caused by the US-Iran war it helped enable through intelligence-sharing with Tehran — earning €6 billion in two weeks from elevated oil prices. Simultaneously, US working-class households face a $1,500-per-year tariff burden from Trump’s new Section 122 tariffs. The intersection of war profiteering, energy markets, and the class distribution of economic costs deserves systematic WSWS analysis.

Recommended reading FDD Russia helps Iran attack US and its allies, Ukraine helps defend them

3. Pentagon admits it failed to model Iran’s Hormuz response — $11.3 billion war cost in 6 days

Multiple US officials acknowledged that the Trump national security team “significantly underestimated” Iran’s willingness to close the Strait of Hormuz. The war has already cost over $11.3 billion in six days. The gap between stated war aims (denuclearization) and the catastrophic economic consequences — including the IEA declaring the largest supply disruption in oil market history — warrants detailed analysis of imperialist strategic incompetence.

Recommended reading NPR Iran effectively closes Strait of Hormuz as US-Israel strikes continue

4. DOGE spending cuts hamper US war-fighting capability as Iran conflict escalates

CNN reported that Elon Musk’s DOGE-driven federal workforce reductions — which ultimately saved only about $20 billion of a promised $2 trillion — are now hampering US military logistics and intelligence capacity during the Iran war. The contradiction between austerity ideology and imperial overextension is a classic case of the internal contradictions of capitalist governance and deserves systematic treatment.

Recommended reading CNN How DOGE spending cuts are hampering the US government amid war with Iran

5. Michigan synagogue attack as blowback — the causal chain from Israeli airstrikes to American soil

The attack on Temple Israel by a man who lost two brothers and two nephews in an Israeli airstrike 10 days prior is a direct, traceable instance of blowback from the US-Israel war. The corporate media frames this exclusively as a hate crime while avoiding the causal chain to US and Israeli military policy. A WSWS article could provide the class analysis that the bourgeois media systematically excludes.

Recommended reading CBS News What we know about the Michigan synagogue attack

6. Die Linke’s fight to win back working-class Germans — and why it will fail

Jacobin published a piece by Die Linke leader Ines Schwerdtner on the party’s strategy to recapture working-class support from the AfD. Given the WSWS’s extensive coverage of German politics and its critique of Die Linke as a prop for bourgeois politics, this would be a timely intervention analyzing why a party that participated in imposing austerity at the state level cannot credibly present itself as an alternative.

Recommended reading Jacobin Die Linke’s fight to win back working-class Germans

With the Iran war dominating headlines, Gaza’s ongoing crisis — 72,134 confirmed dead, a ceasefire violated over 1,620 times, and WCK warning it will run out of food supplies — is being systematically sidelined. The deliberate use of starvation and crossing closures as siege warfare continues with impunity while international attention shifts.
