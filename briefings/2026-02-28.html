1. US and Israel launch war on Iran; at least 201 killed, Iran retaliates across the Gulf

In the early hours of Saturday February 28, the United States and Israel launched a joint military operation against Iran — dubbed “Lion’s Roar” by Netanyahu — constituting the most significant US military strike since the 2003 invasion of Iraq. The USS Lincoln launched strikes from the southern Gulf. Israeli strikes hit Tehran, Qom, Kermanshah, Isfahan, Karaj, Tabriz, and naval facilities at Kenarak. The compound of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei’s “Leadership House” in Tehran was struck; BBC Verify confirmed significant damage through Airbus satellite imagery. Khamenei has been evacuated.

At least 201 people have been killed according to Al Jazeera’s running tally. An Israeli strike on a girls’ school in Minab in southern Iran killed at least 85, per Iranian state media. The IDF confirmed strikes targeting “senior political and security figures.” Trump cited Iran’s “failure to negotiate in good faith” over its nuclear program and openly declared “regime change” as the goal. As David North wrote on the WSWS, the attack constitutes “a massive political crime, illegal under international law and in direct violation of the US Constitution,” comparing it to Hitler’s September 1, 1939 invasion of Poland.

Key facts: - Iranian retaliation: Iran fired missiles at Israel and US bases across the region. Bahrain’s US Navy Fifth Fleet base was struck. Kuwait’s Ali Al-Salem airbase was targeted by ballistic missiles (intercepted). Jordan shot down two ballistic missiles. - Airspace shutdowns: Eight countries including Iran, Israel, Jordan, and Qatar closed airspace. British Airways, Virgin Atlantic, Wizz Air, Qatar Airways, and Emirates suspended or diverted flights. - UK involvement: RAF jets are flying “defensive missions” in the Middle East; PM Starmer confirmed British planes in the air. - Congressional opposition: Multiple US lawmakers condemned the strikes as “acts of war unauthorized by Congress.” - International response: China “expressed deep concern” (SCMP). The EU urged “maximum restraint.” Mediator Oman warned the US “not to get sucked in further.” - Trump quotes: “We’re doing this not for now. We’re doing this for the future.” “Iran is the world’s number one state sponsor of terror.” - Context: The attack came just four days after Trump’s SOTU address — during which he concealed his decision — and days after the Geneva talks where the US claimed “significant progress.”

Japan approves near-total lifting of weapons export ban

Japan’s LDP Research Commission on Security approved lifting nearly all restrictions on lethal weapons exports — a dramatic escalation of Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s remilitarization agenda following the LDP’s landslide in the February general election. Exports of jointly developed weapons to third countries are now permitted, and exports to nations at war are allowed with National Security Council approval. The changes apply to 17 partner nations including the US, UK, Australia, and the Philippines. No Diet approval is required. Japan’s defense budget has now reached 2% of GDP.

As the WSWS’s Ben McGrath noted, Tokyo is “rapidly accelerating its remilitarization agenda in preparation for waging war abroad and war against the working class at home.”

Measles surge continues: 1,136 confirmed cases

As of February 26, the CDC reports 1,136 confirmed measles cases in the United States in 2026, across 28 jurisdictions. Ten new outbreaks have been reported this year, with 90% of cases (1,023 of 1,136) outbreak-associated. For comparison, full year 2025 saw 2,281 cases with 50 outbreaks; 2024 had only 285 cases and 16 outbreaks. The US is on pace to surpass 2025’s record total before the end of April — a catastrophic collapse in vaccine-preventable disease control.

Six cases were among international visitors. Jay Bhattacharya’s control of CDC and NIH has not reversed the anti-vaccination political climate fostered by the Trump administration.

Sources CDC Measles cases and outbreaks data

Nature published a comment article titled “COVID’s origins: what we do and don’t know,” summarizing what it calls “key insights from the world’s first comprehensive investigation into how a pandemic started.” The article provides a synthesis of evidence on the lab leak versus natural spillover debate without reaching a definitive conclusion, noting ongoing scientific debate. This is the first attempt at a comprehensive, peer-reviewed synthesis of COVID origins evidence — directly relevant to the WSWS Global Workers’ Inquest into the COVID-19 Pandemic, which has insisted on rigorous scientific investigation as opposed to the politically motivated “lab leak” narrative promoted by the US intelligence establishment.

Chloé Zhao’s Hamnet reviewed by WSWS

David Walsh reviews Chloé Zhao’s film Hamnet, based on Maggie O’Farrell’s 2020 novel, starring Jessie Buckley as Agnes/Anne Hathaway and Paul Mescal as the young William Shakespeare. The film proposes that Shakespeare wrote Hamlet as a tribute to his 11-year-old son Hamnet, who died in 1596. Walsh acknowledges the film’s genuine emotional depth, strong performances, and period authenticity — Buckley is particularly compelling — while identifying its central limitation: it does not fully engage with the social and historical forces that Shakespeare himself grappled with in his greatest works.

The review’s significance extends beyond a single film. Published on the day the United States launched a war on Iran, Walsh’s insistence that great art must engage with “the very age and body of the time” — Shakespeare’s own phrase from Hamlet — resonates with particular urgency. The question of whether artists today are capable of confronting the social catastrophes of their era, rather than retreating into private emotional landscapes, is among the most important facing contemporary culture.

Jacobin / DSA

The day’s most politically significant Jacobin piece is Meagan Day’s “Zohran Mamdani Is Trying a Fresh Tactic With Trump” — an open apologia for the DSA mayor’s second secret meeting with Trump. Day characterizes the meeting as “conciliatory” and describes it as “both a savvy and potentially perilous strategy,” treating collaboration with fascism as a matter of tactical risk rather than class betrayal. The framing converges directly with the bourgeois press’s “Is Mamdani playing 4D chess?” narrative, providing a left cover for what the WSWS Perspective correctly identified as an act of political treachery comparable to Pierre Laval’s collaboration with the Nazis.

Jacobin also published “Spain’s Radical Left Is in Trouble but Not Defeated Yet”, covering Labor Minister Yolanda Diaz (Sumar) stepping down and calling for a “united left-wing front” for Spain’s next general election. This is standard national-reformist fare: electoral coalitions presented as the path forward for the working class, with no discussion of independent class politics or the bankruptcy of the Podemos/Sumar experiment in managing Spanish capitalism.

A third piece, “The Genocide in Gaza Has Not Ended”, by Jerusalem-based historian Lee Mordechai, correctly argues that genocide continued after the Trump-brokered ceasefire. The article is factually valuable but stops at expose without class analysis, anti-imperialist conclusions, or any call for independent working-class mobilization. On the day the US launched a war on Iran — a direct extension of the same imperialist aggression devastating Gaza — the absence of any connection to the broader war drive is politically significant.

The overall Jacobin/DSA orientation for the day: open apologetics for collaboration with Trumpism (Mamdani), reformist electoral coalitionism (Spain), and factual criticism of Israel that studiously avoids anti-imperialist conclusions (Gaza). The class interests expressed: the upper-middle-class layer that supports the Democratic Party and fears independent working-class action.

Left Voice published “No War on Iran: We Must Build an Anti-Imperialist Movement to Stop Trump” — opposing the US-Israeli attack and calling for “the strongest repudiation in the streets by the working class and young people.” The article correctly characterizes the war as imperialist and provides useful factual details (operation “Lion’s Roar,” USS Lincoln as launch platform). However, the anti-war sentiment is framed within a “movement” perspective that avoids an explicit socialist program, offers no critique of the DSA and reformist misleadership of the anti-war movement, and is likely to channel opposition into DSA-friendly coalition politics rather than independent working-class organization.

A second piece, “UAW Local 2325 Commits to a Strike Vote to Fight ICE”, covers UAW Local 2325 (legal services workers) committing to a strike vote. Left Voice promotes the action without critically analyzing the UAW bureaucracy’s systemic role in containing and betraying past strike waves — treating bureaucratic strike talk as genuine working-class resistance.

The overall Left Voice orientation: anti-imperialist posture without revolutionary program; adaptation to union bureaucracy without class critique.
