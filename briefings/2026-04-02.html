1. Trump addresses nation on Iran war, claims victory “nearing completion”

In a primetime address on April 1, President Trump declared the US war on Iran’s “core strategic objectives are nearing completion,” citing the degradation of Iran’s missiles, drones, and naval capacity over the five-week campaign that began February 28. He set an April 6 deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, threatening strikes on power plants and water desalination facilities if the demand is not met.

The scale of destruction is staggering. US forces have conducted 13,000 combat flights, struck over 12,300 targets, and damaged or destroyed more than 155 Iranian vessels since the war began. Confirmed dead: at least 1,937 Iranians (some estimates higher), 13 US service members, 24 Israelis, and 27 killed in Gulf states. Oil prices spiked to $104/barrel after the speech, with economists warning prices could reach $150–200/barrel if the Strait remains closed — it carries approximately 20% of global oil consumption.

Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi stated “trust is at zero” for negotiations, denying active talks despite US communications. As Andre Damon wrote in the WSWS Perspective published today, Trump’s language — vowing to send Iran “back to the Stone Ages” — represents a qualitative escalation in the public presentation of American war aims, dispensing with even the rhetorical veneer of liberation or humanitarianism that accompanied previous campaigns.

7. WFP slashes food aid for 1.2 million Rohingya refugees in Bangladesh

Effective April 1, the WFP cut monthly food assistance for 1.2 million Rohingya refugees from $12/person to as low as $7/person under a new tiered system based on assessed “need severity.” The 2026 funding for Rohingya programs stands at only 19% of the required amount; 2025 programs were only about 50% funded.

Bangladesh’s Refugee Relief Commissioner warned: “Law and order will be deteriorated” as refugees seek food and work. Dozens of Rohingya staged protests against the cuts on April 1. In November 2023, before rations were briefly restored, 90% of camp residents could not afford adequate diets and 15% of children suffered acute malnutrition — the highest rate ever recorded in the camps. Refugees told reporters they are considering fleeing to Malaysia on dangerous fishing boats or returning to Myanmar despite military violence; hundreds die on such sea crossings annually.

Supreme Court hears landmark birthright citizenship case as Trump attends arguments

The Supreme Court heard oral arguments April 1 in Trump v. Barbara, a landmark case in which the administration seeks to restrict birthright citizenship guaranteed by the 14th Amendment. Trump became the first sitting president to attend oral arguments. ACLU lawyer Cecilia Wang — herself born in Oregon to parents from Taiwan on student visas — argued: “Ask any American what our citizenship rule is, and they’ll tell you, everyone born here is a citizen alike.”

Early indications from SCOTUSblog suggest the Court is likely to side against Trump.

Simultaneously, Trump signed a new executive order directing DHS and the Social Security Administration to construct a federal voter-eligibility master list — what the WSWS’s Patrick Martin characterized as the infrastructure for mass disenfranchisement.

US measles cases hit 1,575 as outbreak season accelerates

As of March 26, the CDC confirmed 1,575 measles cases across 32 jurisdictions, with 16 active outbreaks in 2026. South Carolina leads with over 600 cases; Utah has 318; Florida has 116. An overwhelming 94% of cases are outbreak-linked, and 93% involve unvaccinated or unknown-status individuals. The US is on pace to shatter the 2025 record of 2,284 cases and risks losing its measles elimination status — held since 2000 — which the WHO will formally review in November.

H5N1: 71 human cases in the US, first Antarctic die-off confirmed

The CDC reports 71 total US human cases of A(H5) bird flu since February 2024, with no current indicators of unusual human influenza activity. H5N1 has now been confirmed as the cause of the first Antarctic wildlife die-off, killing more than 50 skuas — a troubling geographic expansion into Earth’s last major unaffected ecosystem. USDA has committed $1 billion to curb HPAI spread. Experts warn surveillance remains uneven in the US dairy sector.

NEJM: enfortumab vedotin–pembrolizumab doubles survival in bladder cancer

A study published in the New England Journal of Medicine found that in cisplatin-ineligible patients with muscle-invasive bladder cancer, enfortumab vedotin combined with pembrolizumab plus surgery achieved 74.7% event-free survival versus 39.4% in the comparator arm, with overall survival of 79.7%. This near-doubling of event-free survival represents a potential new standard of care for a population with few existing treatment options.

Trump administration unveils national AI policy framework

The White House released a six-pronged legislative framework to create a single national AI policy, explicitly preempting all state-level AI laws. Provisions cover child safety, data center energy permitting, and export controls via the new “American AI Exports Program.” Separately, a federal judge ruled the administration’s ban on Anthropic AI models in government violated First Amendment protections — setting a significant precedent for government AI procurement restrictions.

GLP-1 agonists reduce cardiovascular risk in type 1 diabetes

A trial emulation study published in Nature Medicine, using electronic health record data from 174,678 patients with type 1 diabetes, found that initiating GLP-1 receptor agonists was associated with significantly reduced risk of major cardiovascular events and end-stage kidney disease. GLP-1 drugs — already transforming obesity and type 2 diabetes care — now show strong real-world cardiovascular benefit signals in type 1 diabetes, a population largely excluded from prior landmark trials.

Arts and culture

The dominant development in arts and culture is not any single work of art but the systematic destruction of the infrastructure that supports artistic creation in the United States. One year after the Trump administration moved to eliminate the National Endowment for the Arts, the consequences are becoming starkly visible — and they intersect directly with the broader social counterrevolution accompanying the Iran war.

NEA cuts: one year later, US arts infrastructure crumbles

The Portland Mercury’s comprehensive assessment, published in March, documents the cascading damage across theaters, museums, and dance companies now operating without NEA support they had relied on for years. The House Interior Appropriations Subcommittee proposed a 35% reduction for both the NEA and NEH, cutting budgets from $207 million to $135 million. The Corporation for Public Broadcasting has been dissolved entirely after Congress rescinded its federal funding. The NEA cancelled grants across the country — including a $30,000 grant to Oregon Contemporary for its 2026 Artists’ Biennial after requesting the names and biographical information of the curator and artists, then cancelling when identities were revealed. An American Alliance of Museums survey found one-third of US institutions affected by Trump-related grant cuts. Senior NEA staff — including program directors for dance, theater, design, folk and traditional arts, museums, and literary programs — have resigned in protest or been forced into retirement.

The NEA’s new grant priorities are themselves revealing: “skilled trade jobs,” “AI competency,” “support for the military and veterans,” and “economic development of Asian American communities” — a nakedly instrumental vision in which art serves the state rather than expressing the contradictions of human experience.

Gelman Collection controversy: Mexican cultural heritage at risk

More than 300 Mexican cultural professionals have signed open letters demanding transparency after Banco Santander announced in January that it will manage 160 works from the Gelman Collection — including 11 Frida Kahlo paintings, all designated as Monumentos Artísticos de la Nación by presidential decree in 1984. Critics say the agreement, which could see works travel to the Fondation Beyeler in Switzerland, the Neue Nationalgalerie in Berlin, and the Nasjonalmuseet in Oslo, effectively circumvents Mexican heritage law prohibiting permanent export. Mexico’s culture minister claimed the collection will return in 2028, but offered no specific date. One critic warned: “This perverts the law; the contract opens the unprecedented possibility of extending the works’ export indefinitely.” The case exemplifies how cultural heritage becomes a commodity when managed by financial capital — Banco Santander administering Kahlo’s revolutionary muralist legacy is an irony that requires no elaboration.

  • Left Voice — strongest anti-Democratic Party polemic of the cycle with the Mamdani/Hochul critique; correct anti-imperialist framing on Iran

  • Socialist Worker / SWP UK — routing anti-war politics through union structures with demands on the Starmer government rather than independent workers’ action

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output this cycle illustrates the tendency’s fundamental orientation. “Abdul El-Sayed’s Senate Opponent Is a Phony Populist” (Jacobin) backs El-Sayed’s Democratic Party primary campaign against Mallory McMorrow — open promotion of bourgeois electoral politics under a socialist veneer. This is the DSA’s persistent mode: channeling left-wing energy into the Democratic Party at the very moment that party offers zero opposition to five weeks of bombing Iran.

“The US and Israel Are Making Gaza-Style War the New Normal” (Jacobin) frames US/Israeli operations as the normalization of war crimes — anti-imperialist in rhetoric but rooted in international law and “accountability” discourse rather than class analysis. There is no call for working-class opposition to the war, only condemnation. “Capitalism Had a Beginning and Will Someday End” (Jacobin) interviews historian Sven Beckert — the implicit “end” envisioned is reform, not revolution.

Day’s orientation: Class-collaborationist electoralism wrapped in leftist commentary. The gap between Jacobin’s anti-war articles and its active promotion of Democratic Party candidates encapsulates the DSA’s political bankruptcy.

Left Voice

Left Voice produced the strongest anti-Democratic Party polemic of the cycle. “Why Is Zohran Mamdani Endorsing Strikebreaker Kathy Hochul?” (Left Voice) directly names DSA/Mamdani’s subordination to the Democratic Party machine as class collaboration — a notable contrast with Jacobin’s simultaneous promotion of another DSA candidate in the same party.

“With Dwindling Interceptors and Degraded Radar, Israel and the US Enter Uncharted Territory” (Left Voice) analyzes the Iran war through a military-strategic lens, calling for working-class internationalist opposition aligned with a Trotskyist Fraction (FT-CI) declaration. “Argentina Passes Anti-Labor Law Amid Fierce Protest and State Repression” (Left Voice) provides solid class-struggle coverage of the Milei government’s attacks.

Day’s orientation: Left Voice’s consistent refusal to tail the Democratic Party distinguishes it sharply from Jacobin. However, the FT-CI’s Pabloite heritage — and its historical adaptation to Castroism, Chavismo, and various “anti-imperialist” bourgeois governments — limits the tendency’s capacity to draw the necessary political conclusions from its own correct observations.

Liberation News / PSL

“Socialist Response to Trump’s Televised Address on Iran War” (Liberation News) condemns the war as imperialist genocide — strong anti-war rhetoric. But the PSL’s anti-imperialism remains national-reformist: it defends Iran as a sovereign nation rather than grounding opposition in an international working-class program. No call for workers’ action against the war.

“Artemis II: $100 Billion Push to Extend US Capitalism Off Earth” (Liberation News) critiques space militarization competently but identifies no class agency. “New WNBA CBA Shows the Power of Union Organizing” (Liberation News) celebrates the players’ CBA as a success story for collective bargaining within capitalism rather than a lesson toward independent class organization.

Day’s orientation: State-centric anti-imperialism without a working-class program. The PSL’s politics remain those of a Stalinist-influenced tendency that substitutes the defense of oppressed nations for the international mobilization of the working class.

Socialist Alternative

“Trump’s Middle East Disaster — Build Mass Struggle Against Imperialist War” (Socialist Alternative) is the strongest anti-war piece from the CWI/ISA tendency this cycle. It explicitly rejects the Democratic Party as an imperialist vehicle and references general strike models — correctly identifying both parties as imperialist.

“The Midterms Loom Over Trump” (Socialist Alternative) analyzes voter suppression via the SAVE Act but risks sliding into “defend democracy” politics that orient workers toward bourgeois electoral remedies — though SA does call for independent working-class power in the same breath. “From School To The Streets — Walk Out For Trans Rights!” (Socialist Alternative) ties trans rights to anti-capitalist struggle, consistent with SA’s orientation toward direct action.

Day’s orientation: SA’s anti-war and anti-Democratic Party rhetoric is often correct in form, but the tendency’s historical inability to draw a sharp organizational line against the trade union bureaucracy and its adaptation to identity politics as a strategic orientation limits the program’s coherence.

Socialist Worker / SWP UK

“NEU Union Conference: Motion Condemns ‘Barbarous’ Imperial War” (Socialist Worker) reports a union conference motion opposing US/Israeli military operations and demanding the UK close airspace to US military flights. This is the SWP’s core strategy — routing anti-war politics through union structures. The demand, however, is addressed to the Starmer government rather than calling for independent workers’ action.

“Show Solidarity with Palestine Movement Organisers Ben Jamal and Chris Nineham” (Socialist Worker) defends two Palestine solidarity organizers convicted on public order charges — correct solidarity stance, though Nineham is a SWP/Stop the War figure. “‘West Must Understand Resistance Is Our Right’: The Battle of Nabi Chit in Lebanon” (Socialist Worker) validates the right of armed resistance to Israeli occupation without uncritical glorification of Hezbollah, though the political framing supports national resistance over international working-class mobilization.

Day’s orientation: The SWP’s Stop the War Coalition orientation dominates — demands on the capitalist state rather than organization of independent working-class power.

“Trump, Starmer, and the Madness of Capitalism” (The Communist) offers a systemic crisis editorial arguing that capitalism’s contradictions are creating revolutionary openings. Rhetorically militant, but the IMT/RCP tendency’s entryist orientation toward Labour remains the elephant in the room — “revolutionary” analysis coexists with decades of practical orientation toward the Labour left.

“Fiona Lali: ‘The World Is on Fire — Let’s Put Capitalism Out of Its Misery!’” (The Communist) links British complicity in Middle Eastern wars to domestic austerity and calls for strikes and mass movements. Lali positions the RCP explicitly against Starmer — but the RCP’s history of Labour entryism means this anti-Starmer posture is relatively recent.
