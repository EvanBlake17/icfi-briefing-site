1. US submarine sinks Iranian frigate in first torpedo kill since WWII; death toll passes 1,230

On Day 6 of the US-Israeli war against Iran, the Pentagon confirmed that a US submarine torpedoed the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena in the Indian Ocean approximately 40 nautical miles south of Galle, Sri Lanka — more than 1,000 miles from Iranian territory. The attack, using a Mark 48 torpedo, killed at least 87 of the 180 people on board, with 61 still missing. It was the first US submarine torpedo kill since World War II.

The sinking dramatically expanded the war’s geographic footprint to the Indian Ocean while the conflict already spans at least 11 countries. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared the US is “accelerating, not decelerating” and “just getting started,” adding: “We are punching them while they’re down, which is exactly how it should be.” The White House released bombing video “edited like a highlight reel” with kill counts displayed as scores.

Key facts:

  • Total Iranian casualties: 1,230+ killed, 6,000+ wounded in six days
  • Civilian infrastructure targeted: Iran claims 33 civilian sites hit including hospitals, schools, Tehran Grand Bazaar, and Golestan Palace
  • School bombing in Minab: 150+ children killed
  • Israeli casualties: 12 killed from Iranian retaliatory fire
  • Naval operations: 20+ Iranian naval vessels destroyed or struck total
  • Kurdish ground offensive launched in northwest Iran; Kurdish forces in northern Iraq on “standby” for cross-border operations
  • Hegseth on weapons: “500-pound, 1,000-pound and 2,000-pound GPS and laser-guided precision gravity bombs, of which we have a nearly unlimited stockpile”
  • Christian nationalist indoctrination: Reports of commanders telling troops Trump was “anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon”
  • Evacuations: Nearly 18,000 Americans evacuated from Middle East; 6,500 assisted by State Department

As the WSWS Editorial Board wrote today, the assault has assumed the character of “a deliberate war of annihilation targeting civilians,” with civilian casualties that are “intentional, not collateral.”

2. Senate votes down Iran war powers resolution 47-53

The US Senate voted down the bipartisan Kaine-Paul War Powers Resolution 47-53, falling three votes short of the simple majority needed. The resolution would have required Trump to withdraw forces from Iran within 30 days absent congressional authorization. Senator John Fetterman (D-PA) was the sole Democrat to oppose the resolution, while Senator Rand Paul (R-KY) was the only Republican to support it.

The vote effectively ratified the continuation of an unauthorized war. A House vote on a similar resolution is expected Thursday, but even if it passes, Trump holds veto power and an override would require a two-thirds majority in both chambers — an impossibility given the current alignment.

Key facts:

  • Kaine (D-VA): “If you don’t have the guts to vote yes or no on a war vote, how dare you send our sons and daughters into war”
  • Murphy (D-CT): “This is an open-ended operation that hasn’t even really started in earnest yet”
  • Young (R-IN): “The United States and our allies are now in conflict with a brutal, hostile, and dangerous regime. I believe that danger will only grow if we limit the President’s military options”
  • Public opinion: Only 21% of Americans favored the attack (University of Maryland poll); 43% disapprove, only 25% support (Al Jazeera polling)
  • The Democratic Party voted overwhelmingly for the $900 billion NDAA that funded the military buildup (77-20 in the Senate, with 26 Democratic votes), as the WSWS’s Barry Grey noted

3. NATO shoots down Iranian ballistic missile heading toward Turkey

NATO forces shot down an Iranian ballistic missile over the Eastern Mediterranean — the first time the alliance has intervened to defend a member state since the Iran war began. The missile passed through Iraqi and Syrian airspace before being intercepted; debris fell in Turkey’s southern Hatay province with no casualties. A senior Turkish official told AFP the missile was aimed at a base in Greek Cyprus but veered off course.

The incident underscores the war’s rapid geographic expansion. Conflict now spans Turkey, Sri Lanka, Iraq, Lebanon, Qatar, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman, and Azerbaijan. Iraq’s power grid shut down completely nationwide — every governorate lost electricity, affecting 43 million people. Nearly 60,000 people were displaced in Lebanon within 24 hours, including 18,000 children.

Key facts:

  • Hegseth on the intercept: “no sense it would trigger anything like Article 5”
  • Turkey warned all parties against further spreading the conflict
  • Iran attacked an Amazon data center in Bahrain with drones; launched missiles at US bases in Bahrain, Qatar, and facilities in Saudi Arabia and the UAE
  • Israel targeted a building where top Iranian clerics would meet to choose Khamenei’s successor
  • ~200 ships anchored off the Persian Gulf; Strait of Hormuz remains closed to commercial traffic

4. China’s NPC opens with lowest GDP target on record

China’s National People’s Congress opened on March 5 with Premier Li Qiang’s Government Work Report setting the GDP growth target at 4.5-5% — the lowest since the early 1990s, down from the “around 5%” target maintained for the past three years. The budget deficit target was set at approximately 4% of GDP, with inflation at 2% and urban unemployment at 5.5%.

The lowered target reflects an economy entering its fourth year of deflation, beset by a prolonged property slump, weak consumer confidence, and mounting local government debt stress. The Iran war introduces an acute new threat: 75% of China’s oil imports transit the Strait of Hormuz, which remains shut to commercial shipping.

Key facts:

  • CPPCC convened March 4; NPC opened March 5
  • GDP: Economy expanded 5% in 2025, but the new range signals Beijing expects a sharper slowdown
  • Deflation: Fourth consecutive year; consumer prices continue to fall
  • Structural headwinds: Property slump, local government debt, weak domestic consumption
  • Energy vulnerability: Hormuz closure directly threatens China’s energy supply; no short-term alternatives at this scale

5. QatarEnergy declares force majeure as global LNG crisis deepens

QatarEnergy declared force majeure on its liquefied natural gas contracts after two facilities were attacked, threatening approximately 20% of global LNG supply. Qatar accounts for 93% of all LNG traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, and unlike Saudi Arabia, it has no pipeline alternatives to bypass the chokepoint. Restart will require at least two weeks after the decision to restart, with another two weeks to reach full capacity — a minimum one-month disruption.

The impact is already severe across global energy markets. European gas prices surged 52%, with benchmark Dutch and British wholesale prices up approximately 50%. Asian LNG prices jumped 39%.

Key facts:

  • Most exposed countries: India (Qatar is its single largest LNG supplier), China, Japan, South Korea, Pakistan
  • European benchmark gas prices: +52%
  • Asian LNG prices: +39%
  • Minimum disruption timeline: One month from restart decision to full capacity
  • No workaround: Qatar has no pipeline alternative to bypass Hormuz, unlike some Saudi oil routes

H5N1 bird flu entrenched in global wildlife

H5N1 avian influenza is now circulating in more species, across more continents, than at any point in history, according to a Nature analysis. The virus has become entrenched in global wildlife populations, creating a persistent reservoir for potential mutations. The US has recorded 71 human cases and 2 deaths since 2024.

While no efficient person-to-person transmission has been documented, each human infection provides an opportunity for the virus to adapt. The combination of ongoing animal outbreaks, limited US public health surveillance capacity (amid broader CDC funding cuts), and the simultaneous measles crisis creates a compounding risk environment. The WHO continues to assess the overall public health risk as low but has warned that the situation “requires close monitoring.”

Wifredo Lam retrospective at MoMA

The Museum of Modern Art in New York has opened “When I Don’t Sleep, I Dream,” the first full-career US retrospective for Cuban artist Wifredo Lam (1902-1982), running through April 11. Lam — of African and Chinese descent — fought in the Spanish Civil War, collaborated with Picasso, Breton, and Miró, and developed a visual language that fused Surrealism, Cubism, and imagery drawn from Afro-Cuban spiritual traditions into some of the most powerful anti-colonial art of the twentieth century.

The WSWS’s Erik Schreiber argues in a detailed review that MoMA’s curators have framed the exhibition through a contemporary lens of racial identity and “decolonization” while “omitting entirely his relationship with Marxism and socialism.” This is a significant curatorial choice that distorts the artist’s self-understanding. Lam himself articulated his project in explicitly revolutionary terms: “I could act as a Trojan horse that would spew forth hallucinating figures with the power to surprise, to disturb the dreams of the exploiters.”

Lam’s masterwork “The Jungle” (1943), housed in MoMA’s permanent collection, remains one of the defining images of twentieth-century art — a dense thicket of hybrid human-plant forms that evokes the violence of colonialism, the sugar plantation, and the persistence of resistance. The retrospective provides an opportunity to assess his full trajectory, from his early academic training in Havana and Madrid through his wartime exile in Marseille (where he was among the intellectuals aided by Varian Fry) to his later work engaging with the Cuban Revolution.

The curatorial framing is part of a broader pattern in major museums of assimilating radical artists into identity-based categories that strip away their political commitments. When Lam is presented primarily as an artist of the African diaspora rather than as a revolutionary who happened to be of African descent, the challenge his work poses to bourgeois society is domesticated.

Cultural dimensions of the Iran war

The ongoing bombardment of Iran has struck sites of immense cultural significance. Iran claims 33 civilian sites have been hit, including Golestan Palace — a UNESCO World Heritage Site and former seat of the Qajar dynasty — and the Tehran Grand Bazaar, one of the oldest and largest bazaars in the Middle East, a commercial and architectural landmark dating to the Safavid era. The school bombing in Minab, which killed over 150 children, represents an attack not only on human life but on the basic social infrastructure of Iranian civilization.

The targeting of cultural sites echoes the destruction of Iraq’s cultural heritage during the 2003 invasion, when the National Museum of Iraq was looted and ancient sites at Babylon were damaged by military construction. The deliberate or reckless destruction of cultural heritage is recognized as a war crime under the 1954 Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property, to which the United States is a signatory.

  • Israeli targeting of clerical succession meeting site — the bombing of a building where top Iranian clerics would meet to choose Khamenei’s successor represents an attempt to destroy not only military but political and religious institutional continuity
  • Iraq’s cultural blackout — the nationwide power grid failure means museums, libraries, and archives across Iraq are without climate control, threatening collections already damaged by decades of conflict

  • Socialist Alternative (CWI) — focuses on British Labour complicity but orients toward pressure on Starmer’s government rather than building an independent anti-war movement

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin published four significant articles on the Iran war in the past 24 hours, all of which channel antiwar sentiment into the framework of Democratic Party politics and constitutional reform.

“Trump Is in Way Over His Head in Iran” frames the war as a product of Trump’s personal incompetence rather than the logic of American imperialism. The article presents the issue as a policy failure that could be corrected by more competent leadership — implicitly, Democratic leadership. This is the classic Jacobin method: criticize the Republican administration in terms that strengthen confidence in the Democratic alternative.

Jacobin Trump Is in Way Over His Head in Iran

“Trump’s War on Iran Makes a Mockery of American Democracy” highlights polling showing 79% of Americans oppose the war but frames the issue as a violation of democratic norms — the Constitution, the War Powers Act, congressional prerogatives — rather than as an expression of imperialism’s inherent drive toward war. The article never questions whether “American democracy” as currently constituted is capable of preventing wars that serve ruling-class interests.

Jacobin Trump’s War on Iran Makes a Mockery of American Democracy

“The Supreme Court May Legalize Donald Trump’s War on Iran” channels opposition through the legal framework, treating the Supreme Court as an arena where the war might be stopped. “Cut the Military Budget in Half” proposes halving the military budget — a reformist demand that presupposes the continued existence of the imperialist state apparatus, merely at a reduced scale.

Jacobin The Supreme Court May Legalize Donald Trump’s War on Iran · Jacobin Cut the Military Budget in Half

Overall orientation: Jacobin’s output reflects the DSA’s fundamental role: providing a left cover for the Democratic Party during a war launched by the Republican administration. By framing opposition through constitutional mechanisms, Democratic congressional action, and reformist budget proposals, Jacobin directs antiwar energy away from independent working-class mobilization and toward the very political structures that enabled the war. Not one article calls for working-class action, strike action, or political independence from both capitalist parties. The WSWS’s Barry Grey exposed this dynamic in today’s article on the DSA’s promotion of Democrats’ “fake opposition.”

Socialist Alternative (CWI)

Socialist Alternative’s international site published “No war on Iran: Starmer has blood on his hands!”, which focuses on British Labour’s complicity in the war through the deployment of F-35 fighters. The article correctly identifies Labour’s role but orients opposition toward pressuring the Starmer government — demanding Labour “break with imperialism” — rather than building an independent movement of the working class against war. This reflects the CWI’s longstanding method of adapting to social-democratic and Labour politics while nominally criticizing them.

Overall orientation: The CWI’s British section continues to define itself in relation to the Labour Party, treating it as an entity that could in principle be pressured into anti-war positions rather than as a party of imperialist war. The call to “break with imperialism” is directed at Labour leaders, not at the working class itself.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Iraq nationwide blackout: 43 million people in the dark

Iraq’s entire power grid shut down as a direct consequence of the Iran war, leaving all governorates without electricity and affecting 43 million people. This is a humanitarian catastrophe unfolding in real time with almost no dedicated international coverage. The WSWS should document the conditions facing Iraqi workers and the poor — hospitals without power, water treatment facilities offline, food spoilage — and connect the blackout to the broader destruction wrought by three decades of US-led wars and sanctions against Iraq.

Kurdish-Iranian armed groups have launched a ground offensive with CIA-supplied weapons, while Kurdish forces in northern Iraq are on “standby” for cross-border operations. This represents a new dimension of the war — potential ethnic partition of Iran — with catastrophic implications that the WSWS’s Jordan Shilton compared to Yugoslavia.
