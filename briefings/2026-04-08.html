1. US-Iran ceasefire announced after 39 days of war

Trump announced a two-week suspension of US-Israeli bombing of Iran on April 7, contingent on Iran’s “complete, immediate, and safe opening” of the Strait of Hormuz. Pakistani PM Shehbaz Sharif brokered the deal and invited delegations to Islamabad on April 10 for formal negotiations. Iranian FM Abbas Araghchi confirmed two-week safe passage through the Strait and submitted a 10-point peace plan demanding sanctions removal, US military withdrawal from the region, and compensation for war damages.

The ceasefire follows 39 days of devastating strikes. US and Israeli operations targeted Iran’s South Pars gas field — the world’s largest natural gas reserve — three Tehran airports, Khorramabad airport, and railway infrastructure. Israeli strikes expanded into previously untargeted Christian-majority suburbs east of Beirut, displacing 1.1 million in Lebanon. Netanyahu’s office confirmed the ceasefire “does not include Lebanon.”

As Joseph Kishore argued in the WSWS Perspective, Trump’s declaration that “a whole civilization will die tonight” exposed the “American government as an outlaw regime” whose moral authority has been “permanently shattered,” regardless of whether the threat was carried out.

Key facts: - 15 US servicemen wounded in an Iranian drone strike on Ali Al Salem airbase in Kuwait - 4+ killed by an Iranian missile hitting a residential building in Haifa, Israel - Iran’s synagogue in Tehran completely destroyed by US strikes - Pakistan, Egypt, Turkey, and Saudi Arabia form the mediating coalition - Former US Defense Secretary Leon Panetta warned there would be “no winners” if civilian infrastructure remained a target

Sources NBC News Live updates: Iran war, ceasefire · Al Jazeera Iran war: What is happening on day 39 of US-Israeli attacks · WSWS Trump follows genocidal threat against Iran with 2-week ‘ceasefire’

2. Global energy crisis sends oil to $141/barrel before ceasefire plunge

Brent crude reached $141 per barrel — the highest since the 2008 financial crisis — before plunging 8% to approximately $103/barrel on the ceasefire announcement. The extreme volatility underscored the fragility of global energy markets, which remain tethered to the Strait of Hormuz through which roughly one-fifth of the world’s oil passes daily.

Asian countries have begun implementing work-from-home policies and fuel rationing in response to supply disruptions. South Korea deployed five naval ships to Saudi Arabia’s Red Sea port of Yanbu to establish alternative oil supply routes. The war’s impact on European energy prices has been severe, with Italian pump prices nearly doubling and the Eurozone recording its fastest confidence collapse outside of the pandemic.

Key facts: - Brent crude up approximately 74.6% year-over-year from $63.72 in April 2025 - South Korea deploys naval escort for alternative oil supply routes - Asian fuel rationing and work-from-home mandates signal real-economy impact - Iran’s attacks knocked out 17% of Qatar’s LNG export capacity

3. Russia and China veto UN Security Council resolution on Strait of Hormuz

Russia and China vetoed a UN Security Council resolution on the Strait of Hormuz, calling it biased against Iran. The veto deepens the fracture of the post-Cold War international order, with major powers unable to agree on basic frameworks for managing the most consequential military conflict since the US invasion of Iraq.

The ceasefire was brokered not by the Security Council but by a coalition of non-Western powers — Pakistan, Egypt, Turkey, and Saudi Arabia — operating independently of traditional Western-dominated diplomatic channels. Turkey confirmed a third Turkish-owned ship successfully transited the Strait. Pope Leo condemned attacks on civilian infrastructure in Iran as violations of international law.

Key facts: - Expert Trita Parsi told Al Jazeera that Trump “has little credibility to lose” and may accept a new status quo including Iranian transit fees on the Strait - The non-Western mediating coalition signals a structural shift in diplomatic power - Turkey’s successful Strait transits suggest a pathway for partial reopening

4. Israel kills WHO worker, strikes school sheltering displaced Palestinians

Israeli forces fired on a WHO vehicle in Khan Younis on April 6, killing WHO driver Majdi Aslan, 54, and wounding a WHO doctor. Separately, an Israeli airstrike killed at least 10 Palestinians near a school housing displaced persons in central Gaza.

Since the October 2025 “ceasefire,” near-daily Israeli attacks have killed 716 Palestinians and wounded 1,968, according to Gaza’s Health Ministry. Hamas’s armed wing formally rejected disarmament demands, calling them an attempt to continue genocide before Phase 1 of the ceasefire is implemented. Doctors warned Israel is systematically targeting Lebanon’s healthcare system, as Israeli strikes linked to the broader Iran war have expanded into previously untargeted areas east of Beirut.

Key facts: - 716 killed and 1,968 wounded since the October 2025 nominal ceasefire - Hamas rejects disarmament demands as precondition for ceasefire implementation - Israel’s Lebanon operations have displaced 1.1 million people - Washington Post reported systematic targeting of Lebanon’s healthcare infrastructure

Sources Al Jazeera WHO employee killed, several injured in Israeli attack in Gaza · Al Jazeera Israeli air strike kills at least 10 Palestinians near Gaza school

5. Ukraine and Russia trade deadly drone strikes as Zelensky proposes Easter ceasefire

Both Russia and Ukraine launched drone strikes on April 7 killing several civilians on each side. Zelensky proposed an Easter ceasefire (April 12), contingent on Russia halting attacks on energy infrastructure.

In a significant strategic development, Ukraine has struck all three of Russia’s major western oil export ports in Leningrad Oblast, reportedly halting approximately 40% of Russia’s oil export capacity — a move with global energy implications on top of the Iran-related disruptions. Russia claimed full control of Ukraine’s Luhansk region on April 1, though ground reports are contested; Russia lost 12 square miles of territory during March.

Key facts: - Russian combat losses since February 2022 estimated at approximately 1.3 million personnel - Ukraine reported Russia lost 1,230 troops in a single day - Ukraine’s port strikes threaten a major portion of Russia’s oil export revenue - Easter ceasefire proposal conditioned on halt to energy infrastructure attacks

Sources Al Jazeera Civilians killed in Russia and Ukraine following drone attacks · Ukrinform Russia loses 1,230 troops in war against Ukraine over past day

6. US mass protests continue as May Day general strike mobilization builds

The April 5 “General Strike 2026” walkout demanded a halt to ICE operations and the removal of the Trump administration, following the March 28 “No Kings” protests that drew an estimated 8 million participants across approximately 3,300 locations in all 50 states — among the largest protest mobilizations in US history.

Organizers are now building toward “May Day Strong” (May 1), organized as a larger follow-up under the banner “No Work, No School, No Shopping.” The movement is increasingly linking anti-ICE demands to anti-war sentiment over the Iran conflict, reflecting a broadening of working-class opposition to the Trump administration’s domestic and foreign policies.

Key facts: - March 28 “No Kings” protests: ~8 million participants, ~3,300 locations, all 50 states - April 5 “General Strike 2026” demanded halt to ICE operations - May 1 “May Day Strong” being organized as the next escalation - Movement connects deportation raids, the Iran war, and economic grievances

7. Trump tariffs one year on: $1,500 per household, 89,000 manufacturing jobs lost

April 2026 marks the one-year anniversary of Trump’s “Liberation Day” tariff announcement. The tariffs represent the largest US tax increase as a percentage of GDP since 1993, averaging $1,500 per household. US manufacturing employment is down 89,000 since April 2025 — directly contradicting the stated rationale of protecting industrial jobs.

Food prices are up 2.8% overall, with fresh produce up 4% due to tariffs. Economists project the peak consumer impact between April and October 2026. The tariff policy has changed more than 50 times in one year, creating severe planning uncertainty for businesses across all sectors.

Key facts: - Largest US tax increase as % of GDP since 1993 - US trade deficit rose ~2% to $1.24 trillion in 2025 — worsening despite tariffs - Food prices up 2.8% overall, fresh produce up 4% - Tariff policy changed 50+ times in one year - Peak consumer impact projected April–October 2026

Vietnam’s To Lam consolidates power, elected president with 99% of vote

Vietnam’s National Assembly elected Communist Party General Secretary To Lam as State President for the 2026–2031 term, with approximately 99% of 495 delegates voting in favor. Former central bank governor Le Minh Hung was simultaneously elected Prime Minister. Analysts say the move breaks Vietnam’s traditional “four pillars” collective leadership model, concentrating power in a single figure — a structural parallel to Xi Jinping’s consolidation in China.

The consolidation comes amid Vietnam’s growing economic significance in the US-China trade realignment, as manufacturers shift production from China to Vietnam. The US State Department formally recognized the election.

Sudan: 33.7 million need aid as the world’s worst humanitarian crisis deepens

Sudan’s civil war, now nearly three years old, has produced the world’s largest displacement crisis. Some 33.7 million people require humanitarian aid, 24.6 million face acute hunger, and 2 million face famine or risk of famine. The UN launched a $1.6 billion refugee appeal covering 5.9 million people across seven neighboring countries.

Health infrastructure has collapsed: 37% of facilities are non-functional, with simultaneous outbreaks of cholera (all 18 states), dengue (14 states), malaria (16 states), and measles. UN experts issued an urgent April 2 call for protection of civilians in neighboring South Sudan amid escalating violence. The crisis receives a fraction of international media attention compared to the Iran war and Ukraine.

Italy’s Meloni makes emergency Gulf visit amid energy crisis

Italian PM Giorgia Meloni made an unannounced two-day visit to Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the UAE — the first by an EU, G20, and NATO leader since the Iran war began on February 28 — to secure energy supplies after pump prices surged from €1.56–1.71/liter to €2.05–2.50/liter. Italy was notified that its Gulf supplier had cancelled 10 LNG cargoes between April and mid-June due to the near-closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

Italy depends on Persian Gulf sources for roughly 15% of its oil needs. Rome has deployed aerial defenses to the region and signed new cooperation frameworks with all three Gulf states.

France refuses to call Trump’s Iran threat a war crime

French Foreign Minister Jean-Noël Barrot dismissed Trump’s threat to annihilate Iranian civilization as “excessive” but refused to answer directly when asked whether the statements constitute war crimes. Barrot told France 2: “One does not erase a civilisation,” and expressed hope Trump would not follow through, while acknowledging that targeting civilian infrastructure is “barred by the rules of war.”

France simultaneously allows US supply planes at its Istres airbase and has blamed Iran for “overwhelming responsibility” in the escalation through its nuclear programme, ballistic missiles, and support for allied groups. Gas prices in France have exceeded €2/liter amid Persian Gulf disruptions.

Canada Post restructuring threatens up to 30,000 jobs, end of home delivery

Canada Post is moving ahead with a government-mandated restructuring plan that would end home delivery for approximately four million addresses and eliminate up to 30,000 jobs by 2035, converting deliveries to community mailboxes. Workers vote on concessionary contracts from April 20 to May 30, with the full restructuring blueprint withheld from both workers and the public. The Carney Liberal government is backing the plan, framing it as financial necessity after $5 billion in losses since 2018.

European markets were cautious. The DAX dipped below 23,200, falling approximately 0.23% amid mixed global cues and lingering US-EU trade tensions.

BP Whiting refinery lockout enters fourth week as NIPSCO locks out 1,600 utility workers

Over 850 BP Whiting refinery workers — at the Midwest’s largest refinery, processing 440,000 barrels per day — remain locked out since March 19 after rejecting a contract that included $8–10/hour wage cuts, AI implementation without job protections, and elimination of over 100 union positions. BP hired strikebreakers at $127/hour. Separately, approximately 1,600 NIPSCO utility workers were locked out on April 2 after a 98% strike authorization vote, with parent company NiSource reporting $905 million in annual net income.

Indiana Governor Mike Braun joined the BP picket line, and Congressman Mrvan called on BP to end the lockout. The national USW reached agreements with most oil companies for a 15% wage increase over four years, but BP has refused to follow the industry pattern. The NIPSCO dispute centers on the company’s use of outside contractors, mandatory overtime, and safety-related work rules.

JBS Greeley meatpacking workers return after UFCW ends three-week strike

Approximately 3,800 workers at the JBS beef processing plant in Greeley, Colorado returned to work on April 5 after UFCW Local 7 ended the first major US meatpacking strike since 1985 — without a new agreement in place. Workers returned with only a promise from JBS to resume negotiations. Over 99% of workers had voted to strike, demanding higher wages and company-paid personal protective equipment, while JBS posted $415 million in Q4 2025 profits. The plant processes over 6% of US beef, and the strike was led substantially by immigrant workers.

Pentagon overhauls military education, cuts Ivy League ties

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth issued a memorandum discontinuing Senior Service College Fellowships at Harvard, Yale, Columbia, Princeton, Brown, MIT, Carnegie Mellon, and Johns Hopkins for 2026–2027, replacing them with Liberty University, Hillsdale College, George Mason, Pepperdine, and the University of Michigan, among others. In February, the department had already cancelled 93 fellowship positions across 22 institutions.

Hegseth called for officers educated in “founding principles,” “peace through strength,” and “lethality” — while himself being a Princeton and Harvard graduate. The University of Michigan confirmed it is establishing an “expedited review process” for military applicants.

Chinese researcher Danhao Wang dies at University of Michigan after FBI questioning

Danhao Wang, a Chinese postdoctoral researcher whose semiconductor materials work was published in Nature, died on March 19, 2026 — one day after alleged FBI interrogation — falling from an upper level of the George G. Brown Building on campus. The University of Michigan suppressed news of his death for over two weeks. Six days after Wang’s death, UM interim president Domenico Grasso appeared before the House Committee on Education and Workforce to address concerns about Chinese espionage in American higher education.

The Chinese government demands a full investigation, calling Wang’s death the result of “hostile questioning” by federal agents. The FBI has neither confirmed nor denied any investigation involving Wang. The case has generated significant diplomatic tension between Washington and Beijing.

US measles cases near 1,700 in one of worst years since 2000

As of April 2, 1,671 confirmed measles cases have been reported across 33 states and DC — a 6% week-over-week increase, with 94% of cases tied to active outbreaks. No deaths have been confirmed in 2026 (three occurred in 2025). The year is now tracking as one of the two worst measles years in the US since 2000, surpassing last year’s 30-year record. The resurgence reflects declining vaccination rates, compounded by the Trump administration’s ongoing attacks on public health infrastructure and the CDC’s capacity to respond.

H5N1 bird flu claims fatal human cases in India and Mexico

India reported a fatal H5N1 human case in April, and Mexico confirmed its first-ever human H5N1 death — a 3-year-old girl. The US total stands at 71 confirmed human cases and 2 deaths since March 2024. The back-to-back fatal cases in new countries signal continued geographic spread of human spillover events, even as the CDC reports no unusual influenza activity in US surveillance systems. The combination of declining public health surveillance capacity and expanding zoonotic risk remains a critical concern.

COVID-19 “Cicada” variant gains ground nationally

The highly mutated BA.3.2 “Cicada” variant has been detected in at least 25 states, appearing in 7% of national wastewater samples (week ending April 1). Hospitalizations are projected at approximately 1,900 for the week of April 11, slightly down from ~2,000 the prior week. COVID activity is declining or stable in 43 states. BA.3.2’s immune-escape profile warrants monitoring even as overall case burden remains low — it is tracking similarly to how prior variants emerged ahead of summer waves.

OpenAI proposes robot taxes and public AI wealth fund

OpenAI released a formal economic policy blueprint on April 6 proposing a “robot tax” requiring companies to pay into social systems for each worker replaced by AI, a Public Wealth Fund giving citizens a direct equity stake in AI infrastructure, and a pathway to a four-day workweek. This is OpenAI’s most explicit foray into labor and tax policy, representing a notable shift from purely technical safety discussions toward economic governance.

The proposals arrive as GPT-5.5 (“Spud”) approaches release — OpenAI completed pretraining on March 24 — and as GPT-5.4 mini rolls out to free-tier ChatGPT users. Sam Altman described the new model as “a few weeks” from launch as of late March.

AI legislation surge: 19 new laws, 600+ bills active

Since mid-March, the number of enacted AI laws in 2026 jumped from 6 to 25, with 27 more bills having passed both chambers across the country. Over 600 AI bills targeting private entities are active across state legislatures, focusing on synthetic content, companion chatbots, mental health AI, and transparency. The pace of state-level regulation has created a patchwork environment ahead of any federal framework — a new frontier model arriving in this regulatory landscape faces an unprecedented matrix of state-level requirements.

Pina Bausch’s Kontakthof returns at Sadler’s Wells with original 1978 cast

One of the most significant dance events in years is running at London’s Sadler’s Wells Theatre (April 7–11): Kontakthof — Echoes of ’78, a new encounter with Pina Bausch’s seminal 1978 work conceived and directed by Meryl Tankard, who performed in the original production. Nine of the original dancers return to their roles, now decades older, performing alongside projections of archival footage of their younger selves and company members no longer living.

The production, part of the biennial Elixir Festival exploring what it means to be an older dancer, is a profound meditation on time, mortality, and the body as a repository of lived experience. In a cultural landscape that discards both artists and workers the moment their productive capacity wanes, the deliberate centering of aging bodies on stage carries an implicit social critique that extends far beyond the dance world.

V&A East Museum to open April 18 in east London

The V&A East Museum opens April 18 as the anchor of the East Bank cultural complex in the Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park, Stratford. Its inaugural exhibition, The Music Is Black: A British Story, explores how Black British music shaped national culture from 1900 to the present — a significant curatorial statement centering working-class and immigrant contributions to British life. The museum features two free permanent galleries with over 500 objects spanning global art, design, and performance. Eight new artworks by leading contemporary artists including Tania Bruguera and Carrie Mae Weems will be unveiled.

The museum’s location in Stratford — once one of London’s poorest boroughs, now transformed by Olympic-era development that displaced many of the communities whose cultural labour it now exhibits — embodies the contradictions of cultural investment under capitalism.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — frames the Iran war as a policy miscalculation rather than structural imperialism; channels discontent into Democratic electoral politics through voting-rights coverage
  • Left Voice — most independent on the DSA/AOC question, but anti-imperialism remains geopolitical commentary without a working-class program
  • Liberation News / PSL — openly campist: opposes US imperialism while offering no critique of the Iranian bourgeois state; channels opposition into pressure-group activism
  • Socialist Alternative — orients the No Kings movement toward the 2026 midterm electoral cycle; general strike call framed as leverage within bourgeois politics
  • SWP (UK) — covers Iran ceasefire as evidence of US decline without advancing revolutionary defeatism; no critique of Labour’s war complicity
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — strongest objective analysis of US imperial crisis, but consistently avoids the political conclusions their own analysis demands

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s lead Iran article, “Is there a way out from Trump’s Iran ultimatum?”, frames the crisis as a diplomatic miscalculation, arguing “Iran’s position is stronger than the president is willing to admit.” The piece offers bourgeois-diplomatic analysis with no anti-imperialist class content — the implicit solution is Iran outmaneuvering US pressure through statecraft, not international working-class solidarity.

“Trump’s goal is to suppress votes, not prevent election fraud” defends bourgeois voting rights against the SAVE Act, staying entirely within the framework of capitalist electoral democracy with no independent working-class political program. “Workers at cocktail bar Attaboy are unionizing” covers an independent hospitality union drive without visible class-collaborationist framing.

Overall orientation: imperialism treated as policy mistake rather than structural feature of capitalism, consistent with Jacobin’s role channeling socialist sentiment back into Democratic Party electoral politics.

Left Voice

Left Voice demonstrates the greatest independence from the Democratic Party orbit among US pseudo-left outlets. “Promises, promises: AOC, Israel, and the DSA” critically examines AOC’s belated reversal on military aid to Israel, correctly identifying the pressure-group dynamic at work — a sharper critique than anything Jacobin has published.

“Why young people joined the latest No Kings protests” reports positively on youth participation but avoids the critical question: what independent political organization does this movement require? “The US and Israel are winning battles but losing the war” offers geopolitical analysis of US decline without advancing a program for working-class internationalism.

Left Voice (FT/LIT) correctly criticizes the DSA/AOC bloc but does not advance revolutionary defeatism on the Iran war; their anti-imperialism is expressed as geopolitical commentary rather than a political program for the working class.

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL statement “Why Trump failed in his criminal war against Iran” frames the ceasefire as a defeat for Trump’s “criminal” imperialism and calls for sustained grassroots pressure. This is classic PSL campism: opposition to US imperialism is correct, but their “anti-imperialism” means political alignment with the Iranian bourgeois state, not solidarity with the Iranian working class as a distinct social force. The “keep up the pressure” framing channels opposition into protest activism rather than independent political organization.

“Attacks on birthright citizenship” defends democratic rights within a bourgeois framework, offering no class analysis of why the ruling class attacks these rights now, nor any critique of the Democratic Party’s decades of failure to defend them.

The PSL opposes US imperialism while offering no critique of Russian or Chinese imperialism, and politically defends bourgeois nationalist regimes. Their orientation is campist, not internationalist.

Socialist Alternative

SA’s most revealing article is “The midterms loom over Trump”, which argues Republicans face a potential electoral wipeout and highlights May 1 as an inflection point. The midterms framing subordinates working-class mobilization to the logic of the bourgeois electoral cycle — the mass movement is valued for its potential to produce Democratic victories, not for its independent class content.

“Eight million join protests — what’s next for ‘No Kings’?” reports positively on the protests with “transitional demands” but without a revolutionary party program or explicit break from Democratic politics. “Walk out for trans rights” calls for student walkouts without class analysis of the ruling class’s promotion of social reaction.

SA’s general strike call around May 1 is positive, but placing it in the context of “the midterms loom” reveals the movement being configured as electoral leverage rather than independent class mobilization.

SWP (UK)

The SWP’s “Trump forced to agree to ceasefire” frames the pause as a forced climbdown and evidence of US imperial decline. “Iran issues own ceasefire proposal” covers Iran’s counter-proposal. Both articles exhibit the same campist tendency as the PSL — opposition to US imperialism without class solidarity with the Iranian working class as a force distinct from the Islamic Republic.

“UCU workers fighting the erosion of universities” covers strikes against mass job cuts at London Metropolitan and Scottish universities — the SWP’s strongest terrain, though characteristically lacking any critique of the trade union bureaucracy’s role in restraining the strike wave. No critique of the Labour Party’s support for Israel appears in the day’s output. The SWP (state-capitalist, Tony Cliff tradition) historically adapted to left-Labourism and popular-front politics.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The IMT/RCP produces the strongest objective analysis. “Operation Epstein Fury: the mask falls from the American empire” argues Trump’s escalation has exposed the desperation of American imperialism. “Trump faces impossible dilemma as Iran gets the upper hand” offers a fifth-week analysis arguing there are “no good options” for Washington.

Both pieces demonstrate the IMT’s characteristic strength in historical materialist analysis of the objective crisis and its characteristic weakness: consistent avoidance of the subjective question of what political organization the working class needs. The RCP’s Iran coverage contains no call for political independence of the Iranian working class from the Islamic Republic, consistent with their broader avoidance of revolutionary defeatism. The IMT (Ted Grant/Alan Woods tradition) claims the Trotskyist tradition but is regarded by the ICFI as a centrist current that adapted to Stalinism, social democracy, and bourgeois nationalism.

Cross-cutting observations: The Iran war dominates all six tendencies. None advance revolutionary defeatism — opposition to both US imperialism and the Iranian bourgeois state from the standpoint of working-class internationalism. None address the danger of the No Kings/general strike movement being absorbed into the Democratic Party electoral cycle or controlled by NGO/union bureaucracies. SA’s midterms framing and Jacobin’s voting-rights coverage both orient working-class opposition toward the 2026 elections. No tendency calls for an independent workers’ party.
