1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 27 as ceasefire framework collapses

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran entered its 27th day on March 26 with no prospect of a ceasefire. US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff presented Iran a 15-point peace framework via Pakistani intermediaries, but Tehran rejected it as “one-sided,” with Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi stating: “We have no intention of negotiating.” Iran demands it not be required to “relinquish its ability to defend itself.”

Trump extended his pause on strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure to April 6 — his second extension — posting on Truth Social that “talks are ongoing and…they are going very well,” a characterization contradicted by every Iranian official statement. The Pentagon is meanwhile considering deploying up to 10,000 additional troops, including 3,000 paratroopers from the 82nd Airborne, and Trump is weighing the seizure of Kharg Island, Iran’s main oil processing facility.

Key facts: - More than 1,750 people killed in Iran since strikes began February 28, including 217 children and at least 1,167 Iranian military personnel (HRANA) - US has destroyed an estimated two-thirds of Iran’s missile and drone production facilities - Approximately 50,000 US troops now committed to the conflict - IAEA Director General Grossi warned Israeli strikes near Bushehr nuclear plant risk “a major radiological accident affecting a large area in Iran and beyond”

5. Sudan war escalation kills 28 in drone strikes as hospital attack death toll reaches 64

RSF drone strikes on March 26 killed at least 28 civilians, including an infant — one strike hit a crowded market, another a highway. This follows the March 21 attack on al-Daein Teaching Hospital in East Darfur that killed 64 people, including 13 children, 2 nurses, and 1 doctor, and wounded 89 more. Sudan’s civil war — now entering its second full year — has become the world’s largest humanitarian crisis, yet receives a fraction of Western media attention compared to conflicts in wealthier regions. The disparity in coverage is itself politically significant: the lives of Sudanese civilians are treated as less newsworthy than those of populations in countries of strategic interest to the imperialist powers. In just the first two months of 2026, ACLED recorded 198 drone/airstrike incidents, at least 52 causing civilian casualties. 6. Supreme Court tariff ruling triggers $165 billion refund crisis

The fallout from the Supreme Court’s February 20 ruling striking down Trump’s IEEPA-based tariffs continues to deepen. In Learning Resources Inc. v. Trump, the Court ruled 6–3 that “IEEPA contains no reference to tariffs or duties,” invalidating the legal basis for Trump’s sweeping tariff regime. Customs and Border Protection has admitted the agency “is not able to comply” with refund orders, overwhelmed by more than 1,000 lawsuits seeking a combined $165 billion in refunds.

Trump responded to the ruling by raising tariffs to 15% under Section 122 of the 1974 Trade Act — but those expire in 150 days without Congressional approval, and 24 states have sued to block them. Trump publicly attacked Justices Barrett and Gorsuch — his own Supreme Court appointees — saying he was “ashamed of certain members of the court.” Trade adviser Peter Navarro spun the loss as “the best possible outcome,” citing remaining tariff tools under Section 301. The tariff crisis exposes the contradictions within the ruling class: the Supreme Court, including Trump’s own appointees, has rejected the executive’s attempt to bypass Congress on trade policy, while the business community faces a $165 billion liability that could trigger cascading financial disruptions. The combination of the tariff legal battle with the Iran war’s economic fallout creates a level of policy uncertainty that is already redirecting international capital flows away from US-exposed assets.

7. UN General Assembly adopts slavery reparations resolution 123–3

The UN General Assembly adopted resolution A/80/L.48 by a vote of 123–3, with 52 abstentions, on the International Day of Remembrance of the Victims of Slavery. The resolution declares the transatlantic slave trade “the gravest crime against humanity” and calls for reparations, formal apologies, restitution, and return of cultural artifacts. Only the United States, Israel, and Argentina voted against.

Ghana’s President John Dramani Mahama, the African Union Champion for Reparations, sponsored the resolution, which honors the estimated 13 million enslaved Africans trafficked over several centuries. US Deputy Ambassador Dan Negrea stated the US “does not recognize a legal right to reparations for historical wrongs that were not illegal under international law at the time they occurred.” All 27 EU members and the UK abstained, citing concerns over “retroactive application of international rules.” The resolution is non-binding but represents a significant assertion of moral authority by the Global South. The vote exposed the deep fissure between the former colonial powers — which uniformly refused to acknowledge legal obligations arising from the slave trade — and the nations of Africa, the Caribbean, and Latin America that continue to bear the economic and social consequences of centuries of forced labor. The US representative’s argument that historical wrongs “were not illegal under international law at the time” amounts to a retroactive defense of slavery itself — a position that aligns the Biden-era rhetoric of racial justice with the Trump administration’s explicit rejection of reparatory justice.

The US measles outbreak continued its alarming trajectory, with 1,487 confirmed cases reported across 32 states and jurisdictions as of March 19. Oral GLP-1 pill achieves 11.2% weight loss in phase 3 trial

Eli Lilly’s once-daily oral pill orforglipron achieved −11.2% mean body weight change versus −2.1% for placebo in the 72-week ATTAIN-1 Phase 3 trial of 3,127 adults with obesity (p<0.001). At the highest dose (36 mg), 59.6% of participants lost ≥10% of body weight, and up to 91% of participants with prediabetes achieved near-normal blood sugar levels.

An affordable oral GLP-1 option — self-pay starting at $149/month, with no food or water timing restrictions — could dramatically expand access beyond injectable semaglutide and tirzepatide, which cost upwards of $1,000/month without insurance. FDA action is expected in Q2 2026. The development has significant class dimensions: the current GLP-1 market has created a two-tier system where effective weight-loss treatment is available almost exclusively to those with comprehensive insurance or significant disposable income.

The performing arts in the US are being systematically starved of public support and transformed into luxury goods accessible only to the wealthy.

World Theatre Day 2026

March 27 marks World Theatre Day, the 64th observance established by the International Theatre Institute in 1961. Events are being held across more than 90 countries, with central celebrations in Luxembourg City.
