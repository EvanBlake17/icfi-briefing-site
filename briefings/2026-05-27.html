1. Israel shatters Lebanon ceasefire as ground forces push past the Litani River

Israel carried out the sharpest single-day escalation in Lebanon since fighting resumed in March, striking more than 100 Hezbollah-linked sites and killing at least 31 people — including children — over May 26–27. Twelve of the dead were in the eastern village of Mashghara. Israeli ground forces crossed the Litani River “Yellow Line,” violating the US-brokered ceasefire in place since mid-April. “The IDF are operating with large forces on the ground and seizing strategic areas,” Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said.

The entire city of Nabatiyeh, southern Lebanon’s second-largest, was ordered to evacuate, along with 21 additional towns. Strikes hit areas near Lebanon’s largest dam and the UNESCO-protected Beaufort Castle. As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich declared that “for every explosive drone, ten buildings in Beirut must fall” — a statement of open collective-punishment policy.

Key facts: - At least 3,213 Lebanese killed and 9,737 wounded since bombardment resumed March 2, per the Lebanese health ministry; over 1 million displaced - A fourth round of US-mediated talks is scheduled for June 2–3 in Washington - The WSWS situates the Lebanon assault within the broader US-Israeli campaign against Iran that opened in February 2026, arguing negotiations function as cover for deeper offensives rather than de-escalation

Sources Euronews Israel expands ground operations in southern Lebanon · Times of Israel Lebanon escalation liveblog · WSWS Israel escalates rampage in Lebanon

2. Trump convenes Camp David Cabinet as US-Iran war talks reach critical juncture

President Trump held a rare full-Cabinet meeting at Camp David on May 27, described by aides as a “precarious moment” in negotiations aimed at ending the US-Iran war. Trump claimed a memorandum of understanding reopening the Strait of Hormuz was “largely negotiated,” but Secretary of State Marco Rubio said Tuesday that “several more days” of talks remain.

The diplomacy is unfolding amid continued fighting. Iran shot down a US MQ-9 Reaper drone and fired toward a US fighter jet in Iranian airspace on May 26; US forces struck Iranian vessels laying mines and missile sites on May 25. An Iranian delegation led by parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf traveled to Qatar for proximity talks.

Key facts: - Unresolved issues include Iran’s highly enriched uranium stockpiles, the status of its nuclear program, and the scope of Israeli operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon - A 60-day ceasefire-extension framework that would reopen Hormuz is under negotiation but unsigned; Iran’s IRGC has threatened retaliation - Sections of Trump’s own base fear a deal will “embolden Iran,” while hard-right figures in Netanyahu’s coalition press against any agreement

Sources NPR Trump convenes Cabinet meeting on Iran · Washington Post Trump Cabinet meets as Iran nuclear talks falter · CNBC US-Iran war talks

3. Israel kills newly appointed Hamas military chief in Gaza City strike

Israel killed Mohammed Odeh, who had recently assumed command of Hamas’s military wing after Izz al-Din al-Haddad was killed earlier in May, in a strike on Gaza City on May 27. Netanyahu said Odeh was “responsible for the murder, abduction, and wounding of many Israeli citizens and IDF soldiers” and was among the “architects” of the October 7, 2023 attacks. The IDF simultaneously struck Hamas command sites and an apartment used by a Hamas operative.

Key facts: - At least seven Palestinians were killed in broader May 27 strikes, including five in a refugee camp; a strike on the Rimal neighborhood killed three and wounded more than 20 - The total Gaza death toll since October 7, 2023 now exceeds 72,000 - Odeh is the second Hamas military commander killed within a single month, underscoring the continued Israeli decapitation campaign even as ceasefire talks proceed on other fronts

Sources Euronews New head of Hamas military wing killed in Gaza strike · The National Israel targets new Hamas military chief in Gaza strike

4. Gas explosion kills 82 miners in China’s worst mine disaster since 2009

A gas explosion at the privately owned Liushenyu Coal Mine in Shanxi province killed 82 workers and hospitalized more than 120 — China’s worst mining disaster since 2009. As Peter Symonds documented on the WSWS, the safety failures were systemic and known in advance.

Key facts: - The mine was flagged by China’s National Mine Safety Administration in 2024 for elevated gas levels - Inaccurate blueprints understated worker counts, mandatory location trackers were absent, and workers operated unauthorized hidden tunnels to evade taxes; a carbon monoxide alarm was ignored before the blast - Symonds places the disaster in a global pattern — the Congo’s Robaya mines (600 deaths) and the 2010 Upper Big Branch explosion (29 dead) — arguing the profit motive structurally produces workplace death under capitalism, and calls for independent worker safety committees

The disaster, which received international wire coverage, exposes the lethal conditions in China’s energy sector despite the regime’s claims of improved safety oversight.

Sources WSWS Mine disaster in China: Another tragedy in the global industrial slaughterhouse · Reuters China coal mine explosion

5. UK Labour government slides toward leadership crisis as Starmer’s authority collapses

Keir Starmer’s Labour government is in open crisis following catastrophic May local-election losses. At least 83 of Labour’s 403 MPs are now calling for Starmer to resign, and three junior ministers have already quit the government. Health Secretary Wes Streeting resigned May 14, saying he had “lost confidence” in Starmer’s leadership.

Key facts: - Labour lost 1,496 councillors and control of 38 councils; Reform UK won 27% of the vote against Labour’s 15%, with the Greens at roughly 14% - Eurasia Group puts the probability that MPs force a leadership election by September at 35%, with a 20% chance of an immediate contest - Starmer: “I am not running away from the challenge, nor am I going to change course because the political weather has turned”; a Green Party leader declared “two-party politics is not just dying, it is dead and it is buried”

The WSWS frames the collapse not as a story of Starmer’s personal failings but as the bankruptcy of Labourism — a party that governed in the interests of capital and imposed austerity, driving workers toward both the far right and the Greens in search of an alternative.

Sources CNBC UK Starmer faces MP revolt as ministers quit · NPR Starmer’s party lost big in UK local elections — what comes next · Wikipedia 2026 United Kingdom government crisis

6. Record-shattering May heatwave grips Europe as UK posts hottest May day ever

London hit 35°C (95°F) on May 27, breaking the previous day’s record set at Kew Gardens to give the UK its hottest May day ever recorded — exceeding the prior record by 2°C. The normal late-May high for London is roughly 20°C, meaning temperatures ran about 15°C above the seasonal average.

Key facts: - A wildfire broke out near Arthur’s Seat in Edinburgh; hundreds of properties in southeast England lost water as demand overwhelmed supply - Deaths have been reported across Europe, with a precise toll not yet confirmed; government heat warnings are in effect across multiple nations - The Met Office estimates the current climate makes breaking such records roughly three times more likely than in a pre-industrial climate

The WSWS treats such events as products of capitalist production, noting that governments respond with heat warnings and emergency appeals rather than any confrontation with the fossil fuel industry.

Sources CNN Europe’s deadly, early heatwave is smashing records · NPR Europe heat wave deaths · Met Office Provisional spring daily temperature record

7. Famine confirmed in two Sudanese cities as 20 million face acute hunger

The UN has formally confirmed famine in Al Fasher and Kadugli, with 20 additional areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan at risk. The catastrophe — the world’s worst current humanitarian emergency — remains starkly underreported in Western media relative to the wars in Ukraine, Gaza, and Lebanon.

Key facts: - 135,000 people are in catastrophic conditions; nearly 20 million face acute hunger, and more than 800,000 children are at risk of severe malnutrition - 34 million people (65% of Sudan’s population) need urgent humanitarian assistance; 9 million are internally displaced; 17 million lack safe drinking water and 40% of health facilities are non-functional - Sudan’s 2026 humanitarian response plan was only 20% funded as of April; the war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces has raged since April 2023, each faction backed by competing regional powers

Sources UN News Sudan hunger crisis deepens as millions face acute food shortages · NPR Sudan, Darfur war, genocide and famine

US Marines stage rapid-response drill at embassy in Caracas

Two US Marine Corps Osprey aircraft from Marine Medium Tiltrotor Squadron 263, deployed aboard the USS Iwo Jima, flew over Caracas and landed in the parking lot of the recently reopened US Embassy on May 23, in what the Pentagon described as a “rapid response exercise.” General Francis Donovan, head of US Southern Command, observed the drill and made his second official visit to Caracas this year, meeting senior Venezuelan officials and embassy staff. Venezuela’s foreign minister Yván Gil said the exercise was to prepare “in the event of medical emergencies or catastrophic emergencies.”

As Andrea Lobo argued on the WSWS, the maneuver is a demonstration of US military control over Venezuela following the seizure of President Maduro on January 3, with the exercise approved by Acting President Delcy Rodríguez even as protesters opposed it.

Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton defeated three-term incumbent Senator John Cornyn in the Republican Senate runoff, winning 63.4% of nearly 903,000 votes; the AP called the race around 8 p.m. CST on May 26. A last-minute Trump endorsement is credited with sealing Paxton’s margin.

Worker killed, nine missing in chemical tank rupture at Washington paper mill

A chemical tank ruptured at the Nippon Dynawave Packaging pulp and paper mill in Longview, Washington on May 26, killing at least one worker — identified as Gilbert Bernal — and leaving nine missing. Crews responded at 7:18 a.m. after a tank of “white liquor,” a highly corrosive sodium-hydroxide-and-sulfide solution, gave way; eight employees and one firefighter were injured. Recovery efforts were suspended Tuesday evening because of safety hazards.

  • The tank, initially reported to hold 80,000 gallons, in fact contained approximately 900,000 gallons; roughly 90,000 gallons spilled toward stormwater systems connected to the Columbia River
  • The facility employs about 1,000 people and produces packaging, paper, cups and cartons
  • As Jacob Crosse documented on the WSWS, the mill drew four state OSHA citations (2019–2025) and five EPA actions, yet total federal fines collected amounted to just $10,000

Garden Grove chemical-tank crisis ends as Orange County DA opens criminal probe

Orange County authorities lifted the last evacuation orders for some 16,000 residents on May 26, ending a week-long crisis at GKN Aerospace’s Garden Grove facility, where a 34,000-gallon tank of methyl methacrylate overheated after a refrigeration-valve failure on May 21. At its peak the mandatory evacuation covered more than 50,000 people across a nine-square-mile area spanning six cities. A crack in the tank ultimately allowed pressure to release safely, averting a vapor explosion.

  • The Orange County district attorney has launched a criminal investigation, and residents have filed a class-action lawsuit against GKN
  • As Marc Wells reported on the WSWS, GKN is a “super Tier 1” defense supplier producing F-35 fuselage sections and MQ-9 drone components; FEMA refused to cover evacuees’ hotel costs, forcing some immigrant families to sleep in cars
  • The facility had accumulated multiple Cal/OSHA violations since 2018 and a $1 million air-quality settlement in 2021

Science, technology, and public health

  • US measles: 1,952 confirmed cases across 40 jurisdictions in 2026; US “highly likely” to lose elimination status this fall
  • H5N1: WHO logs a fatal pediatric H5N1 case in Bangladesh; CDC sees no unusual US human activity but virus persists in dairy cattle
  • COVID-19: National activity “low,” wastewater at lowest since spring 2021, but Georgia, Florida, and Louisiana “growing”
  • River science: Science Advances finds ~80% of 21,000+ river systems are losing dissolved oxygen, threatening food for 200 million
  • CERN: Strongest hints yet of physics beyond the Standard Model reported at the LHC
  • AI policy: EU finalizes “AI Omnibus”; Trump administration reverses course toward federal AI oversight; Pope Leo XIV issues encyclical on AI

The leading public-health story is measles. As of May 21, the CDC has confirmed 1,952 cases across 40 jurisdictions in 2026, with 93% outbreak-associated; Utah alone accounts for 400-plus cases, and a 600-case South Carolina outbreak was only recently declared over. Analysts now consider the US “highly likely” to lose the measles elimination status it has held since 2000, with summer travel expected to accelerate spread. Internationally, the catastrophe is far graver: as Wimal Perera reported on the WSWS, Bangladesh’s outbreak has killed 555 children, roughly 80% of them under five, with 66,023 suspected cases — a disaster Perera attributes to skipped national vaccination campaigns and IMF-driven austerity, not natural causes.

The most significant peer-reviewed study is a Science Advances analysis of more than 21,000 river systems from 1985–2023, which found nearly 80% have experienced declining dissolved oxygen at an average of −0.045 mg/L per decade, projected to accelerate sharply by century’s end. Fish require 6–8 mg/L for health and die below 2 mg/L; some 200 million people depend on river fisheries for protein. As Philip Guelpa noted on the WSWS, global warming accounts for 62.7% of the deoxygenation and heatwaves for 22.7%.

Roundup: - H5N1: WHO tracked 13 avian/swine flu cases in humans in Q1 2026, including a newly reported fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh; CDC reports no unusual US influenza activity, though the virus remains widespread in dairy cattle and wild birds - COVID-19: Activity is “low” nationally with wastewater at its lowest since spring 2021, but cases are “growing” in Georgia, Florida, and Louisiana; dominant variant is XFG.1.1 (32%); ~1,500 weekly hospital admissions forecast - CERN: Researchers at the Large Hadron Collider report the strongest hints yet of physics beyond the Standard Model — potentially pointing to new particles or forces if confirmed - EU AI Act: On May 7 legislators finalized the “AI Omnibus,” lightening compliance burdens on SMEs, expanding regulatory sandboxes, and banning AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery - US AI policy: The Trump administration is now weighing federal oversight of advanced AI models, driven by national-security concerns over Anthropic’s cyber-capable “Mythos” model — a reversal of its prior deregulatory stance - Vatican: Pope Leo XIV’s first encyclical, Magnifica Humanitas, frames AI regulation as a moral imperative; OpenAI’s Sam Altman, speaking in Sydney, walked back his prior predictions of sweeping near-term white-collar job losses

Arts and culture

The most significant cultural intervention reviewed by the WSWS over the past day is Enough is Enough (Trop c’est trop), Elisé Sawasawa’s documentary on the catastrophe in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, reviewed by David Walsh and Joanne Laurier. The film documents a country ranked 171st of 193 on the Human Development Index, where roughly 120 armed groups operate in the east and the January 2025 fall of Goma to Rwandan-backed M23 rebels displaced millions — all while UN peacekeeping spent $1.5 billion annually over two decades to no protective effect. Walsh and Laurier use the documentary to insist that the crisis cannot be understood apart from imperialism’s historical role, from King Leopold II’s genocide through the present scramble for the DRC’s cobalt (the world’s largest supply), coltan (70% of global supply), and diamonds. The political conclusion — that the catastrophe demands international working-class resistance rather than NGO humanitarian management — exemplifies the WSWS approach of situating art in its full social and historical context rather than treating it as content.

The day’s cultural discourse was also marked by the Vatican’s formal entry into the debate over technology and human life. Pope Leo XIV’s inaugural encyclical, Magnifica Humanitas, frames artificial intelligence as a moral and civilizational question and calls for international governance structures — a significant ideological intervention by the Catholic hierarchy that simultaneously expresses real anxieties about technology’s social effects and seeks to position the Church as moral arbiter over questions that are fundamentally ones of class and ownership.

Roundup: - Film: Independent cinema continues to draw critical attention for offering an alternative to formulaic studio production; the broader trend reflects audiences and critics searching for work with genuine human content amid the dominance of franchise and algorithm-driven entertainment - Cultural politics: The persistent under-representation of catastrophes like the DRC, Sudan, and Bangladesh in mainstream cultural production — even as Ukraine and Gaza receive sustained treatment — reflects the same media hierarchy that ranks some lives as newsworthy and others as invisible, a question serious artists and critics increasingly confront - Historical memory: Renewed cultural attention to militarism and historical revisionism — including debate over the rehabilitation of war and nationalism in several countries — underscores the urgency of art that defends historical truth against official mythmaking

The WSWS framework remains that genuine artistic achievement is recognized regardless of the artist’s politics, while nationalist, militarist, and identitarian tendencies in cultural production are identified for what they are — adaptations to the demands of a ruling class preparing for war and austerity.

Australia: NT laws to permit police interrogation of children without a guardian — Antony Walsh

The Northern Territory’s Country Liberal Party government has introduced amendments to the Youth Justice Act allowing police to detain children in watch-houses for up to 48 hours — double the previous limit — and to interview them without a parent or guardian present where officers declare a “serious and urgent” public-safety concern. Walsh notes the “knowledge” threshold is broad enough to ensnare children who merely viewed social-media content. The NT prison ombudsman has called watch-houses “unreasonable and oppressive”; the NT’s incarceration rate would rank second globally behind El Salvador if it were a separate jurisdiction, and roughly 89% of prisoners are Aboriginal despite Aboriginal people comprising 26% of the population. Walsh frames the legislation as a bipartisan escalation of punitive policy against Aboriginal children and the working class.

Sources WSWS NT laws to permit police interrogation of children without legal guardian

Cargill locks out Colorado beef plant as meatpacking struggles intensify — Dan Conway

Cargill has locked out its Fort Morgan, Colorado beef plant after roughly 1,700 workers — 70–85% of them immigrants from Somalia, Mexico, Guatemala, and Myanmar — rejected the company’s contract by 85%. The offer included only a $0.70/hour raise in year one on a $23.50 base, while Cargill posted $3.57 billion in annual profits and paid $1.46 billion in dividends to the billionaire Cargill-MacMillan family. Conway situates the lockout alongside the JBS Greeley strike — the largest US meatpacking strike in 60 years — arguing that the union leadership’s refusal to coordinate solidarity across plants lets management isolate and exhaust workers facility by facility. He calls for independent rank-and-file organizing to unite meatpackers industry-wide.

Sources WSWS Cargill locks out Colorado beef plant

Nexteer workers: Vote NO! Prepare to walk out! — Nexteer Workers Rank-and-File Committee

The rank-and-file committee at Nexteer’s Saginaw, Michigan plant urges rejection of a third tentative agreement, noting the 1,300 workers already rejected two prior UAW-backed contracts (by 96.2% and 73%) and cast an 86% strike-authorization vote on May 21–22. The new deal still tops production workers out at $27/hour in 2030 — the same nominal wage as 2005, or under 60% in inflation-adjusted terms — with extended 48-month progression for new hires and weaker termination protections. The committee charges UAW president Shawn Fain with suppressing strike action to avoid disrupting production, and frames a Nexteer walkout as a potential catalyst across the auto-parts sector (American Axle, Dana, Magna Seating).

Sources WSWS Nexteer workers: Vote NO! Prepare to walk out!

Staten Island shipyard fire kills worker, injures dozens of firefighters — Robert Milkowski

A fire and secondary explosion at the May Ship Repair shipyard in Mariners Harbor, Staten Island on May 22 killed Xiaoyuan Li, 57, a Chinese immigrant subcontractor who had worked 4 a.m. shifts there for four years; more than two dozen FDNY and EMS personnel were injured when a tank inside a dry-dock structure exploded during rescue operations. Milkowski argues the subcontracting structure “diffuses responsibility and legal accountability for safety compliance,” that immigrant workers are assigned the most hazardous work while being scapegoated through raids and deportation threats, and that OSHA’s staffing means any given workplace is inspected once every 266 years.

Sources WSWS Worker killed, dozens of firefighters injured in Staten Island shipyard fire

Report reveals impact of Australian school crisis on principals — Erika Zimmer

The 15th annual Australian Catholic University occupational-health survey found that 45% of school principals showed warning signs for self-harm or serious occupational problems, over half considered quitting, and physical violence against principals nearly doubled between 2011 and 2025 (27.3% to 47.8%). The backdrop is a widening per-student funding gap — government schools receive $20,916 versus $30,384 in private schools — with the proposed budget raising private-school funding faster than public. Zimmer argues this deliberately entrenches a two-tier system channeling working-class students toward low-wage employment, and that educators must organize independently of the AEU and Labor Party.

Sources WSWS Report reveals impact of Australian school crisis on principals

An event supporting his release is set for May 28 at Humboldt University Berlin. The WSWS itself was banned in Ukraine in June 2025. The article argues Syrotiuk’s imprisonment exemplifies the antidemocratic character of the Zelensky government and the state suppression of anti-war socialist dissent.

Jacobin’s output centered on domestic policy and culture rather than the day’s wars. “NYC’s Economic Development Corporation Can Build Public Options” argues that municipal institutions should “deliver bold, ambitious new public services” — a program of administering capitalist city government more humanely, which substitutes municipal reformism for any challenge to the profit system. Its analysis of Japan’s rearmament and “revisionist ideas about the atrocities” correctly identifies a danger but treats it as a problem of bad ideology rather than the logic of inter-imperialist rivalry into which a Japan allied with US war planning is being drawn. A film review rounded out the day. The overall orientation remains the cultivation of a progressive wing of the Democratic Party and faith in capitalist-state institutions.

Left Voice

Left Voice continued promoting its May magazine package on Minneapolis, including Daniel Kóvacs’s “Notes on a New Chapter of Class Struggle” and Jason Koslowski’s “Image/Revolt: Minneapolis From Below,” presenting the anti-ICE protests as a model “uprising.” While correctly opposing ICE, the framing elevates spontaneous protest and “community” over the construction of a revolutionary party rooted in the working class. Madeleine Freeman’s “Epstein’s Crimes Are Rooted in Patriarchy and Capitalism: Socialist Feminism Is the Answer” channels a real social crime into the gender-essentialist categories of “patriarchy,” subordinating class to identity. The day’s orientation reflects the Morenoite tradition’s adaptation to protest politics and identity frameworks.

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL’s coverage is dominated by Iran and Lebanon, with statements headlined “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” and “Why Trump failed in his criminal war against Iran — and why we need to keep up the pressure,” alongside the claim that “The U.S. & Israel seek to turn Lebanon into a vassal state.” The anti-war agitation is genuine, but the line that Iranian “resistance” has “compelled” US imperialism to “step back” sows illusions in the bourgeois-clerical regime in Tehran as an anti-imperialist force, obscuring its repression of the Iranian working class. The PSL’s orientation remains campist — defending capitalist states opposed to Washington rather than advancing the independent struggle of the international working class.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s “Only Rotten Choices In Their System—We Need A Planned Economy” makes the sharp point that the US government spent over a third of 2025 income-tax revenue on war and weapons, opposing Trump’s proposed $445 billion military increase against cuts to housing, health, and education. The propaganda for a “planned economy” is welcome, but it is divorced from any concrete strategy independent of the existing union and electoral apparatus, and the group’s history of backing left-Democratic and “broad left” formations undercuts the revolutionary content. The day’s orientation is left-propagandist rather than a programmatic break with the labor bureaucracy.

Socialist Worker / SWP

The SWP’s “US strikes Iran again—but Trump’s war is backfiring” frames the war as a strategic miscalculation that is strengthening Iran — a reading that risks treating imperialist setbacks as cause for optimism rather than warning of escalation. “US forces ramp up attacks in Nigeria” notes Trump’s “defending Christians” pretext for intervention in Africa, and “School workers striking back” covers London and Hertfordshire disputes. As ever, the SWP channels strikes toward pressure on the existing union leaderships rather than building independent rank-and-file organization.

Communist / RCP (IMT)

The IMT’s communist.red led with “The 1926 General Strike: Britain’s revolution betrayed,” recasting that defeat as a near-revolution to burnish the group’s “revolutionary” credentials while glossing over the political questions of leadership. “China sets the agenda at the Xi-Trump summit” presents China as confidently “unmoved” and the US as “desperate” — great-power commentary that adapts to a multipolar framing rather than a class analysis — and a piece on the indictment of Raúl Castro defends “the Cuban Revolution against imperialist bullying,” uncritically defending the Castroite bureaucracy. The orientation combines historical mythmaking with geopolitical impressionism.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. ICE deploys iris-recognition scanners nationwide

The Trump administration is expanding ICE’s use of iris-recognition technology, with plans to deploy hundreds of scanning devices and feed scans collected from detained immigrants — without judicial oversight — into federal biometric databases. This is a qualitative escalation of the mass-surveillance state against the working class, particularly immigrant communities, and warrants a sustained socialist analysis of the construction of an authoritarian apparatus.

Recommended reading NPR News — ICE biometric surveillance expansion

2. Trump’s tariffs are the largest US tax increase since 1993 — paid by workers

The Trump tariff regime amounts to roughly a $1,500-per-household tax increase on American workers in 2026, even as the Global Economic Policy Uncertainty Index hits a record high. A socialist analysis would identify who actually pays these “nationalist” trade measures — workers and developing nations — and who profits, cutting through the economic-nationalist framing shared across both capitalist parties.

Recommended reading Tax Foundation Tariff Tracker: 2026 Trump tariffs & trade war by the numbers

3. Texas GOP runoff and the far-right consolidation of the Republican Party

Ken Paxton’s ouster of three-term Senator John Cornyn — sealed by a Trump endorsement — marks the displacement of even entrenched establishment Republicans by the Trumpist far right. The WSWS should analyze this not as electoral horse-race material but as a milestone in the transformation of the Republican Party into an instrument of authoritarian rule and the corresponding rightward collapse of the Democrats.

Recommended reading Texas Tribune Paxton defeats Cornyn in US Senate runoff

4. CERN reports strongest hints yet of physics beyond the Standard Model

Researchers at the Large Hadron Collider have reported what they call the strongest evidence to date of physics beyond the Standard Model — potentially among the most consequential particle-physics developments in decades. The WSWS science coverage could explain the significance for the public and contrast the collaborative, international character of fundamental science with the subordination of research to militarism and private profit.

Recommended reading SciTechDaily CERN sees strongest hints yet of physics beyond the Standard Model

5. Nova Scotia long-term care workers in their seventh week on strike

Some 3,400 long-term care workers in Nova Scotia have now been on strike for seven weeks — a major Canadian labor struggle that has received scant international attention. Given the WSWS’s coverage of the global crisis in care work and the role of union bureaucracies in isolating disputes, a dedicated article connecting the strike to the international assault on social services and the fight for rank-and-file control is warranted.

Recommended reading WSWS Workers Struggles: The Americas

6. Sam Altman walks back AI job-loss predictions amid intensifying labor debate

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman publicly admitted in Sydney that his predictions of imminent, sweeping AI-driven white-collar job losses have not materialized. The WSWS should examine how the AI hype cycle serves capital — disciplining workers with the threat of obsolescence while real productivity gains are captured as private profit — and counterpose a socialist program for the rational, collective use of automation.
