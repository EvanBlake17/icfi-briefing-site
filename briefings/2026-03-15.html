1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 16 as ground invasion planning advances

The US-Israeli air campaign against Iran — Operation Epic Fury — has now entered its sixteenth day with no sign of de-escalation. Iran’s Health Ministry reports at least 1,444 killed and 18,551 injured since strikes began on February 28, with the majority described as civilians. The US and Israel have conducted approximately 7,600 strikes on Iranian territory and 1,100 strikes on Lebanon. Thirteen US soldiers have been killed, including six in a single drone attack on Port Shuaiba, Kuwait.

The most significant development is the Pentagon’s explicit telegraphing of a ground invasion. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared that the Strait of Hormuz — through which one-fifth of global oil passes and which Iran has closed since the war began — will not “remain contested.” Roughly 5,000 additional Marines and sailors are being repositioned to the region. The Australian Strategic Policy Institute assessed that an amphibious landing on Iran’s Hormuz coastline would be “Gallipoli times ten,” facing fortified anti-ship missile batteries, drone launch sites, mine-laying facilities, hundreds of fast attack boats, and 20,000 naval troops.

Key facts:

  • US forces bombed military installations on Kharg Island, Iran’s primary oil export hub; Trump claimed he spared oil infrastructure “for reasons of decency”
  • Hegseth repeatedly declared “no quarter, no mercy” — Congressman Eugene Vindman warned these may constitute illegal orders under international law
  • Iran has fired over 500 ballistic/naval missiles and 2,000 drones since Feb. 28; missile launch rate has dropped over 90%, with analysts citing stockpile depletion
  • Mojtaba Khamenei elected new Supreme Leader on March 8 after his father was assassinated in the opening strikes
  • Pentagon briefers acknowledged to congressional staff that Iran was not planning to strike US forces unless Israel attacked first — undercutting the “imminent threat” justification
  • Brent crude back above $100/barrel despite IEA releasing 400 million barrels from strategic reserves
  • China’s FM Wang Yi called for an immediate end: “This was a war that should never have happened”

6. Sudan: world’s worst famine deepens as civil war enters third year

Sudan now holds the grim distinction of being the world’s largest hunger and displacement crisis simultaneously. An estimated 19.2 million people face severe food insecurity, with 635,000 in active famine — more than the rest of the world combined. The civil war between the SAF and RSF has killed an estimated 150,000–400,000 people since April 2023 and displaced 13.6 million.

Key facts:

  • On March 13, Doctors Without Borders accused Sudan’s army of a drone strike on a market near Chad in besieged Darfur — 4 killed, dozens wounded
  • WHO has verified 201 attacks on healthcare facilities since the conflict began; more than one-third of health facilities are non-functional
  • 3.2 million children under 5 and 1.2 million pregnant/breastfeeding women are acutely malnourished — up 22% since 2023
  • WFP urgently requires $700 million for January–June 2026 operations; USAID cuts have worsened the funding gap
  • Famine now confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli; risk of famine in 20 additional areas

H5N1 bird flu wipes out 7 million+ birds in Pennsylvania

Over 7 million birds have died in Pennsylvania in 2026, concentrated in Lancaster County. The A3 genotype of H5N1 — first identified in mammals — has now reached California, spreading from elephant seals to a sea otter and sea lion at Año Nuevo State Park. Across the Americas, H5N1 has been detected in 37 mammalian species and 94 bird species since January 2025. The genotype’s spread to new mammalian species and geographies is raising surveillance concerns about potential adaptation to mammalian hosts.

Sources South Philly Review Pennsylvania bird flu outbreak: 7 million birds · PAHO/WHO Epidemiological update: avian influenza A(H5N1) in the Americas

Pentagon designates Anthropic a “supply chain risk”

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic — whose Claude AI already runs on classified military platforms — a supply-chain risk after the company refused to grant the Pentagon unlimited use of its technology. Anthropic is suing the government on First Amendment and authority grounds, and at least 100 private-sector customers have paused or cancelled contracts in response to the designation. Hours after the confrontation, OpenAI signed its own deal with the DoD. A hearing on temporary relief is set for March 24. The standoff is the sharpest confrontation yet between AI companies and the federal government over military AI deployment, with no congressional framework in place.

COVID-19 at low levels nationally but metabolic effects emerging in youth

COVID-19 activity is low across the US as of March 13, with infections declining or likely declining in 36 states. However, new RECOVER Initiative data reveal concerning long-term effects: COVID-19 vaccines provide measurable protection against Long COVID in adolescents, while youth who had COVID-19 showed a 24% higher risk of high cholesterol and 15% higher risk of elevated body fat. These metabolic downstream effects represent an emerging long-term public health concern.

Federal AI regulatory deadlines are also reshaping the national landscape. A December 2025 Executive Order directing the Commerce Department to review state AI laws had a March 11 deadline, and the same order conditions approximately $21 billion in BEAD broadband funds on states not maintaining “onerous” AI laws — setting up likely litigation.

Microplastics linked to neurodegeneration in new studies

Two studies published this week found that microplastics can trigger brain inflammation potentially linked to Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s disease, and that severe COVID-19 or influenza infection can alter lung immune cells, leaving chronic inflammation that may raise lung cancer risk years later. Both findings expand the understood long-term health consequences of common environmental and viral exposures well beyond their acute effects.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output this week centers on interpreting the Iran war and broader US decline through a lens compatible with Democratic Party realignment politics.

  • “Hasan Piker on Why the US Empire Is in Decline” (March 14) — The streamer-turned-commentator argues the US is in its “imperial end times,” with Trump entering an Iran quagmire and “MAGA fascism” looming over the home front. The analysis treats imperial decline as a structural inevitability without addressing the role of the Democratic Party in enabling the war — notably the 21 House Democrats who voted for the military funding bill. The piece functions as lifestyle-left commentary rather than political analysis.

  • “Working-Class Resistance Forced ICE Out of Minneapolis” (March 14) — Ramy Khalil reports on labor organizing and activist efforts that have challenged Trump’s immigration enforcement, framing it as a model for “reviving union militancy.” While the reporting on workplace resistance is valuable, the article channels the struggle toward municipal and union-bureaucratic structures rather than independent working-class organization. The Minneapolis model is presented without any critique of the AFL-CIO leadership’s collaboration with the Democratic Party and its failure to mobilize against the war.

  • “A Post-Order World” (March 14) — Juliano Fiori analyzes declining US authority dismantling international norms without any replacement power structure emerging. The article correctly identifies the disintegration of the post-WWII order but treats it as a geopolitical phenomenon rather than a crisis of the capitalist system, avoiding any discussion of socialist internationalism as the alternative.

Jacobin’s overall orientation remains that of a left-liberal publication seeking to provide intellectual cover for a reformed Democratic Party politics. Its Iran coverage is notably thin on programmatic content — no call for workers’ strike action, no analysis of the class forces driving the war, no mention of the ICFI or any revolutionary alternative.

Left Voice

Left Voice has produced the most extensive pseudo-left coverage of the Iran war, reflecting its Trotskyist Fraction (FT-CI) international network.

This dual position, while formally correct in opposing both imperialism and the theocracy, avoids any concrete analysis of how the working class is to be mobilized independently. The FT-CI offers no strategy for building revolutionary parties in the Middle East or the imperialist centers.

  • “Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran” — Reports on the funeral of over 160 girls killed by Israeli strikes on an all-girls school in Minab, noting that the war is “extremely unpopular in the United States — less than one in four Americans say they support the strikes.” The article calls on educators to “take the lead” in anti-war organizing but confines this to campus activism and union resolutions rather than a political break with the Democratic Party. The UAW Local 2325 strike authorization vote over ICE activity is noted approvingly — but the FT-CI does not call for a general strike or independent political action.

Left Voice’s coverage is more militant in tone than Jacobin’s but shares the fundamental limitation of treating the trade unions and existing left organizations as the vehicles for anti-war struggle, rather than calling for the construction of a revolutionary party of the working class.
