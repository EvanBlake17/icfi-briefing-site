1. US-Iran war enters day 47 as ceasefire talks collapse and Trump declares conflict “very close to over”

The US-Iran war, launched without congressional authorization on February 28, has now killed more than 4,000 people across the Middle East — overwhelmingly in Iran and Lebanon — with Iran estimating $270 billion in total war losses. A fragile two-week ceasefire remains nominally in effect but expires April 22, and first-round Pakistan-mediated talks in Islamabad ended without agreement. The core sticking point remains unbridged: Washington demands a 20-year halt to uranium enrichment while Tehran counters with 5 years, calling enrichment rights “indisputable” but levels “negotiable.”

Trump declared the war “very close to over” in a Fox News interview, even as his naval blockade of Iranian ports continues and Iran has threatened to target Gulf and Red Sea shipping if the blockade is not lifted. A second round of talks is expected “within days.”

Key facts: - The last functioning hospital in Tebnin, southern Lebanon, was struck; Israeli forces are encircling Bint Jbeil and demolishing villages using “methods similar to Gaza” - UK PM Starmer stated publicly the UK would not “yield” to pressure to join the US-led Iran coalition - The Washington Post reported over 10,000 additional US troops deploying to the Middle East — 6,000 with the USS George H.W. Bush carrier strike group and 4,200 amphibious forces - As the WSWS’s Kevin Reed documented, Senator Tammy Duckworth’s war-powers discharge motion was defeated 52-47, the fourth such Democratic attempt; Democrat John Fetterman voted against it and explicitly backed “Operation Epic Fury”

3. IMF slashes global growth forecast to 3.1%, warns of near-recession under severe escalation

The IMF’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook cut the global growth forecast to 3.1% in 2026 and 3.2% in 2027 — well below the pre-pandemic average of 3.7% — driven primarily by the US-Iran conflict and the Hormuz blockade’s disruption of global energy markets. Global headline inflation is projected to rise to 4.4% under the base scenario. The IMF described the global economy’s situation as a “major test.”

The report’s downside scenarios are stark. Under the adverse scenario — a prolonged conflict — growth falls to 2.5% with inflation surging to 5.4%. Under the severe scenario — a major escalation — global growth collapses to 2% for two consecutive years with inflation exceeding 6%. Middle East regional growth was slashed from 3.9% to 1.1%.

Key facts: - The Hormuz blockade and energy market disruption are described as the primary downside risk to the global economy - The severe scenario would bring conditions close to the 2008–09 global recession - Buried within the report are recommendations for “fiscal consolidation” — austerity measures that would disproportionately burden the working class and the Global South - The Strait of Hormuz carries roughly 20% of global oil traffic; even partial disruption creates cascading inflationary effects

H5N1 acquires cattle-specific mutations but human risk remains low

A study published in Nature Communications (April 15) identified two new mutations that allow H5N1 to bind NeuGc, a sugar found in cattle mammary glands but not in humans or birds. The mutations enable faster viral replication and higher viral loads in milk. Lab tests showed the cattle-adapted virus had no increased — and sometimes slightly reduced — growth in human nasal cells.

Additional polymerase mutations (PB2 M631L, PA K497R) have accumulated in the B3.13 cattle genotype. The findings clarify the mechanism driving dairy herd outbreaks across the US and confirm the cattle lineage is not currently evolving toward enhanced human transmissibility, though ongoing adaptation warrants close surveillance of any future reassortment events.

Sources Science News New mutations help H5N1 infect cows but not people · Nature Communications Polymerase mutations in dairy cattle H5N1

Eli Lilly’s oral GLP-1 orforglipron achieves ~11% weight loss in Phase 3 trial

The ATTAIN-1 Phase 3 trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, enrolled 3,127 patients over 72 weeks. Orforglipron 36 mg achieved a mean body weight reduction of 11.2% versus 2.0% for placebo. Over half (54.6%) of patients in the top-dose group lost ≥10% body weight. Significant improvements in waist circumference, blood pressure, triglycerides, and non-HDL cholesterol were also documented. Adverse events were predominantly mild-to-moderate gastrointestinal effects.

Orforglipron is a once-daily pill requiring no food restrictions — unlike existing semaglutide tablets — potentially expanding GLP-1 access to the hundreds of millions worldwide who cannot access or tolerate injectable therapies. If approved, it would represent a major shift in the obesity treatment market from injection-dominated to oral delivery.

Afrika Bambaataa dies at 68, leaving contested legacy

Afrika Bambaataa, the DJ, rapper, and producer whose South Bronx block parties in the late 1970s helped give rise to hip-hop culture, died April 9 in Pennsylvania of prostate cancer at age 68. His 1982 recording “Planet Rock” — which fused Kraftwerk’s electronic textures with breakbeat rhythms — defined electro-funk and permanently altered popular music. Bambaataa codified hip-hop’s four foundational elements: DJing, graffiti, breakdancing, and rapping.

His cultural significance is inseparable from the social conditions that produced hip-hop: the devastation of the South Bronx through deindustrialization, municipal austerity, and racist neglect.
