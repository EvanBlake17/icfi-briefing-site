1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 20 as $200 billion funding request reveals invasion plans

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its 20th day on March 20 with no diplomatic offramp in sight, as the scale of Washington’s military ambitions became clearer through a $200 billion supplemental spending request submitted to Congress. The request, confirmed by Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth — who stated “it takes money to kill bad guys” — dwarfs the $51 billion Congress initially appropriated for the 2003 Iraq invasion and approaches the $144 billion annual peak of the Iraq surge, suggesting plans for a protracted ground occupation rather than continued aerial operations alone.

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, the spending request reveals that “the administration is planning the most endless of all endless wars — an open-ended invasion aimed at subjugating or destroying a country of 90 million people.” Combined with the proposed FY 2027 military budget of $1.5 trillion, the war funding would require devastating cuts to domestic programs: $1 trillion from Medicaid, $536 billion from Medicare, and $186 billion through SNAP elimination.

Key facts: - Total regional death toll exceeds 2,300; Iran’s Red Crescent reports 18,000+ civilian injuries inside Iran; the deadliest single strike hit an elementary girls’ school in Minab, killing more than 170 schoolgirls - The US has struck more than 7,000 targets in Iran since February 28 - Israel struck Iran’s South Pars gas field — the world’s largest — prompting Iranian retaliation against Qatar’s Ras Laffan LNG hub, which Qatar says will take five years to repair - Brent crude surged to $115–$118/barrel, up ~50% since the war began; the IEA released 400 million barrels of strategic reserves but failed to move prices - The UN nuclear watchdog confirmed a projectile struck Iran’s Bushehr nuclear plant site - Trump claimed no advance knowledge of the South Pars strike, but Israeli and US officials told Axios it was coordinated with and approved by the White House - Saudi Arabia intercepted a missile over Al Jouf on March 20, indicating widening regional fallout

2. Pakistan’s airstrike on Kabul rehabilitation hospital kills up to 408 civilians

Pakistan’s air force struck the Omid Addiction Treatment Hospital in Kabul on March 16 as part of “Operation Ghazab Lil Haq,” destroying a 1,000-bed facility that was operating at approximately 3,000 capacity. The death toll remains disputed: Afghan authorities report 408 killed and 265 injured, while UNAMA confirmed at least 143 killed and 119 injured after an on-site investigation that found “complete destruction” of a block housing adolescent drug patients.

The strike represents one of the deadliest single attacks on a medical facility since the 2015 US bombing of the MSF hospital in Kunduz. Pakistan has declared itself in “open war” with Afghanistan — a dangerous escalation between two nuclear-armed neighbors (Pakistan possesses nuclear weapons; Afghanistan borders nuclear-armed Pakistan and sits near India’s nuclear umbrella). A five-day ceasefire was announced March 18 at the request of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkey for Eid al-Fitr, but fundamental tensions remain unresolved.

Key facts: - UN mission officer-in-charge Georgette Gagnon confirmed the facility was “a well-known rehabilitation center” run by the Taliban interior ministry - Amnesty International stated the site is “well-documented” as a civilian rehab center since 2016 - Pakistan’s Information Minister denied civilian targeting, claiming secondary explosions indicated an ammunition depot - The conflict is receiving disproportionately little international attention relative to its scale, overshadowed by the Iran war

3. Israel launches ground invasion of Lebanon as death toll tops 1,000

Israel began formal ground operations in southern Lebanon on March 16, with Defense Minister Israel Katz stating operations will continue “until Hezbollah no longer posed a threat to northern Israeli communities.” The IDF has more than doubled troop deployments along the Lebanese border since March 1 and is conducting house-to-house searches in evacuated villages, aiming to clear the town of Khiyam before advancing to the Litani River.

The human toll is staggering. As of March 20, more than 1,000 people have been killed in Lebanon with nearly 1 million displaced — approximately 19% of the country’s entire population. Inside Iran, US-Israeli strikes have displaced an additional 3 million people, according to UN warnings.

Key facts: - France, Canada, Germany, Italy, and the UK issued a joint statement expressing they are “gravely concerned” and called for “immediate de-escalation” - The statement also condemned Hezbollah’s decision to join Iran in hostilities, framing the conflict as reciprocal rather than acknowledging the asymmetry of the Israeli invasion - The Lebanon invasion is occurring simultaneously with ongoing ceasefire violations in Gaza, where at least a dozen Palestinians were killed between March 14–15 alone

4. EU summit refuses to join US-Israel war but prepares Strait of Hormuz intervention

EU leaders at the March 19 European Council summit declared they have “no appetite” to deploy forces in direct support of the US-Israeli war on Iran, but their posture is more complex than the antiwar framing suggests. While German Chancellor Friedrich Merz said Germany “can and will commit ourselves only when the weapons fall silent” and Spanish PM Pedro Sánchez called the war “illegal,” EU leaders simultaneously pledged to keep the Strait of Hormuz open — a commitment that implies naval deployments in contested waters.

As Peter Schwarz argued on the WSWS, the Europeans are not opposing the war but positioning themselves to participate on their own terms, with Germany in particular using the crisis to assert great-power status. Merz’s statement that “we are learning: the others are also dependent on us, not just we on them” reveals the competitive imperialist logic underlying European “restraint.”

Key facts: - EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas declared “no appetite” for Hormuz deployment among EU leaders, but this was contradicted by the summit’s final communiqué - The UK has sent military planners to US CENTCOM to develop “a viable collective plan” to reopen the Strait — the first formal European military coordination with the US war effort - Hungary’s Viktor Orbán vetoed a €90 billion EU loan to Ukraine, linking it to disrupted Druzhba oil pipeline flows; European Council President António Costa said “nobody can blackmail” EU institutions - ECB President Lagarde warned the war’s “material impact on near-term inflation” could push 2026 inflation from 2.6% to 3.5%+ - Belarus released 250 political prisoners in a deal with Washington in exchange for partial sanctions relief

5. BP locks out 900 Whiting refinery workers after 98% contract rejection

BP locked out nearly 900 skilled refinery workers at its Whiting, Indiana facility on March 19 after workers rejected the company’s “last, best and final” contract offer by 98.3% with 94% turnout. The Whiting refinery is the largest in the Midwest, producing 440,000 barrels per day, and the lockout comes as US oil companies stand to earn an additional $63.4 billion if crude averages above $100/barrel — a threshold already surpassed amid the Iran war.

The rejected contract demanded $8–$10/hour wage cuts, elimination of 100 union positions, expanded use of contract labor, closure of the environmental department, and AI implementation without job protections. BP’s only concession in a counteroffer was reducing a signing bonus from $7,500 to $2,500 while maintaining all other demands.

Key facts: - The lockout affects USW Local 7-1 members — skilled operators and maintenance workers - BP is deploying management and contract workers to maintain operations during the lockout - The WSWS noted that the lockout strategy is designed to force “labor peace” while oil companies pocket war-driven windfall profits - The Whiting lockout follows a pattern of aggressive employer tactics across US industry, from the JBS meatpacking strike in Greeley, Colorado to ongoing healthcare walkouts

6. Sudan drone strikes hit hospital as world’s largest humanitarian crisis deepens

A drone strike hit Dilling General Hospital in Sudan’s Nuba Mountains, killing at least four patients, while the city of El Obeid endured drone attacks over five consecutive days. The UN recorded more than 200 civilians killed by drone strikes in White Nile State and Kordofan between March 4–12 alone, including at least 152 victims of Sudanese Armed Forces strikes in West Kordofan.

The war in Sudan — now approaching its second anniversary — has produced what the UN classifies as both the world’s largest humanitarian crisis and the world’s largest displacement crisis. Over 100,000 people have been killed, more than 12 million displaced, and 30 million — two-thirds of the population — need aid. Half the population faces acute hunger.

Key facts: - UN Spokesperson Stéphane Dujarric warned “humanitarian access is also becoming increasingly restrained” as supply routes face ongoing threats - The conflict receives a fraction of the international attention directed at the Iran war despite comparable civilian suffering - A UN report highlighted “domicide” — the systematic AI-assisted destruction of civilian housing — in Sudan, Gaza, Syria, and Ukraine

US eases Venezuela oil sanctions to counter Iran war energy shock

The US Treasury Department announced it is easing sanctions on Venezuela’s state oil company PDVSA, allowing US buyers to purchase Venezuelan oil on world markets — a stark reversal of years of “maximum pressure” policy driven by energy market desperation. Trump simultaneously waived Jones Act requirements for 60 days, allowing foreign-flagged vessels to carry goods between US ports to ease supply-chain pressure.

Brent crude remains above $100/barrel despite the 400-million-barrel IEA strategic reserve release. California gasoline has surpassed $5/gallon. The White House ruled out banning US crude and gas exports, resisting calls from domestic industry groups.

JBS meatpacking strike enters third day in Greeley, Colorado

The largest US meatpacking strike since the 1950s entered its third day at JBS’s Greeley, Colorado facility, with approximately 2,000 workers — predominantly immigrants — manning picket lines. The Greeley plant processes roughly 8% of US beef production. Workers have operated without a contract for over eight months after the UFCW ratified a national agreement excluding this facility.

Workers are seeking a minimum $3/hour raise from a base of $23.75/hour for sanitation workers. JBS offered 60 cents; the UFCW proposed $2. A sanitation worker identified as “Jaden” described dangerous conditions including inadequate PPE, chemical exposure to chlorine-based sanitizers and sodium hydroxide, and management retaliation. Workers on the picket line expressed broader political concerns: opposition to the Iran war, rejection of Trump’s “mass deportation” policies, and solidarity with immigrant coworkers facing deportation threats.

25,000 Kaiser healthcare workers carry out one-day strike

Approximately 2,400 mental health professionals from the National Union of Healthcare Workers (NUHW) conducted a one-day strike on March 19, joined by 23,000 registered nurses from the California Nurses Association in a sympathy action. The strike centers on Kaiser’s failure to provide adequate mental health staffing despite a $28 million Department of Labor settlement in February 2026 for access violations.

The 2022 contract allocated seven hours weekly for non-billable work, but Kaiser has failed to implement meaningful changes. A parallel 31,000-worker UNAC/UHCP strike ended in February with only 21.5% pay increases over four years, which the WSWS characterized as a betrayal by the union bureaucracy.

Ford worker killed at Sharonville Transmission Plant in Ohio

Gregory Knopf, a 64-year-old Ford worker at the Sharonville Transmission Plant in Ohio, died on March 16 when a press machine activated during routine maintenance and pinned him against equipment. He is survived by his wife, three children, and eight grandchildren. OSHA, the Hamilton County Coroner’s Office, and Ford have all opened investigations.

The death occurred just 11 months after Ronald Adams Sr. was killed at a Stellantis plant under similar circumstances. US workplace fatalities reached 5,070 in 2024, with occupational disease claiming approximately 135,000 lives in 2023. UAW presidential candidate Will Lehman connected the death to systemic failures: “These deaths are the predictable, preventable product of a system that treats workers as expendable inputs to production.”

The US Postal Service announced it will exhaust cash reserves by February 2027, with Postmaster General David Steiner warning: “At our current rate we will be out of cash in less than 12 months… the Postal Service will be unable to deliver the mail.” USPS is losing approximately $9 billion annually and has eliminated 30,000–35,000 positions over four years, with 71% of delivery routes operating at a financial loss.

The announcement arrives as the Pentagon requests $200 billion for the Iran war — a contrast that underscores priorities. The cost of the war’s first week alone exceeded $12 billion, more than a full year of USPS losses. USPS serves 170 million addresses six days per week.

US measles cases approach 1,400 as herd immunity erodes

The US measles outbreak continues to worsen, with 1,362 confirmed cases reported across 31 jurisdictions as of March 12 — on pace to be the worst year since measles elimination was declared in 2000. South Carolina leads with 632 cases, followed by Utah (117) and Florida (64). An alarming 93% of patients are unvaccinated or have unknown vaccine status, and 4% have required hospitalization.

Only 10 states now maintain the 95% MMR vaccination threshold required for herd immunity, with 14 active outbreaks underway. PAHO has delayed its decision on whether the United States loses its measles elimination status until November — a potential public health milestone that would mark the reversal of a quarter-century achievement. The erosion of vaccination rates reflects both the anti-scientific propaganda promoted by figures like Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and the systematic defunding of public health infrastructure.

H5N1 mammalian-adapted strain detected in California marine mammals

The A3 genotype of H5N1 avian influenza — specifically noted for its capacity to adapt and spread among mammalian hosts — has been confirmed in a sea otter and sea lion at Año Nuevo State Park, California, marking the first A3 detection in the state. This is a sentinel event for pandemic preparedness, as the A3 strain’s arrival in California marine mammals suggests ongoing mammalian adaptation of the virus.

Separately, Pennsylvania’s 2026 H5N1 outbreak has killed over 7 million birds, with a single Lancaster County egg-layer facility reporting 2.6 million birds affected. Since January 1, 2025, H5N1 has been detected in 37 mammalian species across the Americas, according to PAHO’s March 11 epidemiological update.

Pentagon designates Anthropic a “supply chain risk”

The Defense Department designated Anthropic — maker of the Claude AI system — as a supply chain risk, a label typically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei, barring its use in DoD-linked contracts. Anthropic is suing on First Amendment and separation-of-powers grounds, arguing the Pentagon cannot unilaterally regulate US AI companies through procurement policy. At least 100 customers from pharma to fintech have requested contract pauses or cancellations.

The case sets a precedent for the emerging battle over AI governance: whether military-industrial imperatives or civilian commercial interests will shape the development of the most powerful AI systems. The designation reportedly stems from Anthropic’s refusal to remove safety guardrails that the Pentagon sought overridden for military applications.

Sources Axios AI policy power center: Pentagon vs. Anthropic

Senate releases draft national AI framework

The Senate’s proposed TRUMP AMERICA AI Act would create a federal AI standard superseding all state laws, codify the NIST Center for AI Standards, and bundle in the NO FAKES Act (AI likeness protections) and Kids Online Safety Act. The White House and House GOP are simultaneously preparing legal challenges to state-level AI regulations via a DOJ AI Litigation Task Force established January 9.

If passed, this would be the first comprehensive federal AI law in the US, resolving a growing patchwork of state rules — Washington, New York, Minnesota, and New Jersey all passed or introduced AI bills just this week.

Man survives 48 hours without lungs in surgical first

A 33-year-old man survived 48 hours without functioning lungs after a severe flu-triggered infection caused complete bilateral lung failure and multi-organ failure. Surgeons removed both lungs and kept him alive on ECMO support until donor lungs became available — a procedure previously considered unsurvivable for such a duration. The case may redefine the limits of bridging therapy for end-stage respiratory failure and expand candidacy for lung transplantation in critically ill patients.

Separately, researchers used a supercomputer with nearly 7,000 GPUs to simulate the complete physical behavior of a quantum chip — including real materials, qubit layout, and signal interactions — before any hardware was built. Full-physics pre-fabrication simulation could dramatically cut quantum hardware development cycles and reduce costly failed chip runs.

Arts and culture

New York City’s inaugural Art House Cinema Week launches today, running March 20–26 across nearly 30 independent theaters including Film Forum, IFC Center, and the Museum of the Moving Image. The city’s Mayor’s Office of Media and Entertainment is funding 5,000 free tickets alongside filmmaker Q&As, restored prints, and retrospectives. The initiative represents a significant civic intervention to sustain independent cinema at a moment when arthouse venues across the country face existential pressure from streaming consolidation, rising rents, and declining theatrical attendance for non-franchise films. That a major city government now views independent cinema infrastructure as requiring active public subsidy — much like libraries or parks — speaks to the crisis of cultural production under late capitalism, where art that cannot generate blockbuster returns is structurally threatened.

On Broadway, two notable productions began previews this week. The Fear of 13, written by Lindsey Ferrentino and directed by David Cromer, opened previews March 19 at the James Earl Jones Theatre with Adrien Brody, Tessa Thompson, and Ephraim Sykes. The A-list film cast signals continued crossover between Hollywood talent and the stage. Meanwhile, Cats: The Jellicle Ball began previews March 18 at the Broadhurst Theatre, reimagining Andrew Lloyd Webber’s feline pageant as a drag ballroom spectacular with André De Shields. The radical reinterpretation of one of Broadway’s most commercially iconic productions is a test case for how far revivals can push against their source material’s original cultural assumptions.

In film, Chloé Zhao’s Hamnet — with Jessie Buckley (who won Best Actress) and Paul Mescal as Shakespeare — and Joel Edgerton’s Train Dreams (four Oscar nominations including Best Picture) dominate awards conversation. The momentum around literary adaptations represents a notable, if temporary, counterweight to the franchise-dominated box office.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output this week crystallizes the tendency’s fundamental orientation: managing capitalism more equitably rather than overthrowing it, through the vehicle of the Democratic Party.

  • “Julie Menin Is Protecting New York’s Ultrawealthy” (Jacobin) — Frames the conflict over taxing the rich as a fight between socialist Mayor Zohran Mamdani and obstructionist Democrats. Yet Mamdani ran and won on the Democratic ballot line — a structural dependency Jacobin treats as unremarkable. This is the classic DSA-adjacent pressure-from-within politics: the instrument of working-class betrayal (the Democratic Party) is accepted as the only viable electoral vehicle, while individual “socialist” officeholders are celebrated for attempting reforms within its framework.

  • “Capitalism Has a Lot of Room to Redistribute Wealth Right Now” (Jacobin) — Argues that economists confirm capitalism can tolerate greater redistribution. This is the tendency’s theoretical core stated plainly: the horizon of politics is managing capitalism more fairly, not transcending it. Social-democratic reformism dressed in left academic language, with no challenge to the capitalist mode of production itself.

  • “Politics After Literacy” (Jacobin) — Cultural analysis of postliteracy and political irrationality. The focus on cultural critique over class analysis is characteristic of Jacobin’s drift toward liberal intelligentsia concerns.

Assessment: Jacobin’s political ecosystem is organically integrated with the Democratic Party through the DSA electoral strategy. The tendency promotes a redistributionist rather than socialist program, functioning as the intellectual wing of a project to channel working-class discontent into bourgeois institutions.
