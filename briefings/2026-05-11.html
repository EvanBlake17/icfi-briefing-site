1. Trump rejects Iran counter-proposal as Israel kills 63 in Lebanon

The US-Israel war on Iran entered its 72nd day on Sunday after President Donald Trump rejected Tehran’s counter-proposal to the US 14-point plan, posting on Truth Social that the Iranians “will be laughing no longer!” and calling the response “TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE.” Iran’s Foreign Ministry replied that Washington continues to make “unreasonable demands.” A ceasefire has technically been in place since April 8, but the US naval blockade has continued since April 13, and Iran has kept the Strait of Hormuz closed to foreign shipping.

Iran’s counter-proposal, delivered via Pakistani intermediaries on May 10, demands war reparations, full sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz, an end to all sanctions and release of frozen assets. The US plan would force Iran to halt uranium enrichment for 12+ years and surrender its 440-kilogram stock of 60%-enriched uranium in exchange for gradual sanctions relief. Trump publicly acknowledged that only “around 70 percent” of US war aims had been achieved, explicitly leaving the door open to further strikes.

Israeli forces meanwhile killed at least 63 people in Lebanon over the May 9–10 weekend — the deadliest 48 hours since the April 16 ceasefire there. Two medics were killed on May 11 alone. Cumulative Lebanese casualties since the March 2 resumption of Israeli operations now stand at 2,846 dead and 8,693 wounded, according to figures cited in WSWS reporting.

2. Three-day Russia-Ukraine ceasefire collapses on Victory Day

A US-brokered three-day ceasefire intended to coincide with Russia’s May 9 Victory Day celebrations collapsed within hours of taking effect, with both sides immediately accusing the other of bad faith. The truce was paired with a large-scale prisoner exchange that did proceed, but the underlying fighting never stopped.

Russia’s Defence Ministry charged that Ukraine had violated the truce 16,071 times, while Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy acknowledged that Russia had halted air attacks on Ukrainian cities but said combat continued along every section of the frontline. Ukrainian authorities reported three civilians killed by Russian drone strikes near the front and more than 200 separate battlefield clashes since Saturday.

Key facts: - Russia and Ukraine had previously declared rival, unilateral ceasefires on May 4, with each immediately denouncing the other’s - The breakdown deepens the credibility crisis of Trump’s brokerage role, which has now produced two failed truces in a week - European NATO members continue to push for accelerated weapons transfers and an expanded mandate for the “coalition of the willing”

3. UK Labour suffers worst local rout in decades; Starmer refuses to resign

The UK Labour Party suffered one of the heaviest local election defeats in its history on May 7–8, losing 1,496 councillors and control of 38 councils across England and Wales, with roughly 5,000 contested seats and 136 councils in play. Hard-right Reform UK gained more than 1,400 seats. The Green Party gained more than 300; the Liberal Democrats more than 150.

In Wales, where Labour had governed continuously, the party was driven into third place. Plaid Cymru (pro-independence, soft-left) finished first, with Reform second. The collapse was attributed by virtually every commentator to a combination of working-class anger over Gaza complicity, austerity, welfare cuts and the cost of living. Prime Minister Keir Starmer told reporters at Downing Street that his administration represented “a 10-year project of renewal” and ruled out resignation despite open pressure from within his own parliamentary party.

Key facts: - Labour was “squeezed on all sides” — losing to the right (Reform), the soft-left (Greens, Lib Dems, Plaid Cymru) and to independent candidates, particularly in seats with large Muslim populations - Reform UK’s parliamentary candidate base now spans the entire country for the first time - The result vindicates analysis on the WSWS, which has argued the Starmer government’s combination of austerity, militarism and complicity in the Gaza genocide would generate a political crisis

4. Israel now controls 59% of Gaza as ceasefire violations top 2,400

The October 10, 2025 Gaza ceasefire has been violated at least 2,400 times by Israeli forces through mid-April 2026, according to Gaza’s Government Media Office, with Israeli forces using the truce to push the “Yellow Line” steadily westward. They now control roughly 59% of the Strip — a vast expansion of their footprint since October. The first phase of the agreement has never been fully implemented, and the 600 daily aid trucks promised in the text have never been allowed in.

On May 10, Israeli air strikes killed at least three Palestinians, including Hamas police officials, and a drone strike targeted a motorcycle near the Jabalia refugee camp. Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan held talks with Hamas officials on May 9, ostensibly to revive negotiations; talks remain stalled over the disarmament demand Israel insists must precede any further phase.

Key facts: - The Gaza Government Media Office count averages roughly 12 documented ceasefire violations per day across the seven-month window - Aid groups operating from Egyptian Rafah confirm that food, fuel and medical supply levels remain a small fraction of pre-war baselines - The systematic blockade of the 600 daily aid trucks constitutes ongoing use of mass starvation as a policy instrument

5. Bulk carrier struck by drone in Qatari waters

A bulk carrier transiting from Abu Dhabi to a Qatari port was struck by an unknown projectile at 03:01 UTC on May 10, 23 nautical miles northeast of Mesaieed Port, Qatar. The vessel suffered a small fire that was extinguished, no crew were injured, and the ship continued under its own power to harbour. The UK Maritime Trade Operations (UKMTO) confirmed the strike. Qatar’s Defence Ministry condemned the attack but declined to name a perpetrator.

The incident is part of a pattern of escalating Gulf shipping attacks tied to the US-Iran standoff over Hormuz. Iran has closed the strait to foreign shipping and has seized foreign-flagged vessels attempting passage; the US has maintained a counter-blockade and has fired on Iranian-flagged tankers attempting to circumvent it. Qatar is one of the most important LNG hubs in the world, and its waters were until now considered well outside the conflict zone.

6. Netanyahu calls for cutting US military aid “to zero”

In a CBS interview that aired Sunday, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said “it’s time we weaned ourselves from the remaining military support” from Washington, and explicitly proposed reducing the $3.8 billion annual US military aid package to Israel “to zero.” The statement, delivered while the US-Israel war on Iran is still actively prosecuted, is widely read as Netanyahu asserting Israeli operational independence and pre-empting any US pressure to restrain action against Tehran, Lebanon or the Gaza Strip.

The remarks come amid open tensions between the White House and Tel Aviv over the conduct of the Iran war, after Trump publicly acknowledged that only 70 percent of US objectives had been met. Critics across the Israeli political spectrum noted that Israel’s defence industry is in fact heavily dependent on US technology transfers, and that Netanyahu’s proposal — if implemented — would also dissolve the political constraints the aid package nominally imposes on Israeli military planning.

7. Sudan war enters fourth year as UAE-Ethiopia proxy dimension widens

Sudan’s civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces (RSF) has now entered its fourth year, with the UN’s relief chief saying bluntly that the world is “failing Sudan.” More than 150,000 people have been killed since April 2023, and nearly 15 million have been displaced — one of the largest displacement crises in human history. Twenty-four million face severe food shortages and 19 million lack access to safe drinking water.

The Sudanese government has accused both Ethiopia and the United Arab Emirates of orchestrating at least four drone attacks since March 1, including strikes on Khartoum airport. Sudan has recalled its ambassador from Addis Ababa. The conflict is increasingly characterized as a proxy war driven by control over gold mining and smuggling routes — both sides depend on gold revenue to fund operations. The RSF still controls Darfur, where a UN fact-finding mission has identified “hallmarks of genocide.”

8. India-Pakistan mark one year since aerial war; both arming aggressively

The anniversary of Operation Sindoor — India’s May 7, 2025 missile strikes inside Pakistan, launched in retaliation for the April 22, 2025 Pahalgam attack that killed 26 civilians — has been marked by competing military showcases between the two nuclear-armed states. Pakistan’s military, in a May 7 statement, claimed India had seen only “10 percent” of Pakistan’s military potential and unveiled new supersonic cruise missiles, loitering munitions, reconnaissance satellites, counter-drone systems and a new Army Rocket Force Command. The streets of Karachi, Lahore and Islamabad have filled with banners celebrating military leadership.

India, in turn, has announced the formation of a Joint Operations Centre to address the coordination failures exposed during the four-day 2025 aerial war. Both states continue to claim strategic victories from a conflict that ended in a ceasefire neither side decisively won. Underlying tensions over Kashmir remain unresolved, and no active back-channel diplomacy is publicly known to exist between the two governments — a fact noted with alarm by South Asian security analysts. The region is home to roughly 1.7 billion people.

India’s BJP wins five states amid mass voter purges

The ruling Bharatiya Janata Party of Prime Minister Narendra Modi swept five state elections, including its first-ever victory in West Bengal — a stunning result in a state long controlled by Trinamool Congress and historically a bastion of the Stalinist CPI(M). Wasantha Rupasinghe and Keith Jones, writing on the WSWS, attribute the result to a combination of massive BJP fundraising advantages, institutional capture of the Election Commission, and a so-called “Special Intensive Revision” of the voter rolls that removed millions of voters concentrated in poor and Muslim communities.

The Congress Party — for most of the post-independence period India’s natural party of government — now controls just four of 28 states. The Stalinist CPI(M) has been entirely wiped out in West Bengal. Tens of thousands of Delhi industrial workers struck during the campaign period over fuel prices, and more than 1,000 workers remain imprisoned on fabricated charges from earlier protest waves.

Australia’s Liberal Party collapses to 12.4% in Farrer by-election

The Farrer by-election produced a Liberal Party collapse to just 12.4 percent of the vote — a 31-percentage-point swing in twelve months and one of the worst results in the party’s 80-year history. The seat was won by far-right One Nation candidate David Farley at 39 percent; a local independent finished second at 28 percent. The Liberal Party, historically the dominant force on the Australian centre-right, is now in its worst electoral position since 1944.

WSWS analyst Oscar Grenfell argues the deeper driver is the Australian Labor government’s drift to the right — its embrace of nationalist militarism (AUKUS), austerity (NDIS cuts) and immigrant scapegoating — which has vacated the political centre and allowed far-right formations to capture working-class discontent. The result mirrors developments in the UK and across Europe, where the collapse of social-democratic governments has consistently fed far-right rather than left-wing alternatives.

New Zealand coalition expands deportation rules

The right-wing coalition government in New Zealand has announced new “New Zealand values” citizenship tests, scheduled to begin in 2027, and is expanding the window during which a permanent resident can be deported from 10 years to 20 years, while simultaneously removing the humanitarian appeal route from the immigration code. The announcements come ahead of the November 2026 national election and explicitly draw on Australian and US precedents.

Tom Peters, writing on the WSWS, notes that the same coalition is simultaneously restricting voting rights and weakening labour protections — exposing the hypocrisy of the “democratic values” framing of the citizenship tests. The policies are calibrated to scapegoat migrants for an economic crisis driven by the rising cost of living, falling wages and a deepening housing crisis affecting both migrant and New Zealand-born workers.

Sri Lanka: 12 men face execution over 2022 MP killing

Twelve men have been sentenced to death over the killing of a Sri Lankan parliamentarian during the 2022 mass uprising that toppled the Rajapaksa government. The convictions came despite a divided verdict in which the presiding judge himself wrote that “the prosecution has not presented a strong case,” and despite minimal physical evidence connecting most of the defendants to the killing. The men, who plan to appeal, are predominantly from impoverished backgrounds; they were swept up in violence that erupted when government supporters attacked anti-government protesters at Galle Face in May 2022.

Pani Wijesiriwardena, writing on the WSWS, argues the convictions function as class-based state repression — punishing those at the bottom of the social pyramid for the consequences of violence that was organized by senior politicians who remain untouched.

New Jersey districts cut 1,000+ teachers as ESSER runs out

Public school districts across New Jersey — including Paterson, Camden and several others — are eliminating more than 1,000 teaching and support positions for the 2026–27 school year. The cuts are driven by the exhaustion of federal pandemic-relief (ESSER) funds, declining enrollment tied to immigration enforcement fears that have kept undocumented and mixed-status families from sending children to school, rising healthcare costs and the steady drain of charter-school expansion.

In Paterson, the maintenance budget alone is being cut from $9.2 million to $876,343 — a more than 90% reduction. Sandy English, writing on the WSWS, frames the crisis as a deliberate policy choice: federal funds have not run out for the Pentagon or for upper-bracket tax cuts. The article identifies education-union leadership in New Jersey as collaborating with district administrators in administering the layoffs rather than mobilizing the rank and file to resist.

Harvard graduate strike enters fourth week

Approximately 4,000 graduate-student workers at Harvard University entered the fourth week of their strike on May 11. The workers, who run the bulk of undergraduate teaching and research at the world’s wealthiest university, earn $18–21/hour and have been on strike since April 21. National data show more than 25% of US graduate assistants report housing or food insecurity; at UMass Boston, doctoral stipends remain at $20,000–22,000 annually.

Writing on the WSWS, Mike Ingram identifies the UAW bureaucracy as actively obstructing the strike — delaying solidarity action with other academic workers, accepting management’s framing of the dispute as “narrow,” and refusing to advance demands such as protections for international students against ICE-driven deportation and divestment from Pentagon contractors. The article calls for the formation of an independent rank-and-file strike committee.

Sources WSWS Harvard graduate workers strike enters fourth week

US Steel contests OSHA fines over Clairton explosion

US Steel and contractor MPW Industrial Services have filed formal challenges to the OSHA citations issued in February 2026 over the August 2025 explosion at the Clairton Coke Works that killed two workers. The combined fines total $180,000 — a sum US Steel’s CEO earns roughly every three days in compensation. Federal investigators identified the failure of a 70-year-old cast-iron valve and systemic deferred maintenance as the proximate cause.

Samuel Davidson on the WSWS argues that the case demonstrates the structural inadequacy of regulator-led safety enforcement when the financial cost of an avoidable workplace death is, for an employer of US Steel’s size, an accounting rounding error. The United Steelworkers leadership has not called any strike, work stoppage or safety walkout at the affected facilities.

New York transit and LIRR contracts expire May 16

Contracts covering more than 40,000 New York MTA transit workers and Long Island Rail Road employees expire on May 16, creating conditions for a combined strike that would shut down virtually all transit access to Manhattan and affect millions of daily commuters. The MTA carries $49 billion in long-term bond debt — accumulated through decades of deliberate policy to finance capital projects with worker-borne debt-service costs rather than progressive taxation of the New York wealth concentration.

Alan Whyte, Daniel de Vries and Jerry White, writing on the WSWS, document that transit-union leadership has spent the year preparing the membership for concessions rather than for industrial action, including pressing for binding-arbitration arrangements that historically produce settlements below inflation. They call for the formation of autonomous rank-and-file committees prepared to take strike action regardless of the union leadership’s posture.

US measles cases reach 1,842, elimination status at risk

The US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention reports 1,842 confirmed measles cases through May 7, 2026, spread across 25 distinct outbreaks. 93% of cases are outbreak-associated, and 92% of patients are either unvaccinated or have unknown vaccination status. The Utah-Arizona border outbreak alone has surpassed 600 confirmed cases — the largest active outbreak in the country. Public health analysts now describe the loss of US measles elimination status this fall as “highly likely.” Elimination was first declared in 2000.

MV Hondius hantavirus outbreak: 3 dead, 94 evacuated in Tenerife

The WHO has issued Disease Outbreak Notice DON-599 confirming an unprecedented shipborne hantavirus outbreak aboard the Dutch expedition cruise vessel MV Hondius. As of May 8, eight cases (six confirmed, two suspected) and three deaths have been registered; on May 10, 94 passengers of 19 nationalities were evacuated by military and government aircraft after the vessel reached Tenerife. The pathogen has been identified as Andes virus, a hantavirus variant capable of rare human-to-human transmission with a 30–50% fatality rate and no available vaccine. Seventeen American passengers were transported to a specialized biocontainment unit in Nebraska for evaluation. The WHO rates the global risk as “low” but treats this as the first confirmed shipborne hantavirus event in history.

NEJM: Two IgA nephropathy drugs cut proteinuria; mRNA flu vaccine beats standard

The New England Journal of Medicine published two Phase 3 trials this week showing meaningful proteinuria reductions versus placebo in patients with IgA nephropathy. The sibeprenlimab trial demonstrated a statistically significant reduction; the atacicept trial showed similar efficacy in a parallel cohort. Together, the results represent a substantial expansion of treatment options for a kidney disease that historically had few. A separate trial published in the same issue found an mRNA-platform influenza vaccine outperformed standard-of-care vaccines on confirmed influenza-like illness in adults 50 and older — though with higher reactogenicity. This is the first major demonstration of mRNA vaccine efficacy beyond COVID-19.

Trump administration reverses course on AI oversight

The Trump administration is now actively considering pre-deployment government vetting of frontier AI models — a reversal of its early-2025 position rejecting such measures. The pivot is driven in part by national-security findings about Anthropic’s new “Mythos” model and its demonstrated capacity to identify and exploit previously unknown cybersecurity vulnerabilities. Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI have separately agreed to share pre-release models with NIST for evaluation. Meanwhile, Nvidia has surpassed $40 billion in equity commitments to AI-adjacent firms so far in 2026, including new deals with IREN ($2.1 billion) and Corning ($3.2 billion). At the same time, grassroots resistance to data-center construction is becoming a concrete bottleneck: at least 16 data-center projects worth a combined $64 billion have been blocked or delayed since May 2024, after hundreds of protesters turned out May 4 against a Kevin O’Leary-backed facility in Box Elder County, Utah.

Other notable items: - H5N1: The WHO logged 13 avian/swine influenza cases in humans in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal pediatric case in Bangladesh; the virus is now circulating in more species across more continents than at any prior point, though immediate US human risk remains low. - COVID-19: As of May 5, COVID is declining or likely declining in 26 US states and growing in zero; the WHO is gathering data for May 2026 antigen-composition decisions for fall vaccine formulation. - Nature: Intraoperative neuronal recordings show the human brain can encode new information under general anesthesia, with direct implications for anesthesia protocols. A separate paper identifies four distinct sleep substates in fish — pushing the evolutionary origin of structured sleep back hundreds of millions of years.

Arts and culture

The most politically charged cultural release of the week is Cyril Aris’s “A Sad and Beautiful World” — a Lebanese feature about two childhood friends weighing whether to stay or flee Beirut amid the 2026 Israeli bombardment. Writing on the WSWS, David Walsh argues the film has genuine emotional power but ultimately fails to confront the historical catastrophe it depicts: it centers the romantic, middle-class soul-searching of two characters who can imagine moving to “imaginary islands” while leaving offscreen the population of southern Beirut — workers, refugees, the urban poor — who have no such option. Walsh’s review crystallizes the WSWS line on contemporary war cinema: filmmakers depicting imperialist violence must combine “human sensitivity” with “rigorous historical analysis and an angry, partisan stance.” Romantic introspection in the face of mass slaughter, however well-intentioned, functions as a form of evasion.

The film’s release coincides with a broader pattern of cultural production around the wars in Iran, Gaza and Lebanon. Aris’s film follows a wave of festival selections from Lebanese, Palestinian and Iranian filmmakers being received as humanitarian objects of contemplation rather than as political indictments. The framing tends to depoliticize: the films are read as testimonies of suffering rather than as documents of an actively prosecuted imperialist war for which specific governments — in Washington, Tel Aviv, London, Berlin — are politically responsible.

Other arts and culture developments in the past 24 hours: - Music and the Iran war: Major Western festivals are continuing to invite Israeli state-cultural institutions even as those institutions remain official partners of the IDF. Boycott pressure has intensified on European summer festival programmers, with petitions citing the 72,500+ Palestinian death toll cited in WSWS reporting and the documented destruction of Iranian medical infrastructure. - Theatre: UK and US regional theatres continue to absorb the consequences of post-pandemic public-funding cuts. Arts Council England’s reduced 2026 allocation has driven a wave of mid-scale closures; in the US, the National Endowment for the Arts continues operating under reduced authorization as part of the Trump administration’s broader cultural defunding. - Visual arts and restitution: Major European museums continue to face restitution claims tied to colonial-era looting from Africa and the Middle East; institutional responses remain largely cosmetic. The structural relationship between museum collections and imperialist plunder — a recurrent theme of WSWS arts coverage — is rarely articulated by curators themselves. - Cultural defunding and repression: The pattern across the imperialist powers is now consistent: arts budgets compressed to fund military expansion, with film festivals, public broadcasters and universities used as ideological instruments. The German cultural establishment’s near-total alignment with the Israeli state — including the dis-invitation and de-platforming of pro-Palestinian artists — is the sharpest example, but versions of it now exist in the UK, France and the US.

The WSWS approach to arts and culture insists that genuine artistic achievement is recognized regardless of an artist’s professed politics, but that depictions of war, genocide and class society must be analyzed in their social and historical context. Works that treat imperialist violence as ahistorical tragedy — rather than as the consequence of specific class interests and specific political decisions — function objectively to disarm their audiences, regardless of the filmmaker’s stated intentions. The Aris film is the latest example.

How workers can fight the wave of AI layoffs — Tom Hall

The Perspective uses Cloudflare’s May 7 announcement — 1,100 workers cut (20% of the workforce) despite record quarterly revenue of $639.8 million, a 34% year-over-year increase — as the entry point into a mass layoff wave that is being explicitly justified by AI implementation. Hall quotes CEO Matthew Prince’s line — “Just because you’re fit doesn’t mean you can’t get fitter” — as a literal expression of the ruling class’s contempt for the labour that produced the record profits.

The data Hall assembles is striking: 300,000+ announced US job cuts in the first four months of 2026; 85,411 of those in tech, more than double the transport sector; the information sector has shed 342,000 positions across 16 consecutive months of decline. Specific company moves: Meta cutting 8,000 jobs while cancelling 6,000 open roles; Microsoft’s first voluntary buyout in 51 years targeting up to 8,750 workers; Oracle eliminating up to 30,000 — including the workers who trained the very AI systems replacing them; Spirit Airlines collapsed (17,000 unemployed); UPS targeting 68,000; VW targeting 50,000 by 2030.

The political core of the article is its insistence that AI is “an extraordinary technology with the capacity to eliminate drudgery” — the problem is not the technology but the private monopoly ownership of it. Hall cites Block CEO Jack Dorsey predicting “the majority of companies will reach the same conclusion” within a year, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei forecasting AI will write “essentially all software code within a year,” and Vinod Khosla forecasting that “by 2030, 80 percent of all jobs will be capable of being done by an AI.” Against the existing union-bureaucratic response of “AI labour-management boards,” Hall calls for the IWA-RFC to coordinate four demands: no layoffs from AI; AI productivity gains converted into reduced hours with full pay; democratic worker veto over technology implementation; and expropriation of the major technology corporations as publicly owned utilities under working-class control.

Australia: Health Workers Rank-and-File Committee opposes Labor’s attack on disability services

The Australian Health Workers Rank-and-File Committee passed a unanimous resolution condemning the Albanese Labor government’s $35 billion cut to the National Disability Insurance Scheme — described as the largest cut to any government program in Australian history. The cuts will reduce annual NDIS funding growth from 10% to 2% and remove roughly 300,000 people from eligibility by 2030. The shift from diagnosis-based to so-called “functional assessment” eligibility is identified as the mechanism for mass denial of services. The resolution counterpoises the NDIS cuts against record Australian military spending increases, framing the Labor austerity drive as class policy.

War, genocide and the weaponization of healthcare: An interview with Bilal Irfan — Benjamin Mateus

Bioethicist Bilal Irfan documents that the deliberate targeting of healthcare infrastructure has become a structural feature of the wars in Gaza, Lebanon and Iran — not collateral damage. Specific figures cited only in this interview: more than 200 medical centers damaged in Iran in “Operation Epic Fury”; the systematic suppression of health-data collection in Gaza, where the 72,500+ figure is understood to be a significant undercount. Irfan explicitly links Middle Eastern populations to domestic US labour struggles (Harvard graduate workers) as sharing interconnected causes rooted in capitalist crisis, and argues that captured institutions — the International Criminal Court, the United Nations — structurally cannot provide remedy.

New linguistic expertise deepens debacle for prosecution of Bogdan Syrotiuk — Clara Weiss

A third court-ordered linguistic analysis of statements by the imprisoned Ukrainian Trotskyist Bogdan Syrotiuk has concluded that they contain “no direct calls for undermining national security” and no support for Russian aggression — further demolishing the prosecution’s state-treason charges. Despite this, Ukrainian authorities have prolonged his detention and continue to deny him basic dental care. Weiss frames the case as pure political persecution of a socialist for truthful criticism of the Zelenskyy government, NATO and the historical rehabilitation of Ukrainian fascism — a case Western mainstream media still refuses to cover.

Reckless disembarkation of hantavirus-stricken MV Hondius exposes collapse of public health — Benjamin Mateus

Mateus reports details absent from mainstream coverage: WHO was notified 21 days after the first death aboard the MV Hondius; the CDC overrode WHO guidance and rejected quarantine protocols; a French passenger developed symptoms mid-flight after disembarkation; earlier disembarkations at Saint Helena and Tristan da Cunha occurred without screening and produced deaths in those isolated island populations. Passengers were ultimately sent to 12 countries without quarantine. The article frames the response as capitalism prioritizing cruise-tourism revenue over disease containment — a structural pattern visible since the 2020 Diamond Princess and Ruby Princess incidents.

Defend every job at VW! — Sozialistische Gleichheitspartei (SGP)

VW management plans closures at Emden, Zwickau, Hanover and Neckarsulm affecting 40,000 workers — exceeding the 35,000 cuts already agreed with IG Metall in December 2024 — even as VW revenue grew from €254 billion to €322 billion between 2020 and 2025. The SGP statement charges that IG Metall is now facilitating the conversion of auto plants to weapons production, calls for independent rank-and-file action committees, and frames the fight as inseparable from socialist opposition to German rearmament.

Rank-and-file rebellion and the unity of the working class — Will Lehman

UAW rank-and-file presidential candidate and IWA-RFC member Will Lehman, delivering a speech at the 2026 May Day Online Rally, catalogues nurses’, teachers’ and auto workers’ actions, argues that union bureaucracies consistently undermine resistance through sellout contracts and cancelled strikes, and demands the release of imprisoned labour activists worldwide — explicitly including Bogdan Syrotiuk. The speech frames the rejection of capitalist war and inflation as a single political task with workers’ rank-and-file independence from union officialdom.

Türkiye, the war on Iran and the resurgence of the class struggle — Ulaş Sevinç

Sevinç, chairman of the Socialist Equality Party (Fourth International) in Turkey, documents the April 2026 Doruk Mining workers’ 190-kilometre march on Ankara demanding unpaid wages, the Turkish state’s repression of labour leaders (Başaran Aksu, Mehmet Türkmen and Esra Işık face imprisonment), and the Erdoğan government’s alignment with the US-Israeli war on Iran via the Riyadh Declaration — despite more than 90% of the Turkish population opposing the war. Iranian figures cited: 3,375+ dead, 376+ children killed, 3+ million internally displaced. The conclusion calls for the IWA-RFC to unite Turkish workers — including the roughly 5,000 Migros warehouse workers on wildcat strike — with the international working class.

Jacobin / DSA

Three pieces dominated Jacobin’s output in the past 24 hours. “How to Explain the Inexplicable War in Iran?” offers an anti-war framing centred on ruling-class profiteering. It is correct in opposing the intervention but stops at the level of “who benefits,” declining to advance a programmatic line oriented to either the Iranian or American working class. “Wartime Iran’s Political Transformation” argues that the war has entrenched a comprador oil-dollar elite inside Iran and imposed permanent austerity on the population — analytically useful but agitationally inert.

The piece that most clearly reveals Jacobin/DSA’s class character is “We Need a Left-Labor Presidential Candidate.” Written by DSA co-chairs, it calls for a united “left-labor” 2028 presidential run “to put a working-class program on the national stage.” The proposal is explicit national-reformism: it is structured around pressuring or flanking the Democratic Party rather than building an independent class party. Given DSA’s record of running candidates exclusively on the Democratic ballot line (Bowman, Lee, AOC, the entire elected DSA cohort), the structural independence of any such 2028 campaign is essentially a fiction — it will function as a left tributary into the Democratic primary process.

Left Voice

Left Voice, the US periodical of the FT/PTS-affiliated Revolutionary Socialist Organization, ran three notable pieces. “Biggest No Kings Demonstration Ever Brings Millions to the Streets” reports favourably on the mass anti-Trump demonstrations while making clear that LV members marched with banners explicitly demanding abolition of ICE, opposition to the Iran war and an end to Cuba sanctions — pushing the protest movement beyond generic anti-Trumpism. “Why Young People Joined the Latest No Kings Protests” is a sociological/agitational piece on the radicalization of youth, drawing connections between Trump’s authoritarianism, Gaza and economic precarity. “All Roads Lead to Stagflation” argues that neither Trump’s tariff regime nor a hypothetical Democratic restoration offers an exit from the stagflationary crisis — and refuses to endorse either bourgeois option.

Left Voice maintained the clearest formal line of any US tendency in this 24-hour window against support for either bourgeois party. Its limitation remains its localism — the absence of a programmatic relationship to an international working-class revolutionary movement of the kind constituted by the ICFI.

Liberation News / PSL

“PSL Statement: Why Trump Failed in His Criminal War Against Iran” correctly opposes the US-Israeli war but frames Iran’s resistance as a “people’s victory” — eliding the class character of the Islamic Republic and its repression of Iranian workers, women and political prisoners. This is textbook campism: anti-imperialism collapsed into solidarity with the existing capitalist state of the targeted country, mirroring the “anti-imperialist” national-reformism of late Stalinism.

“ICE and Tennessee Police Terrorize Nashville Immigrants in Mass Raid” is solid reporting on state repression and a strong defence of immigrant workers, framed around community defence rather than electoral pressure. “Trump’s Budget Bill: A Huge Gift to Billionaires” correctly exposes the class content of the reconciliation bill — military expansion financed by gutting housing, health and education — but advances no independent socialist programme beyond opposition.

Socialist Alternative

“Only Rotten Choices In Their System — We Need a Planned Economy” (May 9) acknowledges that 71% of Americans still prefer the free market despite anti-corporate sentiment and argues that Trump’s military budget confirms the case for a planned economy. “The Democrats Are Not the Answer to Fight the Right” (May 8) is an explicit argument for independent working-class politics. The editorial line is formally correct, but should be read against SA’s earlier practice of running Kshama Sawant on the Democratic ballot line; whether the 2026 rhetoric matches the practice remains an open question.

Socialist Worker (SWP UK)

The SWP devoted significant coverage to Reform UK’s local-election surge. “Elections 2026: Reform UK Surges as Mainstream Politics Fractures” treats the fracturing as an opening for the left. “An Opportunity Missed: Your Party, Independents and the 2026 Elections” criticizes the failure of left independents (including “Your Party,” the post-Corbyn formation) to cohere into a genuine workers’-party challenge. “‘We’ll Keep Fighting Reform UK’ Say Anti-Racists” mobilizes the SWP-led Stand Up To Racism front against Reform. The repeated problem: SUTR has consistently shared platforms with Labour politicians and has functioned to channel anti-far-right energy into defending an austerity-and-genocide-complicit Labour government, rather than building an independent socialist alternative. The editorial line gestures at independence; the practice continues to subordinate class politics to broad-front anti-fascism.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The Revolutionary Communist Party (former Socialist Appeal, IMT-affiliated, formally broken from Labour entryism in 2024) published “British Perspectives 2026: Crisis, Instability, and Radicalisation” — a Central Committee document arguing British capitalism faces a structural crisis producing mass radicalisation, and calling for accelerated RCP recruitment. The party also continues a day-by-day General Strike centenary series drawing lessons about the TUC’s 1926 betrayal — historically useful, and an implicit critique of the contemporary union bureaucracy. Their election post-mortem characterizes the Labour rout as a defeat for both Labour and the Tories and a sign of systemic crisis, without calling for any Labour vote — consistent with the post-2024 break. The political culture of decades of Labour entryism, however, runs deep, and the RCP’s organizational independence will be tested by future electoral conjunctures.
