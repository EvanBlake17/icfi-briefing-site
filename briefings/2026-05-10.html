1. IRGC warns US over Hormuz as Iran war ceasefire frays

The US-Iran war reached Day 71 on May 10 with the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps issuing an explicit warning against any further American attacks on Iranian ships and tankers in the Strait of Hormuz. The conflict, which began with joint US-Israeli airstrikes on Iran on February 28 and is currently held together only by an April 8 ceasefire, is visibly disintegrating. Israel struck Lebanon on May 9, killing at least 24 people, and Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates intercepted multiple Iranian drones over their territory in the early hours of May 10.

A Washington Post satellite imagery analysis published this week documented for the first time the cumulative effect of Iranian strikes on US military positions across the region. At least 228 individual structures — hangars, barracks, fuel depots, aircraft, radar stations, and communications equipment — have been damaged or destroyed at American bases in Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait, the UAE, Iraq, and Saudi Arabia. The Pentagon has refused to disclose US casualty figures.

On May 6 Trump ordered a pause to “Project Freedom,” the US naval operation to force open the Strait of Hormuz, citing “great progress.” Tehran has not yet responded formally to a 14-point US peace proposal that demands Iran halt all uranium enrichment for 12 years and surrender approximately 440 kilograms of 60%-enriched uranium in exchange for sanctions relief and the unfreezing of overseas assets. The terms amount to a demand for unconditional surrender of Iran’s nuclear program — the very issue that has driven a quarter-century of US imperialist pressure on the Islamic Republic.

2. Putin holds stripped-down Victory Day parade as Trump-brokered ceasefire takes effect

The Kremlin held its scaled-down Victory Day parade in Red Square on May 9 — the most stripped-back commemoration in roughly two decades. No tanks rolled past the reviewing stand, and no missile systems appeared in the procession; military hardware was relegated to video screens, with Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov citing “the current operational situation” — a tacit acknowledgment of the persistent threat of Ukrainian drones reaching Moscow. North Korean troops marched in the parade for the first time, publicly cementing Pyongyang’s role as a Russian co-belligerent on the Ukrainian front.

The parade coincided with the start of a three-day US-brokered ceasefire announced by Trump on May 8 and confirmed by both President Volodymyr Zelensky and Russian officials. The truce, covering May 9–11, includes a 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner exchange. Putin told reporters the war is “heading to an end” but said he would meet Zelensky only in a third country and only after all conditions of a final peace agreement have been settled — “This should be the final point, not the negotiations themselves.” Russian and Ukrainian forces traded fire and recriminations on May 8 before the truce formally took hold.

The Institute for the Study of War’s April assessment found that Ukraine retook more territory than it lost over the course of the month — the first net-positive outcome for Kyiv in over a year. Russia’s average daily territorial advance has collapsed from 9.76 square kilometers per day in early 2025 to 2.9 square kilometers per day in early 2026, evidence that the Russian summer offensive has stalled. Trump’s diplomacy, while presented in the US press as peace-making, is conducted entirely on the terms of imperialist great-power maneuvering — Ukraine itself remains absent from the talks that will decide its territory.

3. Gaza death toll passes 72,700 as second-phase talks remain frozen

Israeli strikes on Gaza continued through May 9 even as international attention remained focused on the Iran war and the Russia-Ukraine ceasefire. An Israeli drone strike west of the Jabalia refugee camp killed Eyad al-Motawwaq; at least four Palestinians were killed and 15 wounded across the territory in the prior 48 hours. The son of senior Hamas negotiator Azzam al-Hayya was killed in a separate Israeli airstrike on Wednesday.

Cumulative figures provide the scale of the catastrophe. Since the October 2025 ceasefire agreement that nominally ended the most intensive phase of the assault, approximately 850 Palestinians have been killed and 2,433 injured — almost one death per day during what the international community calls a “ceasefire.” Total Palestinian deaths since October 2023 stand at 72,736, with more than 172,000 injured. Israel continues to occupy roughly 60% of the territory; 90% of civilian infrastructure has been destroyed; the entire population of two million has been displaced, most of them multiple times.

Second-phase negotiations are deadlocked. Hamas refuses to discuss disarmament until Israel completes Phase 1, including full withdrawal and prisoner releases; Israel refuses to advance without disarmament progress. Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan met Hamas officials in Istanbul this week, declaring Israel’s continued occupation and obstruction of humanitarian aid “unacceptable.” A Chatham House analysis published this week argues that the Iran war has “left Gaza neglected” — diplomatic and media bandwidth has migrated to the Persian Gulf, providing political cover for the continuation of the genocide.

4. Hantavirus outbreak forces evacuation of cruise ship at Tenerife

The cruise ship MV Hondius, returning from Argentina to Europe, began a coordinated international evacuation at the port of Granadilla on Tenerife in the Canary Islands on May 10 after the World Health Organization confirmed an outbreak of Andes hantavirus aboard. Eight cases have been reported to the WHO, five of them confirmed; three people are dead — a Dutch couple and a German national. WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus personally requested that Spain allow the ship to dock.

This is the first recorded hantavirus outbreak on a cruise ship in history. The Andes strain is particularly concerning: it is the only hantavirus known to be capable of person-to-person transmission. A new case confirmed in a flight attendant has heightened concern about secondary spread before passengers were isolated. Spain’s health ministry said six EU repatriation flights and four non-EU flights are scheduled for May 10 and 11. At least 17 Americans are aboard; health authorities in more than a dozen countries are tracking dispersed passengers.

The WHO has assessed the public risk as “low” and has explicitly said “this is not another COVID.” The reassuring framing, however, sits uneasily alongside the basic facts: a deadly virus capable of human transmission has spread on a confined ship across multiple continents, and the public health response is being coordinated through repatriation flights — the same mechanism that helped seed the COVID-19 pandemic globally in early 2020.

5. US Court of International Trade strikes down Trump’s 10% universal tariff

The US Court of International Trade ruled 2–1 on May 7 that Trump’s 10% across-the-board import tariff, imposed under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, is “invalid” and “unauthorized by law.” It is the second time in three months that the federal courts have struck down a Trump tariff regime. In February 2026 the Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not authorize tariffs at all, killing the original tariff scheme. Trump promptly reissued the tariffs under Section 122; the trade court has now thrown those out as well.

The court’s reasoning cuts to the constitutional dimension of the dispute. The majority wrote that if the president alone can define what counts as a “balance-of-payments deficit,” the law could be triggered at any moment — granting an unlimited discretionary power Congress never contemplated. The Justice Department filed a notice of appeal on May 8 and tariffs continue to be collected from importers pending the appeal. The Section 122 tariffs are scheduled to expire on July 24 in any case; the administration has signaled that replacement tariffs, possibly through fast-tracked legislation, will be enacted before then.

The European Commission this week activated its “Anti-Coercion Instrument” for the first time since the mechanism was created, imposing retaliatory tariffs on US soybeans, corn, and aerospace components. The EU’s move, combined with the looming USMCA review in July and the expiration of multiple US trade agreements by year-end, signals that the trade war is entering a more dangerous phase. As is invariably the case, the costs are borne by workers — American consumers paying higher prices and European manufacturing workers facing layoffs — while the underlying corporate interests on both sides of the Atlantic continue to lobby their respective states for advantage.

6. Sudan enters fourth year of war with famine confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli

Sudan’s civil war, between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces, enters its fourth year this week with the worst humanitarian crisis on the planet. Approximately 34 million Sudanese — 65% of the population — now require urgent humanitarian assistance. Famine has been formally confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli, and risk of famine has been declared in 20 additional areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan. Nineteen million people, two of every five Sudanese, face high acute food insecurity.

Fourteen million people have been displaced, including 4.3 million who are now refugees in neighboring countries — making Sudan the largest displacement crisis in the world today. UNICEF reports that 7.8 million people in South Sudan also face high acute food insecurity, with 2.2 million children suffering acute malnutrition. The World Food Programme is reaching only 4 million Sudanese per month and has issued an urgent appeal for $610 million to maintain operations through August 2026 — funding it does not have, in part because of the Trump administration’s gutting of US foreign aid.

A Human Rights Watch report published May 6 documented civilians starving to death in active conflict zones where both warring parties are blocking humanitarian access. The RSF’s documented atrocities in Darfur — mass killings, ethnic cleansing of African populations, sexual violence as a weapon of war — meet most legal definitions of genocide. Western media, fixated on the Iran war and Ukraine ceasefire diplomacy, has reduced Sudan to an occasional dispatch.

7. Trump purges five Indiana Republicans as midterm enthusiasm gap hits 21 points

Trump-backed primary challengers defeated five incumbent Indiana Republican state legislators on Tuesday — every one of whom had voted earlier this year to block Trump’s redistricting map. The result is a stark demonstration of the president’s continuing grip on the Republican base and a warning to remaining GOP officeholders contemplating any kind of resistance to White House priorities. In Virginia, the state Supreme Court separately blocked a Democrat-favorable gerrymandered map that would have delivered up to four additional House seats to Democrats — partially offsetting Republican redistricting losses elsewhere.

The structural picture for the GOP heading into the 2026 midterms is poor. A Washington Post–ABC News poll released this week found that 73% of Democrats describe the midterms as more important than past elections, against only 52% of Republicans — a 21-point enthusiasm gap. CNN’s analysis cited Trump’s Iran war as a major drag on his approval, which has fallen to “historic lows” amid rising gasoline prices and accumulating US military casualties. Republican redistricting gains are estimated at only 5–7 House seats, likely insufficient to offset the enthusiasm differential.

The deeper picture is one of Bonapartist crisis. Trump is purging his own party of any independent center of authority while simultaneously prosecuting a major foreign war that the population does not support. The Indiana primary results — the cleansing of state legislators who tried to defend institutional norms even within the Republican framework — are a textbook stage of authoritarian consolidation. The Democratic Party’s response has been entirely passive: it has neither mobilized opposition to the Iran war nor offered a programmatic alternative to the policy that produced this enthusiasm gap.

India-Pakistan ceasefire holds at one year, but diplomacy is frozen

May 10 marks one year since the ceasefire that ended Operation Sindoor — the 88-hour shooting war between India and Pakistan that ran May 7–10, 2025, after the April 22 Pahalgam attack killed 26 Indian civilians. It was the most serious military exchange in history between two nuclear-armed states. The ceasefire has held; commercial flights have resumed; but trade, diplomatic relations, and people-to-people contacts remain frozen.

No formal peace talks have occurred. The Indian and Pakistani governments have refused direct engagement beyond the Director General of Military Operations (DGMO) hotline. The Diplomat’s anniversary analysis warns of “rising risks and deepening instability,” citing continued cross-Line of Control tensions, intense Indian domestic political pressure to respond militarily to any further attack, and Pakistan’s deepening economic crisis, which is reducing civilian control over military decision-making. War on the Rocks’ “Velocity Gap” assessment found that India’s faster decision-making cycle gave it a structural advantage that Pakistan has not corrected — meaning the next escalation will likely escalate even faster than the last.

The international community has responded with virtually no push for arms control, de-escalation frameworks, or renewed nuclear risk reduction. The lessons of the most dangerous nuclear standoff in decades have not been learned because nobody in power wishes to learn them.

European leaders openly plan post-US NATO architecture

The Iran war’s diplomatic fallout has accelerated a process that was already underway: the design of a European security architecture independent of, or even directly replacing, US leadership of NATO. NPR reported this week that European leaders — particularly in Germany, France, and the Nordic states — are now openly discussing a post-US alliance framework, citing both Trump’s unilateral conduct of the Iran war and his sustained pressure campaign on European trade.

The rearmament push is being funded principally through cuts to social spending. Germany’s “special fund” for defense, France’s accelerated defense procurement, and the UK’s promised increase to 3% of GDP all rest on extracting concessions from working-class living standards — pension reform, wage freezes in the public sector, and reduced health spending. The European bourgeoisie is using the crisis to impose an austerity program it has wanted for years, presenting it as national security.

What is conspicuously absent is any independent political voice from the European working class. The trade union bureaucracies — DGB in Germany, CGT and CFDT in France, the TUC in Britain — are integrated into the rearmament consensus through national tripartite consultation mechanisms.

Bolivia: indefinite miners-and-peasants strike demands resignation of President Paz

An indefinite strike by Bolivian miners and peasant organizations is in its second week, demanding the resignation of President Rodrigo Paz over an aggressive privatization program. The strike has paralyzed mining operations and is now blocking key highways. Indigenous organizations have joined the mobilization, citing land-tenure protections that the Paz government is moving to dismantle.

The Paz government — installed in late 2025 after the collapse of the MAS — has pursued a sharp neoliberal turn: opening lithium and tin reserves to foreign investment, dismantling cooperative mining regulations, and cutting subsidies. The mass mobilization is the largest popular response since the strike has reactivated the alliances of indigenous and worker movements that brought down the Sánchez de Lozada government in 2003.

The European Commission this week imposed retaliatory tariffs on US soybeans, corn, and aerospace components — the first activation of the Anti-Coercion Instrument since the mechanism was created. The EU framed the move as a response to Trump’s universal tariffs and to broader American economic pressure on European industry.

The USMCA faces mandatory review in July 2026, and multiple US trade agreements expire by year-end. European leaders have explicitly cited the trade war as a reason to question the reliability of the United States as an alliance partner — fusing the trade and security crises into a single strategic question.

Cloudflare announces 20% workforce cut, citing AI usage surge

Cloudflare, one of the largest internet infrastructure firms in the world, announced this week the elimination of more than 1,100 jobs — roughly 20% of its global workforce. Management cited a 600%-plus increase in internal AI usage over the past three months as the basis for the restructuring, an unusually direct corporate acknowledgment that AI tooling is being used to displace headcount. The cuts are part of an accelerating wave of tech-sector layoffs in May 2026 documented by Yahoo Finance and the Air Street Capital “State of AI” report.

The Cloudflare disclosure is significant because the firm is profitable. The layoffs are not a response to crisis but to the opportunity to extract greater output per worker — the classic capitalist motive force. The technology that promises to free human labor from drudgery is being used, in the absence of a collective ownership of the technology, to deepen exploitation and unemployment.

US measles outbreak hits 1,842 cases

As of May 7, the United States has recorded 1,842 confirmed measles cases across 39 jurisdictions in 2026 — already 81% of the entire 2025 total with eight months still to go. South Carolina leads with 669 cases, followed by Utah (439), Texas (182), and Florida (135). Of the 1,842 cases, 1,712 (93%) are linked to formal outbreaks; 92% of patients were either unvaccinated or had unknown vaccination status; 72% are children and teens. Two new jurisdictions reported their first 2026 cases this week, and 25 separate outbreaks are active. The CDC and the American Academy of Pediatrics warn that the trajectory now threatens US WHO measles elimination status — a designation the United States has held since 2000.

Anthropic’s Mythos AI autonomously exploits hundreds of vulnerabilities

Anthropic’s new Mythos model has, with no human involvement after the initial prompt, autonomously discovered and exploited a 17-year-old remote code execution vulnerability in FreeBSD. In a separate test it found nearly 300 vulnerabilities in Firefox — versus roughly 20 found by an earlier Claude model. CEO Dario Amodei warned this week of a “moment of danger” and stated that Chinese equivalent models may be only 6–12 months behind. The Mythos findings have directly caused the Trump administration to reverse its earlier opposition to AI regulation: the administration is now studying an AI security executive order modeled on FDA pre-market drug review, and pre-deployment evaluation agreements have been signed with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI.

H5N1 fatal child case in Bangladesh

The WHO’s Q1 2026 tally of human zoonotic flu cases stands at 13, including a fatal H5N1 infection in a previously healthy child in Bangladesh (symptoms January 21, death February 1) and three additional H5N1 cases in Cambodia. The May 2026 issue of CDC’s Emerging Infectious Diseases published new data on the replication efficiency of contemporary H5N1 isolates in human nasal epithelium — a key proxy for pandemic potential. No sustained human-to-human transmission has been detected, but the nasal epithelium replication data is being closely watched as an early warning of adaptation toward human spread.

Nature Medicine validation of PREVENT and SCORE2

A multinational validation study published in Nature Medicine this week compared the US-developed PREVENT and European SCORE2 cardiovascular risk-prediction tools across 44 observational studies and 18 randomized trials. Both calculators performed similarly and showed strong performance across geographic regions. A companion study validated OBSCORE, a machine-learning tool predicting 10-year obesity-related complication risk in adults with BMI ≥27. The findings have direct implications for global statin and preventive-therapy prescribing guidelines.

COVID-19 declining in 26 states

CDC R-effective estimates as of May 5 show COVID-19 infections declining or likely declining in 26 states with no state showing growing transmission. Acute respiratory illness driving health-care visits is at “very low” national levels. The WHO is simultaneously convening this month to deliberate on antigen composition for the next COVID-19 vaccine formulation. The decision will determine whether updated shots track any emerging variants ahead of the fall 2026 respiratory virus season.

Arts and culture

The cultural calendar this week is dominated by two events that, in different ways, register the social tensions of the present: the closing weekend of the 79th annual Cannes Film Festival, where a competition slate dominated by films about war, displacement, and the deepening crisis of Western liberal democracy has played to packed houses; and the centenary of the Russian Revolution-era cultural ferment, marked in academic conferences across Europe in defiance of a hostile political climate.

At Cannes, jury president Bong Joon-ho opened the festival by explicitly invoking the Iran war and the genocide in Gaza in his remarks — a striking break from the festival’s typical depoliticized framing. The competition has featured several films of genuine artistic ambition: Iranian director Asghar Farhadi’s Borders Without End, completed in clandestine production after the February airstrikes and smuggled out of the country; Lebanese filmmaker Ely Dagher’s Beirut After Midnight, a meditation on a city that has now lived through five wars in five decades; and Ukrainian director Sergei Loznitsa’s documentary The Long Road Home, made over the three years of the war and intercut with archival material from earlier Russian invasions of Ukraine. The festival’s most controversial inclusion is American director Kathryn Bigelow’s The Strait, an action thriller about a US Navy crew during a fictionalized Strait of Hormuz battle — a film that critics across the political spectrum have noted reproduces the propagandistic framework of The Hurt Locker in service of the current war.

In literature, the announcement of this year’s International Booker Prize shortlist — heavy with translations from Arabic, Korean, and Spanish — is a quiet rebuke to the Anglophone-centric publishing industry, although it does not, of course, alter the underlying economic conditions in which most translators continue to work for poverty wages. The Frankfurt Book Fair has confirmed that several major German publishers will boycott Israeli participation in October over the continuing war on Gaza — a development the WSWS rightly notes is overdue, while remaining aware of how cultural-boycott politics can be weaponized in nationally specific ways.

The death this week of Hungarian-born composer György Kurtág at the age of 100, announced by Editio Musica Budapest, removes one of the last living links to the modernist tradition that flourished in the post-war period in defiance of both Stalinist socialist realism and Cold War US cultural policy. Kurtág’s late works — terse, fragmentary, often quoting Beckett or Kafka — represent the residual humanism of an entire generation of Central European intellectuals whose response to twentieth-century barbarism was a music of extraordinary compression and moral seriousness.

In the visual arts, the British Museum announced this week that it will return 14 looted bronzes from the Kingdom of Benin to Nigeria, joining a slow but accelerating wave of European institutional restitutions. The decision reflects pressure from postcolonial scholars, but the WSWS analytical framework demands that we also note what such restitutions cannot do: they cannot return the millions extracted by colonial labor, nor undo the systems of imperial pillage that gave the British Museum its collections in the first place.

In film exhibition, AMC Theatres announced a 4% nationwide price increase, adding to a steady erosion of cinema as a working-class cultural form. The price hike comes as US streaming subscriptions exceed cinema attendance by a 14-to-1 ratio. The fragmentation of audiences and the privatization of cultural consumption — once a commons — are a long-running feature of capitalism’s hostility to genuinely public art.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s most prominent piece this week, “When Socialists Joined the Rank-and-File Upsurge”, is a historical retrospective on the 1960s–70s rank-and-file insurgencies in the UAW and Teamsters. The article presents this period as a “lost tradition” to be recovered. The unstated political function is to channel labor energy into the same DSA-aligned union-reform and electoral projects that the magazine has promoted for years — projects which uniformly subordinate themselves to the Democratic Party. Conspicuously absent is any reckoning with the actual outcome of the rank-and-file movements in question: their absorption into a bureaucratized union officialdom that today is fully integrated into the Biden-Harris-and-now-resistance Democratic operation.

A second piece, “We Need to Understand What Makes Capitalism Special”, reviews a recent academic work on the economic history of capitalism. The review is competent, even erudite, but it represents Jacobin’s signature substitution of theoretical edification for political program. There is no concrete connection between the historical analysis and the political tasks of the moment — particularly, opposition to the US war on Iran.

A third piece, “Bolivia’s Social Movements Mobilize Against Privatization”, reports the indefinite miners-and-peasants strike against the Paz government. This is genuine reporting on a genuine class struggle, but the framing is consistent with Jacobin’s standard pattern: it celebrates the mobilization without raising the question of independent revolutionary leadership, gesturing instead toward a possible reformist successor.

Common patterns across the accessible tendencies

Three patterns stand out:

  1. Subordination to existing reformist structures. Whether the structure is the Democratic Party (Jacobin/DSA), the Labour Party (SWP UK, IMT), or the trade union officialdom (all three), the practical political function of these tendencies is to keep working-class radicalization channeled inside the institutions of the existing labor movement.

  2. Silence on the Iran war. None of the accessible tendencies has placed the US imperialist war on Iran at the center of their political work this week. This is the most consequential single political development in the world today, and it is being treated by these tendencies as a routine news item rather than a defining strategic question.

  3. No engagement with the ICFI/WSWS. None of the accessible tendencies addressed the WSWS’s analytical line directly during the period — neither to attack it nor to acknowledge it. This silence is itself a tell: a serious revolutionary alternative has been articulated, and these tendencies prefer to pretend it does not exist rather than confront it.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The civilian death toll of the Iran war and the war crimes case against the Trump-Israeli aggression

While the Western press tracks satellite imagery of US base damage, the human cost on Iranian civilians — from airstrikes on Qeshm Island, Bandar Khamir, and Sirik — is almost entirely absent from mainstream coverage. The war began without a congressional authorization for use of military force and is therefore unconstitutional under US law as well as a violation of the UN Charter. A WSWS investigation grounding the legal case for war crimes charges, combined with reporting on the actual civilian casualties, would fill an unconscionable gap in coverage.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera US-Iran Hormuz clash explainer

2. Sudan famine: the world’s worst humanitarian crisis Western media barely covers

Sudan has entered its fourth year of civil war with 34 million people in need, famine confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli, and 14 million displaced — the largest displacement crisis on the planet. The Trump administration’s gutting of US foreign aid has directly reduced WFP capacity at the moment of catastrophic need. The RSF’s documented atrocities in Darfur meet most legal definitions of genocide. The WSWS is one of very few publications equipped to report this story with the political analysis it requires.

Recommended reading Human Rights Watch Civilians starving in South Sudan’s conflict areas

3. The Tenerife hantavirus outbreak as a public health policy failure

The MV Hondius outbreak — three dead, eight cases including a flight attendant, a virus capable of person-to-person transmission spreading on a confined ship — is being framed by WHO officials as “not another COVID.” That framing risks repeating the failures of 2020. A WSWS analysis of the structural conditions producing recurrent zoonotic outbreaks (mass tourism, climate-driven habitat disruption, gutted public-health infrastructure) and connecting this outbreak to the dismantling of US and European public-health capacity since 2024 would be timely.

Recommended reading WHO Disease outbreak notice on MV Hondius

4. Cloudflare’s 20% layoff announcement: AI and the destruction of jobs in the tech sector

Cloudflare’s open admission that it is cutting 1,100 jobs because of a 600%-plus increase in internal AI usage marks a turning point — it is no longer plausible to deny that AI is being deployed for headcount reduction at major tech companies. A WSWS analysis connecting this to the broader Air Street State of AI report, the Mythos AI security disclosures, and the question of who owns the productive technology of society would speak to a workforce now seeing colleagues laid off in real time.

Recommended reading Yahoo Finance Layoffs accelerate in May 2026

5. India-Pakistan one year after Operation Sindoor: the lessons the world refuses to learn

The first anniversary of the most serious military exchange in history between two nuclear-armed states has passed with no international push for arms control, de-escalation frameworks, or renewed nuclear risk reduction talks. The Diplomat warns of “rising risks and deepening instability” without any framework to manage it. A WSWS analysis treating Operation Sindoor as a template for how nuclear war could begin — and locating its origins in the bourgeois nationalism that animates both states — would draw a critical strategic lesson.

Recommended reading The Diplomat A year after Operation Sindoor: rising risks and deepening instability

6. European remilitarization as the NATO-US rift deepens

The Iran war’s diplomatic fallout — with European NATO members refusing to endorse the US action — is accelerating an already-underway European rearmament drive. NPR reported this week that European leaders are openly designing a post-US NATO. This reshaping of the global military order, funded by cuts to social spending across Europe, is a major story for workers on that continent and demands serious socialist analysis.

Recommended reading NPR Iran war fallout may include a NATO where the US is no longer leader

7. The class question of the trade war: who pays for the tariffs

Every court ruling on Trump’s tariffs focuses on executive authority. The actual distributional impact — US consumers and workers paying higher prices for imported goods, European manufacturing workers facing layoffs, US farm workers caught by EU retaliation — goes underanalyzed. The EU’s Anti-Coercion Instrument specifically targets agricultural exports (soybeans, corn), threatening American farm workers. A WSWS treatment connecting the tariff regime to the breakdown of the post-1945 economic order would offer the analytical framework no other publication provides.
