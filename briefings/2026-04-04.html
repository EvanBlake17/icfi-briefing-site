1. US-Iran war enters week 5 as two US aircraft downed and Iran strikes Gulf refineries

The US-Iran war marked its deadliest day for American aircraft losses on April 3 as Iranian air defenses shot down an F-15E Strike Eagle over western Iran — the first US combat aircraft lost to Iranian fire — and a second aircraft went down near the Strait of Hormuz. US special forces launched a ground rescue operation inside Iranian territory, recovering one crew member alive; the fate of the second remains unknown. An A-10 Thunderbolt was also downed and two rescue helicopters struck. As Andre Damon reported on the WSWS, US military officials have acknowledged approaching “target saturation” despite 12,000+ combined US-Israeli strikes, with approximately half of Iran’s ballistic missile launchers still operational.

Key facts: - US-Israeli strikes have killed at least 2,076 Iranians and wounded 26,500 since February 28; 13 US service members killed in combat - A US “double tap” airstrike on the B1 suspension bridge in Karaj killed 8 people and wounded nearly 100, including emergency workers responding to the initial strike - US-Israeli strikes destroyed the Pasteur Institute of Iran, a century-old medical research center; Iran’s Red Crescent reports 113,000+ civilian sites hit and 1 million families displaced - Trump threatened to attack Iranian power plants and desalination facilities “by next week” if the Strait of Hormuz is not reopened - Iranian forces struck Kuwait’s Mina Al Ahmadi refinery (346,000 bpd capacity) and Abu Dhabi’s largest natural gas facility; Iran also targeted Amazon cloud infrastructure in Bahrain and an Oracle data center in Dubai - Approximately 7,500 additional Marines and an 82nd Airborne brigade are arriving in the Persian Gulf, joining 50,000+ already deployed - The US has spent a minimum of $25 billion on the conflict; a near-total internet blackout affects 90 million Iranians

2. Oil tops $110 as Strait of Hormuz closure triggers global energy crisis

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil and LNG transits — has produced what the International Energy Agency characterized as the “largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.” Brent Crude hit approximately $112/barrel and WTI reached $102, up from $59 at the start of 2026. US pump prices passed $4/gallon for the first time since 2022. Wall Street analysts are now modeling scenarios where oil hits $200/barrel if the closure persists.

Key facts: - The UK is coordinating with 40 nations on reopening the Strait; Trump told allies to “get your own oil,” refusing to lead a multilateral effort - Trump posted on Truth Social threatening to seize the Strait of Hormuz and “take the oil” - Trump announced a 10-day pause on strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure on April 3, producing a brief relief rally - Oil is up roughly 60% since the conflict began; Brent crude has nearly doubled from start-of-year levels - The crisis is devastating import-dependent economies in South Asia and East Africa with no strategic reserves or leverage

7. Artemis II crew departs Earth orbit on first crewed lunar mission since 1972

Artemis II launched April 1 from Kennedy Space Center, and on April 2 the Orion spacecraft completed its translunar injection burn — a 5-minute, 50-second engine firing that placed it on a lunar flyby trajectory. The four-person crew will loop around the Moon on April 6 and splash down off San Diego shortly after, completing the first crewed mission near the Moon since Apollo 17 in December 1972.

Key facts: - Crew: Reid Wiseman (commander), Victor Glover (first person of color beyond low Earth orbit), Christina Koch (first woman beyond LEO), Jeremy Hansen (first non-US citizen) - The mission is a 10-day lunar flyby — a precursor to Artemis III’s planned landing - The mission launches during an active war in which US bombers are simultaneously striking Iranian infrastructure — a juxtaposition receiving minimal critical commentary in mainstream coverage

US measles cases reach 1,671 in 2026

The CDC reported 1,671 confirmed measles cases across 33 jurisdictions as of April 2 — a figure tracking toward surpassing 2025’s toll of 2,286 cases, which was already the worst year for measles in over three decades. Ninety-four percent of cases (1,570) are outbreak-associated, with South Carolina alone logging approximately 990 cases centered on Spartanburg County. The crisis unfolds as the Trump-Kennedy administration actively dismantles vaccine infrastructure: national kindergarten MMR coverage has fallen to 92.5%, with 39 states below the 95% herd immunity threshold.

As Benjamin Mateus detailed on the WSWS, the administration is purging vaccine skeptics into the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) and pushing for MMR component separation — a strategy designed to lower overall immunization rates. Globally, 19 countries lack routine rubella vaccination, risking an estimated 986,000 congenital rubella syndrome cases between 2025 and 2055.

Arts and culture

The arts and cultural landscape this week is shaped by two intersecting forces: the ongoing destruction of federal arts funding infrastructure under Trump, and a notable cluster of theatrical and cinematic work engaging directly with political repression, class conflict, and historical memory.

Sacco and Vanzetti on stage as political repression intensifies

Curio Theatre Company in Philadelphia opened Sacco & Vanzetti: A Tragedia dell’Arte on April 4 — a world premiere by John Bellomo that revisits the infamous 1920 arrest, trial, and 1927 execution of Italian immigrant anarchists Nicola Sacco and Bartolomeo Vanzetti through the physical vocabulary of commedia dell’arte. The production arrives at a moment when the parallels to the original case — anti-immigrant hysteria, the criminalization of political dissent, show trials driven by prejudice rather than evidence — require no annotation. The case was one of the defining political persecutions of the twentieth century, provoking international protests and exposing the class character of American “justice.” That a small Philadelphia company is staging this material as ICE officers patrol airports and the Attorney General’s office is handed to the president’s personal lawyer speaks to the instinct of artists to reach for historical precedent in moments of democratic crisis.

Wallace Shawn’s The Fever and the political turn of celebrity radicalism

Wallace Shawn is currently performing his 1990 one-man show The Fever off-Broadway at 27 Barrow Street (closing April 26), alongside his new play What We Did Before Our Moth Days directed by André Gregory. The Fever — described by the Guardian as “an elaborate denunciation of global capitalism” — follows a nameless narrator who, confined to a squalid hotel room in a poor nation, grapples with his own complicity in global inequality. Shawn performed the piece in January as a fundraiser for New York City DSA’s “Tax the Rich” campaign. Jacobin published a profile this week framing Shawn’s trajectory as a “road to socialism,” though the piece exemplifies the tendency’s characteristic elevation of celebrity radicalism over working-class organization.

Berlinale retrospective confronts post-reunification disillusionment

The 76th Berlin International Film Festival’s retrospective “Lost in the 90s” — reviewed on the WSWS by Bernd Reinhardt and Verena Nees — deliberately selected films depicting social insecurity and post-reunification disillusionment rather than the triumphalism that dominated earlier Berlinale programs. Films analyzed include Stefan Trampe’s The Border Guard (1995), depicting an unemployed customs officer’s psychological collapse amid industrial ruin, and Želimir Žilnik’s Tito Among the Serbs for the Second Time (1994). The reviewers argue that the 1990s triumphalism about capitalism’s “victory” produced the illusions that enabled today’s crises.

NEA destruction: one year later

The Trump administration’s campaign to eliminate the National Endowment for the Arts continues to devastate cultural institutions across the country. The NEA’s Challenge America program — its primary funding channel for small organizations in underserved communities — has been canceled for FY2026. Grant termination notices have been issued to organizations with pending awards. The Portland Mercury reported in March on “the chaos of cancelled NEA grants: one year later,” documenting how community arts programs that took years to build have been dismantled in months. The House Interior Appropriations Subcommittee proposed a 35% reduction in the NEA budget, from $207 million to $135 million.

Recent cultural deaths: Mary Beth Hurt, Tony Award-nominated actor known for The World According to Garp and numerous Broadway productions, died March 28 at 79 following Alzheimer’s disease. Darrell “Dash” Crofts of the soft rock duo Seals & Crofts died March 25.

Wave of job cuts in New Zealand — Chris Ross, Tom Peters

New Zealand’s unemployment stands at 5.4% (165,000 people), with 220,839 on Jobseeker Support — 6.8% of the working-age population — and one in three households facing food insecurity. South Korean factory fire investigation exposes indifference to safety — Ben McGrath

A March 20 fire at Anjun Industrial in Daejeon — a primary Hyundai/Kia engine valve supplier — killed 14 workers and injured 60. The investigation found nine deaths occurred in an illegally constructed gym area blocking emergency exits. The facility had experienced at least seven fires over 15 years, with faulty alarms, low sprinkler pressure, non-functional emergency lights, and an unlicensed sodium storage facility holding 10x the permitted quantity. Workers were actively discouraged from calling emergency services to avoid production delays. CEO Son Ju-hwan engaged in “gapjil” — verbal abuse — against employees who raised safety concerns. McGrath argues these conditions reflect Hyundai’s systemic pressure on suppliers, noting company sales nearly quadrupled from 2005 to 2024 while safety deteriorated.

Science is not neutral: The rubella vaccine and the attack on public health — Benjamin Mateus

The measles and vaccine policy dimensions are integrated into the Science section above. Mateus’s unique contribution is the detailed history of Dr. Stanley Plotkin’s RA 27/3 rubella vaccine — developed using WI-38 human fetal cell lines because rubella “exists exclusively in human populations and replicates most faithfully in human tissue” — and its decade-long suppression by US regulators who favored inferior animal-based vaccines, until Dorothy Horstmann’s comparative studies forced Merck to adopt it in 1979. Since routine vaccination began in 1969, congenital rubella syndrome cases dropped from 20,000 in the 1964–65 epidemic to just 15 nationwide between 2005–2018. Mateus frames the current destruction of vaccination infrastructure as a fascist attack on “the accumulated sum of social knowledge itself.”

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output this cycle typifies its role as the intellectual organ of the DSA-progressive-Democrat bloc:

“Wallace Shawn’s Road to Socialism” profiles the actor’s conversion to class politics and frames his engagement with socialism through the lens of celebrity radicalism and personal transformation. The piece elevates individual consciousness-raising over working-class organization — no coherent anti-imperialism, no party perspective, no programmatic content. It is characteristic Jacobin cultural-left fare, where socialism functions as a lifestyle orientation rather than a political program.

“Choose Class War, Not Boomer Resentment” argues for taxing the wealthy to fix Social Security, opposing the generational scapegoating pushed by centrist commentators. The framing is instructive: the “class war” Jacobin advocates is a tax policy question to be fought within the existing state, not a challenge to capitalist property relations. No break with the Democratic Party’s programmatic framework is articulated or implied.

“Ethiopia’s Trade Union Movement Is Growing Stronger” celebrates union membership growth as a counter-narrative to global labor decline. The reportage is descriptive, with no class-struggle internationalist framework and no critique of the Abiy Ahmed government’s systematic repression of independent labor organizing.

Assessment: Jacobin continues to function as the left boundary of permissible discourse within the Democratic Party’s social base. No article in this cycle breaks from that orbit.

Sources Jacobin Wallace Shawn’s road to socialism · Jacobin Choose class war, not boomer resentment · Jacobin Ethiopia’s trade union movement is growing stronger

Left Voice

Left Voice produced the most politically coherent output among these outlets this cycle:

“All Roads Lead to Stagflation” provides competent Marxist economic analysis of Trump’s tariff regime as producing inevitable stagflation that hits workers hardest. The class-struggle framing is consistent and the analysis avoids illusions in either party.

“Trump’s Tariffs Are Causing Chaos on Wall Street but It’s the Working Class Who Will Pay the Price” argues workers bear the burden through price hikes, layoffs, and intensified exploitation. Solid anti-capitalist framing without Democratic Party illusions.

“Why Is Shawn Fain and the UAW Leadership Supporting Trump’s Trade Policies?” is Left Voice at its sharpest — directly naming the UAW bureaucracy’s endorsement of Trump’s tariffs as labor chauvinism that divides the international working class. This is the only article across all six outlets that directly identifies the trade-union bureaucracy’s role in economic nationalism, a question of considerable significance given the Nexteer workers’ 96% rejection of UAW-backed concessions.

Assessment: Consistent class-independence line, internationalist critique of the labor bureaucracy. Left Voice’s PTS/FT Trotskyist orientation produces more programmatically coherent output than any of the other tendencies reviewed here, though the organization remains small and its influence limited.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Iran civilian infrastructure destruction — “double tap” strikes and the Pasteur Institute

US forces struck the B1 suspension bridge in Karaj as emergency workers responded to the initial blast, killing 8 and wounding 100 — a tactic international law experts characterize as a potential war crime under the Geneva Conventions. Separately, US-Israeli strikes destroyed the Pasteur Institute of Iran, a century-old medical research center. These acts represent deliberate targeting of civilian and scientific infrastructure that merits frontal analysis as war crimes, not merely “escalation.” The double-tap tactic in particular has a documented history in US drone warfare that the WSWS has previously covered.

Recommended reading Democracy Now! Headlines April 3, 2026

2. Oxfam report — wealthiest 0.1% hiding $2.8 trillion offshore while war spending surges

An Oxfam report released this week found the wealthiest 0.1% are hiding $2.8 trillion in offshore accounts to avoid taxes — a sum exceeding the total wealth of the poorest 4 billion people on Earth. This lands alongside the $1.5 trillion Pentagon budget request, offering an extraordinary materialist framing of who benefits from permanent war and who pays for it. The WSWS’s analytical framework on oligarchic wealth and war spending makes this a natural fit.

Recommended reading Democracy Now! Headlines April 3, 2026

3. The Hormuz crisis and energy poverty in the Global South

The Strait of Hormuz blockade is being covered primarily through the lens of US/European fuel costs and geopolitical maneuvering. Substantive coverage of the catastrophic impact on South Asia, East Africa, and Global South nations with no oil reserves and no strategic leverage is entirely missing. These import-dependent economies face energy poverty and economic collapse as $110+ oil ripples through their systems. The WSWS’s internationalist perspective is uniquely positioned to center these populations.

Recommended reading Bloomberg Iran war Hormuz closure oil shock

With 20% of Lebanon’s population displaced and 1,345 killed in a single month, Lebanon is experiencing one of the fastest per-capita displacement crises in recent history. Mainstream coverage focuses overwhelmingly on Hezbollah military operations while the civilian humanitarian catastrophe — hospitals overwhelmed, food supply chains disrupted, a generation of children out of school — goes largely unreported. The WSWS’s consistent attention to the human cost of imperialist war makes this essential coverage.
