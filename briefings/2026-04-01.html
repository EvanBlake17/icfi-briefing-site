1. US-Israel war on Iran enters second month as Trump signals end “within weeks”

Iran’s foreign minister told Al Jazeera that Tehran is prepared for at least six months of war.

The conflict has escalated sharply. The US military has begun B-52 Stratofortress overland bombing missions over Iran, with Chairman of the Joint Chiefs General Dan Caine announcing the deployment. The USDA has confirmed H5N1 in 989 US dairy herds across 17 states, with 70 total human cases since April 2024. Semaglutide fails to slow Alzheimer’s progression in phase 3 trial

The EVOKE(+) phase 3 trials, published in Nature Medicine, found that semaglutide did not slow clinical progression of Alzheimer’s disease — a significant setback for hopes that GLP-1 receptor agonists might double as dementia treatments. The blockbuster weight-loss and diabetes drug class had shown promise in observational studies. Researchers say the findings offer new mechanistic insights but the negative clinical result dampens enthusiasm for this therapeutic avenue.

Arts and culture

The death of Jürgen Habermas on March 14 at 96 and Gino Paoli on March 24 at 91 bookend a month that saw the passing of two major postwar European cultural figures — one in philosophy, one in music — whose lives illuminate the contradictions of intellectual and artistic production under capitalism in the Cold War and its aftermath.

Habermas (1929–2026) was eulogized across the bourgeois press as a champion of democracy and rational discourse, “the last rationalist” per Foreign Policy. The WSWS’s critical obituary by D. Szejnman and Steve Long offers the only published assessment from the standpoint of Marxist critique: Habermas systematically dismantled historical materialism — building his case on “Stalin’s 1938 pamphlet” rather than engaging Lenin or Trotsky — and replaced it with a theory of communicative rationality that provided no basis for critiquing power structures rooted in economic domination. His support for NATO’s Yugoslavia bombing, the Schröder welfare cuts, German rearmament, and dialogue with Cardinal Ratzinger trace the trajectory of a staatstragender Denker — a state-bearing thinker. The political lesson: Habermas drew the wrong conclusion from 1933, fearing working-class revolution rather than analyzing fascism’s class origins.

Gino Paoli, the Italian cantautore whose “Il cielo in una stanza” and “Sapore di sale” (often arranged by Ennio Morricone) redefined Italian popular music, died in Genoa surrounded by family. A leading figure of the Genoese school alongside Fabrizio De André and Luigi Tenco, Paoli emerged during Italy’s rapid postwar industrialization, which created conditions for a new lyrical introspection displacing traditional bel canto forms. His 1963 suicide attempt — he shot himself in the chest and lived 62 more years with the bullet lodged in his pericardium — reflected the pressures of commodified fame during the economic boom. His later service as a parliamentary deputy for the Italian Communist Party (1987–1992) was characteristic of many postwar artists: an adaptation to existing political structures rather than a break toward genuine class struggle.

The analysis of German Chancellor Merz’s immediate support as reflecting the German ruling class’s longstanding resentment of Nuremberg’s constraints is a distinctive WSWS contribution. Federal President Steinmeier’s criticism is characterized as tactical, not principled.

Bath Iron Works naval shipyard strike betrayed as UAW subordinates workers to the war effort — Mike Ingram

The UAW shut down a strike at Bath Iron Works in Maine after just five days (March 23–28), ratifying a four-year contract that one worker calculated as “about 20 cents an hour over the life of the contract.” BIW is a Navy contractor building Arleigh Burke-class destroyers with a $110 billion backlog — making the shipyard a strategic asset in the Iran war buildup. War Secretary Hegseth visited the yard in February as part of an “Arsenal of Freedom” tour, and UAW President Shawn Fain had laid the groundwork the previous year by signaling the union’s commitment to defense production.

Ingram characterizes the strike suppression as the “direct application” of Biden’s 2024 designation of the AFL-CIO as his “domestic NATO” — trade union bureaucracies functioning as instruments of military-industrial labor discipline. The speed of the sellout — five days, with a contract offering virtually nothing in real wage gains — demonstrates the priority the union apparatus places on uninterrupted military production over workers’ living standards. The article advocates rank-and-file committees independent of the union apparatus as the only means of breaking the stranglehold of the bureaucracy.

Sources WSWS Bath Iron Works naval shipyard strike betrayed as UAW subordinates workers to the war effort

Workers strike auto parts manufacturer in Findlay, Ohio — Samuel Davidson

Approximately 150 workers at Freudenberg-NOK in Findlay, Ohio struck on March 24 demanding higher wages (starting pay: $18–22/hr, maximum $25–30/hr) and affordable healthcare. The company supplies critical seals, O-rings, and gaskets to GM, Ford, and Stellantis, giving the strike significant leverage over Big Three production — a single parts supplier can shut down assembly lines within days.

Davidson criticizes the UAW for deliberately isolating the strikers by refusing to expand the action to other Freudenberg-NOK plants or instruct assembly workers to refuse company-supplied parts. This isolation strategy is the standard playbook of the union bureaucracy: contain the strike, exhaust the workers, and force acceptance of a substandard deal. The Economic Policy Institute is cited showing real wages for motor vehicle workers have declined since 2008, connecting current wage levels to concessions imposed during the 2009 auto industry bailout. Davidson advocates the formation of rank-and-file strike committees to break out of the UAW’s containment strategy.

Dolores Huerta’s allegations against Cesar Chavez and the political bankruptcy of the United Farm Workers — Marc Wells

On March 18, 95-year-old UFW co-founder Dolores Huerta publicly disclosed two incidents of sexual violence by Cesar Chavez: a 1960 coercive encounter and a 1966 rape in an isolated grape field, both resulting in pregnancies that were concealed for decades to protect “the image” of the union. A New York Times investigation corroborates additional victims: Ana Murguia and Debra Rojas allege Chavez groomed and molested them beginning at ages 12 and 13 at UFW headquarters in La Paz.

Wells argues this is not merely a fallen-hero story but an indictment of the UFW’s organizational culture and Chavez’s never-progressive politics. His strategy of boycotts and moral appeals, his alliances with Walter Reuther and Robert Kennedy, his anti-communism, and his purges of grassroots organizers all subordinated farmworkers to capitalist frameworks. By the 1980s the UFW had lost most of its contracts. The political conclusion is that the abuse revelations and the organizational bankruptcy are connected: “Chavez’s personal conduct cannot be separated from this process.” The article represents a distinctive WSWS contribution — the mainstream coverage treats the disclosures as a #MeToo narrative without examining the political character of Chavez’s labor strategy.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output continues to channel all opposition to Trump into the 2026 midterm electoral cycle. “The Midterms Loom Over Trump” frames the entire strategic horizon around Democratic congressional recapture — resistance is valuable insofar as it translates into votes in November, with no space for an independent working-class party perspective. “Socialists Need a Distinctive Economic Policy Agenda” is classic Jacobin social-democratic reformism: socialism distinguished from liberalism by bolder policy positions (industrial policy, price controls, public investment) rather than by the question of class power, state structures, or rupture with capitalism. “A Post-Order World” applies a realist/multipolar framing to US imperial decline, treating “US-led order” as a neutral object rather than a structure of class domination. There is no anti-imperialist solidarity framework and no analysis of the Iran war from the standpoint of workers and the oppressed.

The day’s output confirms Jacobin’s consistent function: providing a left-reformist vocabulary for channeling social discontent back into the Democratic Party. The absence of any mention of the Bath Iron Works strike betrayal or the Findlay, Ohio auto parts strike — both active labor struggles — while devoting space to midterm strategy is telling.

Sources Jacobin The midterms loom over Trump · Jacobin Socialists need a distinctive economic policy agenda · Jacobin A post-order world

Left Voice

Left Voice maintains the cleanest anti-imperialist line on Iran among these outlets. “US and Israel Launch Massive War on Iran — Down With Imperialist Aggression!” explicitly calls for street opposition by the working class and youth, framing the war as US/Israeli imperialism rather than a “defensive” or “preventive” operation. “ICE Out of Our Communities” advocates full status for all immigrants and an offensive rather than defensive posture on immigrant rights — a clear class-independence position.

However, the new magazine issue “The Mamdani Moment and the Fight Against the Far Right” engages with Zohran Mamdani’s Democratic primary mayoral run as a political phenomenon. While framed as opening debates for the rank-and-file left, this orients the tendency’s flagship publication around a Democratic primary campaign — risking the same entry-point problems that plagued DSA’s Sanders/AOC period. The Trotskyist-FT tendency (PTS) generally maintains clearer class independence than most tendencies in this review, but the Mamdani orientation warrants close monitoring.

Sources Left Voice US and Israel launch massive war on Iran · Left Voice The Mamdani moment and the fight against the far right · Left Voice ICE out of our communities

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL’s anti-imperialism remains consistently asymmetric. “Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” is a correct anti-war demand, but the party’s framework defends the Iranian theocratic state apparatus as though it represents “the people” — no class analysis of the Iranian regime’s own repression of workers, women, and national minorities. “Release President Nicolás Maduro and Cilia Flores Now!” treats Maduro — a bourgeois nationalist head of a capitalist state — as a figure whose defense is a socialist cause, without any examination of the Venezuelan government’s record against workers and the left. “The Next Step? May 1st General Strike” reflects the PSL/ANSWER coalition’s activist strengths, but the May Day call operates within a cross-class “No Kings” coalition framework that dilutes independent working-class politics.

The persistent pattern: national-reformist program dressed in anti-imperialist aesthetics — defense of bourgeois-nationalist regimes (Venezuela, Iran, Cuba) as surrogate socialism, with no independent working-class line on these states.

“War on Iran Producing Massive Radicalisation — Trade Union Leaders Lagging Behind” correctly identifies the gap between mass anti-war consciousness and trade union bureaucratic passivity, calling for the RCP to fill that vacuum. Cross-cutting observations: The most striking pattern across these tendencies today is the convergence between Jacobin, Socialist Alternative, and the Democratic Party leadership around the 2026 midterm elections as the strategic horizon for opposing Trump. Despite nominal differences in program, these organizations function as a left flank for channeling mass opposition back into bourgeois electoral politics. The PSL and Left Voice maintain clearer anti-imperialist positions on the Iran war but differ on the class character of the states they defend (PSL’s uncritical defense of Iran and Venezuela vs. Left Voice’s more principled but still inconsistent approach). The UK tendencies — SWP and RCP — show greater class independence, though the SWP’s “state capitalist” theory and the RCP’s recent break from Labour entryism both carry analytical and organizational limitations. Notably, none of these tendencies published on the Bath Iron Works strike betrayal or the Findlay auto parts strike — active labor struggles where the union bureaucracy’s role could be concretely exposed. The silence on actual class struggle, combined with extensive commentary on electoral and protest strategy, reveals priorities that diverge sharply from a Marxist orientation toward the working class.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The Iran war’s catastrophic impact on the global working class beyond oil markets

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has triggered the largest oil supply disruption in history, per the IEA. Gulf Cooperation Council states — which import over 80% of their calories through the Strait — face a “grocery supply emergency.” Urea fertilizer prices are up 50%, threatening global food security. The class dimensions of this crisis — who suffers from food price inflation, who profits from supply disruption — are entirely absent from mainstream coverage, which treats the economic impact as an abstraction about oil prices rather than a concrete assault on working-class living standards worldwide.

2. African labor trafficking into the Russian military as a class question

Over 1,780 Africans from 36 countries have been recruited into Russian combat roles under false pretenses, many through fraudulent employment agencies targeting economically desperate workers via social media. The story illuminates how imperialist war consumes the global poor — yet is framed almost entirely as a diplomatic or geopolitical issue rather than one about labor exploitation, precarity, and the desperation that makes workers vulnerable to trafficking. A WSWS article could connect this to the broader pattern of imperialist powers cannibalizing the working class of oppressed nations for military manpower.

3. Healthcare premium doubling hits 20 million Americans — a class catastrophe without political accountability

Twenty million Americans saw their health insurance premiums roughly double starting January 2026 after ACA subsidies were cut. This is an enormous working-class event — comparable in scale to a financial crisis for affected households — but it receives sporadic coverage subordinated to the Iran war. No major outlet frames it as a systemic failure of the for-profit healthcare model. A WSWS article could document the concrete impact on working families and connect it to the bipartisan defense of the private insurance industry.

Min Aung Hlaing is consolidating a permanent military-civilian hybrid dictatorship through rigged elections and a newly created Union Consultative Council designed to keep him in power regardless of formal office. The 3.5 million displaced by the civil war since 2021 and the Rohingya genocide for which he faces an ICC arrest warrant are barely mentioned in coverage focused on electoral mechanics and diplomatic reactions. A WSWS article could analyze the international dimensions — which powers are accommodating this consolidation, and what it means for workers in Southeast Asia.

5. NEA funding devastation one year later — the systematic destruction of public arts infrastructure

Nearly a year after the Trump administration proposed eliminating the NEA and cancelled hundreds of grants, the downstream effects are becoming visible: theaters, museums, and arts education nonprofits across the country are closing programs or shutting down entirely. Every NEA dollar generates nine more from private and local partners, meaning the $207 million cut cascades into nearly $2 billion in lost arts funding. This is a story about the ruling class’s hostility to cultural production that doesn’t serve its interests — and about the precarious labor conditions of artists and cultural workers.

6. Sudan’s systematic drone war on civilian infrastructure — the world’s largest humanitarian crisis, virtually ignored

The Sudanese Armed Forces have killed 200+ civilians in drone strikes since early March 2026, hitting markets, hospitals, and a secondary school. This systematic targeting of civilian infrastructure echoes documented war crimes. The conflict remains the world’s largest humanitarian crisis — with more displaced persons than any other — yet receives a fraction of the media coverage of the Iran war despite comparable civilian casualties. A WSWS article could document the imperialist interests fueling both sides and the silence of the “international community.”

No sitting president has ever attended Supreme Court oral arguments. Trump’s announced attendance at Wednesday’s birthright citizenship hearing represents an unprecedented act of executive intimidation of the judiciary. The constitutional implications — the blurring of separation of powers, the implicit pressure on justices to rule favorably while the executive sits in the gallery — deserve analysis that goes beyond the horse-race framing of mainstream coverage.
