1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 12 with most intense strikes yet

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran entered its twelfth day on March 11 with what Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth called “the most intense day” of American strikes since the war began on February 28. US forces have now struck more than 5,000 targets across Iran, at a cost the Pentagon estimated at $11.3 billion in munitions and operations during the first six days alone — a figure that has grown substantially since.

Iran reports 1,348 civilians killed and nearly 10,000 civilian sites struck. The Pentagon acknowledges 7 US service members killed and approximately 140 wounded, of whom 108 have returned to duty. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed in the opening strikes; his designated successor, son Mojtaba Khamenei, suffered a fractured foot and has not appeared publicly or issued any statement. Trump demanded “unconditional surrender,” declaring “We won. The first hour, it was over,” while simultaneously stating the war could last “four to five weeks.”

The UN Security Council voted 13–0 on a resolution condemning Iranian attacks on regional states, with Russia and China abstaining. Some 135 nations co-sponsored the resolution. Iran has threatened to block all oil exports until strikes cease.

Key facts: - Over 5,000 US targets struck in Iran since February 28 - $11.3+ billion spent in first six days; total cost rising rapidly - 1,348+ Iranian civilians killed per Iranian government figures - 7 US service members killed; ~140 wounded - Mojtaba Khamenei, designated successor, has not appeared publicly - Trump demands “unconditional surrender” while estimating “four to five weeks” for the campaign

2. Pentagon investigation confirms US Tomahawk missile struck Iranian girls’ school

A preliminary Pentagon investigation, first reported by the New York Times and confirmed by NPR and other outlets, has established that a US Tomahawk cruise missile destroyed the Shajareh Tayyebeh girls’ elementary school in Minab, Hormozgan province, on February 28 — the first day of the war — killing between 165 and 180 people, the vast majority girls aged 7 to 12.

Eight independent munitions experts confirmed to the Washington Post that the weapon was a Tomahawk cruise missile. Only the United States, Australia, and the United Kingdom possess Tomahawk variants, and neither Australia nor the UK is a party to the strikes. The Defense Intelligence Agency had used outdated targeting databases listing the school as part of an adjacent IRGC naval base that was decommissioned and converted to a school between 2013 and 2016. Despite the physical separation — new fencing, sports fields, and repainting — the target list was never updated.

Trump responded by claiming the weapon was “generic” and held by “many countries including Iran” — a statement fact-checkers confirmed is false. The Pentagon office created specifically to prevent civilian targeting had been “dramatically scaled back” by Hegseth after taking office. A UN panel warned the attack “raises the most serious concerns under international law” and may constitute a war crime. As Kevin Reed noted on the WSWS, the strike is not an aberration but “an expression of an objective underlying logic” of illegal aggressive war.

Key facts: - 165–180 killed, mostly girls aged 7–12 - Tomahawk missile confirmed by 8 independent munitions experts - Target intelligence was at least 10 years out of date - Pentagon civilian protection office had been “dramatically scaled back” by Hegseth - UN panel warned the attack may constitute a war crime

3. Iran closes Strait of Hormuz as oil surges past $100 per barrel

Iran has deployed mines in the Strait of Hormuz and struck at least four commercial vessels in the past 24 hours, effectively closing the world’s most critical oil chokepoint. A Thai bulk carrier, the “Mayuree Naree,” was damaged near the strait. Two tankers were hit at Iraq’s Umm Qasr port, killing one crew member and requiring the rescue of 38 others. A container ship was struck near Dubai.

Iran laid “a few dozen” mines in the strait; US Central Command destroyed 16 Iranian mine-laying vessels, but Iran claims the capability to deploy hundreds more. Brent crude exceeded $100 per barrel, with the US benchmark hitting approximately $95. Both benchmarks briefly spiked near $120 on March 9. In response, the IEA’s 32 member states unanimously authorized the release of 400 million barrels from strategic reserves — the largest coordinated emergency drawdown in the agency’s history. The US share is 172 million barrels over approximately 120 days beginning next week.

The war is costing the Middle East travel and tourism sector at least $600 million per day. Major aviation hubs in Dubai, Doha, Abu Dhabi, and Bahrain — which collectively serve approximately 526,000 passengers daily — have faced near-total closure for nearly two weeks.

Key facts: - At least 4 commercial vessels struck in 24 hours; 1 crew member killed - Iran laid mines in the strait; US destroyed 16 mine-laying vessels - Brent crude past $100/barrel; spiked near $120 on March 9 - IEA authorizes record 400-million-barrel strategic reserve release - Middle East tourism sector losing $600 million per day

More than 800,000 Lebanese have been displaced since the war began on February 28, with over 100,000 newly displaced between Monday and Tuesday alone — the majority from southern Lebanon, where Israel has issued sweeping evacuation orders. 5. Spain permanently withdraws ambassador to Israel

Spain’s Council of Ministers voted on March 10 to permanently terminate the appointment of Ambassador Ana María Salomón Pérez to Israel, with the decree signed by King Felipe VI and enacted March 11. Spain becomes the first EU member state to take this step over the combined weight of the Gaza war and the US-Israeli campaign against Iran.

The ambassador had already been recalled for consultations in September 2025 after Israeli Foreign Minister Gideon Sa’ar accused Spain of “antisemitism” over measures targeting Israel-bound ships and aircraft. Prime Minister Sánchez has also refused to allow the United States to use jointly operated military bases in southern Spain for strikes on Iran. Trump accused Spain of being “unfriendly” and threatened trade consequences. Sa’ar accused Spain of “standing with tyrants.” Both embassies will now be led by chargés d’affaires.

Spain recognized Palestinian statehood in May 2024 alongside Ireland and Norway, prompting Netanyahu to recall Israeli ambassadors from all three countries at the time.

6. Iran imposes most severe internet blackout on record

Iran’s internet connectivity collapsed to 1% of normal levels at 07:00 UTC on February 28, coinciding with the opening US-Israeli strikes, and has now exceeded 240 hours — among the most severe nationally imposed shutdowns ever recorded by the monitoring group NetBlocks. Approximately 90 million Iranians have been left dependent on state television and government text messages as their primary information sources. State texts warn that accessing international services may result in legal consequences.

NetBlocks director Alp Toker stated: “No other country has shut off access at such scale in so many instances and for such a duration.” The shutdown is costing the Iranian economy $35.7 million per day, with online sales down 80%. Iranians have spent roughly one-third of 2026 offline. Diaspora activists have launched the campaign #DirectToCellForIran, urging Elon Musk to activate Starlink’s Direct-to-Cell connectivity over Iran.

Key facts: - Internet at 1% of normal for 240+ consecutive hours - $35.7 million per day in economic losses; online sales down 80% - 90 million people dependent on state TV and government texts - Iranians have spent roughly one-third of 2026 offline

Kosovo locked in constitutional crisis

Kosovo faces its third parliamentary election in just over a year after President Vjosa Osmani dissolved parliament on March 6 when MPs failed to elect a new president. Only 66 of 120 MPs were present — 14 short of quorum — due to an opposition boycott by the LDK, PDK, and AAK parties, which blocked PM Albin Kurti’s Vetëvendosje party from installing Foreign Minister Glauk Konjufca as president.

The Constitutional Court issued an injunction on March 9 freezing the dissolution decree until at least March 31, creating a paradox in which neither new elections nor parliamentary business can proceed. If the court upholds the dissolution, elections would be expected by April 19. Kosovo already held a snap election on December 28, 2025, after failing to form a government following a February 2025 poll.

US measles surges to 1,281 cases across 31 states

The US measles outbreak continues to accelerate, with the CDC reporting 1,281 confirmed cases as of March 6 — including 145 new cases in the latest reporting week. A PAHO/WHO epidemiological update published March 11 reports 75 total human H5N1 infections and 2 deaths across 5 countries in the Americas since April 2022, with no new human cases confirmed since November 24, 2025. Major clinical trials published

Three significant clinical trial results were published in the past week:

  • NEJM: Obinutuzumab superior to placebo in lupus trial. In a randomized controlled trial of patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) without advanced nephritis, obinutuzumab plus standard therapy was superior to placebo plus standard therapy in achieving sustained clinically meaningful reductions in disease activity over 52 weeks. SLE affects an estimated 1.5 million Americans and has limited treatment options; this trial establishes a potential new standard of care.

  • Nature Medicine: Fecal microbiota transplants boost cancer immunotherapy. Three landmark phase 2 trials published in the same issue of Nature Medicine confirmed that fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) enhances immunotherapy efficacy in patients with advanced solid tumors, with response rates meaningfully above immunotherapy-alone historical controls. The convergence of three independent trials constitutes unusually strong evidence for a combination approach that could benefit the large population of cancer patients who fail to respond to checkpoint inhibitors alone.

  • NEJM: Finerenone reduces albuminuria in type 1 diabetes with CKD. Adults with type 1 diabetes and chronic kidney disease showed significantly greater decreases in urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio with finerenone versus placebo — extending to type 1 diabetes a benefit previously demonstrated only in type 2. CKD affects approximately 40% of people with type 1 diabetes and is a leading cause of dialysis.

Arts and culture

The cultural landscape this week is dominated by the reverberations of the US-Israeli war on Iran, which has created both a crisis of censorship and an uneasy cultural silence in Western capitals — a silence broken in part by artistic and literary production that attempts to reckon with the social catastrophe unfolding in real time.

Roy Medvedev (1925–2026): death of a Soviet dissident historian

The death of Roy Medvedev at 100 marks the passing of one of the most significant figures in Soviet dissident historiography. His landmark work Let History Judge (1971), smuggled to the West and published while he remained in the Soviet Union, was the first systematic documentation by an insider of the crimes of the Stalin era — the purges, the show trials, the destruction of an entire generation of Bolsheviks. The book was a genuine contribution to historical truth at a moment when Stalinist falsification was still official state policy.

Yet Medvedev’s political trajectory illustrates the limitations of opposition that remains within the framework of Stalinism. He never broke organizationally with the Communist Party, never developed a Trotskyist analysis of the degeneration of the Soviet state, and in the Gorbachev era accommodated himself to “reform” currents that ultimately liquidated the Soviet Union. His importance lies in what he documented rather than in the political conclusions he drew — a distinction that the WSWS’s critical assessment, published today by M. Schönman, develops in substantial detail. His death invites renewed attention to the question of how historical truth and political program relate to one another.

The Best American Essays 2025 and American literary culture in crisis

The WSWS published a literary review by James McDonald of The Best American Essays 2025, assessing how contemporary American nonfiction prose engages with the period’s social upheavals — anti-war sentiment, labor militancy, political polarization, and the deepening crisis of democratic forms. McDonald evaluates individual essays for depth of social insight and aesthetic quality, and draws broader conclusions about the state of American literary culture at a moment when the gap between what writers know and what they are willing to say has never been wider.

The best nonfiction writing of the period reflects an awareness that the old frameworks — identity-based personal essays, memoir-as-politics, the confessional mode that dominated the 2010s — are inadequate to the scale of what is happening. The question is whether literary culture can develop forms adequate to the historical moment, or whether it will continue to retreat into private experience as the world burns.

The cultural cost of Iran’s internet blackout

Iran’s 240-hour internet shutdown has severed 90 million people not only from news and communication but from the entire infrastructure of modern cultural life — streaming music and film, digital publishing, social media as a space for artistic community, and the diaspora networks that connect Iranian artists to global audiences. The blackout has effectively erased Iranian cultural production from the digital sphere for the duration of the war. Independent filmmakers, musicians, and writers inside Iran are unable to share work, receive payments, or communicate with international collaborators. The shutdown represents a form of cultural destruction that compounds the physical destruction of aerial bombardment.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — channeling working-class discontent back toward the Democratic Party; anti-war sentiment routed into the 2028 electoral cycle rather than independent class action
  • Left Voice — consistent anti-imperialist line on Iran and Latin America; correct opposition to liberal feminism’s reliance on the imperialist state, though organizational conclusions remain limited
  • Liberation News / PSL — broad coalition anti-war mobilization toward April 5 March on Washington, but class independence not demanded of coalition partners; campist tendency in defense of the Iranian state
  • Socialist Alternative — anti-war stance without campism; May Day general strike organizing with “No Kings Day” branding that signals possible popular-front crossover
  • SWP UK — correct line opposing Labour’s ban on Palestine solidarity marches; solid labor coverage of Argentine factory occupation

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s output this cycle centers on managing working-class discontent within the boundaries of the Democratic Party:

  • “Workers Are Leaving the Trump Coalition” — Uses survey data showing Trump’s working-class voters wavering to frame the desirable political destination as Democratic realignment rather than independent class politics. The article identifies correct grievances (stagnant wages, war, social cuts) only to route them back toward the bourgeois party. This is the classical Jacobin maneuver: name the problem, then propose the problem as the solution. Flagged: tacit Democratic Party orientation.

  • “What San Francisco Educators Won on Their Strike” — Straightforward labor coverage of SF educators’ first strike in approximately 50 years, documenting gains on healthcare and special education workloads. More defensible content with no class-collaborationist framing visible, though it lacks any analysis of the union bureaucracy’s role.

  • “Robber Barons Are Doing Better Than Ever” — Frames Canadian wealth inequality around “democracy” being threatened by concentrated wealth — a liberal-democratic rather than class-struggle register. The article treats inequality as a problem of democratic governance rather than an inherent feature of capitalist property relations. Flagged: national-reformist framing (democracy, not socialism).

The WSWS published a direct polemic today — Jacob Crosse’s critique of Eric Blanc’s “Why Is There No Antiwar Movement in the US?” — which identifies Blanc’s prescription of channeling anti-war sentiment into the 2028 Democratic cycle and defending AOC from criticism as a mechanism for trapping opposition within the framework of imperialism. This is the central political function of Jacobin: providing a left-sounding rationale for subordination to the Democratic Party.

Left Voice

  • “Trump ‘Shield of the Americas’ Alliance Aims to Advance US Imperialism in Latin America” — Correctly identifies the Miami right-wing military summit as a vehicle for US imperial containment of Chinese expansion and control of the Panama Canal. Anti-imperialist, class-independent framing throughout.

  • “This International Women’s Day, We Need an Anti-Imperialist, Socialist Feminism Against War” — Explicitly rejects liberal feminism’s reliance on the imperialist state and frames the US-Israeli operations against Iran as requiring anti-imperialist working-class solidarity rather than Western “protection” narratives. Consistent with their FT-CI tendency’s positions.

  • “On This International Women’s Day, Down with Imperialist War!” — Bread and Roses statement opposing military interventions in Venezuela, Iran, and Palestine; calls for independent working-class struggle.

Left Voice maintains a consistent anti-imperialist line that avoids the campism of the PSL and the Democratic Party orientation of Jacobin. However, their organizational conclusions — centered on party-building within their FT-CI international framework — remain limited by their failure to address the fundamental strategic questions of program and historical perspective that distinguish the ICFI from all other tendencies.

Liberation News / PSL

  • “Nationwide Coalition Announces April 5 March on Washington for Palestine” — Broad coalition (ANSWER, JVP, PYM) mobilizing for a DC march demanding ceasefire, arms embargo, and defense of arrested activists like Mahmoud Khalil. Flagged: PSL’s persistent broad popular-front coalition-building with liberal NGOs fails to demand class independence from coalition partners — the political content of the march is thereby diluted to the lowest common denominator acceptable to liberal organizations.

  • “PSL Statement: Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” — Opposes US military action against Iran. However, PSL’s consistent defense of states targeted by US imperialism — Cuba, Iran, Venezuela, Russia, China — tips into uncritical campism in which these states are defended because they are targets rather than evaluated by their treatment of their own working classes. The Iranian regime’s 240-hour internet blackout on 90 million of its own citizens receives no mention. Flagged: campist tendency — defense of bourgeois-nationalist regimes rather than class-independent opposition to imperialism.

Socialist Alternative

  • “Middle East: A Regional War Spiraling Out of Control” — Condemns US/Israeli strikes on Iran as imperialist aggression without campist defense of the Iranian regime. Correctly identifies the war as driven by inter-imperialist rivalries.

  • “Stalin’s ‘Socialism in One Country’ — A Century of Disaster” — Historical critique of the 1925 Soviet turn, arguing it betrayed internationalism. Useful polemical content with no class-collaborationist deviations.

  • “5 Steps to Get Strike Ready for May 1” — Organizing guide for a May Day general strike, with an intermediate step of “No Kings Day” (March 28). The general strike orientation is positive, though the “No Kings Day” branding signals possible popular-front crossover with liberal anti-Trump forces who oppose Trump’s authoritarianism without opposing the capitalist system that produced it. Background note: SA’s unresolved tension from running candidates adjacent to Democratic Party ballot lines during the Kshama Sawant era warrants monitoring as May Day organizing proceeds.

SWP UK

  • “Why You Should Oppose Labour’s Ban on Al-Quds Day March” — Explicitly criticizes the Labour government’s attempt to restrict Palestine solidarity demonstrations. This is the correct line and notable given the SWP’s historical vacillation between soft Labour Party pressure and outright opposition.

  • “The Argentine Factory Occupation That Could Shake Milei” — Strong labor coverage of 920 workers occupying a tyre factory for four weeks against Milei’s austerity. Internationalist, class-struggle framing with no notable deviations.

  • “National Housing Demo Will Unite the Fightback” — Promotes a national housing demonstration using broad “fightback” language without naming political independence from Labour. Routine SWP organizing coverage.

The SWP’s output today is more politically defensible than typical — the Palestine and Argentina pieces take clear class-struggle positions. The housing piece remains characteristically vague on the question of the Labour Party.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

  • “Germany: Over 50,000 School Students Strike Against Conscription” — Coverage of 50,000+ German students striking across 140+ cities against military conscription under the slogan “The rich want war — young people want a future!” Strong anti-militarist, class-struggle framing consistent with IMT’s opposition to NATO rearmament. This is a significant development that connects directly to the European entry into the Iran war analyzed in the WSWS Perspective.

Correctly identifies the absence of Marxist organization as the central weakness. Note: the IMT’s own organizational practice — deep entry tactics inside reformist parties — sometimes contradicts their sharp theoretical critiques of reformism.

The IMT’s international coverage today is substantive and politically serious, particularly on German conscription resistance. Their analysis correctly identifies class dynamics but avoids the programmatic specificity of the ICFI — the call for “revolutionary leadership” remains abstract where the ICFI would specify the construction of sections of the International Committee.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Iowa Army reservists — working-class soldiers paying for a billionaire’s war

Five of the seven US troops killed in the Iran war were Army reservists from Des Moines, Iowa — weekend soldiers holding civilian jobs who died in a Kuwait port drone strike. Their deaths expose the class composition of the US military: working-class men and women, not the children of the political and financial elite who designed the war. Specialist Declan J. Coady was 20 years old. No congressional authorization was sought or obtained. A WSWS profile of these soldiers — who they were, what communities they came from, and the political establishment’s indifference to their deaths — would be powerful and unique.

Recommended reading NBC News What we know about the US service members killed in the Iran war

2. Oil giants reap windfall profits while IEA releases emergency reserves

As Brent crude spiked to $120 per barrel and now trades above $90, major oil corporations are posting enormous windfall profits while governments scramble to release 400 million barrels from strategic reserves. The gap between corporate profit and public sacrifice during an engineered war deserves systematic WSWS analysis — connecting defense contractor revenue, oil company windfalls, and the simultaneous cuts to Medicaid, housing, and education. Greece’s emergency price caps hint at the political pressure building from below.

Recommended reading NBC News No relief from high prices despite petroleum reserve deal

3. The $11.3 billion war bill — who profits, who pays

Pentagon officials estimated the first six days of the Iran war cost $11.3 billion in munitions and operations — a figure growing daily. That sum represents defense contractor revenue at the direct expense of public budgets. The same administration pursuing the war has enacted sweeping cuts to social programs. A WSWS analysis of the class redistribution embedded in war financing — tracking specific contractors, weapons systems, and the Congressional districts that benefit — would connect the war directly to the domestic austerity agenda.

Recommended reading CBS News Trump vows to end war soon as Iran hits ships

4. Greece’s price caps and the European working class under war-inflation pressure

Greece’s emergency profit margin caps on fuel and food are the first direct governmental response to war-driven inflation threatening working-class purchasing power in Europe. No other EU state has acted. As energy prices rise and austerity-era safety nets remain threadbare, the policy may signal a broader European political crisis. This story connects imperialist war directly to the kitchen table and warrants coverage alongside the European naval deployment analyzed in today’s Perspective.

Recommended reading Havana Times International news briefs, March 11, 2026

5. Lebanon’s mass displacement and the collapse of a second country

Over 800,000 Lebanese have been displaced in 12 days, with the majority from the south under Israeli sweeping evacuation orders. This mirrors the 2006 and 2024 displacement patterns and may represent a permanent demographic restructuring of southern Lebanon. International aid capacity is collapsing as donor attention fixates on Iran. The humanitarian catastrophe in Lebanon is being overshadowed by the Iran war but represents a separate and equally devastating crisis requiring its own WSWS analysis.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war: Day 12

6. The $170 billion IEEPA tariff refund crisis

The Supreme Court’s 6–3 ruling striking down executive tariff authority under IEEPA has created a $170 billion refund obligation that the government cannot technically process, with interest accumulating at $700 million per month. The estimated long-run fiscal impact reaches $2 trillion. This is a major constitutional and fiscal crisis that intersects with the WSWS’s coverage of trade war politics and the erosion of democratic norms — yet it has received almost no attention from left media.

Recommended reading Financial Content The $170 billion tariff reckoning

7. 50,000 German students strike against conscription

Over 50,000 German school students struck across 140+ cities against military conscription under the slogan “The rich want war — young people want a future!” This is the largest German student mobilization in years and connects directly to the European military escalation analyzed in today’s WSWS Perspective. The movement warrants its own WSWS coverage — distinct from the IMT’s reporting — analyzing the class dynamics, the connection to the €100 billion defense fund, and the political tasks facing the anti-war movement in Germany.
