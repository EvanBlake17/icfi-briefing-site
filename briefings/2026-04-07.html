1. US-Iran war enters week 6 as Trump sets Tuesday night deadline to destroy Iran’s infrastructure

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its sixth week on April 7 with President Trump issuing an ultimatum that threatens to escalate the conflict into an unprecedented campaign of infrastructure destruction. At a White House press conference, Trump set an 8:00 p.m. ET Tuesday deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, warning that failure would result in the destruction of Iran’s power plants and bridges. “Every bridge in Iran will be decimated by 12:00 tomorrow night,” Trump declared, adding: “The entire country can be taken out in one night, and that night might be tomorrow night.”

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth confirmed that Monday was the highest-intensity US strike day since the war began on February 28. US-Israeli strikes killed more than 25 Iranian officials, including Revolutionary Guard intelligence chief Maj. Gen. Majid Khademi. Iran’s Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei responded that the country’s forces “will not be deterred by assassinations of commanders.”

  • Pakistan proposed a 45-day ceasefire; Trump called it “significant” but “not good enough”; Iran called for a permanent end to hostilities

As Patrick Martin wrote in today’s WSWS Perspective, Trump’s threats amount to openly discussing genocide against a nation of 93 million people, with the Democratic Party bearing responsibility through its inaction.

2. Strait of Hormuz blockade drives oil above $115, threatening global food supply

The Iranian blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of the world’s seaborne oil supply passes — is now generating cascading economic consequences that extend far beyond energy markets. WTI crude reached $115.21/barrel on April 7, with Brent at $108.67 after peaking at approximately $141 at the crisis height. This represents the largest energy supply disruption since the 1970s oil crisis.

The blockade’s most dangerous downstream effect is on global food production. The Gulf region produces nearly 50% of the world’s urea and 30% of global ammonia — essential fertilizer inputs. Urea prices have surged 50% since the war began on February 28, hitting US farmers at the start of the spring planting season. American farming groups are lobbying the Trump administration to prioritize a Hormuz deal.

Key facts:

  • Bloomberg Economics estimates that at $110/barrel, the impact adds ~1 percentage point to annual inflation and cuts 0.6% off GDP in the euro area; at $170/barrel the shock roughly doubles
  • Products trapped behind the strait include fertilizer, helium, sulfur, plastics, and aluminum, disrupting global manufacturing supply chains
  • The Philippines has recorded its 13th consecutive week of fuel price hikes and 15th for diesel and kerosene
  • Oil prices in Peru have surged up to 75% due to the closure
  • The dual shock of war and Trump’s tariffs on Canadian and Mexican agricultural inputs is stoking food price inflation globally
  • The Dallas Fed warned the fertilizer crisis directly threatens 2026 food security, particularly for smallholder farmers in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa who cannot afford inputs at any price

7. NASA Artemis II crew completes historic lunar flyby

NASA’s Artemis II crew — Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch (NASA), and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA) — completed a lunar flyby on April 6–7, becoming the first humans to leave Earth’s orbit since Apollo 17 in 1972. The Orion spacecraft Integrity passed within 4,070 miles of the lunar surface and experienced a 41-minute communications blackout as it passed behind the Moon.

The mission is expected to break the distance record set by Apollo 13 (~248,655 miles / 400,171 km from Earth). The crew is conducting radiation measurements, space weather forecasting tests, and three human health studies monitoring sleep patterns, immune response, and cognitive function during deep space travel. Splashdown is scheduled for April 10.

8. JBS meatpacking workers end historic three-week strike with no guaranteed gains

Approximately 3,800 workers at JBS USA’s Greeley, Colorado plant — the largest US meat processor, handling 6% of total US beef slaughterhouse capacity — returned to work after a three-week strike, the first major meatpacking strike since 1985. UFCW Local 7 ordered workers back despite JBS making no improvements to its original offer of roughly 60 cents/hour the first year and 30 cents/year thereafter — barely 1.5% annually. JBS agreed only to reopen negotiations.

The workforce is predominantly immigrant, speaking approximately 57 languages. Workers demanded company-funded PPE (currently deducted from wages) and meaningful wage increases after healthcare costs rose 37% over five years. JBS reported $415 million in quarterly profits on $23.06 billion in revenue.

  • UFCW Local 7 President Kim Cordova: “This company is one of the richest companies in the world, and the wage increases that they’re offering simply just don’t keep up with the economy”
  • The WSWS warned that the UFCW bureaucracy was preparing a sellout, noting the union previously sabotaged Colorado grocery strikes using similar “return now, negotiate later” tactics
  • At least six JBS Greeley workers died during the pandemic’s first year; a whistleblower lawsuit alleges systematic safety failures

Rocket attack in India’s Manipur kills two children, triggers curfew and internet shutdown

A suspected rocket attack struck a civilian home in Bishnupur district’s Tronglaobi Awang Leikai village in the early hours of April 7, killing a 5-year-old boy and a 5-month-old infant girl. Their mother sustained critical injuries and was rushed to hospital. Security officials said the projectile was fired from Kuki-dominated Churachandpur hills approximately five kilometers away.

The attack triggered immediate escalation. Enraged locals set ablaze two oil tankers and a truck passing through Bishnupur, reportedly en route to Churachandpur district. The Manipur government suspended mobile internet for three days across five districts and imposed curfew. Schools across valley districts remained shut on April 7.

The violence represents the latest episode in the Meitei-Kuki ethnic conflict that has displaced tens of thousands since 2023, with the Modi government failing to resolve the crisis or protect civilian populations.

UK workers’ rights law takes effect amid Starmer backlash

Key provisions of the Employment Rights Act 2025 are being phased in from April 2026, including day-one entitlements for statutory sick pay and paternity leave, protections for pregnant workers, an end to exploitative zero-hours contracts, and a new right to bereavement leave. Over 15 million workers across the UK are expected to benefit.

However, the Starmer government executed a significant U-turn on its flagship pledge: rather than protecting workers from unfair dismissal from day one, the threshold was set at six months after pressure from business groups. Labour backbenchers have called the reversal a “complete betrayal” of the party’s manifesto commitment. The previous threshold was two years.

May Day general strike mobilization gains union backing

The mobilization reflects growing working-class opposition to the Iran war, tariff-driven price increases, and the Trump administration’s attacks on immigrants and democratic rights. However, the leadership of the action remains firmly within the orbit of Democratic Party-aligned organizations like Indivisible, raising fundamental questions about whether it will be channeled into electoral politics rather than independent working-class action.

World Health Day 2026 marks “Together for health — stand with science”

April 7 is World Health Day, with the WHO’s 2026 theme — “Together for health. Stand with science” — carrying particular urgency as public health institutions face political assault globally. The campaign is anchored by the International One Health Summit hosted by France under its G7 presidency in Lyon, bringing together heads of state, scientists, and civil society. The inaugural Global Forum of WHO Collaborating Centres (April 7–9) gathers nearly 800 scientific institutions from over 80 countries.

The theme implicitly pushes back against anti-science political currents, including the Trump administration’s proposed cuts to the CDC and NIH and the broader undermining of public health infrastructure worldwide.

US measles cases surge to 1,671

The US measles outbreak continues to accelerate. As of April 2, the CDC reported 1,671 cases — an increase of 96 cases and 6.1% from the previous week. Cases have been reported by 33 jurisdictions. There have been 17 new outbreaks in 2026, with 94% of confirmed cases (1,570 of 1,671) being outbreak-associated. Children under 5 are disproportionately affected, with 9% in that age group requiring hospitalization.

The surge reflects declining vaccination rates driven by anti-vaccine sentiment, amplified by Trump’s appointment of Robert F. Kennedy Jr. to lead the Department of Health and Human Services.

H5N1 bird flu monitoring continues

CDC surveillance systems currently show no indicators of unusual influenza activity in people, including avian influenza A(H5). However, H5N1 remains widespread in wild birds globally and continues causing outbreaks in US poultry and dairy cows, with sporadic human cases among dairy and poultry workers. The risk assessment remains low but the CDC cautions that mutations in recent severely ill patients could potentially boost transmissibility.

Medical research developments

  • Treatment-resistant hypertension: A new drug called baxdrostat showed strong results in a large global trial, lowering blood pressure by nearly 10 mmHg in patients unresponsive to standard treatments. If approved, it could benefit millions with dangerously high blood pressure who have exhausted existing options.
  • Cancer-targeting DNA system: Scientists created a programmable drug delivery system from synthetic DNA that activates only when it detects a precise combination of tumor markers, offering unprecedented targeting accuracy and potentially reducing chemotherapy side effects.
  • Aging lungs and respiratory viruses: A new study reveals that aging lungs may play a major role in why flu and COVID become especially dangerous for older adults, with implications for vaccine and treatment strategies.
  • Senescent cell therapy: Research published on EurekAlert identified specific fatty acids that selectively induce death in senescent (aging) cells, opening new avenues for age-related disease therapies.

Trump budget proposes eliminating NEA, NEH, and IMLS

Trump’s fiscal 2026 budget proposal calls for the complete closure of the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the Institute of Museum and Library Services — the three primary federal agencies supporting arts and cultural programming across the United States. Smithsonian institutions serving Latino and Black communities were also stripped of dedicated funding.

The proposal represents the most sweeping attempt to dismantle federal arts infrastructure in US history. While previous administrations (including Trump’s first term) proposed cuts, this marks the first time a budget has sought simultaneous elimination of all three agencies. The NEA alone supports thousands of grantee organizations in every congressional district, many in rural and underserved communities. The proposed elimination reflects the broader pattern of the Trump administration attacking institutions that support critical cultural engagement, historical understanding, and democratic public discourse.

The timing is significant: the assault on public arts funding comes as the war on Iran and domestic political repression intensify, precisely the moment when independent cultural expression and historical perspective are most needed.

Jacobin’s output is dominated by domestic policy framing that systematically avoids the revolutionary implications of the current crisis.

  • “Trump’s $1.5 trillion for war comes from Americans’ pockets” (Ben Beckett) frames the Iran war budget as a guns-versus-butter question — arguing the war is financed through cuts to social programs — rather than as an expression of the fundamental crisis of American imperialism. The article’s implicit message is that a different budget allocation within capitalism could resolve the contradiction.
  • “Americans (still) support a federal jobs guarantee” (Jared Abbott) promotes polling data showing public support for a federal jobs guarantee as evidence that the program remains viable — within the framework of the Democratic Party, which has never adopted it. The article treats public opinion as a pressure mechanism on bourgeois politicians rather than an argument for independent working-class political organization.
  • “A socialist teacher is running for Kentucky State House” (Robert LeVertis Bell) profiles a self-identified socialist running for the Kentucky 43rd District — the standard DSA approach of channeling socialist sentiment into the Democratic Party’s electoral machinery at the state level.

Assessment: Jacobin continues to function as the left flank of Democratic Party politics. On a day when the US is openly threatening genocide against Iran and a national general strike is being organized, Jacobin’s leading content concerns budget allocation, polling, and a state legislative race. The class interests expressed are those of the upper-middle-class milieu that seeks modest social reform without challenging capitalist property relations or US imperialism.
