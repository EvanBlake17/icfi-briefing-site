1. US awaits Iran’s response to 14-point peace memo as Hormuz clashes continue

Secretary of State Marco Rubio said Friday that Washington expected a formal Iranian response on May 9 to a US-drafted 14-point memorandum of understanding that would declare an end to the war and open a 30-day negotiating window covering the Strait of Hormuz, Iran’s nuclear program, and the release of billions in frozen Iranian funds. The principal sticking point is the duration of an enrichment moratorium — Iran has proposed five years and the US 20, with diplomatic sources placing the likely settlement at 12 to 15 years. Iran’s Fars news agency reported “sporadic clashes” between Iranian and US naval forces in the Strait of Hormuz on May 9; Rubio warned that Iranian boats engaging US forces would face “military consequences.”

The diplomacy is unfolding alongside continued US military strikes. As Andre Damon documented on the WSWS, US Navy F/A-18s from the USS George H.W. Bush disabled two Iranian-flagged oil tankers — M/T Sea Star III and M/T Sevda — in the Gulf of Oman on May 8. Trump issued what the WSWS described as a nuclear threat: “You’re just going to have to look at one big glow coming out of Iran, and they better sign their agreement fast.” The blockade now holds approximately 70 tankers carrying 166 million barrels of oil worth more than $13 billion. Israeli operations in southern Lebanon killed at least 31 people on May 9, including a rescue worker, according to Lebanon’s National News Agency.

Key facts: - War now at day 70 (begun February 28); Pentagon strikes have targeted ~13,000 objectives across 38 combat days - Iran reports 81,000 damaged civilian structures, 275 medical facilities struck, 3,636 deaths through April 7 (1,701 civilians, per Iranian Human Rights Activists News Agency) - US confirmed losses: 13 dead, 400+ wounded; 16 US bases hit; Al Udeid’s $1 billion radar destroyed; ~50% of Patriot and over half of THAAD interceptors expended - China — which receives 37.7% of all oil through the Strait of Hormuz and bought over 80% of Iran’s seaborne exports in 2025 — is the primary target ahead of Trump’s May 14 Beijing summit with Xi - US intelligence concluded the war produced “limited new damage” to Iran’s nuclear program; the 440-kg highly enriched uranium stockpile remains intact in deeply buried sites - University of Michigan economist Justin Wolfers wrote in the New York Times that “the Iran war will cost hundreds of billions of dollars, and very possibly trillions”

2. Trump announces three-day Russia-Ukraine ceasefire; Victory Day parade scaled back

Trump announced on May 8: “I am pleased to announce that there will be a THREE DAY CEASEFIRE (May 9th, 10th, and 11th)” covering all kinetic military activity, alongside a 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner swap. Zelensky confirmed the prisoner exchange on X. Russian aide Yuri Ushakov stated that “an agreement on this matter was reached during our telephone contacts with the US administration.” The truce is timed to Russia’s Victory Day commemorations.

For the first time in nearly 20 years, the May 9 parade in Red Square featured no tanks, missiles, armored vehicles, or junior cadets — only marching foot soldiers and an aerial flyover. Officials cited “the current operational situation” and the threat of “Ukrainian terrorist activity”; Moscow imposed multilayered air defense, electronic warfare, and mobile internet shutdowns across major cities. The Kremlin had warned that any disruption of the parade would trigger a mass missile strike on Kyiv, and told foreign diplomats to evacuate the Ukrainian capital. Ukrainian drones recently struck the Tuapse oil refinery on the Black Sea, causing what regional officials described as a major ecological disaster requiring civilian evacuation. Trump’s exclusion of NATO allies from pre-strike planning on Iran has sharpened alliance tensions and is driving new urgency in European discussions about strategic autonomy.

Key facts: - 88-hour ceasefire window covers May 9–11 - Putin attended and reviewed the Red Square parade; soldiers and sailors who served in Ukraine marched and chanted - Ukrainian drone campaign now targets Russian infrastructure almost daily - Pre-truce day saw 208+ recorded combat engagements along the front line

3. US Court of International Trade strikes down Trump’s 10% global tariffs

The US Court of International Trade ruled 2-1 on May 7 that Trump’s 10% across-the-board tariffs — effective February 24, 2026 — were unlawful under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974. The court rejected the administration’s argument that a $1.2 trillion annual goods trade deficit and a current account deficit of 4% of GDP justified the action. The ruling follows the Supreme Court’s 6-3 decision in February striking down Trump’s 2025 tariffs imposed under IEEPA, after which the administration pivoted to Section 122 as an alternative authority. Customs and Border Protection activated Phase 1 of the IEEPA tariff refund process on April 20.

Trump simultaneously gave the EU until July 4 to comply with US trade demands or face tariff hikes on European cars and trucks from 15% to 25%. The EU is the United States’ largest trading partner; a 25% auto tariff would significantly impact Germany, France, and Italy. Moody’s chief economist declared the data on a year of Trump tariffs “definitive”: they have done “significant damage” to the US economy. The average US tariff rate now stands at just over 12%, up from approximately 2.5% on January 20, 2025. The Tax Foundation and Moody’s place the additional cost per US household at roughly $1,230 per year — a flat regressive burden falling hardest on working-class consumers, a class dimension that has been almost wholly absent from mainstream coverage of the legal chess match between the courts and the White House.

4. Sudan famine spreads as humanitarian appeal sits at 5.5% funded

Sudan has entered the fourth year of its civil war and is now simultaneously the world’s worst famine and the world’s largest displacement crisis. UNICEF and Action Against Hunger confirmed this week that famine has been officially identified in Al Fasher and Kadugli, with 20 additional areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan facing imminent famine risk. Nearly 34 million people — 65% of Sudan’s population — require urgent humanitarian assistance. Sudan’s 2026 humanitarian response plan, which calls for $2.9 billion, has received only 5.5% of the funding needed.

Key facts: - 19 million people facing high levels of acute food insecurity - 4.2 million cases of acute malnutrition projected for 2026, including 800,000+ cases of severe acute malnutrition - 11.5 million people internally displaced — the largest such crisis in the world - WFP requires $610 million urgently to sustain operations through August 2026 - Funding shortfalls have forced agencies to prioritize only the most critical cases

The contrast with hundreds of billions of dollars committed by Western powers to wars in Ukraine and Iran is the pivotal class and racial fact obscured in mainstream coverage.

5. Gaza ceasefire fragments under daily Israeli strikes; second phase blocked

The UN Security Council was briefed on May 9 on the need to consolidate the “tenuous ceasefire” in Gaza after the body of the last Israeli hostage was retrieved. Despite the formal ceasefire, Israel has conducted near-daily airstrikes, shelling, and gunfire across Gaza since the agreement took effect. The second phase of Trump’s 20-point Comprehensive Plan — covering Hamas disarmament, deployment of international forces, and a governance transition — remains blocked, with Israel telling negotiators it will not withdraw from the so-called “yellow line” inside Gaza.

Humanitarian deliveries continue at minimal scale: tents and bedding reached approximately 4,500 households in the devastated southern Khan Younis area in the past week, against a population of more than two million in need. Al Jazeera commentators have written that “the ceasefire did what it was meant to do — make Gaza invisible.” International coverage has shifted decisively to Iran and Ukraine, while conditions for Palestinians remain catastrophic. Israeli operations in southern Lebanon killed at least 31 people on May 9, including a rescue worker.

6. Britain’s Labour Party suffers historic electoral collapse; Reform UK surges to 27%

Britain’s Labour Party suffered catastrophic losses in the May local, Welsh, and Scottish elections, dropping from a majority to just 9 of 98 Welsh Senedd seats and losing approximately 1,000 of the 2,550 council seats it had defended. Labour ceded Birmingham, Sunderland, Hartlepool, and Leeds. Reform UK gained 34 Welsh Senedd seats and more than 1,000 councillors and now polls at 27% nationally — nearly double its 2024 general election result of 14%, and ahead of the Conservatives (20%) and Labour (15%). The Greens recorded their first mayoral win, in Hackney.

As Thomas Scripps wrote in the WSWS, Labour’s collapse is the result of the Starmer government’s delivery of austerity “in the interests of the bond markets” while ramping up military spending after promising change; young people and Muslim voters defected to the Greens chiefly over Gaza. The piece places Britain within a broader European pattern of social-democratic collapse and far-right growth (Italy, France, Germany, Austria, Romania, Czech Republic). Mainstream British coverage treats the Reform surge as a populist insurgency to be managed by the existing parties; the WSWS frames it as the political vacuum produced by Labour’s open identification with finance capital and imperialism.

7. China shadows Balikatan 2026 with warships and nuclear-capable bombers

The PLA Southern Theater Command deployed four surface vessels to shadow Balikatan 2026 — the seven-nation drill running April 20 to May 8 involving the US, Philippines, Japan, Australia, New Zealand, Canada, and France. China additionally deployed two H-6 bombers armed with anti-ship missiles near Scarborough Shoal during the exercises, a significant escalatory signal. The PLA Navy carrier Liaoning transited the Taiwan Strait southbound on April 20 as the exercises commenced.

Taiwan’s Ocean Affairs Minister Kuan Bi-ling visited Taiping Island in the Spratlys and oversaw exercises that analysts at the American Enterprise Institute characterized as “calculated assertiveness,” with coast guard special task units and armed boarding footage deliberately publicized. Analysts describe China as pursuing a multi-domain strategy involving military posturing, cyber-espionage, and economic coercion to isolate Taiwan ahead of Trump’s May 14 Beijing summit with Xi. The militarization of the South China Sea is being driven by inter-imperialist competition rather than any genuine threat to the smaller nations of the region.

One year on from Operation Sindoor, India-Pakistan ceasefire holds amid deepening risk

May 9 marks one year since the closing days of the four-day India-Pakistan war that began on May 7, 2025 — the most serious confrontation between two nuclear-armed states in nearly three decades. The 88-hour conflict began after India struck Lashkar-e-Taiba and Jaish-e-Mohammed headquarters; Pakistan retaliated with missile and drone strikes on Indian military bases; a ceasefire took effect May 10. The truce has technically held for a year, but analysts at The Diplomat warn of “rising risks and deepening instability.”

Key factors identified: - Compressed escalation timelines that leave decision-makers little space for off-ramps - Louder domestic political pressures in both Delhi and Islamabad - Weakened external constraints — the US is distracted by Iran and Ukraine - A growing belief in both capitals that nuclear escalation can be “managed” — a dangerous illusion in a region where command-and-control assumptions have never been tested - A January 2026 handshake between Pakistani and Indian officials in Dhaka raised hopes of dialogue, but substantive normalization remains elusive

Lula meets Trump at White House, dropping prior criticism

Brazilian President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva met Trump at the White House on May 7. United flight attendants vote on second contract as Spirit collapse rocks industry

Approximately 30,000 United Airlines flight attendants are voting on a second tentative contract this week after rejecting the first 71%-to-29% on 92% turnout. The workforce has had no pay raises since 2020, despite the contract becoming negotiable in August 2021. The headline $100/hour rate applies only to attendants with 13+ years of service and only takes effect July 30, 2030; a worker who earned $60,000 in 2024 would receive $2,400 in back pay for that year. The AFA-CWA invoked the Railway Labor Act of 1926 to block strikes for 20 months despite a 99.99% strike authorization vote. Workers quoted in the WSWS describe voting yes “under duress” due to exhaustion and housing costs.

The vote occurs a week after Spirit Airlines collapsed on May 2, eliminating 17,000 jobs without severance — a development that received only modest coverage in the bourgeois press. Jet fuel prices have surged from approximately $80/barrel in March to $179/barrel by April 24, the direct result of the US-Iran war. As Bryan Dyne wrote on the WSWS, the convergence of fuel-cost pressure, mass airline bankruptcy, and union-bureaucratic strike suppression is producing an industry-wide crisis whose only socialist solution is the expropriation of the airlines as a public utility.

US measles cases surge past 1,800; elimination status under threat

As of May 7, the US has recorded 1,842 confirmed measles cases in 2026 across 25 outbreaks, with 93% of cases outbreak-associated, according to CDC and CIDRAP analyses. The US is reporting more than 90 cases per 10 million people — versus the elimination threshold of fewer than 1 per 10 million — and has missed 4 of 7 CDC elimination markers. The Pan American Health Organization has pushed its US measles elimination status review to November 2026. Loss of elimination status would be the first such reversal since the disease was declared eradicated in the US in 2000.

H5N1 fatal pediatric case in Bangladesh

WHO tracked 13 avian/swine influenza cases in people in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh and three cases in Cambodia. US dairy herds and domestic poultry remain affected; CDC surveillance shows no unusual influenza activity in people in the US as of May 1. The Bangladesh pediatric fatality underscores ongoing zoonotic spillover risk.

MV Hondius hantavirus outbreak spreads

A hantavirus cluster traced to the cruise ship MV Hondius has killed three people and sickened at least eight more, with dozens of exposed travelers dispersed across multiple countries. A new suspected case has emerged on Tristan da Cunha — not a ship passenger — potentially the first secondary infection outside the original ship population. As Evan Blake documented on the WSWS, Trump dismissed the outbreak as something that “should be fine,” directly echoing his 2020 COVID-19 minimization that preceded approximately 1.5 million American and 30 million global excess deaths. The Trump administration eliminated the CREID research program in June 2025, which studied the precise Andes-strain hantavirus now driving the outbreak. RFK Jr. and Jay Bhattacharya have made no public statement.

NEJM: oral semaglutide 25 mg achieves 13.6% weight loss

The New England Journal of Medicine on May 7 published the OASIS 4 phase-III trial showing oral semaglutide 25 mg produced a mean −13.6% body weight change vs. −2.2% for placebo over 71 weeks (difference −11.4 percentage points; p<0.001). Thirty percent of the treatment group achieved ≥20% weight loss vs. 3% on placebo. The FDA subsequently approved the formulation. The pill establishes a clinically meaningful alternative to injectable semaglutide (Wegovy) and could broaden — though the price structure is not yet public — access to obesity pharmacotherapy.

EU AI Act high-risk rules pushed to 2027–2028

The Council of the EU and the European Parliament reached a provisional agreement on May 7 to push Annex III standalone high-risk AI compliance to December 2, 2027, and embedded-product AI to August 2, 2028 — both originally due in August 2026. The deal also bans AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (“nudifiers”). The delay is a significant concession to industry lobbying under the EU’s Digital Omnibus initiative and deepens the regulatory divergence between the EU’s comprehensive legal framework and the US federal approach, which remains effectively voluntary.

Big Tech AI capex hits $725B as workforces are gutted

Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, and Alphabet have collectively signaled approximately $725 billion in 2026 capital expenditures — up more than 75% year-over-year — almost entirely directed at AI infrastructure. Simultaneously: Meta is cutting 8,000 employees this month; Amazon has shed roughly 30,000 roles recently; Microsoft has offered voluntary buyouts to approximately 125,000 employees. OpenAI has surpassed $25 billion in annualized revenue and Anthropic is approaching $19 billion. The simultaneous surge in infrastructure investment and mass workforce reduction marks a structural shift across the technology sector, not a cyclical one.

J. M. Coetzee denounces Israel’s “genocidal campaign in Gaza”

The most significant cultural development of the past 24 hours is the public release of Nobel laureate J. M. Coetzee’s letter declining an invitation to Jerusalem’s International Writers Festival. The 86-year-old novelist wrote that Israel has conducted “a genocidal campaign in Gaza that has been vastly disproportionate to the murderous provocation of 7 October 2023,” and that “long-time supporters of Israel have turned away in revulsion.” Coetzee’s intervention is freighted with personal history: he received the Jerusalem Prize in 1987 — at the height of his international renown for Waiting for the Barbarians and Life and Times of Michael K — and his subsequent decision to attend the 2016 Palestine Festival of Literature, where he compared Israel’s system to apartheid, marked a public turning point.

As David Walsh wrote on the WSWS, Coetzee’s stance must be understood in continuity with the moral concerns of his fiction: the victimization of the colonized, the violence of state power, the responsibility of the writer to bear witness. The festival’s director, Fermentto-Tzaisler, predictably defended Israeli military operations and presented Coetzee’s withdrawal as politically motivated; the response evades the substance of what a writer of Coetzee’s stature has said. The WSWS lists Annie Ernaux, J. M. G. Le Clézio, Arundhati Roy, and Sally Rooney among other Nobel laureates and prominent writers who have publicly condemned the Gaza campaign.

The Coetzee letter is cultural news of the highest order because it reflects the sharpening polarization within world literature over imperialism. The intelligentsia that came of age in the 1960s and 1970s — formed by anti-colonial struggles, the Vietnam War, and the South African anti-apartheid movement — is finding itself, in many cases, on a different side from the cultural institutions that once celebrated it. The bourgeois cultural press treats the denunciation as a “PR problem” for the festival; what is in fact happening is a moral repudiation of the political machinery that built a literary establishment willing to absorb genocide.

Arts and culture roundup

The available material is dominated by the Coetzee story; the research agent’s arts and culture pass timed out before broader coverage could be compiled. Items to note in continuing coverage in the days ahead:

  • The widening cultural BDS movement, in which mid- and late-career writers — those least vulnerable to commercial reprisal — increasingly speak openly while many younger writers remain silent under contractual and reputational pressure
  • The continuing question of cultural institutions (festivals, museums, foundations) financially entangled with Israeli state cultural diplomacy, and how their boards are responding to the public withdrawals
  • The contrast between the literary establishment’s response to Russian state violence in Ukraine and to Israeli state violence in Gaza, which exposes the political conditioning of “universal” humanist values

The political importance of Coetzee’s intervention is that it recasts the writer as a moral actor in a period when that role has been hollowed out by the marketization of literary life. The WSWS framework is correct to insist that art cannot be evaluated in isolation from its social and historical context — and that an author’s refusal to lend his name to a state that is committing a genocide is itself an artistic-political act of the first order.

A magnitude 4.5 earthquake — the largest ever recorded in the Central NSW region — struck on April 14 near Newmont’s Cadia Valley gold and copper mine (~35 km from Orange), trapping 153 workers underground in refuge chambers for over 10 hours. University of Melbourne geophysicist Dan Sandiford stated that for a site experiencing two M4+ quakes, “most seismologists would certainly suggest it’s very likely that the mining there is responsible for stress changes that would have led to seismic slip.” Geoscience Australia noted that only ~100 publicly accessible seismic monitors exist across all of Australia, with the nearest to Cadia 90+ km away — a deliberate state of monitoring under-investment that allows induced seismicity to be denied. Australia: Coal mine company handed small fine for “alarming” death of worker — Mike Head

Mastermyne Crinum Operations — Queensland subsidiary of ASX-listed Mastermyne Group, operating for Japanese owner Sojitz Corporation — was fined $7 million for the industrial manslaughter of 62-year-old Graham Dawson, killed in a September 2021 roof collapse at Crinum underground coal mine. It is Queensland’s first industrial manslaughter conviction since the offense became law in 2020, and only the sixth nationally ever. Judge Clarke found that shotcrete was applied without steel support beams and without risk assessment, calling the lack of safety consultation “alarming.” The fine is less than Mastermyne’s reported H2 2025 net profit ($4.1 million). Queensland recorded 53 worker deaths in 2024, the highest of any Australian state. The piece is a structural indictment: industrial-manslaughter laws have been enacted, but the level of fines is calibrated below the rate of profit, ensuring that the law operates as a regulatory cost item rather than a deterrent.
