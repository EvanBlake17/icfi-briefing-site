1. US–Iran war reaches day 69 as Trump pauses Hormuz escort, MOU framework emerges

President Trump declared “Operation Epic Fury” — launched on February 28 — formally “concluded” and paused the US Navy’s escort mission through the Strait of Hormuz, citing what he called “great progress” and a personal request from the Pakistani government. Negotiators are now drafting a one-page memorandum of understanding that would open a 30-day window to resolve frozen Iranian assets, the nuclear file, and Hormuz security. Iran’s core demand — settle Hormuz first, nuclear last — appears to have been implicitly conceded by Washington. Netanyahu told reporters there is “full coordination” between the US and Israeli governments on the talks.

The military reality, however, was anything but a wind-down. As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, three US Navy destroyers transited the Strait on May 5, drew Iranian defensive fire, and the Pentagon retaliated with strikes on Iranian targets — confirming the war’s persistence even as diplomacy proceeds. Trump told a Lincoln Memorial audience Iran would “become one big glow” if talks stalled.

Key facts:

  • Iran death toll: between 3,468 and 6,000+ military and 1,701 confirmed civilian (HRANA through April 7); US: 15 soldiers killed, 538 wounded; Lebanon: 2,659+ killed; Israel: 18 soldiers and 28 civilians killed.
  • The Strait carries roughly 20 percent of global oil and gas; the crisis has trapped about 13 million barrels per day of crude supply.
  • World Bank projects a 24 percent rise in energy prices and a 31 percent rise in fertilizer costs in 2026 directly attributable to the war.
  • Trump’s approval has collapsed to 34 percent, with 61 percent of Americans — including 25 percent of Republicans — opposing the war (Damon, citing The Intercept polling); US gasoline now stands at $4.50/gallon and credit-card debt at a record $1.28 trillion at 21 percent APR.

2. European naval flotilla deploys to the Strait of Hormuz

France’s nuclear-powered aircraft carrier Charles de Gaulle, British destroyers, and warships from Germany, Greece, Spain and Italy are now en route to the Strait of Hormuz under what their governments describe as a “neutral” mission to protect commercial shipping. The deployment is being reported alongside an EU energy-supply scramble: Israel has agreed to supply jet fuel to Germany at Berlin’s request, the first time Berlin has sourced fuel from Tel Aviv, signaling that the Hormuz blockade is now reshaping European supply chains. The deployment was formalized at the European Political Community summit in Yerevan, where the EU also advanced its “Global Gateway” infrastructure corridor — including the so-called “Trump Route for International Peace and Prosperity” through Armenia.

As Peter Schwarz argued on the WSWS, the framing of the flotilla as humanitarian shipping protection is a fiction: each European power is pursuing distinct strategic interests in the Middle East and competing with Washington for postwar influence — a dynamic Schwarz characterizes as imperialist powers “caught in the spiral of an escalating world war.”

3. WHO confirms Andes hantavirus cluster on cruise ship MV Hondius

The WHO held an emergency briefing on May 7 confirming eight cases and three deaths of Andes hantavirus aboard the Dutch-flagged expedition cruise ship MV Hondius. The strain — endemic to Latin America — is the only known hantavirus capable of human-to-human transmission and carries a 38 to 40 percent case fatality rate, “roughly 40 times that of COVID-19” per Acting Director Maria Van Kerkhove. The deceased are a Dutch couple and a German national; a British physician who treated patients on board is in intensive care, and a KLM flight attendant who had brief contact with a dying disembarkee has been hospitalized in Amsterdam with mild symptoms — the first potential secondary case outside the ship.

The timeline reveals a catastrophic regulatory failure. The index case developed fever on April 6 and died overnight on April 11. The ship’s doctor took no samples, ordered no isolation, and the captain told remaining passengers the death was “not infectious.” The body remained aboard for 13 days while passengers continued to dine communally without masks. South Africa’s NICD identified the virus on April 27. The WHO was not formally notified under International Health Regulations until May 2 — three weeks after the first death — by which time 30 disembarkees had dispersed across 12 countries on commercial flights with no testing or contact tracing.

Key facts:

  • 2,500 diagnostic kits have been shipped from Argentina to laboratories in five countries; passengers from the US to Singapore are being tracked.
  • WHO and a CDC–State Department joint statement insist public health risk is “extremely low”; Tedros publicly stated, “This is not the start of a COVID pandemic.”
  • Argentina has also reported a separate spillover cluster traced to environmental rodent contact.
  • US public-health response capacity has been hollowed out: the CDC’s Vessel Sanitation Program — the sole American institution oriented to cruise ships — was abolished in April 2026, and the US withdrew from the WHO in January, severing IHR notification channels.

4. Sudan war enters fourth year with UN confirmation of “hallmarks of genocide”

Sudan’s civil war has entered its fourth year with what the UN Human Rights Council now formally describes as situations exhibiting “clear hallmarks of genocide” by the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) against the Zaghawa and Fur communities around El-Fasher. Roughly 400,000 people have died, 11.5 million have been displaced — the largest displacement crisis on earth — and 28.9 million people, more than half the country’s population, are acutely food insecure. Civilian killings rose by more than 2.5 times in 2025 versus 2024, with at least 11,300 confirmed civilian dead.

The Sudan Doctors Network reports the RSF is currently holding 20 doctors, more than 1,470 civilians, and 907 military personnel in detention centers in El-Fasher under “dire conditions including killings during torture and ethnically motivated killings.” The World Food Programme requires $610 million to operate from March through August 2026; international aid for all of 2026 is only 40 percent funded. Save the Children projects 4.2 million acute malnutrition cases for 2026, including 800,000 cases of severe acute malnutrition among children. Western diplomatic pressure on the RSF’s Gulf-state backers — most prominently the United Arab Emirates — remains essentially nonexistent.

5. Tennessee Republicans eliminate state’s only majority-Black congressional district

Tennessee Governor Bill Lee signed a new congressional map into law on May 7, less than two hours after it cleared the special-session legislature. The map splits Memphis — a majority-Black city — into three congressional districts, eliminating the state’s only majority-Black House district and threatening the seat of Tennessee’s sole Democratic representative, Steve Cohen. To pass the map, Republicans first repealed a 50-year-old state law barring mid-decade redistricting. Democracy Docket reports it is the first congressional map approved as a direct result of the Supreme Court’s recent ruling gutting Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act.

Key facts:

  • Tennessee Democrats characterized the move as a “Jim Crow-era voter suppression tactic”; one Memphis state legislator publicly called for the city to consider seceding from the state.
  • The map is part of a coordinated, Trump-directed nationwide strategy to protect the Republicans’ slim US House majority ahead of the November 2026 midterms.
  • Multiple other Republican-controlled states are openly watching the legal outcome to determine whether they will replicate the Tennessee maneuver.

6. Russia–Ukraine war: 9,113 kamikaze drones launched in a single 24-hour period

The Russia–Ukraine war has reached day 1,535 of large-scale Russian aggression, with Ukraine’s General Staff reporting 208 combat engagements over the past 24 hours as of 08:00 May 8. Russian forces deployed 9,113 kamikaze drones, dropped 292 guided aerial bombs across 99 airstrikes, fired 1 missile, and conducted 3,126 shelling attacks including 131 using multiple-launch rocket systems. Ukrainian defenders repelled 33 assault actions in the Pokrovsk direction alone, 23 in Huliaipole, 13 in Lyman, and 4 in Kupiansk. Kyiv claims Russian losses for the day at 1,130 personnel, 91 artillery systems, 1,817 UAVs, 17 armored vehicles, 5 air-defense systems and 1 tank.

The war remains the largest conventional conflict in Europe since World War II. The drone tempo — exceeding 9,000 launches in a single day — represents an unprecedented industrial scale of aerial attrition warfare, dependent on supply chains spanning Iranian Shahed-pattern designs, Chinese components, and an expanding Russian domestic production base. Western European deployments to the Middle East are now diverting munitions and air-defense capacity away from Ukraine just as the drone war intensifies.

Private credit market triples in size since 2019, FSB warns of systemic opacity

A new Financial Stability Board report has warned of accumulating systemic risks in private credit, a market that has expanded ten-fold since 2009 to between $1.5 and $2 trillion globally — with a three-fold increase in US private credit since 2019 and annual growth of 16–17 percent in Canada and the UK. US measles cases surpass 1,814 across 24 outbreaks in 2026

The CDC has recorded 1,814 confirmed measles cases in 2026 across 24 outbreaks, with 93 percent of cases (1,688) linked to active outbreaks per CIDRAP. A record-breaking South Carolina outbreak was declared over on April 27. The figure makes 2026 one of the two worst years for measles in the US since 2000. The outbreaks have been driven primarily by under-vaccinated communities and amplified by the Trump administration’s gutting of CDC outbreak-response capacity.

H5N1 bird flu spreading across more species and continents than ever recorded

CDC reports no unusual influenza activity in US human populations as of May 1. Globally, however, the WHO has tracked 13 avian/swine flu cases in humans during Q1 2026, including a fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh and three cases in Cambodia. Human-to-human transmission remains rare, but the unprecedented geographic and species spread of H5N1 — across more continents and more mammalian species than ever recorded — substantially increases the probability of an adaptive mutation that would enable sustained human transmission.

WHO TAG-CO-VAC weighs LP.8.1, NB.1.8.1, XFG and BA.3.2 for fall booster

COVID-19 is declining or likely declining in 31 US states as of April 28 and growing in only 1. The WHO’s Technical Advisory Group on COVID-19 Vaccine Composition (TAG-CO-VAC) is meeting this month to decide the next vaccine composition; variants under evaluation include LP.8.1, NB.1.8.1, XFG and BA.3.2 alongside JN.1 and KP.2. The decision will shape fall 2026 booster formulations globally — and arrives at a moment when US public-health institutions are operating under constraints unprecedented in the post-1945 era following Trump administration funding and personnel cuts.

NEJM final analysis: hemophilia B gene therapy maintains durable benefit at 5 years

The HOPE-B trial’s 5-year follow-up, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, found a 63 percent reduction in annualized bleeding rate after a single infusion of etranacogene dezaparvovec, with mean factor IX levels sustained at 36.1 IU/dL and a 96 percent decrease in bleeding treatment events. The result confirms single-dose gene therapy as a durable, potentially curative approach for hemophilia B, with no major long-term safety signals identified. The therapy nonetheless remains priced beyond the reach of most patients globally — a textbook expression of the contradiction between the productive power of biomedical science and the property relations of pharmaceutical capital.

Pentagon awards classified AI contracts to seven companies; US–China AI talks before May 14–15 summit

The Pentagon announced contracts with seven AI companies for deployment in classified defense systems on May 1, formalizing a shift toward AI-native classified operations that Congress has not addressed legislatively. Arts and culture

The week’s most politically charged cultural development is the release on Netflix of Chavín de Huántar: The Rescue of the Century, a dramatized account of Peru’s 1997 hostage rescue at the Japanese ambassador’s residence. As Armando Cruz argues in a sharp WSWS review, the film — produced with active Peruvian Army advising — functions as state propaganda. By presenting the operation as a binary of heroic soldiers against undifferentiated “national enemies,” it erases the historical context of the Fujimori regime’s death squads, the 1992 self-coup launched with army tanks, and the systematic human-rights violations that produced the MRTA insurgency in the first place. Cruz frames the film as part of a broader effort to restore the Peruvian military’s prestige at a moment when Peru’s Congress holds the lowest approval rating in the world (4–6 percent) and the far right is actively exploiting that institutional crisis to consolidate power. The review situates the film within a global pattern of post-2020 cultural production normalizing security forces and state violence — Top Gun: Maverick, the Mosul genre, and the steady stream of military-cooperation thrillers — while Latin American cinema’s anti-imperialist tradition is starved of state and platform funding.

The German-language book of the moment is Berlin historian Jörg Baberowski’s Am Volk vorbei (Bypassing the People), dissected by Christoph Vandreier on the WSWS as “a deliberate political campaign to secure the AfD a place in government.” Baberowski argues that “populism is a corrective” to liberal elite politics, packaging fascism as a legitimate democratic input rather than its negation. The book’s prominent placement in Süddeutsche Zeitung and FAZ feuilletons — and its uncritical reception by the Frankfurt Allgemeine Sonntagszeitung — reflects the broad German academic establishment’s accommodation to the AfD’s electoral rise. Vandreier traces the lineage to Ernst Nolte’s 1980s historians’ debate (Historikerstreit) revisionism: the same operation by which the crimes of fascism are recast as “responses” to communism.
