1. US-Iran peace talks collapse in Islamabad after 21 hours with no deal

The first direct US-Iran negotiations since the six-week war began collapsed Sunday morning in Islamabad with no agreement reached. US Vice President JD Vance led the American delegation alongside special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner. Iran’s 70-member team was led by Parliamentary Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi.

Iran demanded an end to Israeli attacks on Lebanon and Hezbollah, unfreezing of $6 billion in assets, guarantees around its nuclear program, and the right to charge ships passing through the Strait of Hormuz. The US demanded Iran commit to never developing nuclear weapons. The collapse pushes the existing two-week ceasefire — agreed April 8 — closer to expiration with no clear path to extension. As Kevin Reed noted on the WSWS, over 600 commercial vessels including approximately 400 oil tankers remain stalled in the Persian Gulf while only 22 ships passed through the Strait of Hormuz since the ceasefire began — indicating the blockade’s economic stranglehold is largely intact.

Key facts: - Vance: “The bad news is that we have not reached an agreement. And I think that’s bad news for Iran much more than it’s bad news for the U.S. … they have chosen not to accept our terms” - Ghalibaf: “Our experience in negotiating with the Americans has always been met with failure and broken promises” - Trump declared on social media: “The only reason they are alive today is to negotiate!” - Pakistan pledged to continue mediating

Israel rejected a separate ceasefire with Hezbollah while agreeing to Washington-hosted talks with the Lebanese government, as the 2026 Lebanon war — now in its sixth week since the March 2 invasion — has killed nearly 2,000 militants and civilians combined and displaced approximately 1 million people, roughly 20 percent of Lebanon’s population.

5. Artemis II astronauts return to Earth after historic lunar mission

NASA’s Orion spacecraft splashed down in the Pacific Ocean off San Diego at 5:07 p.m. PDT on April 10, completing a 10-day, 695,081-mile journey around the Moon — the first crewed mission to travel to the lunar vicinity since Apollo 17 in December 1972, more than 53 years ago.

The crew — Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch (all NASA), and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (Canadian Space Agency) — conducted a free-return trajectory with a close lunar flyby, passing within approximately 4,067 miles of the lunar surface at speeds reaching 23,864 mph during re-entry with heat shield temperatures of roughly 3,000°F. The mission sets the stage for Artemis III, which aims to land humans on the Moon.

As Bryan Dyne noted on the WSWS, the mission’s strategic orientation — funneling billions to SpaceX and Blue Origin while countering China’s lunar program to control water ice resources — reveals that space exploration has been increasingly subordinated to capitalist competition and military planning. The mission proceeded “without soaring rhetoric” amid genocide threats and warfare, in stark contrast to the Apollo era.

JBS meatpacking strike enters third week

Over 3,000 workers at the JBS USA meatpacking plant in Greeley, Colorado remain on strike entering their third week — the largest meatpacking strike in more than 40 years. Workers represented by the UFCW are demanding free personal protective equipment and fair wages amid soaring inflation, with management and union negotiators meeting on April 9–10 without resolution.

The strike takes place against the backdrop of the sharpest consumer price increases in years. Meatpacking workers, already among the lowest-paid in the industrial workforce, face the dual squeeze of stagnant wages and escalating costs for food, fuel, and housing. JBS — the world’s largest meatpacker — posted billions in net revenue in 2025, underscoring the gulf between corporate profitability and worker compensation. The Greeley plant is one of the largest beef processing facilities in North America.

Key facts: - Workers demand free PPE — basic safety equipment the company refuses to provide despite massive profits - The strike coincides with tariff-driven food price inflation costing the average US household $1,500 in 2026 - UFCW negotiations have not produced a settlement after multiple sessions - The action is the largest in the meatpacking industry since the 1980s, signaling deepening labor militancy in a sector historically defined by dangerous conditions and low pay

COVID-19 “Cicada” variant BA.3.2 detected in 25+ states

BA.3.2, dubbed “Cicada” for its dormant-then-surge pattern, has been detected in at least 25 states and is present in 20 percent of sequenced sewage samples as of early April. Overall COVID activity remains very low, with 38 states showing declining trends, but the variant’s immune-escape mutations are being closely monitored.

No surge in hospitalizations has been reported yet, but virologists warn that BA.3.2 represents a highly mutated lineage that may partially evade vaccine-induced immunity. The continued emergence of novel variants underscores the inadequacy of the “COVID is over” narrative promoted by both parties, even as public health infrastructure has been systematically defunded.

H5N1 bird flu confirmed in Antarctica for the first time

H5N1 avian influenza has been confirmed as the cause of a die-off killing more than 50 skuas in Antarctica — the first H5N1 wildlife mortality event on the continent. The virus has now reached every major landmass on Earth. In the US, H5N1 continues to circulate in dairy cattle and poultry, with sporadic human cases among farm workers but no unusual influenza activity detected in the general population.

Virologists describe the global spread as “completely out of control,” raising alarm for both wildlife conservation and pandemic preparedness. The failure to contain H5N1 in domestic animal populations — driven by the agribusiness industry’s resistance to testing and culling protocols — mirrors the same profit-over-safety logic that characterized the early COVID response.

Arts and culture

Afrika Bambaataa, hip-hop pioneer and founder of the Universal Zulu Nation, dead at 67. Afrika Bambaataa died April 9 in Pennsylvania from complications of prostate cancer. Born Lance Taylor in the South Bronx, Bambaataa founded the Universal Zulu Nation — which encompassed rappers, taggers, and B-boys — and placed social and political awareness at the center of the early hip-hop cultural movement. His 1982 single “Planet Rock,” which fused breakbeat with Kraftwerk-inspired electronic music, is one of the most influential recordings in popular music history, effectively inventing electro-funk and anticipating the entire trajectory of electronic dance music.

His legacy, however, is deeply shadowed by allegations of child sexual abuse dating to the 1970s, which surfaced publicly in 2016. In 2025, Bambaataa lost a civil case brought by a plaintiff who alleged he was sexually abused and trafficked at age 12 in 1991. Kurtis Blow of the Hip Hop Alliance acknowledged the contradiction: “We recognize that his legacy is complex and has been the subject of serious conversations within our community.” The death raises unresolved questions about the cultural industry’s willingness to separate artistic contribution from personal criminality — and about the structural conditions in impoverished communities that enabled predation to occur unchecked for decades.

The production raises familiar questions about the biopic as hagiography: whose story gets told, what gets suppressed in the service of commercial viability, and how the entertainment industry weaponizes the biographical form to rehabilitate profitable brands.

“Oppose the draft! Build a working class movement against imperialist war!” — SEP and IYSSE

The SEP and IYSSE jointly issued an urgent statement warning that the 2026 NDAA contains a provision — effective December 2026 — automatically registering every American male aged 18–26 for the Selective Service using Social Security Administration records. This replaces the prior system in which 46 states enrolled men through driver’s license databases covering approximately 81 percent of eligible men. The new law federalizes and universalizes registration, making it compulsory, passive, and government-controlled. The statement calls this “a major step toward conscription.”

The military arithmetic is stark: the US active-duty force numbers approximately 1.3 million, but a sustained ground campaign in Iran — with four times the land area and three times the population of Iraq — would require an estimated 300,000–500,000 troops, far exceeding what the all-volunteer system can provide. The legislation is bipartisan: Democratic Representative Chrissy Houlahan sponsored automatic registration, the NDAA passed the House 312–112 and the Senate 77–20, and White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt refused to rule out a draft on March 8, saying it was “not part of the current plan right now, but the president again wisely keeps his options on the table.”

Internationally, the statement cites Germany’s December 2025 Bundestag vote approving a military service questionnaire for all 18-year-old men and France’s announcement of a voluntary military service scheme, with French General Fabien Mandon declaring France must be prepared to “lose its children” in a war with Russia. Trump’s FY2027 budget requests $1.5 trillion for the Pentagon — described as “the largest Pentagon request in history” — while driving non-defense spending to its lowest share of GDP since the 1950s.

The SEP demands immediate repeal of automatic registration, withdrawal from the Middle East, abolition of the standing army, an end to the “economic draft” that coerces enlistment through poverty, and redirection of war spending to healthcare, education, housing, and jobs.

  • Jacobin / DSA — Multiple articles framing the Iran ceasefire as “a stunning defeat for militarism” while promoting the trade union bureaucracy as a vehicle for democratic revival against Trumpism; the overall orientation channels anti-war and anti-austerity sentiment into reformist and Democratic Party-adjacent politics

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin published several articles on the Iran war. “The Iran Ceasefire Is a Stunning Defeat for Militarism” by Branko Marcetic (April 8) argues that Trump was forced into the ceasefire because the war became “strategically and politically untenable within just six weeks.” While correctly identifying limits to US imperial power, the article frames opposition to war as a matter of rational imperial management rather than class struggle, criticizing Democrats for “goading Trump toward war” without examining the bipartisan class interests driving imperialist aggression. The piece suggests that peace depends partly on “allowing Trump to claim victory while Democrats refrain from undermining negotiations” — effectively counseling the working class to rely on a faction of the ruling class for peace. “On Iran, Trump and the American Empire Blinked” advances a similar thesis, arguing “even the vast power of the globe’s foremost imperial hegemon has limits” — imperial overreach framing that avoids the question of working-class agency.

“The Fight Against Trumpism Can Reinvigorate Labor” by Stephen Lerner and Joseph McCartin (April 9) argues unions should leverage the anti-Trump crisis for movement revival, citing Brazil, South Africa, and South Korea as precedents. This is classic DSA reformism: it promotes illusions that the existing trade union bureaucracy can serve as a vehicle for democratic revival. The article rejects “magical thinking” about electoral victories while channeling workers back into the very union structures that have overseen decades of declining strike participation — from 12.1 percent of union members in 1955 to merely 1 percent annually in recent decades — and systematically suppressed independent class struggle.

Sources Jacobin The Iran ceasefire is a stunning defeat for militarism · Jacobin The fight against Trumpism can reinvigorate labor

Left Voice

Left Voice covered the Iran war and protest movements with a more critical edge toward the Democratic Party than Jacobin. “Trump’s Ultimatum to Iran Could Backfire” analyzes the crisis of conscience among US troops triggered by the Iran war, reflecting Left Voice’s connection to the PTS tradition of engaging with military questions — but stopping short of calling for independent working-class opposition to war. “Why Young People Joined the Latest No Kings Protests” (April 10) reports on youth radicalization through No Kings demonstrations, but frames opposition primarily through identity categories — immigrants, trans people, anti-war youth — rather than as a class question requiring independent political organization and a break with the Democrats. “Promises, Promises: AOC, Israel, and the DSA” by James Dennis Hoff (April 7) correctly exposes AOC’s betrayals on Palestine and the DSA’s adaptation to the Democratic Party, but Left Voice’s own framework — which promotes “revolutionary” parties participating in bourgeois elections, as the PTS does in Argentina — represents the same fundamental adaptation at one remove.
