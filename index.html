Trump prepares new offensive against Iran days after Beijing summit

Less than 72 hours after returning from his state visit to Beijing, Donald Trump and his national security team are actively preparing for the resumption of major military operations against Iran, with options under formal consideration that include the deployment of US ground troops inside Iranian territory. The New York Times reported on May 17 that “the United States and Israel are engaged in intense preparations — the largest since the cease-fire took effect — for the possible resumption of attacks against Iran as early as next week.”

Trump posted on Truth Social late Sunday, after a phone call with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu: “For Iran, the Clock is Ticking, and they better get moving, FAST, or there won’t be anything left of them.” He paired the message with an AI-generated image of arrows converging on Iran from every direction; earlier in the week he had posted an image of himself pressing a red button with mushroom clouds visible on a screen behind him — a deliberate nuclear menace.

As Andre Damon wrote in the WSWS Perspective today, “Having launched the war, Trump has staked the prestige of American imperialism on subjugating Iran. Failure to achieve that aim is seen by the ruling class as a catastrophe that would accelerate the collapse of the dollar-denominated financial order on which American capitalism’s solvency depends.” The escalation is being openly demanded across the bourgeois press: a Sunday Wall Street Journal op-ed by former Reagan/Bush deputy navy undersecretary Seth Cropsey, titled “How to Finish the Job in Iran,” explicitly called for “a multistage operation, including boots on the ground, that forcibly reopens the Strait of Hormuz to accelerate the collapse of the Iranian state.”

Key facts:

  • A Saturday war-council session at Trump’s Virginia golf club included VP JD Vance, Secretary of State Marco Rubio, CIA Director John Ratcliffe, and Middle East envoy Steve Witkoff; Axios reported Trump will convene his national security team in the Situation Room on Tuesday.
  • Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi told state media Sunday that Tehran “cannot trust the Americans at all” but is attempting to maintain the Pakistan-brokered April 7–8 ceasefire “to give diplomacy a chance.”
  • The Strait of Hormuz remains largely shut to commercial shipping. Iran’s parliament security commission chief Ebrahim Azizi announced Saturday that Iran will charge “necessary fees” from only “cooperating” vessels using a designated transit route.
  • Operation “Epic Fury” — the joint US-Israeli opening bombardment of February 28 — included nearly 900 strikes in 12 hours, killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, and produced ~170 civilian deaths in a single strike on a girls’ school near Bandar Abbas. The Pentagon Comptroller concedes the war has cost $29 billion already; Harvard economist Linda Bilmes projects the eventual total will reach $1 trillion.
  • Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer’s only public criticism of the Beijing trip was that Trump had been insufficiently hawkish toward China: “For the sake of democracy and the stability of the global economy, Trump must not sell out Taiwan.” Senator Lindsey Graham, on Meet the Press, called for renewed bombing of Iran’s “soft underbelly” energy infrastructure.

Drones strike UAE’s Barakah nuclear plant in unprecedented attack

In a development that marks a dangerous new threshold in the region-wide war set off by Operation Epic Fury, three drones penetrated United Arab Emirates airspace on May 17 from the western border. Two were intercepted; the third struck an electrical generator on the perimeter of the four-reactor Barakah Nuclear Energy Plant in Abu Dhabi’s Al Dhafra region, igniting a fire. One reactor was forced briefly onto emergency diesel power before being restored to the grid. Radiation levels were confirmed normal and no injuries were reported.

IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi issued a statement declaring military activity threatening nuclear facilities “unacceptable” and expressing “grave concern” — language the agency has used sparingly since the early phase of the Russia-Ukraine war. The UAE called the incident a “treacherous terrorist attack” and launched an investigation; no group has claimed responsibility. Saudi Arabia and Qatar both condemned the strike.

Iran has previously warned that Gulf states hosting US military bases could become targets, and the UAE has faced repeated Iranian-attributed drone and missile attacks since February 28. The strike on a working nuclear facility — even on a peripheral generator — confirms the structural pattern long warned against by the ICFI: an imperialist war launched in pursuit of “regime change” has now extended into terrain where a single misjudgment could produce a Chernobyl-scale catastrophe.

Ukraine launches largest drone barrage of the war; Russia retaliates with 524 drones

Ukraine launched what Russian air defense authorities described as the largest single drone barrage of the war on May 16–17 — more than 1,000 drones in a 24-hour period. Moscow Mayor Sergei Sobyanin reported 81 drones intercepted as they bore down on the Russian capital, with at least 12 wounded near the entrance to the city’s principal oil refinery. A woman was killed in Khimki in northwest Moscow; two men died in the village of Pogorelki, 10 km north of the capital. One man was killed in Belgorod region when a drone struck a truck. Debris landed near a Moscow airport.

President Volodymyr Zelenskyy declared the strikes “entirely justified” and hailed Ukraine’s growing long-range capability as a “significant” shift in momentum. Russia retaliated overnight May 17–18 with 524 drones and 22 missiles (including 14 ballistic missiles); Ukrainian air defenses claimed 503 drones and four missiles intercepted. At least 20 people, including two children, were wounded across central and southern Ukraine. Ukrainian forces recorded 234 combat clashes with Russian forces in the past 24 hours, with the heaviest fighting in the Pokrovsk and Huliaipole sectors.

Putin said Sunday he is ready to hold direct talks with Zelenskyy “in Moscow or a neutral country,” after signaling earlier in the week that the war may be nearing an end. Zelenskyy has continued to demand a “long-term, reliable and guaranteed” ceasefire backed by security guarantees; France and the United Kingdom have proposed installing “military hubs” inside Ukraine as part of any peace settlement — an arrangement that would constitute permanent NATO infrastructure on Russia’s border. Both sides simultaneously declared competing unilateral ceasefires around May 4–9; neither held, with Ukraine reporting more than 400 Russian violations during an alleged Easter truce.

Russia has lost an estimated 1,350,010 troops since February 24, 2022, according to Ukrainian military counts, including 1,220 casualties in the last 24 hours.

Moody’s strips US of last triple-A rating; 30-year Treasury yields breach 5%

Late on Friday May 16, Moody’s cut the United States sovereign credit rating from Aaa to Aa1 (outlook stable), becoming the last of the three major agencies to remove the triple-A status the US had held since 1917. The agency cited the trajectory of federal debt — projected to reach 134% of GDP by 2035, up from 98% in 2024 — and annual deficits running at roughly 7% of GDP and rising to ~9% by 2034. The 30-year Treasury yield jumped above 5% in the immediate aftermath. The Dow lost 537 points Friday (−1.07%); the S&P 500 fell 1.24% and the Nasdaq 1.54%. Asian and European indices opened sharply lower Monday.

The downgrade lands at the moment Washington is preparing renewed war on Iran and Pentagon spending is being expanded — Trump signed a $445 billion defense increase earlier this year — while Treasury must finance escalating deficits at sharply higher interest rates. The contradiction sharpens the structural crisis Damon described in today’s WSWS Perspective: an American ruling class that demands both an unfinished war for “regime change” in Tehran and the preservation of the dollar-denominated financial system on which it rests.

Trump-Xi Beijing summit delivers Boeing deal, Taiwan warning, no concessions

Trump’s first state visit to China since 2017 — his first face-to-face with Xi Jinping since October 2025 — concluded May 15 with both leaders agreeing to develop a “constructive China-U.S. relationship of strategic stability.” Beijing is now treating this formula as the framework for the next three-plus years of bilateral relations. The one-year trade truce set in October 2025 continues. Xi accepted Trump’s invitation to visit the United States on September 24.

The substantive achievements were almost entirely Chinese. Xi delivered a direct warning that mishandling Taiwan would put the relationship “in great jeopardy,” telling Trump that Taiwan independence and peace in the Taiwan Strait “are as irreconcilable as fire and water.” Trump announced China will order 200 Boeing jets — more than the 150 Boeing had projected. Analysts at the Washington Post concluded that Xi secured stability without making substantive concessions on trade, technology controls, or Taiwan. CNN’s analysis described the summit as delivering “more vibes than details” — a striking judgment given how regularly Trump-Xi encounters have been billed as historic turning points.

The Democratic Party’s response, as noted above, was to attack Trump from the right for not having been more confrontational. The structural reality the bourgeois press will not concede: in less than 72 hours, the United States moved from a state visit aimed at “strategic stability” with its principal great-power rival to active war planning against Iran in coordination with Israel. The two postures are not contradictory — they are the same policy of escalating US imperialism on every front.

Pentagon confirms 5,000-troop withdrawal from Germany; Trump signals “a lot further”

The Pentagon has confirmed that approximately 5,000 of the roughly 39,000 US troops stationed in Germany will be withdrawn within 6 to 12 months, leaving 30,000+ in place. Pentagon spokesman Sean Parnell framed the cut as following “a thorough review of the Department’s force posture in Europe” and “theater requirements and conditions on the ground.” Trump told reporters Saturday the reduction would go “a lot further than 5,000,” language that alarmed every NATO chancellery on the continent.

The withdrawal follows a sharp clash with German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, who publicly said the United States was being “humiliated” by Iran and criticized Washington’s lack of strategy in the war. The decision reverses the buildup begun under Biden after Russia’s 2022 invasion. Time magazine and Washington Post reporting indicates Spain and Italy could be next; NBC News described European capitals as “rattled” by what one diplomat called a “disastrous trend.”

Key facts:

  • The drawdown coincides with the largest US Middle East military deployment in two decades supporting Operation Epic Fury and the ongoing Hormuz crisis.
  • European NATO partners have been ordered to accelerate their own military buildups: the UK announced a 3% of GDP defense target; Germany itself is mobilizing under a €500 billion fiscal package adopted under the previous Scholz-CDU coalition.
  • Republican lawmakers including Senate Armed Services chair Roger Wicker have privately registered alarm with the White House, according to the Washington Post.

Israel kills Hamas military chief Izz al-Din al-Haddad in Gaza

The strike came in direct violation of the October 9, 2025 ceasefire. Israeli strikes on May 15 killed at least seven people and wounded more than 50, who were taken to Al-Shifa Hospital. Hamas said al-Haddad was killed alongside his wife, his daughter, and other civilians. The IDF formally confirmed the killing the following day.

Gaza partitioned: Netanyahu admits Israel controls 60% beyond ceasefire lines

Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu publicly acknowledged that Israel now controls approximately 60% of the Gaza Strip — territory significantly exceeding the boundaries permitted under the October 9, 2025 ceasefire framework. The admission landed alongside a UN Security Council briefing this week from the UN Deputy Special Coordinator warning that Gaza risks becoming “permanently divided” under current conditions.

Reconstruction cannot begin because Israeli troop withdrawal, the future Palestinian governance arrangement, and Hamas’ status remain unresolved. The material consequence is that two million Palestinians remain in a hardening occupation zone with no functioning economy, no rebuilding of housing or infrastructure, and no resolution to the population displacements imposed during the 2023–25 destruction.

Lebanon remains in catastrophe seven months after Israel’s limited ground invasion that began March 17, 2026, targeting Hezbollah positions toward the Litani River. Hormuz shipping shutdown crushes developing economies

The UN Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) released its May 2026 Global Trade Update finding that non-tariff measures now drive more trade costs than tariffs in 88% of countries, and that the cumulative impact of the Trump tariff regime and the Hormuz disruption has produced the sharpest cost increases for the poorest nations. Global tariff rates rose in 2025 by 10% for developed countries, 16% for developing countries, and 18% for least-developed countries; tariffs on South Asia and Latin America exports roughly doubled. Roughly 20% of global oil normally transits the Strait of Hormuz, which has been operating at severely reduced capacity since late February.

LIRR strike: 3,500 rail workers walk out; first strike in 32 years

Approximately 3,500 Long Island Rail Road workers walked off the job at midnight Friday in the line’s first strike since 1994, coinciding with contract expiration for 40,000 New York City transit workers across the MTA system. The dispute centers on stagnating real wages against rising New York-area cost of living. The walkout disrupted Monday morning rush-hour service across one of the densest rail commuter networks in North America.

The political development with the sharpest class implication is the role of Zohran Mamdani, the DSA-backed mayor of New York City, who has openly supported management’s deployment of replacement shuttle buses — an act of strikebreaking. Mamdani’s first budget, criticized this week by Left Voice as well as the WSWS, manages austerity rather than challenging it. The WSWS today calls for the formation of rank-and-file organizing committees independent of the union bureaucracy to coordinate action across the MTA system.

Nexteer autoworkers force UAW to schedule strike vote after rejecting two sellouts

1,300 UAW members at Nexteer Automotive in Saginaw, Michigan have rejected two successive concessionary contracts by overwhelming margins (96% and 73%), forcing the union to schedule a strike authorization vote for May 20. The Nexteer revolt joins a 98% strike authorization at American Axle, signaling an accelerating wave of rank-and-file rebellion against UAW concession bargaining.

Federal criminal charges over Baltimore Key Bridge collapse

Federal prosecutors filed criminal charges against Synergy Marine Pte Ltd., Synergy Maritime Pte Ltd., and technical superintendent Radhakrishnan Karthik Nair for the March 26, 2024 MV Dali collision that destroyed Baltimore’s Francis Scott Key Bridge and killed six construction workers. The loose-wire power failure that disabled the vessel was a known flaw: two sister ships had experienced the identical problem, and the Dali itself had blacked out twice in port the day before the disaster without investigation or reporting. The federal indictment also exposes decades of government failure to retrofit the 1977-built bridge with modern collision barriers.

WHO declares Ebola PHEIC as Trump cuts gut global surveillance

On May 16, the World Health Organization declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) for a Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in DRC and Uganda. There are 246 suspected cases and 80 suspected deaths so far; the true counts are likely substantially higher given diagnostic delays. The Bundibugyo strain carries a 25–40% fatality rate and has no approved vaccines or treatments — all existing countermeasures were developed for the Zaire strain. Strikingly, WHO issued the declaration without convening its Emergency Committee — the first time the procedure has been bypassed in this way.

As Benjamin Mateus documents on the WSWS, the Trump administration bears direct responsibility for the gutted state of the global response: the $100 million STOP Spillover Program was terminated by email in January 2025; USAID was dissolved; the US formally withdrew from WHO on January 22, 2026, removing ~$700 million annually from the agency’s budget; and a CDC gag order has barred all communication with WHO personnel.

US measles cases reach 1,893; on pace to exceed 2025 total

As of May 14, the CDC has recorded 1,893 confirmed measles cases across 40 states plus the District of Columbia, with 93% (1,761 cases) linked to declared outbreaks. Twenty-seven outbreaks have been declared in 2026 alone. The full-year 2025 total was 2,288 — the United States is on track to exceed it well before the year is half over. Kindergarten MMR coverage has slipped from 95.2% in 2019–20 to 92.5% in 2024–25, putting the country below the 95% herd-immunity threshold for community spread.

First human H5N5 case described in NEJM

A New England Journal of Medicine letter describes the first confirmed human infection with H5N5 avian influenza (Eurasian lineage clade 6), expanding the number of avian flu subtypes documented in humans. WHO logged 13 total zoonotic influenza cases globally in Q1 2026, including one fatal H5N1 in a child in Bangladesh. There are now 71 cumulative US human A(H5) cases since February 2024, including two deaths. Sustained human-to-human transmission has not been detected, but the H5N5 case adds significant pressure on already-degraded surveillance systems.

NIST to pre-screen Google, Microsoft, xAI models before public release

On May 5, the NIST Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) announced pre-deployment evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI — joining existing arrangements with Anthropic and OpenAI. CAISI has now conducted 40 evaluations, including on unreleased models. NEC Director Kevin Hassett publicly compared the proposed pre-release vetting framework to the FDA drug-approval process; the trigger was reportedly Anthropic’s new “Mythos” model previewed in early May.

Lancet: tirzepatide must be continued indefinitely to maintain weight loss

The Lancet’s May 16 issue published SURMOUNT-MAINTAIN, finding that continuing tirzepatide at the maximum tolerated dose maintains body-weight reductions and health benefits in adults with obesity, while discontinuation produces rapid regain. The study is the strongest evidence to date that GLP-1/GIP agonist therapy for obesity must be sustained indefinitely — a finding with major implications for Medicare, Medicaid, and private insurance coverage policies as costs run into the tens of thousands of dollars per patient per year.

Also noted: a Nature Communications paper (May 12) describes a novel method for detecting damage to the endothelial lining of microvessels at presymptomatic stages, potentially enabling earlier intervention in cardiovascular, diabetic, and inflammatory diseases. An NEJM trial showed the 3C-like protease inhibitor ensitrelvir significantly reduced COVID-19 transmission in exposed household contacts — the first antiviral demonstrated to provide post-exposure prophylaxis efficacy.

Peter Symonds dissects Dikötter’s anti-communist Red Dawn over China

The most substantial arts and culture intervention from the WSWS today is Peter Symonds’ polemical review of Frank Dikötter’s Red Dawn over China — How Communism Conquered a Quarter of Humanity (Bloomsbury, 2026). Dikötter, affiliated with the Hoover Institution, has produced a roughly 8,000-word treatment of Chinese revolutionary history that Symonds documents as systematically falsifying both the events of the 1925–27 Second Chinese Revolution and the 1949 victory of the Chinese Communist Party.

Symonds’ analysis is significant precisely because it counterposes a Trotskyist account against both Dikötter’s anti-communist liberalism and the Stalinist celebration of “Mao Zedong Thought.” The 1925–27 revolution was crushed not by inherent backwardness but by the deliberate Stalinist subordination of the CCP to the Kuomintang under Chiang Kai-shek — a policy Trotsky opposed and that produced one of the most devastating defeats of the international working class in the twentieth century. The 1949 revolution, by contrast, was a genuine social revolution carried out under conditions Trotsky had not anticipated, producing a deformed workers’ state that has nonetheless transformed material life for a fifth of humanity. Symonds traces how Dikötter’s framing — that the CCP simply “conquered” China — is calibrated to align with the current US imperialist propaganda offensive that frames China as an alien, illegitimate enemy.

The review is a model of cultural criticism in the WSWS tradition: a contemporary book by a credentialed academic, marketed through a prestige publisher, is shown to function as ideological infrastructure for the war drive analyzed in today’s Damon Perspective.

Cannes Festival opens under shadow of Iran war and Gaza partition

The 79th Cannes Film Festival opened May 13 under conditions wholly different from any preceding edition. Festival programmers and the jury — chaired this year by Spanish director Pedro Almodóvar — have faced organized public pressure over the participation of Israeli co-productions and Russian-linked talent. Films directly engaging the wars include a documentary on the displacement of Lebanese civilians during the March 2026 ground invasion and a Ukrainian-French feature dramatizing the early weeks of the Russian invasion of 2022. Iranian filmmaker Mohammad Rasoulof, who has been working in exile, premiered a new feature on the suppression of the “Woman, Life, Freedom” movement.

The festival’s broader significance lies in the way it organizes the encounter between a serious international cinema and the censorship pressures generated by the wars and the platform-economy collapse of independent distribution. Hollywood studio presence is sharply reduced; the major American media conglomerates have continued to cut acquisitions budgets amid streaming consolidation. Independent and international cinema remains the principal terrain where the historical reality of the present period — mass displacement, war, the breakdown of social democratic institutions — can be represented at all.

Major arts institutions face renewed funding crisis as NEA cuts deepen

Trump’s fiscal year 2026 budget proposal zeroes out the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the Corporation for Public Broadcasting. Mid-sized regional orchestras, small theaters, and humanities programs at public universities have reported new rounds of layoffs and program closures in recent weeks. The pattern is not new — Reagan attempted the same in the 1980s — but the cumulative effect of the post-2008 decline of arts education, the platform-economy disruption of recorded music and journalism, and the targeted attacks on “DEI” programming has hollowed out the institutional infrastructure of American cultural life. The arts in the United States, increasingly, exist where they can be commodified or where local working-class and immigrant communities sustain them outside corporate channels.

Australia: Elderly residents of Melbourne apartment complex face forced eviction — Paul Bartizan and Eric Ludlow

Twenty-five primarily elderly, low-income residents of the Oasis complex in St Kilda face forced eviction after community housing association HousingFirst declared the properties “too expensive to operate” and moved to sell them — despite having promised residents these were “forever homes.” The article situates this within the broader Victorian state Labor government policy to demolish 44 public housing towers and replace them with mixed-tenure developments dominated by private capital, and calls for construction workers and residents to organize joint resistance through the Neighbourhood Action Committee. The piece is a useful case study for how Australian Labor-led state governments have become the principal political instrument for the privatization of public housing assets across the country.

Vote ‘NO’ on the AFSCME-University of California contract! — Evelyn Rios

A statement urging UC AFSCME-represented workers to reject a tentative contract agreement accepted by union leadership after it abruptly called off a strike. Science magazine investigates FBI’s witch-hunt against Chinese researchers — Stephen St. Clair

Reports on a Science magazine investigation documenting that since the 2018 launch of the China Initiative, FBI persecution of researchers of Chinese descent has shifted from senior faculty to “junior researchers — postdoctoral fellows and graduate students on temporary visas.” Attorney General Pam Bondi and FBI Director Kash Patel are named as driving the campaign. Lead investigator Special Agent Edward Nieh of Detroit’s counterintelligence division has brought felony charges against six scientists for mislabeling nonhazardous biological material. The WSWS article adds context the Science investigation omits: the March 19, 2026 suicide of postdoctoral researcher Danhao Wang under pressure of these prosecutions — a fact that should be at the center of any honest reporting on the campaign. The piece argues that the FBI campaign is the McCarthyite enforcement arm of the broader US drive to decouple from China.

Canadian imperialism: predator, not prey, in global war — Keith Jones

Speech at the 2026 May Day Online Rally by SEP (Canada) national secretary Keith Jones. Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — open call for a 2028 “left-labor” presidential run inside the Democratic Party orbit; flirtation with “market socialism” as a programmatic alternative
  • Left Voice — sharpest critique on the US left of Mamdani’s first NYC budget as class collaboration; consistent anti-imperialist position on Gaza and Die Linke
  • Liberation News / PSL — unconditional solidarity with Palestinian resistance combined with the campist defense of Cuba and Venezuela without class-independent critique
  • Socialist Alternative — formal independence from the Democrats counterposed to organizational record of Democratic-primary entry; no concrete anti-war program
  • Socialist Worker / SWP UK — Greens framed as symptom of Labour crisis rather than alternative; principled defense of Palestine activists
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP IMT — “Make May Day communist again” rhetoric paired with continued strategic embedding in the Labour Party

Jacobin / DSA

  • “We Need a Left-Labor Presidential Candidate” (jacobin.com) calls for building toward a 2028 left-labor presidential run, explicitly framed around working within or adjacent to the Democratic Party. Anti-Marxist position: direct political support for operating inside a bourgeois party; no independent class program is advanced.
  • “How Socialism in the 21st Century Could Work” (jacobin.com) argues for “market socialism” — markets without capitalists — as an alternative to both central planning and capitalism. Classic social-democratic framework; no challenge to the capitalist state, no perspective of workers’ power.
  • “Mark Carney’s Industrial Policy” (May 17) critiques the Canadian PM’s policy as converting the state into a “guarantor of private capital” while asking workers to trust trickle-down. The critique is sharp but the implicit alternative remains social-democratic state intervention.

Assessment: The defining feature of Jacobin’s output continues to be the channeling of left energy into Democratic Party reform projects. The “left-labor candidate” piece is the clearest statement to date of the perspective that there is no separation between the working class and the party of Wall Street and the wars.

Left Voice

  • “Mamdani’s First Budget Is a Masterclass in Class Collaboration” (May 17, leftvoice.org) is the sharpest critique on the US left of the DSA-backed mayor’s first budget, accurately identifying it as managing austerity rather than challenging it.
  • “Die Linke Leadership Is Attempting to Silence Pro-Palestinian Voices” (leftvoice.org) documents Die Linke’s suppression of Palestine solidarity, tying it to a pattern of capitulation by the German left to pro-Zionist pressure.
  • “YouTube Has Removed Over 700 Videos of Israeli War Crimes” (leftvoice.org) documents Big Tech’s role in suppressing Gaza documentation.

Assessment: The PTS-linked Left Voice continues to maintain greater independence from social-democratic milieus than Jacobin or DSA proper. Its weakness remains the absence of a concrete strategic perspective beyond critique.

Liberation News / PSL

  • “PSL Statement: We Will Stay in the Streets Until Palestine Is Free!” (liberationnews.org) offers unconditional solidarity with Palestinian resistance and demands an end to all US military aid to Israel.
  • “PSL Statement: Cuba Is Not a ‘Threat’ — End the Blockade!” (liberationnews.org) defends Cuba against US sanctions. Anti-Marxist position: standard ML campist defense of the Cuban state without any working-class-independent critique of its internal politics or its dependent foreign-policy alignments.
  • “PSL Statement: We Salute the Resilience of the Palestinian People” marks a Palestinian national date with a mobilization call.

Assessment: PSL is consistent on imperialism abroad but operates with a campist reflex — defense of allied capitalist or Stalinist states without class-independent critique of their leaderships. This reflex produced their accommodation to Putin in 2022 and continues to constrain their analysis of multipolar war.

Socialist Alternative

  • “The Democrats Are Not the Answer to Fight the Right” (May 8, socialistalternative.org) argues both parties serve the billionaire class and that an independent working-class alternative is needed. Formally correct, though SA’s electoral record (running within Democratic primaries, the Sawant Seattle council work that often pressured rather than opposed Democrats) creates organizational tension with this principled stand.
  • “Only Rotten Choices In Their System — We Need A Planned Economy” (May 9) attacks Trump’s $445B military spending increase and counterposes a democratic planned economy. Anti-Marxist position: notably absent is any concrete anti-war analysis of where the military budget goes — no Iran, no Hormuz, no Pentagon Germany withdrawal.
  • “Workers Take Action On May Day” (May 3) reports on May Day work stoppages SA helped organize. Among their more grounded pieces.

Assessment: SA’s formal political line is closer to the WSWS on questions of independence from the Democrats than Jacobin’s, but its organizational practice and absence of a serious anti-war perspective remain the defining contradictions.

Socialist Worker / SWP UK

  • “Elections 2026: Greens Make Huge Breakthrough as Voters Reject Labour at the Ballot Box” (socialistworker.co.uk) frames the Green surge as symptom of Labour’s crisis rather than endorsing the Greens.
  • “Four Palestine Activists Found Guilty of Criminal Damage for Destroying Weapons of Death” (socialistworker.co.uk) defends activists prosecuted for sabotaging arms export infrastructure.

Socialist Appeal / RCP IMT

  • “May Day 2026: Make May Day Communist Again!” (marxist.com) uses the 2026 General Strike centenary to argue for revolutionary communism over reformism and explicitly repudiates Labour and the union bureaucracy. Anti-Marxist position: the rhetoric is sharp but the IMT/RCP’s strategic line of entryism inside the Labour Party remains in place — a fundamental contradiction with the communist branding.

Assessment: The RCP/IMT is the fastest-growing pseudo-left tendency in Britain on its own metrics, and the most rhetorically Marxist. Its political contradiction — communist branding combined with Labour entryism — will define the trajectory of its rapid growth.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The Moody’s downgrade and the dollar-denominated financial order

The May 16 Moody’s downgrade of US sovereign debt from Aaa to Aa1, ending a status the United States held since 1917, is the most consequential financial-system development of the past 72 hours and demands sustained socialist analysis. The ICFI’s perspective on the collapse of the post-1944 dollar order — most recently sketched in Damon’s Iran Perspective — should be extended into a dedicated treatment connecting the downgrade to the war drive, the surging Treasury yields, and the structural insolvency the ruling class will attempt to resolve at workers’ expense.

Recommended reading Moody’s US sovereign rating downgrade announcement

2. Drone strike on UAE Barakah nuclear plant: the war’s nuclear edge

The May 17 drone strike on the Barakah nuclear plant in the UAE — the first attack on a working civilian nuclear facility in the Gulf region — marks a qualitative escalation that the bourgeois press has covered only as a security incident. The WSWS should treat this as the predictable consequence of an imperialist war that has now extended into terrain where Chernobyl-scale catastrophe is one misjudgment away, and as a warning of what an Iran ground invasion would unleash across the entire Gulf.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Drone strike sparks fire at UAE’s Barakah nuclear power plant

3. The civilian massacre at Bandar Abbas — three months on

Three months after Operation Epic Fury opened with a strike that killed approximately 170 civilians at a girls’ school near Bandar Abbas, the event has effectively disappeared from sustained Western coverage even as Trump prepares a renewed offensive. A WSWS feature investigating the Bandar Abbas attack — including the institutional silence of the major bourgeois press, the role of US targeting protocols, and the names and biographies of the dead — would be a powerful contribution at the moment the bombing is set to resume.

Recommended reading Britannica 2026 Iran war

4. The Hormuz blockade and the cascading crisis in the Global South

UNCTAD’s May 2026 Global Trade Update documents that the poorest countries have absorbed the steepest costs of the combined Trump tariff regime and the Hormuz disruption — 18% tariff increases for least-developed countries and a near-doubling of duties on South Asian and Latin American exports. The political consequence — food and fuel inflation, currency collapse, debt distress — is producing the next wave of mass anti-imperialist revolt across the periphery and warrants systematic WSWS analysis.

Recommended reading UNCTAD Global Trade Update — May 2026

5. The structural reconfiguration of US empire: out of Germany, into Iran

The simultaneous withdrawal of 5,000 US troops from Germany — with Trump promising deeper cuts and reports indicating Spain and Italy are next — and the largest US Middle East deployment in two decades reveals a deliberate restructuring of imperial priorities away from European NATO and toward direct control over Middle East energy. The political economy of this shift — military-industrial interests, Gulf monarchies’ role, the eclipse of post-1945 alliance structures — warrants the kind of analytical treatment beyond Trump-Merz horse-race coverage that only the WSWS produces.

Recommended reading Time The US Military Drawdown in Europe Has Only Just Begun

6. Lebanon’s reconstruction crisis and the 1.1 million displaced

Lebanon’s catastrophe — seven months after the Israeli ground invasion of March 2026, with 1.1 million people internally displaced, the central bank’s reserves at historic lows, and the April 16 ceasefire fragile — has effectively dropped out of Western coverage even as the broader Iran war dominates headlines. A WSWS feature combining on-the-ground reporting on the displaced population with analysis of the role of imperialism in producing Lebanon’s collapse would address a major coverage gap.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Iran war live coverage

7. The Nvidia earnings call and the AI capital-investment bubble

Nvidia reports earnings Wednesday May 20 — the most important corporate event of the week and the principal vehicle through which the entire AI capital-investment cycle is mediated. The WSWS has been one of the few outlets connecting AI to militarism (Pentagon-Anthropic, NIST evaluations), to mass layoffs, and to the broader political economy of the US ruling class. A pre- or post-earnings analytic piece would situate Nvidia’s results inside the dual crisis: the productive irrationality of the bubble and the social devastation its underlying technology is being used to inflict.
