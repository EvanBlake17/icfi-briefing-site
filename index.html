1. US–Iran ceasefire expires Wednesday as Tehran refuses negotiations

The 52-day US–Israeli war on Iran enters its decisive phase as the fragile ceasefire expires Wednesday evening Washington time. President Trump called an extension “highly unlikely” without a deal, telling reporters: “They’re going to negotiate, and if they don’t, they’re going to see problems like they’ve never seen before.” Vice President JD Vance departed for Islamabad, Pakistan Tuesday for a second round of nuclear talks Wednesday. 2. Japan scraps post-WWII ban on lethal weapons exports

Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s cabinet approved Tuesday the dismantling of the 1967 Three Principles on Arms Exports, a cornerstone of Japan’s post-WWII pacifist order. Fighter jets, missiles and warships may now be sold abroad for the first time since 1945. “With this amendment, transfers of all defence equipment will in principle become possible,” Takaichi said. At least 17 nations qualify under rules requiring recipients to commit to “use in accordance with the UN Charter.” Bloomberg reports Japan simultaneously signed a $7 billion frigate deal with Australia — Mitsubishi Heavy Industries will build 11 Mogami-class vessels.

Takaichi combined the announcement with a ritual offering to Yasukuni Shrine, which honors more than 1,000 convicted WWII war criminals. China’s Foreign Ministry condemned the policy shift, vowing to “firmly resist Japan’s reckless new-style militarisation.” The timing is calibrated to the Iran war and the US pivot to confront China in the Asia-Pacific — placing Japan’s arms industry, dormant for 80 years, on a footing for imperialist alliance production.

3. 7.7-magnitude earthquake strikes off Iwate, Japan

A magnitude 7.7 earthquake struck at 4:53 p.m. local time Monday in waters off Iwate prefecture on Japan’s northern Pacific coast, felt as far south as Tokyo. The Japan Meteorological Agency initially warned of tsunami waves up to 3 meters; 80 cm waves were detected before the warning was downgraded and then lifted. Prime Minister Takaichi established a crisis management team, telling residents: “Tsunami waves are expected to hit repeatedly. Do not leave safe ground until the warning is lifted.” No casualties had been confirmed at publication.

Separately, the JMA issued a rare elevated megaquake advisory for the Nankai Trough, an 800-km undersea trench, citing a “slightly heightened risk” of a catastrophic event in the coming week. The advisory is the first since the system was revised after the 2011 Tohoku disaster.

5. Sudan civil war enters fourth year with 400,000 dead

The war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces (RSF), now entering its fourth year, has produced what the UN calls the world’s largest displacement catastrophe. Estimated dead: 400,000, with tens of thousands more lost to famine and disease. UN Under-Secretary-General Tom Fletcher said bluntly, “the world is failing Sudan.”

Key facts: - 34 million people — 65% of Sudan’s population — in urgent need of humanitarian assistance - 14 million displaced: 9 million internally, 4.4 million fled to Chad, Egypt and South Sudan - Nearly 700 civilians killed in drone strikes in the first three months of 2026 alone - 61.7% of the population now acutely food insecure - UN Fact-Finding Mission concluded both SAF and RSF committed war crimes including killings, torture, and indiscriminate attacks on civilians

Both belligerents are armed by regional powers (UAE to RSF, Egypt and Saudi Arabia to SAF), with Russia and Iran also drawn in — a textbook proxy conflict that receives a fraction of the media attention devoted to Ukraine or Gaza.

US measles cases hit 1,748 in 33 jurisdictions

The CDC confirmed 1,748 measles cases across 33 US jurisdictions as of April 16 — on pace to exceed 2025’s full-year total of 2,288. Nineteen new outbreaks have been declared this year; 94% of cases are outbreak-associated, 98 patients (6%) have been hospitalized, no deaths confirmed in 2026. Kindergarten MMR vaccination coverage has fallen to 92.5%, leaving approximately 286,000 children inadequately protected against a disease that requires ~95% immunization for herd immunity. The US CDC-certified measles elimination status, held since 2000, is at serious risk of lapsing.

Oral GLP-1 drug orforglipron produces 11% weight loss in phase 3

Eli Lilly’s ATTAIN-1 trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, reports that once-daily oral orforglipron (36 mg) produced a mean 11.2% body weight reduction over 72 weeks in 3,127 patients with obesity but without diabetes, versus 2.1% for placebo (p<0.001). 54.6% of the highest-dose group lost ≥10% of body weight. The Lancet-published ATTAIN-2 trial confirmed efficacy in Type 2 diabetic patients. Orforglipron is the first oral small-molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist to demonstrate robust phase-3 efficacy matching injectable alternatives — without refrigeration or needles, dramatically lowering the barrier to global deployment.

Anthropic discloses “Mythos” model bypassed bank and agency cybersecurity

A House Oversight subcommittee roundtable April 16 raised concerns spanning AI-generated child sexual abuse material, AI restricting military lethal-force decisions, federal workers feeding classified data to chatbots, and the climate cost of AI compute. Most significantly, Anthropic disclosed that its “Mythos” model has demonstrated the capability to bypass cybersecurity defenses at banks and government agencies. No legislation was advanced, but the disclosure sharpens the federal-state preemption debate already underway.

BA.3.2 “Cicada” COVID variant detected in 31 US states, 33 countries

The heavily mutated BA.3.2 “Cicada” SARS-CoV-2 variant has been detected in at least 31 US states and 33 countries as of April 13. Test positivity stands at 1.8% (below the 5% threshold); projected hospital admissions for the week ending April 18 are ~1,600. The variant’s mutation profile may enable partial immune evasion from prior infection and vaccination and warrants monitoring despite currently low transmission.

H5N1 entrenched globally as US cases reach 71

The CDC reports 71 total human A(H5) cases in the US since February 2024; 64 detected through targeted animal-exposure monitoring, 7 through routine flu surveillance; no US deaths currently confirmed. Recent developments include the first confirmed wildlife die-off in Antarctica — the virus has now reached every continent. Current human risk remains assessed as low but pandemic preparedness experts warn the multi-continental animal reservoir keeps baseline risk elevated.

Disney, Sony, Snapchat, Artnet, Condé Nast announce mass media layoffs

A wave of layoffs across entertainment, technology and publishing has eliminated thousands of creative-labor jobs, continuing the industry-wide liquidation of skilled artistic and journalistic work in favor of shareholder returns and AI substitution. As Sandy English reports on the WSWS:

  • Disney — CEO Josh D’Amaro announced 1,000+ layoffs across studios, television and sports divisions. The Marvel Studios in-house visual development team has been eliminated; future work will be outsourced to freelancers on project terms.
  • Sony Pictures — “A few hundred” positions cut globally; the Pixomondo VFX studio, founded in 2001 and Emmy-honored for its work on Game of Thrones, has been shuttered.
  • Snapchat — 1,000 jobs cut, 16% of workforce, following pressure from Irenic Capital Management to slash over $500 million in annual expenses.
  • Artnet — post-merger with Artsy, 35% of editorial staff eliminated in a declared shift to “AI tools.”
  • Condé Nast — ceased publication of Self magazine and closed international editions, eliminating approximately 300 jobs.

Disney’s chief executive officer receives $45 million annually — roughly 900 times the median compensation of the creative workers being eliminated. This is not crisis management; it is the systematic destruction of the institutional knowledge and craft labor that make cultural production possible, on the bet that generative AI and freelance precarity can extract equivalent output at a fraction of the wage bill. The casualties include the VFX houses, artists, animators, designers and editors who built twentieth-century visual culture.

Kennedy Center board includes Trump fixer Paolo Zampolli

The elevation of Paolo Zampolli — the Milan-born modeling-industry operator who introduced Melania Knauss to Donald Trump in 1998 and was appointed US Special Envoy for Global Partnerships in March 2025 — to the Kennedy Center board of trustees marks another step in the absorption of US cultural infrastructure into Trump’s patronage machine. Zampolli’s ties to the Epstein network through his partner Amanda Ungaro, and to an alleged $5 million UN grant fraud scheme, are detailed in Marc Wells’s WSWS investigation (see WSWS coverage section). The Kennedy Center, once nominally non-partisan, has been progressively restaffed since early 2025 with figures whose qualifications lie in proximity to Trump rather than artistic or administrative merit. The phenomenon is continuous with the broader pattern of “Mafioso-style” governance — cultural institutions transformed into instruments of personal loyalty rather than sites of collective creative production.

Yasukuni offering accompanies Japan’s rearmament

Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s ritual offering to the Yasukuni Shrine — which enshrines more than 1,000 convicted WWII war criminals, including Class-A war criminals executed after the Tokyo Tribunal — was delivered the same week her cabinet lifted the 1967 ban on lethal weapons exports. The combination is not accidental. The historical memory that Yasukuni sanitizes — the crimes of Japanese imperialism in Korea, China and Southeast Asia — is precisely what must be rehabilitated to legitimize the revival of Japanese arms production. The cultural politics of militarism proceed in lockstep with the economic and military reconstruction of the same.

Across the arts roundup, a consistent pattern emerges: creative labor treated as a cost center to be automated, cultural institutions subordinated to oligarchic networks, historical memory rewritten to serve rearmament. This is what the crisis of late capitalism looks like inside culture.

WSWS coverage — last 24 hours

Perspective published: “The ‘Global Progressive Mobilization’: A bankrupt spectacle in Barcelona” by Joseph Kishore — read in full on wsws.org (link).

Kishore’s Perspective frames the Barcelona summit convened by Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez and Brazilian President Lula da Silva as “a spectacle of political fraud.” The assembled figures are presented not as opponents of austerity, repression and war but as their active administrators. Sánchez heads a Spanish government that has raised military spending while invoking social justice; Lula has himself conceded “‘left-wing’ governments had implemented austerity” across Latin America; European social democrats in attendance have been the most fervent supporters of NATO’s war in Ukraine. The symbolic keynote — a video address from Hillary Clinton, “one of the high priestesses of imperialist regime change” — is presented as entirely fitting given the gathering’s actual function.

Kishore draws a sharp structural contrast: the speeches were conducted in the register of “democracy” and “rights,” but the actual business transacted by these governments is “arms contracts, supplies of critical minerals and trade agreements.” The progressive summit is therefore a networking event for managers of the existing order, not a gathering of opponents. The political conclusion is unambiguous: “The task facing the working class is not to pressure these people, or to wait upon them. It is to break politically from the parties they represent.” Kishore calls for the construction of an independent international socialist movement rooted in the working class, taking as its starting point a complete rupture with bourgeois politics including its progressive wing.

Borneo fire devastates thousands of Malaysia’s poorest people — Mike Head

The blaze burned for over 10 hours; tightly packed structures and limited water supply accelerated its spread. The Sabah Chief Minister offered initial aid of just 2,000 Ringgits ($505) per victim. Head frames the disaster structurally: stateless and immigrant populations — including the 34,000 arrested in immigration raids between January and May 2025 — are forced into dangerous informal water villages by lack of citizenship rights and unaffordable formal housing. Federal court orders release of El Gamal family after 10 months in ICE detention — Jacob Crosse

Federal Magistrate Judge Elizabeth Chestney in Texas issued a 33-page habeas corpus ruling ordering the release of Hayam El Gamal and her five children — including 5-year-old twins — from the Dilley Immigration Processing Center, where they had been held since June 3, 2025; they are described as “believed to be among the longest detained” at the facility. The family was detained after El Gamal’s former husband — not the family itself — allegedly threw Molotov cocktails at a pro-Israel demonstration. The judge found their detention violated constitutional due process, writing that “each additional day a child is detained increases the risk of severe and lasting adverse effects on their psychological and physical development.” Despite the court order, the family remained detained pending a Thursday hearing. Crosse frames the case as collective punishment and a symptom of authoritarian immigration enforcement that threatens democratic rights broadly. Read on wsws.org

A connecting link between Trump and Epstein: Who is Paolo Zampolli? — Marc Wells

Wells profiles Paolo Zampolli — the Milan-born modeling-industry operator who introduced Melania Knauss to Donald Trump in 1998, served as Trump Organization Director of International Development (2004–2006), was appointed US Special Envoy for Global Partnerships in March 2025, and sits on the Kennedy Center board of trustees. His ties to Jeffrey Epstein run through his partner Amanda Ungaro, a Brazilian model who flew on Epstein’s plane as a teenager. When Ungaro was arrested on fraud charges in 2025, Zampolli allegedly contacted ICE to have her deported before she could post bail — a maneuver that strengthened his custody position over their son. An internal UN audit has linked his involvement to “a far larger fraud scheme” involving a $5 million UN grant. Wells argues Zampolli’s prominence reflects not individual corruption but systemic decay: ruling classes increasingly govern through personal loyalty networks and informal deals that bypass formal accountability, what the article calls “‘Mafioso’-style” oligarchic consolidation. Porto Alegre Conference: How Not to Fight Fascism — Tomas Castanheira

Castanheira condemns a conference of Brazilian pseudo-left forces in Porto Alegre as incapable of genuine antifascist struggle because it subordinates politics to bourgeois electoral calendars and alliances with the PT. Workers Struggles: The Americas — WSWS staff

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — frames Iran ceasefire as “Trump blundered” rather than systemic critique; advocates fixing the Democrats on AI policy
  • Left Voice — principled anti-reformist critique of AOC and DSA on Palestine; strong on independence from bourgeois parties
  • Liberation News / PSL — celebrates Mamdani’s Democratic primary victory as “movement for socialism”; uncritical of Iranian and Venezuelan states
  • Socialist Alternative — building May Day “No Work, No School, No Shopping”; soft on Cuban state repression
  • Socialist Worker (SWP UK) — joint Nakba Day / anti-far-right mobilizations; Stop Reform campaign risks centering electoral far-right over independent class organization
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP — most theoretically consistent anti-imperialist framing; organizational insularity limits intervention

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s twin Iran pieces — “The Iran Ceasefire Is a Stunning Defeat for Militarism” and “On Iran, Trump and the American Empire Blinked” — treat the ceasefire as proof that US imperial overreach has limits. The framing is essentially celebratory anti-hawkism without structural critique of imperialism itself: “Trump blundered” rather than “the system produces war.” Neither piece advances an analysis of class forces, nor calls for independent working-class anti-war action; the analysis halts at the level of geopolitical commentary. “The ‘Moderate’ Think Tank Pushing Dems to Loosen AI Rules” exposes corporate capture of Democratic tech policy — a legitimate critique — but the implicit framework is “fix the Democrats,” consistent with Jacobin’s DSA/dirty-break electoralism. No independent class-political alternative is proposed. Orientation today: criticism of the imperial state disciplined by loyalty to the Democratic Party’s left flank.

Left Voice

“Promises, Promises: AOC, Israel, and the DSA” sharply criticizes Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez’s record on Palestine and the DSA’s failure to break with her, calling out accommodation to a bourgeois party figure. It is the most principled anti-reformist piece in today’s pseudo-left output. “Why Young People Joined the Latest No Kings Protests” channels spontaneous anti-Trump street anger toward independent socialist organization with relatively solid class framing. “CUNY Stands With Amazon and Starbucks Workers on Strike” reflects the PTS/FT tendency’s consistent emphasis on worker-student unity over bureaucratic channels. Orientation today: strongest on political independence from the Democratic Party; the weakest on offering a concrete national electoral alternative — the programmatic vacuum the WSWS fills with the SEP.

Liberation News / PSL

The most politically significant PSL statement today is “Mamdani’s Victory and the Movement for Socialism,” which celebrates Zohran Mamdani’s New York City mayoral primary win as “an advance for socialism.” Flag: Mamdani ran and won within the Democratic Party primary. Framing this as a “movement for socialism” rather than a Democratic Party electoral result is a significant concession to bourgeois party politics — national reformism in practice, channeling socialist energy into the Democratic ballot column. “Why Trump Failed in His Criminal War Against Iran” opposes the US war forthrightly but is characteristically uncritical of the Iranian state’s repression of its own working class and left opposition. “We Reject Trump’s Naval Blockade on Venezuela” follows the same pattern: correct opposition to US imperialism combined with uncritical coverage of the Maduro government’s record against Venezuelan workers and socialist opposition. Orientation today: anti-imperialism organically combined with apologetics for bourgeois-nationalist states and accommodation to the Democratic Party through Mamdani.

Socialist Alternative

“From Coast to Coast: Socialist Alternative Is Building for May Day Action” organizes for a May 1 general strike — “No Work, No School, No Shopping” — explicitly framed against ICE, war and billionaires. This is more class-combative than most of today’s output and emphasizes independent working-class action rather than Democratic Party alignment. “Beyond ‘Security Culture’: How to Actually Fight Repression” argues mass mobilization, not small-group defensive tactics, is the effective answer to state repression — a practical organizing perspective. “A Manufactured Crisis: US Imperialism’s War on Cuba” opposes US economic warfare on Cuba using “imperialism” forthrightly, but does not critique the Cuban state’s suppression of independent labor and left opposition. Orientation today: correct anti-imperialist posture combined with soft treatment of Stalinist states; no sharp programmatic break with broad reformist coalitions in practice.

Socialist Worker (SWP UK)

“March Together on Nakba Day” calls for joint demonstrations marking the Nakba alongside anti-far-right mobilizations, framing Palestine solidarity as central to British anti-racist work. This reflects the SWP’s “united front” method but risks diluting anti-Zionist politics within a broad anti-racist coalition. “‘Urgent Need for a New Left Alternative in Scotland’” reports the collapse of the broad-left “Your Party” formation and calls for a new class-independent left party — broadly correct politically, though the SWP’s own electoral practice has been inconsistent. “Thousands Join Anti-Racist Marches in Scotland” frames grassroots mobilization as a counter to Reform UK’s electoral advance; tactically sound, but the “Stop Reform” campaign risks centering the far-right electoral challenge over the construction of independent working-class organization. Orientation today: “united front” tactics without clarity of program; activism absent a developed analysis of the bourgeois state’s role.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

Jorge Martín’s “The War in Iran: A Strategic Defeat for US Imperialism” (marxist.com) analyzes the Iran war as a turning point exposing US imperial decline — framed through Marxist crisis theory with a call for international working-class solidarity rather than support for any state. This is the clearest class-analytical piece across today’s pseudo-left coverage of the war. “Iran: The Human Cost of Trump’s War” documents civilian casualties (2,000+ dead per Iranian Red Crescent) as evidence of imperialism’s character, framed internationalistically with opposition to both US aggression and the Iranian theocratic state. “Revolutionary Communists of Ireland Hold 3rd Congress” is a routine organizational report reflecting the tendency’s focus on formal structures. Orientation today: most theoretically consistent tendency in the roundup; organizational insularity continues to limit its mass intervention.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. 7.7 magnitude earthquake off Iwate and Nankai Trough megaquake advisory

Japan’s 7.7-magnitude earthquake Monday and the Japan Meteorological Agency’s rare elevated megaquake advisory for the Nankai Trough are major natural disasters requiring a socialist analysis. The class dimensions — preparedness infrastructure underfunded, workers in vulnerable coastal industries at highest exposure, and the cynical coincidence with Takaichi’s weapons-exports liberalization — are precisely the material that mainstream disaster coverage cannot articulate.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Powerful earthquake strikes northern Japan, tsunami warning issued

2. Kyiv mass shooting and the social collapse of wartime Ukraine

The Vasylchenkov shooting is not simply a criminal act. The shooter’s biography — Moscow-born, decades in both countries, returning to Ukraine in 2017 — reflects the violent fragmentation of populations divided by the NATO proxy war. A WSWS analysis could situate the attack within the systematic collapse of mental-health infrastructure, mass impoverishment under Zelenskyy’s war economy, and the SBU’s own use of terrorism designations to repress dissent.

Recommended reading NPR Mass shooting in Kyiv, Ukraine

3. Japan’s weapons-export liberalization and the remilitarization of the Pacific

Prime Minister Takaichi’s cabinet decision to dismantle the 1967 Three Principles on Arms Exports is the most significant rupture with Japan’s postwar constitutional order since WWII. The Yasukuni Shrine visit delivered in the same week, the $7 billion Australian frigate deal, and the direct linkage to US pressure for China containment deserve a full WSWS analysis as a textbook case of imperialist alliance rearmament and its ideological preconditions.

Recommended reading Bloomberg Japan scraps most weapons export limits in boost to arms sector

4. UK arson campaign and the domestic spillover of the Iran war

The UK Metropolitan Police’s investigation of Iranian criminal proxies behind a sustained arson campaign against Jewish sites is significant on its own, but the WSWS analysis should center the political consequence: governments in the UK, Europe and US will weaponize the episode to criminalize any critique of the Iran war as “pro-Iran.” The material for a class analysis is dense — MI5’s 20+ disrupted plots, the targeting of a Persian-language outlet critical of Tehran, and the coinciding Australian arrests under “hate speech” laws.

Recommended reading Washington Post UK arson attack on Jewish community in London

5. Sudan’s abandoned war enters fourth year

The Sudan civil war — 400,000 dead, 34 million in need, 14 million displaced — has entered its fourth year with no end in sight and a fraction of the media attention devoted to Ukraine or Gaza. The proxy dimensions (UAE backing RSF, Egypt and Saudi Arabia backing SAF, with Russia and Iran drawn in), the famine data (61.7% of the population acutely food insecure), and the UN relief chief’s statement that “the world is failing Sudan” make this a textbook case of imperialist-backed war against African workers and peasants.

Recommended reading UN News World failing Sudan as war enters fourth year

6. IMF recession warning and the class character of Trump’s tariffs

The IMF’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook identifies US tariffs as the primary driver of potential global recession — growth reduced by up to 1.3 percentage points — and confirms tariffs constitute the largest US tax hike as a share of GDP since 1993. The WSWS can translate the IMF’s technical language into a class analysis: $1,500 average household cost is not a trade dispute but a deliberate upward redistribution from labor to capital, with the most severe impacts on workers in the Global South dependent on export manufacturing.

Recommended reading IMF World Economic Outlook, April 2026

7. Apple’s CEO transition and the post-globalization tech industry

Apple’s transition from Tim Cook to John Ternus — announced amid tariff reengineering of supply chains, AI-chip cost pressures, and a pending foldable iPhone launch — is a management response to the breakdown of the post-1990s globalization regime that made Apple’s valuation possible. A WSWS analysis could use the transition to illustrate how the Silicon Valley oligarchy is adapting to trade war, US–China decoupling, and the energy crisis driven by the Iran war.
