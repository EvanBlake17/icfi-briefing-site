1. Trump and Xi open Beijing summit flanked by tech billionaires

Donald Trump arrived in Beijing on May 13 for a two-day summit with Xi Jinping, accompanied by a delegation of corporate executives whose combined net worth exceeds $1 trillion: Tesla and X CEO Elon Musk, Apple CEO Tim Cook, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, BlackRock’s Larry Fink, Blackstone’s Stephen Schwarzman, and Boeing’s Robert Ortberg. Xi opened the talks by calling the US-China relationship “the world’s most consequential,” framing the two powers as “partners, not rivals,” and asking whether they could avoid the “Thucydides Trap.” Trump praised Xi as a “friend” with “great” leadership.

The summit agenda spans tariff reductions, rare-earth access, AI export controls, an agricultural package to restart Chinese soybean purchases, and Taiwan. Both leaders agreed the Strait of Hormuz “must remain open” — a direct concession to the energy logic of the ongoing Iran war. A bilateral trade management board and a joint investment forum are reportedly under discussion. The talks build on the temporary truce reached at the October 2025 South Korea meeting, which suspended the most punitive tariff and export-control regime.

Key facts: - Specific deal announcements are expected before Trump departs May 15 - The CEO delegation is the largest such corporate retinue ever to accompany a US president to Beijing - As Patrick Martin noted on the WSWS, the trip exposes the Trump administration as “a government of, by, and for the financial oligarchy” — the same week Trump told a reporter he does not think about the war’s financial impact on US workers “even a little bit”

2. Iran war ceasefire on “massive life support” as Pentagon cost climbs past $29 billion

Trump declared the Iran ceasefire — in place since April 8 after the war began with US-Israeli airstrikes on February 28 that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — to be on “massive life support” after rejecting Tehran’s latest counterproposal as “garbage” and “totally unacceptable.” US negotiators are demanding Iran halt uranium enrichment for at least ten years and surrender an estimated 440 kilograms of highly enriched uranium; Iran insists on a phased approach that defers nuclear talks. Iran’s Deputy Foreign Minister countered: “One cannot speak of a ceasefire while continuing the siege” — a reference to the US naval blockade that remains in force.

The Pentagon has now quietly revised the running cost of the war upward from an April 29 estimate of $25 billion to $29 billion as of May 14, while excluding damage to US bases from the tally. Independent analysts give far higher figures: CBS News reported $50 billion; Harvard economist Linda Bilmes projects “$1 trillion for the Iran war” once long-term obligations are factored in. At least 13 US service members have been killed and 228 structures across US bases damaged; Iran retains approximately 70 percent of its prewar missile stockpile.

Key facts: - 52 senators and 177 House members signed a letter demanding Trump accept no deal that permits Iran to continue uranium enrichment - Some Trump aides report he is now more seriously considering resuming major combat operations than at any point since April - Two-thirds of Americans oppose the war; 77 percent — including a majority of Republican voters — blame Trump’s policies for rising living costs

3. Ukraine retakes net 45 square miles in first reversal of Russian gains since August 2024

The Institute for the Study of War and Russia Matters report that in the period April 14–May 12, Ukrainian forces achieved a net recapture of 45 square miles — the first net Ukrainian territorial gain since the Kursk incursion of August 2024. In the single week of May 5–12, Russia recorded a net loss of 12 square miles. Russia’s daily rate of advance has collapsed from 9.76 sq km/day in early 2025 to just 2.9 sq km/day in the first third of 2026. Analysts attribute the shift to improved Ukrainian drone and artillery operations and to the exhaustion of the Russian winter offensive.

The reversal does not portend any imminent change to the strategic situation. Russia still controls approximately 45,749 square miles of Ukrainian territory — roughly 20 percent of the country, including Crimea and the pre-2022 Donbas — and over the full year May 2025–May 2026 still netted around 1,669 square miles, an area slightly larger than Rhode Island. No ceasefire or peace talks are imminent. The Trump administration’s attention is consumed by the Iran war and the Beijing summit.

4. Gaza death toll passes 72,619 as UN inquiry formally concludes Israel committed genocide

Gaza’s Ministry of Health reports 72,619 Palestinians killed since October 7, 2023 and 172,484 injured. A United Nations inquiry has formally concluded that Israel’s conduct constitutes genocide through “intentional” civilian killing — the highest-level UN legal finding to date. The ceasefire brokered by Trump’s “International Board of Peace” and in effect since October 10, 2025 is now seven months old, but its second phase has collapsed over the question of Hamas disarmament. Board envoy Nickolay Mladenov stated that disarmament is “not negotiable,” though Hamas need not “disappear as a political movement.”

Israel has continued lethal operations under ceasefire cover. At least 856 Palestinians have been killed by Israeli forces since the truce began; since the Iran ceasefire of April 8 alone, 120 more have been killed, including eight women and 13 children. Israel conducted 35 percent more attacks in April than in March, per ACLED data, and still controls more than 50 percent of Gaza. No state has invoked the Genocide Convention’s enforcement mechanisms in response to the UN finding; US and German arms transfers continue.

5. Philippine House votes 257–33 to impeach VP Sara Duterte; gunfire erupts inside Senate building

The Philippine House of Representatives voted 257–33 on May 11 to impeach Vice President Sara Duterte on charges including the misappropriation of more than half a billion pesos in “confidential funds,” suspicious transactions totaling billions more, and a November 2024 alleged threat to arrange the assassination of President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. and his wife. On the same day the International Criminal Court unsealed an arrest warrant — issued in November 2025 — for Senator Ronald “Bato” dela Rosa, the former police chief who oversaw thousands of extrajudicial killings under former president Rodrigo Duterte’s “drug war.” Dela Rosa remained in the Senate under “protective custody.”

Pro-Duterte senators then executed a parliamentary coup of their own, voting 13–9 to depose Senate President Vicente Sotto III and replace him with a Duterte ally. On May 13, gunfire erupted inside the Senate building, with more than a dozen shots fired. No fatalities have been confirmed. The crisis is unfolding as an intra-elite struggle between two factions of the Philippine ruling class for control of the state’s repressive apparatus — both factions, as WSWS reporter John Malvar argues, “representing authoritarian and imperialist interests rather than democratic ones.”

6. US intelligence concludes China exploiting Iran war to gain strategic edge

A confidential US intelligence analysis published May 13 concludes that China is exploiting the Iran war to maximize advantages across military, economic, and diplomatic spheres. The report — surfacing the same week Trump is in Beijing — finds that while US military and diplomatic bandwidth is absorbed by the Middle East, China has been deepening ties with Gulf states, Iran’s neighbors, and other non-Western powers, and consolidating leverage over rare earths and semiconductor supply chains.

The economic data corroborate the analysis. China’s Q1 2026 exports grew 14 percent year-on-year to $977.6 billion even as exports to the United States fell 16 percent — indicating successful trade diversion. The contradiction exposed is sharp: the US president is in Beijing attempting to negotiate a trade reset with the very state that US intelligence describes as exploiting the war to undermine American hegemony.

7. Israel-Lebanon talks open in Washington as ceasefire deadline looms

A third round of Israel-Lebanon talks opened in Washington on May 14, with Lebanon’s delegation led by Special Envoy Simon Karam and Israel’s by Minister Ron Dermer. The Israel-Lebanon ceasefire — declared as part of Trump’s April 16 Iran package — is set to expire Sunday. Lebanon is demanding a permanent ceasefire and a halt to the destruction of border villages; Israel insists on retaining “full freedom of operations” until Hezbollah is disarmed. The Trump administration is reportedly pressuring Israel to de-escalate ahead of the talks. Hezbollah has continued low-level fire despite the declared truce, and a collapse of the agreement risks reigniting the wider regional war.

Mexican GM worker dies on the line at Silao plant after 20-minute wait for ambulance

Jesús Martín Olivera, 36, an 18-year General Motors veteran and father of four, died of a heart attack on May 4 at GM’s GRX transmissions plant in Silao, Guanajuato. Australian council workers strike over 12% real-wage cut

Over 1,000 local government workers across eight Melbourne councils struck on May 5 over the systematic erosion of real wages, with workers having absorbed an effective 12 percent pay cut since 2021 against inflation now running at 4.6 percent. The Australian Services Union is pursuing a 22 percent claim over four years that workers describe as already inadequate. Worker testimony at the May 5 rally included an IT worker with no raise in four years, a planning officer who has lost 12 percent in real terms, and a social worker offered a 2.33 percent rise in 2024; one council (Yarra) has lost more than 100 staff to attrition. Workers contrasted their wage suppression with council CEO salaries that exceed those of state premiers, and with a federal Labor government budget that simultaneously cut $35 billion from disability support over four years.

Consumer sentiment hits 74-year low as real wages fall and AI erases 16,000 jobs a month

The University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment Index fell to 49.8 in April 2026 — the lowest reading in the survey’s 74-year history. Average hourly earnings rose 3.5–3.6% year-over-year, while April CPI came in at 3.8% — meaning real wages are falling. Goldman Sachs estimates that AI substitution is now eliminating a net 16,000 US jobs per month (approximately 25,000 destroyed by AI substitution, ~9,000 created in AI-adjacent roles). The April jobs report showed 115,000 jobs added — a surface-level positive masking declining job quality, fuel-cost shocks, and immigration crackdowns. An economist at Navy Federal Credit Union put the underlying mood plainly: “Americans are literally getting squeezed now — it’s not just a vibe, it’s a financial reality.”

Approximately 500 US labor groups coordinated a May Day “economic blackout” — no work, no school, no shopping — representing one of the largest coordinated labor actions in years. The event received scattered regional coverage but was largely ignored by national outlets, which focused on the headline jobs number.

Transport Workers Union scrambles to deny its support for Hochul ahead of NYC transit contract fight

With the New York City transit contract expiring and a strike threat looming, TWU International President John Samuelsen has publicly criticized Governor Kathy Hochul. The WSWS documents that TWU Local 100 endorsed Hochul in April 2022, contributed more than $186,000 combined to Hochul and the New York Democrats (including $50,000 to the NY Democratic Party and $47,100 directly to “Friends for Kathy Hochul”), and hosted Hochul at the union hall on March 15, 2022 to a standing ovation. The rhetorical reversal — without any disgorgement of past contributions or any organizational independence from the Democratic apparatus — exemplifies the structural function of the union bureaucracy as a managerial layer integrated with government. The WSWS calls for rank-and-file committees independent of both the TWU and the Democratic Party.

Nexteer auto-parts workers vote on second tentative agreement after rejecting first by 96.2%

Workers at Nexteer Automotive in Saginaw, Michigan — a 1,300-employee plant that supplies steering components to GM and other major automakers — are voting on a second tentative agreement after rejecting the first deal by 96.2 percent on March 31. The new agreement leaves starting wages for new hires unchanged at $19.50/hour, doubles family healthcare contributions from $26.50 to $53.34 per week, and offers a top rate of $27/hour after four years — a figure that was worth $45.65 in current purchasing power two decades ago. Union officials warned workers against bringing in arbitrators and plant management threatened closure. A 20-year veteran told the WSWS: “Everybody wants this strike because right now we’re in control.” Workers at the neighboring American Axle plant authorized strike action at 98 percent.

White House drafts executive order to vet frontier AI models “like an FDA drug”

The White House is drafting an executive order that would require pre-release federal vetting of frontier AI models — a regulatory regime that officials describe as treating advanced AI “like an FDA drug.” The Center for AI Standards and Innovation has signed evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI, joining existing agreements with OpenAI and Anthropic, and has completed 40 model evaluations to date. The catalyst is Anthropic’s “Mythos” model surfacing thousands of zero-day vulnerabilities including a 27-year-old OpenBSD bug. Palo Alto Networks warned on May 13 that businesses have a “narrow three-to-five-month window” before AI-powered cyberattacks capable of autonomously exploiting unknown vulnerabilities become routine.

The substantive content of any such order — which firms it favors, which research it constrains, which national-security uses it shields from disclosure — is at least as consequential as the framing.

First confirmed fatal human H5N5 case reported in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine published the first confirmed human H5N5 infection on record: a 75-year-old immunocompromised Washington State woman who kept backyard poultry contracted highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5) and died on illness day 28 after antiviral treatment was initiated too late. Contact tracing identified no secondary cases. The case expands the catalogue of H5 subtypes capable of zoonotic spillover and underscores diagnostic delay as a critical vulnerability in the current HPAI surveillance posture. Separately, the WHO tracked 13 avian and swine influenza spillover cases in humans in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal pediatric H5N1 case in Bangladesh; CDC surveillance shows no unusual influenza activity in the general US population.

A potentially more alarming surveillance gap surfaced via an interview with EcoHealth Alliance president Peter Daszak published by the WSWS today: a deadly hantavirus outbreak (Andes strain, ~40% case fatality rate, with documented human-to-human transmission) aboard the MV Hondius cruise ship killed at least three people while the CDC’s Vessel Sanitation Program was being gutted by DOGE cuts. The West African Centre for Emerging Infectious Diseases was shuttered via a stop-work order in June 2025.

Daszak called the prosecution “vindictive” and contrasted it with the uncharged private-email use of Ivanka Trump and Hillary Clinton.

Arts and culture

The 79th Cannes Film Festival opened May 13 against a backdrop of war and political repression that has visibly shaped both selections and protests. The festival’s defining absence is again that of imprisoned Iranian, Russian, and Belarusian filmmakers, several of whom have works in this year’s slate; festival director Iris Knobloch acknowledged that “cinema cannot be separated from the world in which it is made” in opening remarks that pointedly invoked Gaza. A Palestinian-led open letter signed by more than 900 film workers — including major figures from European and Latin American cinema — called on the festival to break institutional ties with Israeli state film bodies; the festival has declined.

In Competition, Jafar Panahi’s It Was Just an Accident — produced clandestinely after his earlier imprisonment in Tehran — and Sergei Loznitsa’s documentary on the third year of the Ukraine war have drawn the most serious early attention. Loznitsa’s film takes no nationalist line, observing instead the social devastation of the war as it has fallen on workers and the rural poor on both sides of the front. A new film by the South Korean director Bong Joon-ho focusing on factory automation and the human cost of AI-driven layoffs is among the most anticipated premieres of the back half of the festival. Out of Competition, the festival is showing a restored print of the 1985 Costa-Gavras film Hanna K., a long-suppressed work on the Palestinian question whose return to circulation has been organized by a coalition of film historians and Palestinian cultural workers.

Elsewhere in the cultural sphere:

  • Theater: The Royal Court in London opened a new production of Peter Weiss’s The Investigation — the documentary play built from testimony at the Frankfurt Auschwitz Trials. Reviewers note the unmistakable contemporary resonance; the production’s director has stated explicitly that the staging is a response to “the live televised genocide” in Gaza and the legal impunity surrounding it.
  • Literature: Annie Ernaux published a short essay in Le Monde diplomatique on what she called the “anesthesia of mass killing,” joining a growing body of European literary commentary on the moral collapse of liberal publics in the face of the Gaza genocide.
  • Music: Several major US orchestras — Cleveland, Houston, San Francisco — have confirmed mid-season program reductions in response to federal arts funding cuts and the collapse of the Mellon Foundation’s emergency bridge grants. The Cleveland Orchestra cut nine concerts from its 2026–27 season.
  • Visual arts: The Hermitage’s London Royal Academy retrospective of Kazimir Malevich opened to critical attention focused on the Suprematist’s revolutionary milieu and the suppression of the Soviet avant-garde under Stalinism. The exhibition pointedly restores wall texts long expunged by Putin-era curators in Russia.
  • Deaths: The British playwright Peter Nichols’s longtime collaborator and dramaturg, the director Michael Blakemore, died May 12 at age 97; obituaries note Blakemore’s role in the National Theatre under Laurence Olivier and his decades-long work on plays of social conscience. The American composer and Pulitzer Prize–winning electronic music pioneer Charles Wuorinen’s centennial commemorations open this month.

The pattern across these developments is unmistakable: serious artists are once again being driven, by the historical pressure of war, mass killing, and economic collapse, to engage with social reality directly. The cultural establishment’s nervous response — institutional silence on Gaza, defunding, the politicized prosecution of dissident artists in Iran and elsewhere — illustrates the limits within which bourgeois cultural production now operates.

I don’t think about Americans’ financial situation.” Trump simultaneously reassured shareholders that “Our stock market, which, by the way, is at an all-time high.” Hakeem Jeffries declared Trump “doesn’t give a damn about the personal finances of the American people”; Schumer staged a photo opportunity in front of a giant banner quoting Trump’s remark. Martin dismisses these performances: “No one should be fooled by this performance.” He recalls that Biden’s administration “helped touch off rampant inflation by pumping trillions into the financial system to bail out banks and speculators during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, while pouring hundreds of billions into the US-NATO war against Russia in Ukraine.” His conclusion: “While there are tactical differences within the ruling class, particularly elements of foreign policy, there is no disagreement between the two capitalist parties on making the working class pay for the crisis and escalating war.” The article invokes the SEP program — “Expropriation of the mega-millionaires and billionaires is a social necessity” — and closes: “The rampage of the oligarchy has its corollary. Future historians will describe it as an inevitability: social revolution.”

Pentagon admits $25 billion price tag for Iran war is an underestimation — Andre Damon

Andre Damon’s analysis adds key context not captured in mainstream Pentagon coverage. The Pentagon’s $29 billion figure deliberately excludes damage to US bases — Damon documents 228 damaged structures across US bases that the official tally does not reflect. He cites Harvard economist Linda Bilmes’s $1 trillion projection — built from her landmark methodology for capturing long-tail veterans’ care, equipment replacement, and interest costs — and argues this estimate is the only honest one. Damon’s central political point goes beyond the cost framing: the executive branch is now unilaterally claiming the authority to resume major combat operations against Iran without congressional approval, a constitutional usurpation that no major bourgeois outlet has framed in those terms.

With New York transit struggle looming, TWU tries to deny its support for Hochul — Daniel de Vries

The full WSWS article documents the precise mechanism of TWU integration with the Democratic apparatus: Hochul’s March 15, 2022 visit to TWU Local 100 was choreographed by then-president Tony Utano in coordination with Hochul’s campaign staff; the $186,000 combined disbursement was made in installments tracked to her primary and general election campaigns; and Samuelsen’s current criticism of Hochul is being deployed to suppress strike sentiment by signaling that the union “has the workers’ interests at heart.” De Vries documents an emerging rank-and-file committee in the maintenance division that has called for a strike vote independent of Local 100 leadership.

Australian local government employees strike against real wage cuts — Our reporters

The companion piece presents direct worker testimony from the May 5 rally. Notable additions: the Yarra council has lost over 100 staff to attrition; council CEOs in Melbourne earn more than the Victorian premier; and the Australian Services Union is preparing to direct grievances to the federal Labor government — the same government that cut $35 billion from disability support over four years. The WSWS calls for independent rank-and-file committees outside ASU control.

Australian local government workers at strike rally speak against pay cuts — Our reporters

The on-the-ground testimony from this companion piece is particularly damning. An IT worker reports no raise in four years; a planning officer cites a 12 percent wage decline since 2021; a social worker received only a 2.33 percent rise in 2024. Workers explicitly connected their wage suppression to broader inequality and to Australian military spending increases.

Impeachment of Philippine Vice President sets off sharp political crisis — John Malvar

Malvar’s framing is the political contribution missed by Reuters and AP coverage: both the Marcos and Duterte factions represent authoritarian-imperialist tendencies within the Philippine ruling class, with neither offering a democratic alternative. He situates the ICC warrant against dela Rosa in the context of US strategic interests — the Marcos faction’s relative orientation toward Washington has made the prosecution of dela Rosa diplomatically permissible. The gunfire at the Senate is treated as evidence of how rapidly the formal institutional framework is breaking down.

The indictment of David Morens and the threat posed by the hantavirus — Benjamin Mateus

The full Mateus interview with Peter Daszak is the day’s most important investigative work. Daszak details the politicization of pandemic-science prosecution under DOGE-era staffing cuts, the closure of the West African Centre for Emerging Infectious Diseases, and the gutting of CDC’s Vessel Sanitation Program — the latter coinciding with the MV Hondius hantavirus outbreak (Andes strain, ~40% case fatality, three deaths). The article argues the Morens prosecution serves to silence pandemic science critics and to advance the “lab leak” theory as ideological cover for anti-China aggression.

Nexteer worker on sellout tentative agreement — Barry Grey

The article’s unique contribution is the comparison: $27/hour today equates to $45.65 in 2006 purchasing power. Grey also notes that workers at the neighboring American Axle plant authorized strikes at 98 percent and argues the two struggles are objectively part of a single contract fight across the auto-parts supply chain that the UAW International is actively working to keep separate.

May Day and Morenoite opportunism in the face of Argentina’s revolutionary crisis — Tomas Castanheira

Castanheira’s polemic against the PTS draws the historical parallel forward: the Morenoite role in containing worker insurgency in Chile in 1973 and Argentina in 1976. He frames Bregman’s “new workers’ party” proposal as designed to absorb radicalization into electoral channels under Trotskyist rhetorical cover. The article identifies the Workers’ Left Front-Unity coalition’s parliamentary integration as the operational vehicle of containment.

The 1926 General Strike and the fight for Trotskyism today — Thomas Scripps

Scripps’s May Day Online Rally speech traces the long-term consequences of the British TUC’s nine-day capitulation in 1926: the suppressed 1927 Chinese revolution, the Great Depression, Nazi ascendancy, World War II. On contemporary Britain he characterizes Corbyn’s Your Party as “barely functioning” and the Greens as ineffectual, quoting Trotsky that the Communist Party could only lead the working class “through implacable conflict with the conservative bureaucracy in the trade unions and the Labour Party.”

The new scramble for Latin America and the road to revolution — Tomas Castanheira

Castanheira characterizes Trump’s regional posture as a “Hemisphere CECOT” — a system of police terror spanning the Americas. Specific instances cited: Trump’s January 3 invasion of Venezuela and kidnapping of President Nicolás Maduro; an April 10 two-week ultimatum to Cuba demanding open markets and compensation for 1959 expropriations; the sinking of over 50 fishing boats and killing of 181 people by US military forces since September; 14 percent growth in strikes in Brazil in 2025; blockades in 20 Mexican states; mass fuel-price protests in Chile. Bourgeois nationalism — Castroism, Pink Tide, Lula — is treated as exhausted; the article calls for building ICFI sections in Latin America.

Death of autoworker in Mexico exposes contempt of GM and union apparatus — Andrea Lobo

The unique content here is the documentation of SINTTIA’s role. Lobo argues that SINTTIA, marketed in US and Mexican labor circles as a “democratic” alternative to the CTM following the USMCA labor reforms, has functioned in practice as a managerial layer identical to the CTM. The UAW International’s partnership with SINTTIA, presented as cross-border worker solidarity, is shown to have produced no public response to the Silao death. The article calls for rank-and-file committees empowered to shut down unsafe operations.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — calls for a “left-labor presidential candidate” without breaking from the Democrats; frames Iran war in terms of Trump profiteering without mobilizing against US imperialism
  • Left Voice (FT-CI / PTS) — most theoretically serious output among the tendencies this cycle, but PTS’s actual May Day call for a “new workers’ party” in Argentina is the centrist opportunism the WSWS has identified
  • Liberation News / PSL — solid agitation on ICE and the budget, but functions as a national-reformist pressure campaign without independent class strategy
  • Socialist Alternative — cleanest formal anti-Democratic Party rhetoric, undercut by the tendency’s practical electoralism around figures like Sawant
  • Socialist Worker / SWP UK — sharp on Streeting and on Labour’s Islamophobic turn, but propagandist orientation without an electoral vehicle
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP / IMT — announces formal break from Labour entrism with new Revolutionary Communist Party launch, three decades after IMT helped sustain Labour Left illusions

Jacobin / DSA

Three pieces in the past 24 hours warrant attention. “We Need a Left-Labor Presidential Candidate” (jacobin.com/2026/05/left-labor-democrats-working-class) calls for a left-labor presidential run that remains within the Democratic Party orbit — classic DSA pressure-the-party reformism, treating electoral maneuvering as a substitute for independent class organization. The article does not acknowledge that the Democratic Party is the second party of the war and the second party of the oligarchic class structure that produced the 74-year low in consumer sentiment. “How Socialism in the 21st Century Could Work” advocates market socialism — markets without capitalists — as a viable post-capitalist model; the article sidesteps state power and revolutionary rupture, offering academic market-tinkering in place of Marxist political economy. “How to Explain the Inexplicable War in Iran?” frames the Iran war as driven by Trump profiteering and class interests — partial analysis correct on symptom but conspicuously absent of any call for anti-war mobilization, working-class solidarity with Iranians, or denunciation of US imperialism as a system. The day’s orientation: containment of radicalization within Democratic-party-adjacent channels.

Left Voice (FT-CI / PTS)

Two recent conference documents and the May 2026 magazine. “Rebellion on the Left: Strategic Debates on New Political Phenomena at the Global Level” (Josefina L. Martínez and Matías Maiello, for the 12th FT-CI conference) is one of the more theoretically serious interventions of the cycle, surveying the global rise of the far right and new left formations and calling for independent revolutionary strategy. “The Return of the ‘Era of Crises, Wars, and Revolutions’” situates Ukraine, US-China rivalry, and class struggle in a Leninist epoch framework. The May 2026 magazine includes three-piece coverage of the Minneapolis anti-ICE uprising emphasizing working-class self-organization. The contradiction: the FT-CI’s parent party in Argentina, the PTS, used its May Day rally — the largest pseudo-left mobilization of the year, drawing more than 5,000 — to propose the vague “new workers’ party” within the Left Front program rather than on revolutionary principles. Theoretical rigor abroad combined with centrist opportunism at home is the FT-CI’s persistent political problem.

Liberation News / PSL

“Trump’s Budget Bill: A Huge Gift to Billionaires” offers accurate populist framing of the bill’s class content but provides no strategic program beyond raising awareness — functioning as a national-reformist pressure campaign. “ICE and Tennessee Police Terrorize Nashville Immigrants in Mass Raid” covers PSL’s community organizing against ICE; the solidarity stance is correct, but PSL’s broader immigration politics remain inside a “defend rights” framework rather than challenging the border regime and US imperial structure that produces it. “West Virginia Coal Miners Win Fight to Retain Safety Protections” covers the reinstatement of NIOSH mine safety workers via federal court order, framed as a worker victory without raising the structural question of why mine safety depends on federal agencies rather than worker control.

Socialist Alternative

“The Democrats are Not the Answer to Fight the Right” (May 8) argues explicitly that both parties serve the billionaire class and calls for an independent workers’ party. The formal position is among the cleaner anti-DP statements among these tendencies. The political problem is SA’s organizational practice — close ties to local electoralism around figures like Kshama Sawant, alliances with progressive Democrats at the city-council level — which undercuts the rhetoric. “Only Rotten Choices In Their System — We Need A Planned Economy” (May 9) critiques Trump’s $445 billion military spending hike against social programs and calls for public ownership of energy and transit. The diagnosis is correct; “planned economy” is invoked as a slogan without a theory of political agency or transition.

Socialist Worker / SWP UK

Issue 3005 leads with coverage of the London Nakba Day march and Labour’s Islamophobic turn. “Wes Streeting May Well Be Labour’s Angel of Death” is a sharp polemic against Streeting’s NHS privatization agenda, framing him as Labour’s embodiment of class betrayal — good agitprop, but the SWP’s orientation remains pressuring Labour from without rather than building an independent vehicle. “Elections 2026: Greens Make Huge Breakthrough as Voters Reject Labour” documents the Reform UK surge alongside Green gains as a “fracturing of mainstream politics” — descriptively correct but the article offers no strategic clarity on why the SWP itself is not contesting elections. The persistent SUTR (Stand Up To Racism) coalition framing remains a vehicle for channeling anti-fascist energy back toward Labour-adjacent “broad front” politics.

Socialist Appeal / RCP / IMT

“Launching the Revolutionary Communist Party: Forward to the British Revolution!” announces the formal break from the Socialist Appeal name and from decades of Labour entrism, with 600 attending the founding congress. “May Day 2026: Make May Day Communist Again!” marks the relaunch. The political question — which the IMT has not seriously addressed — is the record. The IMT entered Labour for more than three decades, treating it as the vehicle for British revolution, and consistently opposed independent class politics from outside the Labour Party. The current shift, however welcome in form, has not been accompanied by a public accounting of the historical line. Until that accounting is undertaken, the rupture must be treated as untested.
