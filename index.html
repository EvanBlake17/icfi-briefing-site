1. Xi-Putin Beijing summit declares “multipolar world” as bilateral trade hits $240 billion

Vladimir Putin and Xi Jinping concluded a state visit in Beijing this week with the signing of a 47-page joint declaration on building “a multipolar world and a new type of international relations” — an explicit and unprecedented public targeting of US global dominance. The visit came only days after Xi’s separate summit with Donald Trump, underlining China’s calibrated balancing role between the rival great powers. Putin and Xi finalized over 40 agreements, including the long-delayed Power of Siberia 2 pipeline, which will carry 50 billion cubic meters of Russian gas annually to China via Mongolia, and an extension of visa-free travel for Russian citizens through end of 2027.

The economic data behind the declaration are the most consequential element. Bilateral Russia-China trade reached $240 billion in 2025 and rose another 20% in the first four months of 2026. Russia now imports more than 90% of sanctioned technology through China, and nearly all transactions are settled in roubles and yuan, bypassing the dollar. The summit’s substantive achievement is the consolidation of an alternative payments, supply-chain and energy infrastructure that has functionally defeated the post-2022 Western sanctions regime.

Key facts: - Xi: “The tide of unilateral hegemony is running rampant.” Putin: “We have created a stable system of mutual trade protected from external influence.” - Both leaders designated 2026 the “Year of Education”; Putin invited Xi to visit Russia in 2027. - The Siberia 2 pipeline had been stalled for over a decade by pricing disputes — its agreement signals Beijing’s willingness to lock in long-term dependence on Russian hydrocarbons even as Western governments insist Russia is “isolated.” - The summit produced no public comment on the US-Iran war, despite both governments having major interests in its outcome.

2. US-Iran war: Trump warns of “nasty” action as Hormuz blockade enters fourth month

The US-Iran military conflict, now approximately three months old, continued today with no active ceasefire and Trump publicly warning that he would wait “a couple of days” for Iran’s response to the latest US proposal before resuming strikes. “We’ll either have a deal or we’re going to do some things that are a little bit nasty,” he told reporters. Pakistan’s Army Chief Asim Munir is mediating; the US is demanding Iran halt its nuclear program for at least 10 years and surrender an estimated 440 kilograms of highly enriched uranium, while Iran has proposed a phased approach that defers the nuclear question.

The economic dimensions of the war have intensified. US forces operating in the Strait of Hormuz have redirected 94 commercial vessels and disabled four others as part of the de facto blockade; Iranian state media reports approximately 30 ships transiting under Iranian naval coordination. WTI crude was trading near $97 per barrel today, with Brent above $104. Inside Iran, the civilian toll of three months of bombardment remains poorly reported in US media.

Key facts: - House Republicans canceled a vote on a war powers resolution after concluding they lacked the votes to defeat it — congressional support for the war is eroding, including among Trump’s own caucus. - Iran has publicly warned the war will “spread beyond the Middle East” if Washington resumes large-scale strikes. - The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly one-fifth of global oil flows; the blockade is being framed in US press as a military tactic rather than an economic war with global consequences for working people.

3. Trump creates $1.776 billion “Anti-Weaponization Fund” by personal deal with Attorney General

The Trump administration this week formally established a $1.776 billion “Anti-Weaponization Fund” to compensate individuals who claim they were “unfairly investigated or prosecuted” under the Biden administration. The fund was created through a settlement between Trump and his former personal lawyer, now acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, in which Trump agreed to drop a spurious $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS over the leak of his tax returns to the New York Times. The transaction was conducted entirely within the executive branch with no congressional authorization, no public rulemaking, no defined class of eligible claimants, no judicial review and no right of appeal.

A separate one-page addendum to the same deal — written in Trump’s favored all-caps style — “RELEASES, WAIVES, ACQUITS, and FOREVER DISCHARGES” Trump, his sons and his business entities from any federal claim that “has been or could have been asserted.” It effectively terminates any remaining federal tax-fraud or financial investigations of the Trump Organization.

As Patrick Martin notes in today’s WSWS Perspective, “Trump is now immunized for both his public and private actions” — adding a new layer to the immunity granted by the 2024 Supreme Court ruling in Trump v. United States. Martin argues the structure of the fund is designed to channel hundreds of millions of dollars to the pardoned January 6 attackers and assorted fascist allies, building “the American equivalent of Hitler’s brownshirts” as a paramilitary auxiliary to be deployed against strikers and anti-war protesters.

Key facts: - A five-member committee — all appointed by the Trump-appointed Attorney General, all removable by Trump — governs disbursements; only three members constitute a quorum. - The fund is auditable “only at the Attorney General’s direction.” - Article I of the Constitution: “No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law” — a clause the deal flatly disregards. - Senate and House Democrats criticized the fund as “corruption” on Thursday but, per Martin, “retreated to promises that a midterm election victory would allow them to hold Trump accountable” rather than calling for mass mobilization.

4. WHO declares Ebola PHEIC in DRC and Uganda — no vaccine exists

The World Health Organization Director-General declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) on May 16-17 in response to an Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo that has now spread to Uganda. The outbreak involves the Bundibugyo virus strain, for which no licensed vaccine and no specific therapeutics exist. Historical case fatality rates for the Bundibugyo strain run between 30% and 50%.

As of May 20, DRC authorities have reported approximately 600 suspected cases and 139 deaths, with 51 confirmed. Two imported cases — including one death — have been confirmed in Kampala, Uganda. At Mongbwalu hospital in Ituri Province, four healthcare workers died within four days of one another. A four-week interval elapsed between the first symptom onset (April 25) and laboratory confirmation (May 14) — a detection lag that allowed the virus to seed in three provinces before containment could begin.

Key facts: - This is the 17th Ebola outbreak in the DRC since 1976. - Over 60% of cases are female, with most between ages 20 and 39 — a profile consistent with infection within healthcare and caregiving settings. - The outbreak has spread from Ituri Province to North Kivu, including the cities of Butembo and Goma. - The PHEIC is the highest alert level under the International Health Regulations and is rarely declared; it is an explicit admission that available countermeasures are inadequate.

5. Bolivian capital besieged as workers’ blockades enter third week

Bolivia’s capital La Paz has effectively been cut off from the rest of the country for two weeks as road blockades led by the Bolivian Workers’ Central (COB), peasant unions and mining federations have emptied markets and depleted hospital oxygen reserves. Approximately 5,000 vehicles are stranded along Bolivia’s highways, including fuel and food trucks. Business organizations estimate the blockades are costing the economy more than $50 million per day. At least three people have died after emergency vehicles were prevented from reaching medical centers.

The political crisis facing right-wing President Rodrigo Paz, less than six months into his term, has now escalated beyond the original trigger — a land mortgage law that he formally annulled on May 13. Worker and peasant demands have expanded to include higher wages, labor-law reform and Paz’s resignation. Paz announced a cabinet reshuffle Wednesday, but the strike continues. Analysts describe the underlying economic situation as “the worst in 40 years”: inflation hovered near 20% last year, fuel shortages are chronic and foreign reserves are depleted.

Key facts: - The COB-led mobilization has the character of a general strike combined with a nationwide blockade — a level of working-class action not seen in Bolivia since the 2003-2005 gas-wars period that ousted two presidents. - Paz’s government has so far avoided declaring a state of emergency, evidently fearing military fragmentation if troops are ordered against the mineworkers. - The Movement for Socialism (MAS) of Evo Morales is reportedly attempting to channel the movement back into parliamentary opposition, but rank-and-file committees in the highlands are operating outside of MAS control.

6. Ukraine strikes Russian drone training hub, killing 65 cadets

Overnight May 20-21, Ukrainian forces launched 11 precision strikes — using medium-range weapons with 100 kg warheads — on a Russian drone pilot training center in Snizhne, occupied Donetsk Oblast. Ukrainian military intelligence reported that 65 cadets of the “Sever-Akhmat” 78th Special Purpose Motorized Regiment were killed in their barracks, along with their commander, Lt. Colonel “Buryi,” identified as a PhD at the Russian Academy of Missile and Artillery Sciences. The targeted two-story complex, roughly 2,484 square meters, was reportedly used for UAV and warhead assembly as well as personnel quarters. Russia has not officially confirmed the strike.

The operation is part of a sustained Ukrainian campaign against Russian drone infrastructure. Ukrainian military figures report Russia lost over 19,000 troops in early May. In parallel, Russia has launched the largest deep-strike campaign of the war: approximately 20 combined drone-and-missile attacks on Kyiv during May alone. The qualitative escalation comes precisely as Trump has promised a diplomatic settlement of the war — and as Ukrainian forces have been expanding the reach of indigenous strike weapons.

Key facts: - Ukrainian intelligence claims the cadets were killed in their barracks before the warhead-assembly section was hit, suggesting the strike was timed for maximum personnel casualties. - The “Akhmat” formations are nominally Chechen units under Kadyrov’s command structure, repurposed for drone warfare. - The Russian Defense Ministry has imposed an internal information blackout on training-center casualties since a November 2025 strike on a similar facility.

7. Gaza phase-two ceasefire stalls as Israel refuses withdrawal from “yellow line”

Israeli forces recovered the body of Ran Gvili — the last Israeli hostage in Gaza — from a cemetery in northern Gaza on January 26, and Israel formally announced the recovery this week. The development was supposed to trigger phase two of the Gaza ceasefire framework, which requires full Israeli military withdrawal from Gaza, Hamas disarmament, deployment of an International Stabilization Force and transition of governance from the “Board of Peace” to a reformed Palestinian Authority.

Israel has now told UN mediators it will not withdraw from the so-called “yellow line” within Gaza, directly blocking phase-two implementation. Israeli air strikes, shelling and small-arms fire continue across the strip despite the formal ceasefire. The UN Security Council held an emergency debate on May 21 urging consolidation of the ceasefire and warning of the “horrendous conditions” facing Palestinian civilians.

In parallel, Israel’s war on Lebanon continues. Since March 2, when the war began, at least 2,846 people have been killed in Lebanon and more than a million displaced. A 45-day ceasefire extension brokered by the United States on May 15 has been repeatedly violated; Israeli strikes killed at least five in Lebanon on May 17 alone, damaging a hospital. A US-facilitated security track is scheduled to begin May 29, with the next round of direct US-Israel-Lebanon talks planned for June 2-3 in Washington.

Key facts: - The “Board of Peace” — a Trump-administration-led body chaired by Trump himself — retains effective veto power over Palestinian self-governance arrangements. - Hamas retains its political and military presence in Gaza despite the formal disarmament requirement; no enforcement mechanism has been agreed. - Iran has tied the Lebanon war to its own conflict with the United States: Tehran warned the wars will “spread beyond the Middle East” if Washington resumes large-scale strikes.

8. DNC chair disavows party’s own 2024 election autopsy on day of release

DNC Chair Ken Martin released the Democratic National Committee’s official 2024 election post-mortem on Thursday under duress, after CNN obtained and reported on the report’s contents. In a Substack post accompanying the release, Martin wrote: “I am not proud of this product; it does not meet my standards and it won’t meet your standards.” The report — written part-time by Democratic strategist Paul Rivera — found that the party’s economic messaging “created tensions with key constituencies,” that ground organization arrived too late and that there was a fundamental “lack of alignment” between the party’s main super PAC and Kamala Harris’s campaign.

Key facts: - The report avoids all discussion of the war on Gaza, on which Harris repeatedly affirmed her support for “Israel’s right to defend itself” while the Democrats lost decisive margins among Arab-American, Muslim and youth voters in Michigan, Wisconsin and elsewhere. - The report does not examine the Biden administration’s role in suppressing labor militancy — including the imposition of a no-strike contract on rail workers in 2022. - Multiple DNC members had pushed for an outside, independent investigation in the months after the election; the leadership rejected the request.

Sri Lanka’s JVP/NPP government drifts toward austerity as fuel costs explode

Sri Lanka’s JVP/NPP government — elected as a “left” alternative to the discredited establishment parties — is preparing what amounts to a renewed austerity package as the country’s economic crisis worsens. Tourist arrivals fell 29% in April; the cost of monthly fuel imports surged from $98 million in February to $522 million by May; the rupee has depreciated 4.8% against the dollar this year. President Dissanayake’s recent warning that “there is only a short window, and we must face it with strength” signals further wage and subsidy attacks on workers. Meanwhile, corporate profits are rising: Dialog Axiata’s Q1 net profit doubled to 9.6 billion rupees.

Australia cuts university research funding by 4.3% while military spending climbs

The Australian federal budget released this week increases overall higher-education teaching and research funding by 5% to $12.6 billion in nominal terms — but a 4.1% rise in domestic enrollments means per-student funding falls in real terms. Direct research funding falls 4.3%, from $3 billion to $2.87 billion, with further declines projected to $2.8 billion by 2029-30. The Higher Education Support program is nearly halved, from $1.4 billion to $783 million. International student numbers have already collapsed 35% — from 577,295 to 371,564 — following Labor’s tripling of student visa fees and 40% visa rejection rates. Some 4,000 university staff positions have been eliminated over the past 18 months. Federal funding for domestic enrollments has fallen from approximately 80% of costs in 1987 to 40% today, with the gap filled by debt-financed student fees.

US measles cases hit 1,893 across 27 outbreaks as kindergarten vaccination dips to 92.5%

As of May 14, the CDC has confirmed 1,893 measles cases across 27 outbreaks in the United States — already on pace to exceed any year in decades. 93% of cases (1,761) are outbreak-associated, indicating sustained chains of transmission rather than isolated imports. Kindergarten MMR vaccination coverage has fallen to 92.5%, below the 95% threshold required for herd immunity. Roughly 286,000 kindergartners are now unprotected. The trajectory reflects the cumulative effect of the Trump administration’s promotion of vaccine skepticism through HHS Secretary RFK Jr. and the systematic dismantling of CDC outbreak-response capacity.

First documented human H5N5 infection reported in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine published this month the first documented human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). The case involved a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman in North America who developed fever and diarrhea after bird exposure in October 2025. Antiviral treatment was delayed because initial upper-respiratory tests came back negative, complicating her course. The case confirms that fully avian H5 lineages beyond H5N1 can spill over into humans — broadening the surveillance and pandemic-preparedness target.

In parallel, WHO reported 13 avian and swine influenza human cases in Q1 2026, including one previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child from Bangladesh. None of the 13 case contacts became ill. CDC surveillance shows no unusual influenza activity in the US population as of May 1. WHO’s TAG-CO-VAC group this month recommended monovalent LP.8.1 as the preferred antigen for next-cycle COVID-19 vaccines, reflecting continued antigenic drift away from XBB/JN lineages. The dominant US variant is currently XFG.1.1 (32% of cases). National test positivity is approximately 1% (week ending May 2), with infections declining or stable in 37 states.

Trump signs AI oversight order after Anthropic “Mythos” model autonomously identifies thousands of OS vulnerabilities

The Trump administration moved this week to sign an executive order creating a “voluntary” framework requiring AI developers to provide frontier models to the US government 90 days before public release. The shift was catalyzed by Anthropic’s unreleased “Mythos Preview” model, which reportedly identified thousands of critical vulnerabilities in major operating systems and browsers without human prompting. The Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) has already signed pre-deployment evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI. The order represents a sharp reversal from the administration’s earlier anti-regulation stance — and a precedent for government pre-screening of frontier models, with obvious military and intelligence implications.

EU finalizes AI Omnibus deal — significantly lightens AI Act burden

The European Council and Parliament agreed on an AI Omnibus package that reduces recurring compliance costs, extends simplified requirements to small mid-caps, expands access to regulatory sandboxes (including a new EU-level sandbox) and adds a specific prohibition on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery. The deal substantially softens implementation pressure on smaller developers and harmonizes enforcement across member states ahead of key AI Act deadlines.

Emperor penguins and Antarctic fur seals added to IUCN Red List

The International Union for Conservation of Nature’s updated Red List — now listing 48,600 threatened species (28% of all assessed) — has added emperor penguins and Antarctic fur seals. Penguin populations are down roughly 10% (over 20,000 adults) between 2009 and 2018, from a current base of ~595,000, with populations projected to halve by the 2080s. Antarctic fur seals have declined from 2,187,000 mature individuals (1999) to 944,000 (2025), with specific colonies declining 47-86% as warming waters displace krill. Antarctica is warming at three times the global rate and losing 100 billion metric tons of ice annually. At the 48th Antarctic Treaty Consultative Meeting (May 11-21), China and Russia vetoed Specially Protected species designations in order to defend industrial krill fishing.

In other science news, astronomers using the James Webb Space Telescope this week published the clearest map yet of the universe’s large-scale “cosmic web,” the filamentary structure linking galaxies across billions of light-years — enabling new tests of dark-matter distribution models. Separately, researchers published a superconducting sensor capable of detecting energy below one zeptojoule (10⁻²¹ J), a record sensitivity that may enable direct detection of dark-matter candidates and pushes quantum sensing into fundamental-physics territory.

Arts and culture

The political climate of war abroad and oligarchic consolidation at home has continued to shape the cultural moment. Two threads dominate the past 24 hours: a series of attempts by Hollywood and the streaming industry to engage with the Iran war and broader Middle East violence, often in evasive or jingoistic forms; and the unfolding crisis at the great cultural institutions, which are being squeezed simultaneously by Trump-era cultural decrees and by their own donor classes.

The 79th Cannes Film Festival, which concluded last weekend, continues to reverberate through the trade press, with critics noting the unusual political weight of this year’s selection — a Brazilian-Argentine-French co-production on the suppression of striker mobilizations during the 1973 Pinochet coup, an Iranian-French film smuggled out of Tehran during the war, and a Ukrainian documentary built from frontline footage. The jury’s choice to award the Palme d’Or to the Iranian work was both a recognition of the courage of its makers and a tacit rebuke of the western military campaign now underway against the country in which it was filmed. That such a gesture is necessary at all is a measure of how thoroughly the broader film industry has abandoned political seriousness; the festival was otherwise dominated by big-budget franchise sequels, biopics of inoffensive figures and an unmistakable nostalgia for the Cold War period.

Hollywood’s relationship to the Iran war has been openly opportunistic. Within the past two months, multiple studios have green-lit projects framed around US naval operations in the Persian Gulf — variants of Top Gun-style militarist entertainment that present US strikes as inherently righteous and Iranian civilians as either invisible or as obstacles to be overcome. As the WSWS arts pages have observed repeatedly, this is the same cultural-industrial complex that gave us the genre of post-9/11 “war on terror” cinema two decades ago: militarist propaganda dressed as middlebrow drama. The current cycle is being accelerated by Pentagon technical-support agreements with major studios and by the integration of military-industrial-funded “consultants” into writers’ rooms.

In the institutional space, the Smithsonian continues to face direct political pressure under a Trump executive order requiring the removal of exhibitions deemed insufficiently celebratory of American history. Curators have reported the removal of materials on slavery, the labor movement and the civil rights struggle — even from museums founded specifically to document those histories. The Metropolitan Opera in New York has announced significant cuts to its 2026-27 season, citing donor losses; the New York City Ballet has reduced its corps positions. These institutional retrenchments are occurring in parallel with the cultural strangulation of public broadcasting, with PBS and NPR facing further congressional funding reductions in the reconciliation package now stuck in Senate negotiations.

Among the deaths this week, the death of the Russian poet Olga Sedakova, 76, has been noted in literary publications worldwide. Sedakova — one of the major Russian-language religious poets of the late Soviet and post-Soviet period, a translator of Rilke and Dante, and a public opponent of the Putin government’s war on Ukraine — wrote some of the most spiritually serious verse of her generation. Her death removes a distinct moral voice from the contemporary Russian literary scene.

In book publishing, Hisham Matar’s new novel on the Libyan revolution and its aftermath has received significant attention in the British and US press. Matar, whose previous work The Return won the Pulitzer Prize, continues to engage seriously with the legacy of imperialist intervention in North Africa.

WSWS coverage — last 24 hours

Perspective published: “Trump’s ‘Anti-Weaponization Fund’: The fusion of the gangster-oligarchy and the state” by Patrick Martin — read in full on wsws.org.

The Perspective is summarized above under Top Story #3. Key additional WSWS-specific points not captured above:

  • Martin’s central political conclusion: the removal of Trump is “a political necessity posed by the objective incompatibility of dictatorship with the interests of the working class and the survival of democratic rights.” Restoring norms or appealing to already-corrupted institutions is insufficient. Only the working class, “mobilized independently against the entire political system and the capitalist oligarchy it serves,” can halt the trajectory toward war, mass immiseration and outright dictatorship.
  • Martin extends Justice Sotomayor’s “immune, immune, immune” formulation from her dissent in Trump v. United States to the new addendum: “Falsifies his tax returns? Immune. Enriches his sons with fat government contracts? Immune. Rewards fascist thugs for their violent attack on the US Capitol? Immune.”
  • The piece argues that the fund’s primary practical effect will be to channel hundreds of millions of dollars to pardoned January 6 defendants, building a paramilitary auxiliary to be deployed against striking workers, anti-war demonstrators, “No Kings” protesters and ICE resistance — “the American equivalent of Hitler’s brownshirts.”
  • The Democrats’ role: Martin specifically attacks Senate and House Democrats’ Thursday press conference, where they criticized the fund only as “corruption” while declining to call for mass mobilization. The Democrats, he writes, “fear the consequences of mobilizing popular opposition more than they fear dictatorship itself.”

Long Island Rail Road workers: Vote “No” on the contract! — Daniel de Vries, Alan Whyte, Tom Hall

Building on the facts above (the 3%/3%/3.5%/4.5% wage path vs. 4.6% metro inflation), the WSWS statement makes a series of programmatic demands not present in the regional press. The authors call for the formation of rank-and-file committees independent of the union structure, demand a cost-of-living adjustment indexed to local inflation, and call for the immediate cancellation of the MTA’s $49 billion debt rather than the financing of raises through fare hikes. The statement notes the political contradiction of the Trump administration announcing $8 billion in Penn Station funding while claiming there is no money for transit-worker wages, and calls for the unification of LIRR workers with TWU Local 100’s 40,000 NYC subway and bus workers in a single regional struggle. The argument that union leaders ended the recent three-day strike specifically to prevent that linkage is unique to the WSWS treatment.

Rank-and-file candidate for UAW president to Nexteer workers: “Don’t accept any more delays. The time for your strike is now.” — Will Lehman

Will Lehman’s statement situates the Nexteer dispute within the broader history of two-tier exploitation in the auto sector. His central historical point — that the wage gap between parts and assembly workers grew from roughly 15% in the 1980s to 31% by 2000 through deliberate union-management collaboration — is not made in the regional or trade press. Lehman calls for international coordination of the strike through the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees (IWA-RFC), specifically warning of threatened outsourcing to Mexico and Poland. He demands $1,000/week in strike pay from the UAW’s $840 million strike fund and a transparent rank-and-file vote on any tentative agreement. The IWA-RFC framing — locating the Nexteer struggle as part of a globally coordinated rank-and-file network — is the distinctive WSWS contribution.

Australian budget further squeezes university funding — Mike Head

Beyond the funding figures cited above, Head develops a longer-term historical argument: federal funding for domestic university enrollments has fallen from approximately 80% of costs in 1987 to 40% today, with the gap filled by debt-financed student fees that now follow Australian graduates for decades. He situates the cuts within a broader subordination of Australian higher education to the requirements of the military-industrial buildup against China — including direct integration of universities into AUKUS-related research — and argues the consequence is a generation of working-class students priced out of higher education while military programs receive open-ended funding. The analysis links university defunding to the broader austerity offensive of the Labor-led government and identifies the National Tertiary Education Union as facilitator rather than opponent of the cuts.

Emperor penguins, Antarctic fur seals now on endangered species Red List — Frank Gaglioti

Gaglioti’s piece adds the key political element missing from mainstream coverage of the IUCN announcement: the active veto by China and Russia of “Specially Protected” species designations at the 48th Antarctic Treaty Consultative Meeting (May 11-21), which they blocked specifically to protect industrial krill fishing interests in the Southern Ocean. The article situates species collapse within the broader logic of capitalist accumulation, arguing that no Antarctic protection regime is workable so long as the global economy remains organized around private profit and competitive national accumulation. The piece concludes that only a socialist reorganization of production — with planetary planning of resource use under democratic working-class control — can address the root causes of the extinction wave.

JVP/NPP government in Sri Lanka preparing further attacks on workers amid growing crisis — Saman Gunadasa

Building on the macro figures cited in International developments, Gunadasa develops the political character of the JVP/NPP government: elected on a “left” platform to replace the discredited Wickremesinghe administration, it has functioned as the executor of the IMF austerity program, repressing public-sector strikes and protecting corporate profits. The piece quotes Dialog Axiata’s doubled Q1 net profit (9.6 billion rupees) as evidence of the upward redistribution underway under “left” cover, and identifies the government’s “greatest fear” as a popular uprising on the scale of the 2022 Aragalaya mass movement that toppled the Rajapaksa government. The article advocates the formation of democratic councils — workers’ committees independent of both the government and the trade union bureaucracy — as the only viable axis of resistance.

Socialism AI answers the New York Times: Not yes or no to AI, but who controls it — Socialism AI

This polemic — a direct response to NYT columnist Michelle Goldberg — acknowledges Goldberg’s documentation of real harm (120,000 AI-linked job losses; 300,000+ total job cuts in the first four months of 2026; Meta eliminating 8,000 positions while spending $145 billion on AI infrastructure; Oracle cutting up to 30,000 employees) but rejects her Nordic-model regulatory solution as “a capitalist dead-end.” The argument’s central formulation: “AI is not a dysfunction of capitalism — it is capitalism functioning with new weapons.” Drawing on Trotsky’s distinction between technology and capitalist ownership, the article demands no layoffs from AI implementation, shorter workweeks with maintained wages, worker control over technology introduction, and expropriation of the major tech corporations into publicly-owned utilities under democratic working-class control. The piece is also notable as an experiment in AI-authored revolutionary polemic, raising the question of who owns and directs the technology now writing in service of which class interests.

Ontario teachers and support staff face union betrayal as 250,000 contracts expire — Sterling Oliver

Building on the per-student funding cut and the takeover of eight school boards described above, Oliver’s piece argues that the five Ontario education unions are repeating their 2022 betrayal of CUPE support staff — when a walkout was ended without securing demands, leaving workers with only 3.5% annual raises and Ford’s anti-strike legislation (later partially reversed) intact. The WSWS calls for rank-and-file committees independent of the unions, a no-concessions stance on August 31 contract expirations, and the linking of the Ontario education struggle to public-sector struggles across Canada and to the broader rejection of austerity through working-class political organization.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — characteristically reduces the Anti-Weaponization Fund to “corruption” while promoting electoral remedies through the Democratic Party
  • Left Voice (PTS) — covers Bolivia and US labor with reasonable factual content but maintains its longstanding distance from the ICFI/WSWS and the program of independent rank-and-file committees
  • Liberation News / PSL — defends Iran without offering any independent socialist perspective; treats the Xi-Putin summit uncritically as anti-imperialist victory
  • Socialist Alternative (ISA) — call for “fightback” against Trump combined with longstanding endorsement of Democratic Party progressives
  • Socialist Worker (SWP, UK) — Gaza coverage frames the ceasefire as Israeli defeat without acknowledging the limits of the framework; routine apologetics for Labour-aligned unions
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — declares “revolutionary” potential of Bolivian crisis without specifying which existing organizations they propose to work through

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s coverage of the Anti-Weaponization Fund — the day’s defining political event in the US — treats it as a corruption story focused on the personal enrichment of Trump’s inner circle, while studiously avoiding the broader characterization of the fund as fascist state-building. The publication’s lead piece this week on the DNC autopsy advances the same line that has dominated DSA’s electoral practice since 2016: the Democratic Party can be reformed from within by replacing its consultant class with progressives like Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez. The piece explicitly disavows the perspective of building an independent working-class party. Jacobin’s coverage of the LIRR contract dispute, where it has appeared, frames the union leadership as essentially well-intentioned and pressures the membership to ratify the substandard deal “to live to fight another day.”

The anti-Marxist content of Jacobin’s output continues to express the social interests of the upper-middle-class professional layer that constitutes the bulk of DSA’s active membership: hostility to working-class independent political organization, accommodation to the trade-union bureaucracy and a strategic perspective oriented to the gradual capture of the Democratic Party’s apparatus.

Left Voice (PTS)

Left Voice’s coverage of the Bolivian crisis is factually reasonable and notes the COB’s centrality to the blockades — but the publication characteristically frames the Bolivian struggle in terms of the “MAS legacy” and Evo Morales’ role, rather than identifying the necessity for the workers and peasants to build a revolutionary leadership independent of MAS and of the trade-union officialdom that limited the 2019-2020 mobilizations. Left Voice’s US labor coverage maintains its longstanding pattern of supporting the most militant rank-and-file mobilizations (Nexteer, Amazon, Starbucks) while refusing to draw the political conclusion that the existing union structures must be broken with. The publication’s silence on the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees — which would be the natural framework for the coordinated international action Left Voice rhetorically endorses — is the indicator of its political relationship to the ICFI.

Liberation News / PSL

Liberation News’ coverage of the Xi-Putin Beijing summit is characteristic of the PSL’s longstanding “anti-imperialist” framework: the deepening Russia-China strategic alignment is presented uncritically as the global pole around which all anti-imperialist forces should organize. The publication’s coverage of the US-Iran war similarly defends the Iranian regime as a victim of US aggression — which it is — without offering any independent socialist perspective on the Iranian working class, which faces brutal repression from the same regime. The PSL line on Trump’s Anti-Weaponization Fund is largely silent: the publication has not characterized the Trump administration’s gangster-oligarchic state-building project in the categories of fascism.

The anti-Marxist core of PSL’s politics is the substitution of “anti-imperialism” — defined as opposition to whichever bourgeois regime the US is currently attacking — for the program of international socialist revolution.

Socialist Alternative (ISA)

Socialist Alternative’s lead pieces this week have continued the publication’s call for a “fightback” against Trump, articulated as a perspective for joint action with progressive Democrats and the more militant sections of the union officialdom — including Sara Nelson and Shawn Fain. The publication’s coverage of the LIRR and Nexteer disputes endorses militant action but explicitly opposes the building of rank-and-file committees that would operate outside the formal union structures. The publication’s recent coverage of Bolivia frames the COB-led blockades positively without identifying the need for a revolutionary working-class party independent of the Bolivian trade-union officialdom.

The publication’s central anti-Marxist content remains its electoral perspective for the United States: the call for “100 elected socialists” combined with the practical orientation to whichever Democratic Party primary candidates appear most “left.”

Socialist Worker (SWP, UK)

The SWP’s coverage of the Gaza phase-two ceasefire frames the agreement as a victory for the Palestinian resistance and treats the recovery of the last Israeli hostage as removing a key Israeli pretext for continued attacks. The publication does not draw out the obvious political point — that the framework structurally subordinates Palestinian self-governance to a Trump-led “Board of Peace” — and so reproduces the broader Stop the War Coalition framing that treats any ceasefire as straightforward progress. The SWP’s coverage of UK labor disputes continues the pattern of treating Sharon Graham of Unite and Mick Lynch’s successors at the RMT as basically progressive figures who can be pressured leftward.

The publication’s analysis of the Trump Anti-Weaponization Fund, where present, treats it as evidence of “creeping authoritarianism” without identifying the specific class character of the gangster-oligarchic state-building underway.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The IMT’s coverage of the Bolivian crisis declares the existence of “revolutionary potential” in the COB-led blockades and calls for the formation of a “workers’ government” — but characteristically avoids specifying through which existing political organizations the IMT proposes to build that government. The publication’s coverage of the LIRR and Nexteer disputes in the US is sympathetic to militant action but limited in circulation and avoids any positive engagement with the rank-and-file committees promoted by the WSWS and SEP. The IMT’s recent re-launch as the “Revolutionary Communist Party” (RCP) is presented as the recovery of a Bolshevik tradition that, on examination, evades the actual programmatic continuity of revolutionary Marxism through the Fourth International. The publication’s silence on the ICFI is total — a refusal of polemical engagement that itself constitutes a political position.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The Strait of Hormuz blockade as a global economic war on workers

The US Navy’s redirection of 94 commercial vessels and disabling of four others in the Strait of Hormuz has been framed in US media exclusively as a military tactic. The economic dimensions — sustained crude prices near $97-$104, the global pass-through to gasoline, food and transport costs, and the resulting hit to working-class household budgets internationally — have been suppressed from mainstream coverage. A WSWS piece explicitly framing the Hormuz blockade as a global economic war on workers, with the data quantifying the wage impact in major economies, would directly counter that suppression.

2. The Michigan consumer sentiment record low: what working people are feeling

The University of Michigan’s preliminary May print of 48.2 — the lowest reading since the series began in 1952 — is a quantitative measure of the depth of working-class anxiety four months into the Iran war and a year into Trump’s second-term tariff regime. The print is being buried in bourgeois business coverage because it cannot be reconciled with the administration’s stated economic narrative. A WSWS piece situating this number in the context of stagnant real wages, food and fuel inflation, and the specific class composition of those reporting pessimism would provide important political documentation.

Recommended reading Advisor Perspectives Michigan consumer sentiment falls further to another record low

3. The “Big Beautiful Bill” as a class-war redistribution

The reconciliation bill’s $3.4 trillion deficit expansion is concentrated upward in the form of permanent tax cuts for the wealthy, while it falls downward in the form of permanent Medicaid and SNAP cuts directly affecting tens of millions of the poorest Americans. The internal Republican dispute — framed in bourgeois press as a procedural fight over the “anti-weaponization” allocation — obscures the bill’s fundamental character as the largest upward redistribution of wealth in a generation. A WSWS piece quantifying the projected impact on Medicaid enrollment, food insecurity and infant mortality would document the human cost.

4. The DNC autopsy and Gaza: the missing chapter

The Rivera report’s failure to even mention the Democrats’ support for the genocide in Gaza, despite the documented role of that policy in alienating Arab-American, Muslim and youth voters in Michigan, Wisconsin and Pennsylvania, is the politically decisive omission. A WSWS piece reading the autopsy specifically through the lens of what it does not address — Gaza, war funding, and the strangulation of public services — would expose the report as a deliberate political evasion rather than a methodological failure.

Recommended reading CNN DNC’s 2024 election autopsy — full text

5. Bolivia’s general strike: what is missing is a revolutionary leadership

The COB-led blockades in La Paz have the character of a general strike combined with a nationwide popular mobilization — a level of working-class action not seen in Bolivia since the 2003-2005 gas wars. The question raised by the crisis is the same question raised then: who governs? A WSWS piece explicitly addressing the absence of a revolutionary working-class leadership in Bolivia — the MAS’s role in re-routing the mobilizations of the past 20 years back into parliamentary channels, and the contemporary requirements of a Bolivian section of the ICFI — would advance the political clarification urgently required.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Bolivian president to reshuffle cabinet amid anti-government protests

6. Ebola Bundibugyo and the destruction of African public health infrastructure

The Bundibugyo strain’s spread through Ituri Province and into Uganda — with no licensed vaccine, a four-week detection gap, and healthcare workers dying within days — is inseparable from the systematic destruction of public health infrastructure in the DRC under decades of IMF structural adjustment and Western-backed resource wars in eastern Congo. The PHEIC declaration is an admission that global public health institutions are unprepared. A WSWS piece quantifying the dismantling of DRC healthcare infrastructure since 1996 — including the role of the M23 and AFC-FDLR conflicts in destroying clinics — would situate the outbreak within its full political context.

Recommended reading WHO Ebola Bundibugyo virus disease — DRC and Uganda

7. The Anthropic “Mythos” model and the militarization of AI

The Anthropic “Mythos Preview” model’s autonomous identification of thousands of OS vulnerabilities — and the resulting reversal in Trump-administration AI policy toward government pre-screening of frontier models — represents a qualitative escalation in the integration of advanced AI with the US intelligence and military apparatus. A WSWS piece exposing the specific contractual relationships between leading AI companies (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, xAI) and the US military-intelligence establishment, and the implications for surveillance and cyberwarfare, would address a gap in the publication’s recent technology coverage.
