1. US–Iran talks stall on day 57 as Hegseth declares blockade “going global”

The 57th day of the US-led war on Iran opened with both Washington and Tehran sending envoys to Islamabad — but with no contact between them. Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi arrived Saturday for consultations with Pakistani officials, while the White House confirmed Jared Kushner and Middle East envoy Steve Witkoff were also en route. Iran’s Foreign Ministry spokesperson stated unequivocally: “No meeting is planned to take place between Iran and the US,” with Iran’s positions to be conveyed through Pakistani intermediaries only. Tehran has called the unilateral US ceasefire extension “meaningless” so long as the US Navy blockade of Iranian ports — imposed April 13 — remains in force.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth simultaneously announced the blockade is “going global.” US warships have now turned back 34 vessels and seized two Iranian tankers in the Indian Ocean, including the M/T Tifani carrying 2 million barrels of crude bound for China. Treasury imposed secondary sanctions on China’s Hengli Petrochemical refinery and 40 shipping entities, while freezing $344 million in cryptocurrency tied to Iranian sales networks. The US has assembled three carrier strike groups, more than 15,000 personnel, and over 200 combat aircraft in the region. As Andre Damon notes on the WSWS, a leaked Pentagon memo threatens NATO allies who refuse to participate by stripping Spain from planning meetings, questioning Britain’s claim to the Falklands, restricting French intelligence-sharing, and canceling German military exercises — the architecture of an inter-imperialist confrontation framed as collective defense.

Key facts: - Iran’s confirmed civilian death toll has reached 3,375, including 376 children, with 1.3 million jobs destroyed and 3.2 million internally displaced. - The US military has redirected at least 33 ships since the blockade began April 13; Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan said Turkey could join post-agreement demining operations. - Trump previously called any further extension “highly unlikely” before extending it unilaterally without setting a new expiration date.

2. Strait of Hormuz crisis becomes “biggest energy security threat in history”

International Energy Agency chief Fatih Birol used that phrase to characterize the dual blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, in which Iran closed the chokepoint on February 28 after the US-Israeli air strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, and the United States imposed a counter-blockade of Iranian ports on April 13. Roughly 13 million barrels per day — nearly a quarter of global seaborne oil and a fifth of LNG flows — have been removed from the market. The IMF’s April World Economic Outlook, subtitled “Global Economy in the Shadow of War,” compares the disruption to the 1970s oil shock and warns it has become the dominant macroeconomic variable of 2026.

Brent crude settled Friday at $106.96 per barrel, with WTI at $97.36 — WTI on track for a 17% weekly gain, the largest in years. JPMorgan modelers project $150 oil if the closure persists into mid-May. The IMF reference forecast assumed only a 19% increase in energy commodity prices and a short conflict; current market conditions already exceed that scenario by a wide margin.

Key facts: - Brent is up approximately 40% since the conflict began and 73% year-to-date. - The Strait previously carried about 25% of world seaborne oil trade and 20% of global LNG. - IMF projects 2026 global headline inflation at 4.4%, with growth slowing to 3.1%, citing the Middle East war as the principal driver.

3. EU finalizes €90 billion wartime loan to Ukraine after Hungary lifts veto

The EU Council finalized a €90 billion ($106 billion) loan package for Ukraine on April 23 after Hungary’s Viktor Orbán dropped a months-long veto, reportedly in exchange for a side deal restoring the Druzhba pipeline’s oil flow to Budapest and Bratislava. The package will be borrowed from EU capital markets and disbursed in two equal tranches: €45 billion ($53 billion) in 2026 and €45 billion in 2027 — covering roughly two-thirds of Kyiv’s projected funding needs across both years.

Approximately one-third of the loan goes to Ukrainian budgetary support; the remaining two-thirds, around $70 billion, is earmarked for weapons procurement and the expansion of European arms production capacity. Repayment is structured to come from future Russian war reparations rather than from Ukrainian taxpayers, but the loan is conditioned on continued “rule-of-law” and “anti-corruption” benchmarks — language that has historically introduced IMF-style austerity. The IISS warns Ukraine’s capacity to deny Russian breakthroughs is “under increasing strain” given recruitment shortfalls and reduced Western military support; the loan directly addresses that funding gap, anchoring Ukraine to a multi-year war economy.

Key facts: - The Ukrainian military reported 910 Russian personnel killed in the 24 hours to April 24; cumulative confirmed Russian casualties since February 2022 are now estimated at roughly 1,323,460. - 194 combat engagements were logged on April 23, with the heaviest fighting in the Pokrovsk and Huliaipole sectors. - Mediazona’s count of confirmed Russian officer deaths reached 7,043 as of April 24.

4. Israel kills two civilians in Lebanon hours after three-week ceasefire extension

Within 24 hours of the White House’s April 23 announcement of a three-week extension to the Lebanon ceasefire, Israeli forces killed two people in the village of Touline in Marjayoun district. The IDF also struck what it described as Hezbollah rocket launchers in Yater and Kafra; Lebanese media reported a vehicle targeted in Yohmor. Hezbollah fired rockets in response and downed an Israeli drone with a missile. UN Secretary-General António Guterres said the strikes and rocket fire “may breach international law” and demanded full respect for the cessation of hostilities.

Hezbollah lawmaker Ali Fayyad was direct: “It is essential to point out that the ceasefire is meaningless in light of Israel’s insistence on hostile acts.” Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu insisted Israel was “maintaining full freedom of action against any threat.” Israeli forces remain stationed in southern Lebanon enforcing a so-called “yellow line” demarcation; CNN satellite analysis published this week likened the scale of destruction along that line to Gaza.

Key facts: - Lebanese Health Ministry casualty totals since the war resumed March 2: 2,491 killed and 7,719 wounded. - The ceasefire extension is the third since Trump took office; each has been followed by Israeli strikes within days. - Multiple southern Lebanese villages remain depopulated under standing IDF evacuation orders.

5. UN/FAO report: 318 million face crisis-level hunger as Gaza and Sudan slide toward famine

A joint UN, EU, and FAO report released April 24 found that 318 million people globally now face IPC Phase 3 (crisis) hunger or worse — and that two-thirds of acute global hunger is concentrated in just ten conflict-hit countries. Deputy UN Secretary-General Amina Mohammed told reporters the situation has become “deeply entrenched” rather than an emergency expected to ebb. The World Food Programme separately warned six critical operations face aid pipeline breaks before year’s end.

Sudan, now in its fourth year of civil war, accounts for the largest single share. Half the country’s population — 25 million people — faces acute hunger, with 6.3 million in the IPC Phase 5 famine emergency category. Famine has been formally confirmed in some areas since August 2024. An estimated 400,000 have died in the broader civil war. The UN’s 2026 Sudan Humanitarian Response Plan, requiring $9 billion, is funded at under 16%. Gaza’s situation is the most rapidly deteriorating: all four governorates are now classified IPC Phase 4 or higher, with the UN warning that famine risk has “intensified sharply” since Israel cut off all aid on March 2, 2026 — fully reversing the modest gains made under the earlier ceasefire.

Key facts: - WFP requires $600 million over the next six months to maintain operations in Sudan alone. - Gaza aid trucks have been halted at the Rafah and Kerem Shalom crossings since March 2; the Israeli government has provided no resumption schedule. - Yemen, DRC, Haiti, Afghanistan, Syria, South Sudan, Somalia, and Nigeria round out the ten worst-hit countries.

6. Pentagon convenes Detroit Three CEOs to integrate auto industry into “Arsenal of Freedom” buildup

Pentagon officials convened meetings this week with General Motors CEO Mary Barra and Ford CEO Jim Farley to map out auto-industry participation in what the Trump administration is calling the “Arsenal of Freedom” — a planned conversion of US manufacturing capacity to military production at a scale not seen since World War II. With munitions inventories severely depleted by the Iran war, a retired Army general told reporters it “could take five years or more to replenish the munitions used in the last 40 days.” The administration’s proposed defense budget for the coming fiscal year is $1.5 trillion.

As Shannon Jones notes on the WSWS, the parallel to WWII Detroit reconversion is being drawn explicitly inside the administration; the WSWS labels the project an “Arsenal of Genocide” given the principal customers — a US war on Iran, a Ukrainian war effort backed by the new EU loan, and continuing Israeli operations in Lebanon and Gaza. UAW reform candidate Will Lehman is quoted calling for rank-and-file action to halt war production; the UAW International leadership has publicly endorsed both the militarization drive and Trump’s tariff agenda.

Key facts: - The Pentagon-auto meetings were first reported by the Detroit Free Press and confirmed by both companies. - Stellantis was not invited to the initial meeting; reporting suggests the administration is steering contracts toward GM and Ford. - US munitions stocks for Patriot interceptors, JASSM cruise missiles, and 155mm artillery shells are reported below operational reserves.

El Salvador stages mass show trial of 486 alleged gang members

The Bukele government opened proceedings against 486 alleged MS-13 members on charges encompassing more than 47,000 alleged crimes, including approximately 29,000 homicides — a single proceeding of historically unprecedented scale. The hearings are being conducted virtually under the country’s four-year state of emergency, which a UN expert panel said in its assessment this week “undermines the right to defence.” El Salvador’s prison population has surged past 118,000 — more than double designed system capacity — and an NGO database documents 517 deaths in custody at facilities including the CECOT mega-prison; nine in ten of those who died were never convicted of any crime.

As Andrea Lobo notes on the WSWS, Bukele’s authoritarian model is being explicitly embraced and replicated by the Trump administration. The structural drivers of MS-13’s growth — the 1996 Clinton-era immigration law that mass-deported gang members from Los Angeles to Central America, and an entrenched economic inequality in which a tiny elite controls roughly half of national wealth — are absent from the official narrative. Poverty has risen from 22.8% to 25.8% under Bukele’s tenure even as the regime accumulates international plaudits.

Four killed at 200-year-old German tannery in hydrogen-sulphide accident

Five workers were found unconscious in a pit at the 200-year-old Beuleke leather and fur factory in Runkel, Hesse on April 16. Three died at the scene from hydrogen sulphide poisoning; a fourth — 35-year-old Yuri, who leaves a wife and young daughter — died in hospital April 22; a fifth was successfully revived. The deaths effectively wiped out nearly the entire workforce of the small family enterprise. Marianne Arens on the WSWS traces the accident to the systematic erosion of occupational-safety enforcement under EU-wide deregulation, noting that the EU’s supply-chain accountability legislation has been significantly weakened and its implementation pushed back to 2028 — and that hazardous tanning conditions globally, including child-labor exposure to chromium and other toxic chemicals across South Asia, remain unaddressed.

El Gamal family released from Dilley after federal court order

US District Judge Fred Biery ordered the immediate release of Hayam El Gamal and her five children from the CoreCivic-run Dilley family detention center in South Texas, where the family had been imprisoned since June 2025 without criminal charges. The family was seized two days after a firebombing attack in Boulder, Colorado, allegedly carried out by Hayam’s estranged husband, Mohamed Sabry Soliman; the FBI confirmed that none of the six family members had any advance knowledge of the attack. Their release follows sustained legal action and a public campaign led by the family’s attorney Eric Lee. The El Gamals are now under electronic monitoring and remain in active deportation proceedings.

As Jacob Crosse argues in today’s WSWS Perspective, the family’s prolonged detention was a deliberate application of Sippenhaft — kin liability — and represents the lead edge of an architecture of mass detention. Between April 2025 and February 2026, more than 5,600 individuals passed through Dilley alone, where documented abuses included contaminated water, a measles outbreak, untreated medical emergencies, and pervasive mold. The Dilley facility was opened under the Obama administration; congressional Democrats voted in March 2026 to approve DHS funding underwriting its continued operation.

White House AI policy framework calls for federal preemption of state laws

The Trump administration’s National Policy Framework for AI, released March 20 and now beginning to be implemented through executive action, recommends Congress preempt state AI laws that “impose undue burdens” — establishing a single, minimally restrictive national standard. The Department of Justice’s newly established AI Litigation Task Force is empowered to challenge state AI laws it deems unconstitutional or preempted. The framework sets up a direct collision course with California, which signed its own AI procurement executive order in March, and with the EU’s stricter AI Act. Industry analysts at Alston & Bird describe it as an explicit federal-preemption strategy designed to consolidate AI policymaking authority in Washington while clearing space for the largest US firms.

Tariff refund stream concentrates wealth as households absorb the cost

The CBP’s CAPE refund portal is now actively processing claims from more than 330,000 importers on $166 billion in tariffs paid since 2025, after the Supreme Court struck down Trump’s universal tariff structure in February. The expected refund wave will be largely disbursed by the end of April. The refunds, however, flow to corporate importers — not to US households, which the Tax Foundation calculates absorbed an average of $1,500 in higher prices in 2026 from the original tariffs, the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993. The asymmetric flow is fueling a wave of bolt-on M&A as firms recycle the refund liquidity into vertical supply-chain acquisitions. The Section 232 duties on steel, aluminum, and copper imposed April 6 remain in force.

US measles cases reach 1,792 in 33 states

The CDC’s April 24 update reports 1,792 confirmed US measles cases across 33 states and jurisdictions, with 22 active outbreaks. 93% of cases (1,668) are outbreak-associated. The largest cluster remains in Spartanburg County, South Carolina, which has functioned as a seed for cross-state spread since the 2025 outbreak there never fully resolved. The 2026 case count is running ahead of where 2025 stood at the equivalent point. The fundamental driver is sustained collapse of vaccination uptake; Trump-administration cuts to CDC outreach funding and HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s public skepticism of MMR vaccine schedules have not been reversed.

H5N1: 71 US human cases, 2 deaths, third Cambodia case in 2026

The CDC reports 71 confirmed US human H5N1 cases and 2 deaths to date. In the past 30 days alone, H5N1 was confirmed in 85 poultry flocks — 45 commercial and 40 backyard. A 3-year-old boy in Cambodia became that country’s third human case of 2026 this week, per CIDRAP. The CDC has streamlined H5N1 surveillance reporting to monthly updates, citing reduced case volume — a shift critics say also reduces public visibility of the spread in dairy cattle and among farm workers. The agency continues to rate the public health risk “low” while acknowledging the persistent dairy-cattle reservoir.

NEJM SERENA-6 trial: camizestrant nearly doubles progression-free survival

The New England Journal of Medicine published the SERENA-6 results this week. Among 315 patients with HR+/HER2- advanced breast cancer who developed ESR1 mutations during first-line aromatase inhibitor + CDK4/6 inhibitor therapy, switching to camizestrant produced median progression-free survival of 16.0 months versus 9.2 months for those who remained on the aromatase inhibitor (hazard ratio 0.44; p<0.0001). Time to deterioration in quality of life was delayed from 6.4 to 21.0 months. The trial is potentially practice-changing — it establishes liquid-biopsy-guided treatment switching as a viable precision-oncology strategy, allowing therapy adjustment on molecular evidence of resistance before clinical progression.

Geoffrey Hinton warns of “runaway AI” at UN Geneva conference

Speaking April 23 at the UN Research Institute for Social Development’s “AI for Social Development” conference in Geneva, Geoffrey Hinton compared AI to “a very fast car with no steering wheel,” warning that superintelligent systems could outpace human control if global governance does not keep pace. He flagged that AI development capacity is concentrated in just a few economies — a concentration that, he argued, threatens to deepen global inequality even as it accelerates technical capability. The intervention came during a peak week of UN AI governance negotiations and adds moral weight to calls for binding international oversight, in direct tension with the Trump administration’s federal-preemption push.

Arts and culture

The week’s most significant cultural event is the death of Michael Tilson Thomas, the American conductor and music educator, at age 81. Tilson Thomas — who led the San Francisco Symphony for 25 years (the longest tenure since the orchestra’s 1911 founding), founded the New World Symphony academy for emerging musicians in 1987, and continued Leonard Bernstein’s Young People’s Concerts tradition through PBS’s Keeping Score — represented a particular American synthesis: a conductor descended from Yiddish theater pioneers Boris and Bessie Thomashevsky, mentored for 22 years by Bernstein, who treated democratic access to classical music not as an outreach program but as the central political and pedagogical task of a working artist’s life. He was diagnosed with glioblastoma in 2021 and gave his final performance in April 2025; his husband Joshua Robison, whom he married in 2014, had died only two months earlier. As Fred Mazelis writes on the WSWS, Tilson Thomas’s career was unusual in mainstream American musical life precisely because of its sustained refusal to treat orchestral repertoire as an elite consumer product — a refusal informed by a leftward family inheritance and by Bernstein’s own Popular Front-era cultural politics.

The week’s other major openings reflect a different set of pressures shaping the American stage and screen.

  • August Wilson’s Joe Turner’s Come and Gone opens on Broadway. A revival starring Taraji P. Henson, directed by Debbie Allen, opened April 25 at the Ethel Barrymore Theatre. Wilson’s 1988 play — set in 1911 Pittsburgh among Black migrants from the Jim Crow South — remains the most rigorous treatment in modern American drama of the relation between historical memory, displacement, and the question of agency under capitalism. The casting choices have been received as significant for the play’s continued life on the commercial stage.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Who profits from the €90 billion EU Ukraine wartime loan?

Roughly $70 billion of the EU’s newly finalized loan is earmarked for weapons procurement and arms-production expansion — flowing primarily to Rheinmetall, BAE Systems, KNDS, Thales, MBDA, and US suppliers such as Raytheon, Lockheed Martin, and General Dynamics. The loan is conditioned on “rule-of-law” benchmarks that have historically delivered IMF-style austerity. No bourgeois outlet has examined the contractor flow, the asset-class distribution of the underlying EU borrowing, or what repayment-via-reparations means for Ukrainian workers’ futures should the war end on terms unfavorable to NATO.

2. Working-class energy costs of the Strait of Hormuz crisis

Brent at $106.96 per barrel and up 73% year-to-date is being treated by mainstream coverage as a geopolitical and corporate-revenue story. The actual transmission to working-class household budgets — heating, food, transportation, fuel-subsidy reserves in the Global South — has received no systematic class-analytical treatment anywhere. Wage data from Egypt, Pakistan, Indonesia, Bangladesh, and the Philippines is available; a structural piece linking the Iran war to a wage-price compression across the working class globally is a major coverage gap.

Recommended reading Fortune IEA chief Fatih Birol: “the biggest energy security threat in history”

3. Sudan in its fourth year of war: the political economy of selective humanitarianism

Sudan has 25 million people in acute hunger, 14 million displaced, and an estimated 400,000 dead, yet its UN response plan is funded at less than 16%. Western media treat this as an “abandoned crisis.” A WSWS analysis of the political-economic logic of selective imperial attention — why billions for Ukraine and the Middle East war, and effectively nothing for Sudan — would frame the structural relation between imperialist priorities and which lives are made visible.

Recommended reading NPR “Humiliated, broken, powerless”: Sudan enters fourth year of war

4. The tariff refund-to-corporation pipeline

The CBP CAPE portal is processing $166 billion in refund claims from 330,000+ corporate importers. Meanwhile, US households absorbed an average $1,500 in higher prices — a regressive tax that has already been paid and will not be refunded. The asymmetry — workers paid the tariff, corporations are getting the money back — is hidden behind procedural language and ought to be made the subject of a major analytical piece. The corporate liquidity surge from the refunds is fueling M&A activity, further concentrating capital.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera US launches tariff refund system as thousands of importers line up

5. Geoffrey Hinton and the political economy of “runaway AI”

Hinton’s UN intervention compared AI to a “very fast car with no steering wheel” and flagged that AI capacity is concentrated in a handful of economies. The WSWS has covered the Pentagon’s confrontation with Anthropic but has not yet produced a sustained piece on the structural relationship between AI concentration, the federal-preemption push in the new National Policy Framework, and the labor-market implications for white-collar workers in the imperialist core. Hinton’s intervention is a useful entry point.

Recommended reading UN News Geoffrey Hinton calls for brakes on runaway AI

6. Hungary and Bulgaria after Orbán: what European far-right realignment actually means

Hungary’s April 12 election ended 16 years of Fidesz-KDNP rule with Péter Magyar’s Tisza Party winning in a landslide; the near-simultaneous Bulgaria result saw the Progressive Bulgaria coalition take 44% and an outright majority. Mainstream coverage has framed this as a liberal victory. A WSWS analysis of what Magyar’s Tisza actually represents class-politically — and whether the near-simultaneous result in Sofia signals a broader EU-Atlanticist consolidation against the Trump-aligned far-right in Eastern Europe — is a significant gap in left coverage.
