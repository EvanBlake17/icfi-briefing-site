1. US strikes Iran as peace talks stall in Qatar

US Central Command conducted what it called “self-defense” strikes against Iranian missile launch sites and mine-laying boats near Bandar Abbas and the Strait of Hormuz on May 25 — the same day Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf arrived in Doha for Qatar-mediated negotiations over sanctions relief and reopening the strait. CENTCOM spokesman Captain Tim Hawkins characterized the strikes as “restraint during the ongoing ceasefire.” Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps declared it retains a “legitimate right to respond to any violation of the ceasefire.”

Secretary of State Marco Rubio said negotiations could extend “several more days,” with disputes centering on the language governing Iran’s nuclear program and the scope of sanctions relief. President Trump posted on Truth Social: “It will only be a Great Deal for all or, no Deal at all — Back to the Battlefront.” As Kevin Reed noted on the WSWS, the strikes expose the fiction that Iran’s military has been “functionally defeated,” demonstrating that Washington wields military force as a coercive instrument alongside diplomacy, not as an alternative to it.

Key facts: - Strikes targeted missile launch sites and mine-laying boats near Bandar Abbas in southern Iran. - Talks are mediated through Qatar in Doha; Rubio projects “several more days.” - The IRGC asserts a “legitimate right to respond to any violation of the ceasefire.”

2. Israel escalates Lebanon war, 12 killed in Bekaa Valley strike

An Israeli airstrike on the village of Mashghara in Lebanon’s Bekaa Valley killed 12 people on the night of May 25–26. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu posted on social media: “What this requires of us now is to increase the blows, to increase the intensity. We will smite them hip and thigh.” Israel has called up an additional battalion. The war began on March 2 when Hezbollah fired rockets in solidarity with Iran.

Lebanon’s Health Ministry reports 3,185 killed and 9,633 wounded since the war began, with over 1 million Lebanese displaced. Israeli airstrikes also killed at least eight people in the southern towns of Ad-Doueir, Tibnin, Burj el-Shamali, and Shebaa on May 25–26. Lebanese and Israeli military delegations are scheduled to meet in Washington for direct talks within three days; Hezbollah opposes the negotiations.

Key facts: - 12 killed in the Mashghara strike; at least 8 more in southern Lebanon towns. - Cumulative toll: 3,185 killed, 9,633 wounded, more than 1 million displaced. - Direct Lebanese-Israeli military talks set for Washington in three days.

3. Ebola outbreak in DRC and Uganda passes 1,000 cases; no vaccine exists

The Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda has surpassed 1,000 cases. As of May 24, at least 1,010 suspected and confirmed cases and 231 deaths have been reported — a case fatality ratio of roughly 25–50 percent. The outbreak is caused by the Bundibugyo virus strain, for which no approved vaccine or treatment exists, despite the strain being known since 2007. WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus stated: “We are facing an extremely serious and difficult outbreak. It will get worse before it gets better.” The WHO declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern on May 17.

Cases have reached Kampala via travel from the DRC, and the US, UK, India, and Australia have imposed travel restrictions and airport screening. The response has been hampered by ongoing armed conflict in eastern DRC, which has displaced tens of thousands and bred “local population distrust of outside authorities.” Imperial College London modeling cited by the WSWS suggests the true case count is “far more entrenched than official surveillance had detected”; two suspected cases have appeared in Lombardy, Italy.

Key facts: - 1,010+ cases, 231 deaths; Bundibugyo strain has no approved vaccine or treatment. - PHEIC declared May 17; cases in Kampala and two suspected in Italy. - The strain has been known since 2007 — nearly two decades without a treatment.

Sources WHO Ebola in DRC and Uganda determined a PHEIC · ABC News Ebola outbreak will get worse before it gets better, WHO chief warns · WSWS Ebola outbreak in DRC and Uganda passes 1,000 cases

4. Russia launches largest aerial barrage of the Ukraine war

Russia launched its biggest aerial barrage against Ukraine since the start of the full-scale invasion shortly after a brief mid-May ceasefire expired, striking residential areas, energy sites, and critical infrastructure. UN-linked protection monitors have documented a chilling new pattern: FPV and fiber-optic drones being used to track and strike civilians during “everyday movement” — a tactic monitors describe as a “human safari” deliberately targeting aid workers, caregivers, and civilians seeking healthcare.

Ukraine and Russia are holding direct talks in Abu Dhabi, but “substantial differences remain before a peace agreement can be reached.” The most concrete outcome of negotiations so far remains the February prisoner swap (Russia returned 157 soldiers and 3 civilians; Ukraine returned 150 soldiers). The escalation underscores the failure of the US- and NATO-backed proxy war, waged on the backs of the Ukrainian and Russian working classes, to produce anything but mounting civilian death.

Key facts: - The barrage is the largest since the start of the full-scale invasion in 2022. - Monitors document drones used in a “human safari” against civilians. - Direct talks continue in Abu Dhabi with “substantial differences” remaining.

Sources Security Council Report May 2026 forecast: protection of civilians · Georgetown GIWPS Conflict tracker — May 2026 updates

5. Gaza ceasefire holds in name only as killings continue

The Gaza ceasefire is technically in effect, but approximately 400 Palestinians have been killed since it nominally began. Israel has “nearly obliterated Gaza,” with more than 1 in 10 of the strip’s Palestinians killed or injured over the course of the conflict; the vast majority of the 2.3 million residents are homeless. The “ceasefire” framework has effectively normalized a continuing lower-intensity killing campaign while providing Western governments political cover to declare the crisis resolved.

The contradiction is sharpened by Israel’s simultaneous escalation in Lebanon (see above). What is being counted in diplomatic ledgers as a success functions on the ground as a managed continuation of the genocide, with the machinery of siege, displacement, and bombardment intact.

Key facts: - ~400 Palestinians killed since the ceasefire nominally took effect. - More than 1 in 10 of Gaza’s Palestinians killed or injured during the war. - The vast majority of 2.3 million residents remain homeless.

6. Iran ends 87-day nationwide internet blackout

Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian ordered the rollback of nearly three months of internet restrictions on May 26, ending an 87-day nationwide blackout imposed following the outbreak of the US-Iran war. The shutdown — among the longest national internet outages in modern history — affected most of Iran’s roughly 87 million people and was imposed as a wartime information-control measure.

The restoration coincides with the ongoing peace negotiations in Doha and signals Pezeshkian’s political effort to demonstrate normalizing conditions. The blackout suppressed internal anti-war dissent, blocked families from reaching conscripted relatives, and severely disrupted the economy while the government negotiated behind closed doors — a template for wartime digital repression that warrants scrutiny well beyond Iran.

Key facts: - 87-day blackout, imposed at the war’s outbreak, now fully reversed. - Affected most of Iran’s ~87 million people. - Timed to the Doha negotiations as a signal of normalization.

Erdoğan removes CHP leader in “preemptive coup”

Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan orchestrated a court ruling stripping Republican People’s Party (CHP) leader Özgür Özel of the party leadership, followed by a police seizure of CHP headquarters. The CHP is Turkey’s main parliamentary opposition and had been leading in national polling. Özel is to be replaced by Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu. As Ulaş Sevinç argues in the WSWS Perspective, the move is a “preemptive political coup” — a national expression of the international collapse of bourgeois democracy under capitalist crisis, comparable in form to Trump’s January 6 coup attempt. Western governments and major outlets have framed it narrowly as a domestic rule-of-law crisis. Over 90 percent of Turks oppose the US war on Iran, even as Ankara facilitates the flow of Azerbaijani oil to Israel and deepens NATO cooperation.

Sudan opposition approves post-war roadmap in Nairobi

At a May 22–23 summit in Nairobi, Sudanese opposition groups — including the Sudan Liberation Movement (Abdul Wahid al-Nur), the National Umma Party, the Sudanese Congress Party, and the Federal Gathering — endorsed a “Roadmap for Stopping and Ending the War.” The roadmap covers humanitarian access, ceasefires, and political transition, and explicitly rejects any future role for the National Congress Party and the Islamic Movement (Omar al-Bashir’s former apparatus). The war has entered its fourth year, with an estimated 12 million people displaced and the UN reporting a “horrific milestone” of rising sexual violence and mass atrocities. The Nairobi framework received almost no Western media attention.

Japan and the Philippines deepen military collaboration against China

Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr.’s Tokyo visit produced a “comprehensive strategic partnership” — one step below a formal military alliance — with both countries negotiating a General Security of Military Information Agreement (GSOMIA) to enable intelligence and radar sharing. Japan, whose 2026 military budget reached 10.6 trillion yen ($66.58 billion, 1.9 percent of GDP), is set to export Type 88 surface-to-ship missiles, Abukuma-class destroyers, TC-90 aircraft, and radar systems following its newly lifted ban on lethal weapons exports. A Japanese diplomatic source said the aim is to “drive a wedge to prevent tilting too close to China,” tying the deal to the broader US-led containment strategy against China, Iran, and Russia.

China launches Shenzhou-23 with a record year-long mission planned

China launched the Shenzhou-23 spacecraft on May 24 at 11:08 p.m. Beijing time from the Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center. The crew — commander Zhu Yangzhu (a Shenzhou-16 veteran) alongside first-time flyers Zhang Zhiyuan and Li Jiaying — will conduct more than 100 science projects spanning space life sciences, materials science, microgravity fluid physics, and aerospace medicine. One astronaut is slated for a record year-long stay aboard the Chinese Space Station to study human adaptability for deep-space missions. The flight is part of China’s program targeting a crewed lunar landing by 2030.

Cambodia pardons Kem Sokha but preserves political ban

Cambodian Senate president and acting head of state Hun Sen granted a pardon to opposition leader Kem Sokha, ending his 27-year treason sentence. The pardon maintains restrictions on his political rights and international travel — Sokha cannot run for office or leave the country. He had led the Cambodia National Rescue Party, dissolved by court order in 2017; his conviction was widely condemned abroad as politically motivated. The gesture appears aimed at improving Cambodia’s international standing amid economic pressure, while leaving the structure of one-party rule fully intact.

16,000 still displaced by GKN Aerospace toxic emergency

A 34,000-gallon storage tank at GKN Aerospace’s Garden Grove, California facility — owned by UK-based Melrose Industries PLC (£3.4 billion/$4.6 billion annual revenue) — has been venting methyl methacrylate (MMA) for five days, forcing a mandatory evacuation now reduced from 50,000 to 16,000 residents across six Orange County cities. A construction worker, “Robert,” said he “used to build those tanks back in the ’80s” and that the company had prior knowledge of leaks. FEMA declined to cover hotel stays, forcing displaced residents into vehicles or shelters. A class-action lawsuit citing negligence and ultra-hazardous activity has been filed. The WSWS draws a direct parallel to the 2023 East Palestine derailment, situating the disaster in the corporate prioritization of profit over predominantly working-class, immigrant communities.

Arts and culture

The most substantive cultural intervention in the past 24 hours comes from the WSWS’s review of Netflix’s Beef Season 2, which doubles as a lens on the broader state of socially conscious television. Critic Matthew Brennan praises the season’s performances — particularly veteran Korean actors Youn Yuh-jung and Song Kang-ho — and its central conceit: a California country club functioning as a metaphor for capitalist exclusion and class hierarchy, distilled in the line, “The people in charge have made it impossible for us.” Yet Brennan faults the season for retreating into fatalistic pessimism. Rather than depicting resistance or an alternative, the struggling working-class couple Ashley and Austin end by absorbing the corruption of the wealthy elite. The ending is “cyclical,” and Brennan argues the creators must pursue their social themes “with greater seriousness and historical awareness” if the work is to amount to more than a stylish portrait of despair.

This pessimism is itself a symptom of the cultural moment. Across contemporary television and film, genuine class antagonism is increasingly acknowledged on screen — only to be resolved in resignation, individual collapse, or the substitution of identity for class. The WSWS framework insists that art be understood in its social and historical context: Beef’s instinct to name capitalist exclusion is a real artistic strength, but its inability to imagine a way out reflects the demoralization cultivated by decades of political reaction and the absence, until recently, of a visible working-class movement.

The period also underscores the deepening assault on cultural and media workers as political actors. The Treasury Department’s OFAC subpoenas targeting streamer and commentator Hasan Piker — one of the most-watched political broadcasters of his generation — alongside roughly 40 others over a humanitarian trip to Cuba represent a direct attempt to criminalize the work of independent media and cultural figures who dissent from imperialist policy. The targeting of a major streaming personality signals that the state increasingly regards the cultural sphere itself as a battleground, where commentary, journalism, and art that oppose war and blockade are to be policed as security threats.

Roundup: - Television: Beef Season 2’s class metaphor and its retreat into fatalism exemplify the limits of liberal social commentary that names inequality without imagining its overcoming. - Media and free expression: The OFAC probe into Hasan Piker and Medea Benjamin marks an escalation in the policing of cultural and journalistic figures, raising direct censorship implications for the broadcasting and documentary sphere. - Cultural climate: The convergence of war, austerity, and authoritarianism continues to shape artistic production, rewarding works that depict suffering while discouraging those that point toward collective struggle.

The international significance of Erdoğan’s preemptive coup against the CHP — Ulaş Sevinç

Beyond the facts covered above, Sevinç advances several distinct programmatic conclusions worth noting for editorial discussion. He stresses the rapidity of the CHP’s capitulation — Özel accepted the court ruling and vacated headquarters despite initial rhetoric of “resistance” — as proof of the party’s structural inability to oppose the bourgeois state. He identifies Kılıçdaroğlu as “His Majesty’s loyal opposition,” and notes the CHP under Özel had already submitted a NATO report on Iran that “essentially reproduced US-Israeli propaganda.” Theoretically, Sevinç grounds the analysis in Trotsky’s theory of Permanent Revolution: in the imperialist epoch, “no faction of the bourgeoisie… is capable of establishing a democratic regime or establishing independence from imperialism” in late-developing nations. He links the Polyak mine and Doruk Mining strikes to the global strike wave, and advances the demand for a Socialist Federation of the Middle East and the building of an ICFI section in Turkey as the programmatic horizon. The Kurdish question, he argues, can be resolved only through the unity of the Turkish and Kurdish working classes, not nationalism.

US launches missile strikes on Iran in advance of talks in Qatar — Kevin Reed

Supplementing the Top Stories account, Reed’s analysis emphasizes the political logic the bourgeois coverage obscures: the strikes, conducted as Iranian negotiators landed in Doha, expose the contradiction in the administration’s own claim that Iran’s military has been “functionally defeated.” If true, the strikes would be unnecessary; their occurrence reveals that Washington treats military violence and diplomacy as a single coercive continuum. Reed connects Trump’s “Back to the Battlefront” rhetoric to the broader ICFI analysis that US imperialism is driving toward a regional and ultimately global war, with the Iranian working class as the immediate victim of a settlement designed to reopen the Strait of Hormuz on Washington’s terms.

Ebola outbreak in DRC and Uganda passes 1,000 cases — Benjamin Mateus

Mateus supplies the structural data absent from wire coverage: the outbreak unfolds amid 7.8 million internally displaced persons (the highest figure globally), 25.6 million people facing food crisis, 42 percent chronic stunting among under-fives, and 72.3 percent of the population surviving on under $2.15 a day. ICRC data show a 50 percent drop in pediatric medical visits and the looting of 60 percent of health facilities. Mateus argues recurrent disease emergence is not random “spillover” but the product of “imperialist and capitalist exploitation,” constituting deliberate “social murder.” This framing — and the Imperial College modeling suggesting far higher true case counts — is unique to the WSWS account.

Nepal’s RSP government shifts Iran war burden onto working people — Rohantha De Silva

De Silva documents how Nepal’s RSP government — which won 182 of 275 parliamentary seats in March 2026 after the September 2025 protests that killed 76 people — has betrayed its reform promises by passing the Iran war’s costs onto workers. Fuel prices are up over 50 percent since January; rice and cooking oil rose ~15 percent in a single month; LPG cylinders climbed from 1,910 to 2,010 rupees. Growth is projected at 2.3 percent (down from 4.6 percent), and the World Bank estimates 17,000+ additional Nepalis will fall into poverty — against 22.7 percent youth unemployment and 25 percent overall poverty, with remittances at 26 percent of GDP (77.3 percent from the Middle East). The article characterizes the RSP as a “right-wing bourgeois government” that has banned student unions and enacted forced evictions, and calls for building a genuine Trotskyist party.

The Nexteer workers’ revolt and the case for rank-and-file committees — Jerry White

White argues that Nexteer Automotive workers in Michigan — who gave an 86 percent strike authorization — should reject a third UAW-negotiated contract offering stagnant wages ($19.50 starting, $27 top rate by 2030) and instead build independent rank-and-file committees. He traces the collapse of parts-industry wages from near-parity with assembly work in 1957 (95.3 percent) to a 31-percent differential by 2000, attributing it to deliberate UAW concessions beginning with the 1979 Chrysler bailout. The political conclusion: the union bureaucracy defends “labor-management partnership,” not workers; only independent organization can restore wage parity, COLA, and company-paid benefits.

Workers Struggles: The Americas — WSWS

The roundup documents an international strike wave across the hemisphere: hundreds rallied at the Bolivian embassy in Buenos Aires in solidarity with a general strike against President Paz (with the Milei government providing military aid to suppress it, and peasant leaders giving Paz 90 days to resign); city-wide protests in Brazil for a 40-hour workweek without pay cuts; Ecuadorian health workers protesting budget cuts amid rising military spending; UPR student strikes in Puerto Rico over funding slashed from $879M (2016) to $400M (2022); Cargill’s lockout of 1,700 meatpackers at Fort Morgan, Colorado (the firm earned $3.6 billion in 2024 and paid shareholders a record $1.5 billion); Jefferson Health closing four Philadelphia pediatric facilities while its CEO earns ~$8.4 million; KEYT workers in California rallying over wages as low as $16/hour; and 3,400+ Nova Scotia long-term care workers in their seventh week of strike.

Japan and the Philippines to deepen military collaboration against China — Ben McGrath

Supplementing the International account, McGrath’s article stresses the qualitative shift represented by Japan’s lifting of its lethal-weapons export ban and the GSOMIA negotiation, situating the Marcos-Takaichi partnership within Tokyo’s drive to push its military budget to 2 percent of GDP ahead of the 2027 deadline. The unique WSWS conclusion is that the “comprehensive strategic partnership” is a building block of the US-led war coalition against China, Russia, and Iran simultaneously — a remilitarization of Japanese imperialism that the working classes of the region will be made to pay for.

Vote No to the AEU/Labor sell-out deal! — Committee for Public Education

The CFPE argues the Australian Education Union’s proposed four-year deal — 28.3–32.4 percent increases over four years (~6.7–7 percent annually) — is a real pay cut against inflation that reached 4.6 percent by March 2026 and is projected near 6 percent, with no indexing, no class-size caps, no planning-time guarantees, and a four-year no-strike clause to May 2030. It situates the deal within Labor’s austerity agenda: a $35 billion NDIS cut (the largest cut to a social program in Australian history), student wellbeing funding cut from $4 billion to $1 billion, and public-school funding growing 4.1 percent against private schools’ 19.3 percent. Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s coverage continues to refract the war on Iran and the domestic law-and-order offensive through the prism of Democratic Party electoral politics. Its commentary on the NYC mayoral contest and figures such as Zohran Mamdani treats the election of self-described socialists within the Democratic Party as the strategic road forward, while its labor reporting on the transit and auto disputes frames the answer as electing better union leaders and “pro-worker” Democrats rather than building organizations independent of the bureaucracy. Assessment: a left cover for the Democratic Party at the precise moment the party is collaborating in war abroad and repression at home.

Left Voice

Left Voice (US co-thinkers of the Argentine PTS/FT) offers sharper anti-war and pro-strike rhetoric, amplifying the Bolivian general strike, the auto and transit fights, and opposition to the strikes on Iran. Its coverage of the Buenos Aires solidarity actions and the campaign against Milei’s repression is among the more combative on the pseudo-left. Anti-Marxist line: despite invoking permanent revolution, Left Voice substitutes movementist “self-organization” and a recurring adaptation to identity politics for the concrete fight to build a unified world party under a single program — the very task the ICFI insists is decisive. Assessment: militant in tone, but its rejection of the historical struggle against Pabloite liquidationism leaves it orbiting broader “broad left” regroupments rather than the construction of a Trotskyist vanguard.

Liberation News / PSL

Liberation News frames the US strikes on Iran and the Gaza and Lebanon wars as crimes of US imperialism — correctly, as far as it goes. Anti-Marxist line: its anti-imperialism slides into campism, presenting the Iranian bourgeois regime and the bloc of “anti-US” capitalist states as progressive poles rather than analyzing them as reactionary national bourgeoisies hostile to their own working class. Its coverage of the OFAC subpoenas against Medea Benjamin (a figure close to PSL’s milieu) emphasizes free-speech defense without locating the crackdown in the bipartisan drive to war. Assessment: energetic mobilization around antiwar slogans, hollowed out by a perspective that looks to bourgeois nationalist regimes and Popular-Front-style coalitions rather than the independent mobilization of the international working class.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s output centers the transit and auto disputes and the cost-of-living squeeze, calling for strike action and “fighting unions.” Anti-Marxist line: it consistently demands that the existing union bureaucracy and “left” elected officials lead the struggle, rather than warning workers that these apparatuses are the chief obstacle — the opposite of the rank-and-file committee perspective advanced by the WSWS over the Nexteer and MTA contracts. Assessment: genuine trade-union militancy harnessed to pressure tactics on the bureaucracy and the Democratic “left.”

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

The SWP foregrounds Gaza, the Lebanon escalation, and Britain’s complicity, mobilizing for demonstrations. Anti-Marxist line: its united-front method dissolves the working class into cross-class “movements” oriented toward pressuring the Labour government and the TUC, and it tails left-talking Labour and union officials rather than fighting for political independence. Assessment: street-level activism in the service of a perspective that keeps workers tethered to the reformist apparatus.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The IMT’s British and international press continues its “Are you a communist?” recruitment drive, presenting the war and capitalist crisis as confirmation of the need to “build the revolutionary party.” Anti-Marxist line: the IMT’s abstract communist branding is detached from any reckoning with the historical struggle against Stalinism and Pabloism; its national party-building proceeds without the ICFI’s concrete analysis of the eruption of inter-imperialist and regional war, leaving its propaganda formally radical but strategically disarmed. Assessment: maximalist rhetoric paired with an organizational opportunism that substitutes growth metrics for programmatic clarity.
