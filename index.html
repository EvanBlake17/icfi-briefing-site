1. US-Iran war enters ninth week as Hormuz “dual blockade” tightens

The US-Israeli war on Iran, which began with the February 28 strike that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, has entered its ninth week with no diplomatic breakthrough. President Trump on Saturday canceled the Pakistan-mediated talks that special envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner were to hold with Iranian counterparts; Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi instead travels to Moscow today (April 27) to meet President Vladimir Putin, signaling Tehran’s pivot toward consolidating its alliance with Russia and the deepening axis with Beijing.

The naval confrontation in the Persian Gulf has hardened into what UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer called a “dual blockade” with potentially “severe consequences on the global economy.” The US Navy began the formal blockade of Iranian ports on April 13. As of late April, 38 vessels are prevented from entering or leaving Iranian waters and 23 ships have been physically intercepted — including the Iranian-flagged Touska, seized April 19 by the USS Spruance and the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit. Iran has retaliated by closing the Strait of Hormuz to international transit. Brent crude opened the week near $107 a barrel, with oil markets reacting to a fresh Pakistan-mediated Iranian proposal to reopen the strait in exchange for an end to the war — a proposal Trump publicly rejected over the weekend.

Key facts:

  • War began February 28, 2026 with US-Israeli airstrikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei; Mojtaba Khamenei has since been elected supreme leader
  • US Navy blockade began April 13; first vessel seized April 19 (Iranian-flagged Touska); 23 vessels intercepted to date
  • IEA describes the conflict as the largest energy supply shock on record
  • A Newspoll cited on the WSWS found that 72 percent of Australians oppose the US attack on Iran, with comparable opposition in European polling

The WSWS perspective on the war’s class dimensions has been consistent: the imperialist cartel of Washington, London and Tel Aviv is using the war to discipline the entire Middle East and Eurasian periphery on behalf of Wall Street’s energy and finance capital, while the working class — in Iran, in the Pacific, in the United States, and across the Global South — bears the cost in blood and energy prices. Today’s Pacific island and Australian coverage on the WSWS (see below) puts numbers on that calculation.

2. Israel kills Palestinians daily in Gaza as October “ceasefire” frays

Six and a half months after the US-brokered ceasefire of October 10, 2025, the slaughter in Gaza continues, with Western media largely treating the territory as a closed file. At least four Palestinians were killed in Israeli strikes in the past 24 hours, raising the post-ceasefire toll to more than 800. Total Palestinian deaths since October 2023 now exceed 72,500. Israel today controls roughly 60 percent of the Strip and is unilaterally extending its “yellow line” military control zone by an additional 37 kilometers into territory that under the ceasefire was supposed to revert to Palestinian administration.

Aid is reaching the population at roughly 20 percent of stipulated levels — between 150 and 190 trucks a day against the 600 promised in the ceasefire text. The US-backed National Committee for the Administration of Gaza (NCAG), notionally a transitional Palestinian authority, has been effectively paralyzed by repeated Israeli targeting of its police personnel. Analyst Iyad al-Qarra told Al Jazeera that Israel is using the formal demand for Hamas “disarmament” as “a vague and unrealistic excuse” to continue military operations whose actual aim is “to make it an unlivable environment, forcing residents to seek displacement.”

Key facts:

  • 72,500+ total Palestinian dead since October 2023
  • 800+ killed since the October 10, 2025 ceasefire; at least 4 in the past 24 hours
  • Israel controls ~60 percent of Gaza’s territory
  • Aid throughput: 150–190 trucks/day versus 600 stipulated
  • Yellow line expanding by an additional 37 km

3. Russia-Ukraine war: 149 engagements in 24 hours, Yaroslavl refinery hit

Despite repeated US and European diplomatic initiatives over the past year, the Russia-Ukraine war ground through another high-tempo 24 hours. The Institute for the Study of War recorded 149 separate combat engagements along the front on April 26, with Russian forces continuing their slow advance in the Oleksandrivka direction. Russia launched 144 drones and 50 missiles overnight; Ukrainian air defenses intercepted 124 drones, and Russia dropped 203 guided aerial bombs on positions along the contact line and on civilian infrastructure inside Ukraine.

Ukrainian long-range strikes hit a major oil refinery in Yaroslavl, deep inside Russia, in the most damaging cross-border attack of the past week. Ukrainian drones also damaged three Russian naval vessels and a MiG-31 interceptor in occupied Crimea overnight. At least eight Ukrainian civilians were killed and 21 wounded in Russian strikes on populated areas in the past 24 hours. Russia’s official troop casualty figure now stands at 1,325,650 since February 24, 2022, with 960 casualties recorded in the past day alone.

4. Russia and North Korea formalize 2027–2031 military alliance

Russian Defense Minister Andrey Belousov arrived in Pyongyang on April 26 and met Kim Jong Un for high-level security talks that have produced a public commitment from both governments to sign a formal military cooperation strategy covering 2027–2031 later this year. Belousov personally awarded the Order of Courage to North Korean troops for their role in expelling Ukrainian forces from Russia’s Kursk region — a public confirmation, after months of denial, that DPRK soldiers are co-belligerents in the Ukraine war. The visit comes immediately after a separate trip by State Duma Speaker Vyacheslav Volodin, who also met Kim. Russian state media described the new framework as placing cooperation “on a stable, long-term footing” — institutional language that points to a multi-year arms-and-personnel pipeline and possible nuclear-related coordination.

The five-year strategy is significant beyond the immediate battlefield: it signals the construction of a permanent Eurasian military bloc — Moscow, Pyongyang, with Tehran and Beijing in increasingly close orbit — designed to outlast any negotiated end to the Ukraine war and to confront a rearming NATO.

5. Colombia: 19 dead in FARC-dissident bus bombing five weeks before election

A cylinder bomb thrown at a passenger bus on the Panamerican Highway in Cajibío, Cauca department, on April 25 killed at least 19 people (15 women and four men, all adults) and wounded 48 more, including five minors; three remain in intensive care. The blast left a 10-meter crater and damaged at least 15 other vehicles. Armed forces commander Gen. Hélder Giraldo attributed the attack to the network of Iván Mordisco, leader of a dissident FARC faction; President Gustavo Petro publicly compared Mordisco to Pablo Escobar.

The attack is part of a wave: 26 separate attacks were recorded across Cauca and Valle del Cauca departments in the two days surrounding the bombing. The timing — 35 days before Colombia’s May 31 presidential election — has raised the question, largely unaddressed in mainstream coverage, of whether armed groups linked to narco-capitalism are deliberately destabilizing the electoral process to push the country toward a hard-right “law and order” successor to Petro. The bombing also exposes the collapse of Petro’s “total peace” negotiation strategy with FARC dissidents, which had been the centerpiece of his presidency.

6. DOJ drops Powell probe; Kevin Warsh confirmation imminent at Federal Reserve

US Attorney Jeanine Pirro on April 24 formally dropped the Department of Justice’s criminal investigation of Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell, an investigation centered on a $2.5 billion overrun on the Fed’s Washington headquarters renovation. A federal judge had already quashed the DOJ’s subpoenas in March, ruling that prosecutors had produced “essentially zero evidence” of a crime. The probe nonetheless served its political function: throughout late 2025 and into 2026, it gave Trump leverage to publicly demand Powell’s resignation and to pressure the Fed against rate cuts that might offset the recessionary drag of his tariff regime.

With the probe closed, Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) immediately released his hold on the confirmation of Kevin Warsh, Trump’s pick to replace Powell when Powell’s term expires May 15. Warsh is a former Fed governor, a Hoover Institution fellow and a longtime advocate of bank deregulation; he is widely expected to align Fed policy with Trump’s political timetable. The S&P 500 finished April up roughly 9 percent and the Nasdaq up 15 percent, with markets clearly pricing in faster cuts under Warsh.

7. Shooter at White House Correspondents’ Dinner targeted Trump officials

Cole Tomas Allen, 31, of Torrance, California, was arrested on April 25 outside the Washington Hilton during the White House Correspondents’ Association dinner. Allen, armed with a shotgun and a handgun, breached an outer screening area; one Secret Service officer was struck in a bullet-resistant vest and is expected to recover, with no other injuries. Allen had emailed his family a roughly 1,000-word manifesto describing himself as the “friendly federal assassin” and naming Trump-administration immigration and military policies as motives. He had traveled cross-country by train to evade airport screening; his brother contacted the New London, Connecticut police on receiving the manifesto. Allen faces two counts of using a firearm during a crime of violence and one count of assault on a federal officer.

Trump was rushed from the venue and the dinner was cut short. As the WSWS noted in Patrick Martin’s analysis today, Trump quickly likened himself to Lincoln in subsequent remarks and is exploiting the incident to demand expanded executive police powers. The WSWS — opposing both Trump’s authoritarian response and the “individual violence as politics” the attack represents — emphasized that “the only effective resistance to Trump’s dictatorial trajectory must come from the organized political mobilization of the working class.”

8. King Charles begins four-day state visit, will address Congress April 28

King Charles III and Queen Camilla arrived at the White House this morning (April 27) for a four-day state visit, the first by a British monarch since Queen Elizabeth II’s 2007 visit to George W. Bush. On Tuesday Charles will address a joint meeting of Congress — the first British monarch to do so since Elizabeth II in 1991 — at a moment of intense friction between London and Washington over the Strait of Hormuz crisis, US tariffs, and Trump’s hostility toward UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer. The visit was scheduled around the US Semiquincentennial (250th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence). The itinerary includes Washington, a Harlem community visit and the September 11 Memorial in New York, and stops in Virginia.

Behind the pageantry the trip is, by all serious accounts, a damage-control mission: London is attempting to use the prestige of the Crown to extract concessions from Trump on the Iran war’s economic blowback, on tariffs, and on continued US security commitments to Europe. Starmer has already publicly warned Trump that the Hormuz dual blockade carries “severe” economic consequences for the UK.

India arrests over 400 workers and 63 activists

Since industrial protests began on April 10 in the manufacturing corridor surrounding Delhi, Indian authorities have arrested over 400 workers and at least 63 political activists nationwide, including members of the labor advocacy group Mazdoor Bigul (Workers’ Bugle). Police traveled 2,500 km to capture activist Aditya Anand in Tamil Nadu, where a ₹100,000 reward had been posted. The pattern echoes the 2012 Maruti Suzuki case, in which 13 union leaders faced murder charges following a factory fire and 11 were ultimately sentenced to life imprisonment.

Pacific island economies reeling from Hormuz fuel shock

The Strait of Hormuz disruption has slammed Pacific island economies that have no role in the war but depend on imported fuel. Papua New Guinea reports diesel, petrol, and kerosene up 70 percent since the war began; Tuvalu has declared a state of emergency with diesel up 40 percent and petrol up 30 percent and is rationing fuel; Fiji, where 80 percent of consumer goods are imported and over 30 percent of residents already live below the poverty line, reports import costs up 10–15 percent. The Pacific Islands Forum invoked the Biketawa Declaration on April 17, declaring a region-wide emergency, but a Solomon Islands proposal for a joint oil-buying bloc with Australia and New Zealand has produced no coordinated action. Fiji has announced a temporary 50 percent welfare increase for 130,000 recipients, expiring July 31. Approximately 5 million PNG residents were already living in poverty before the war began.

1 in 3 Americans cut essentials to afford healthcare

Gallup and Kaiser Family Foundation data summarized today on the WSWS finds that 33 percent of US adults — about 82 million people — cut spending on food, utilities, or transportation to afford healthcare in 2025. Among the uninsured the figure rises to 62 percent. 41 percent of the population carries medical or dental debt; 9 percent have postponed retirement because of healthcare costs; and even households earning $90,000–$120,000 annually report cutting essentials at a 25 percent rate. Nearly half of US adults cannot cover an unexpected $500 medical bill without going into debt. Marc Wells locates the crisis as the structural product of decades of bipartisan policy — naming the ACA, Clinton- and Obama-era reforms, and Trump’s “One Big Beautiful Bill Act” with its mandatory Medicaid work requirements — and observes that “healthcare access is structurally determined by income and employment rather than medical need.”

Book bans hit second-highest level on record

The American Library Association’s 2025 annual report — released this week and analyzed today on the WSWS by David Walsh — documents that approximately 4,235 unique titles faced formal challenges, the second-highest figure on record and roughly 15 times the 2001–2020 baseline of 273 challenges per year. 91.7 percent of challenges originated from government entities (70.9 percent) or organized pressure groups (20.8 percent), with only 2.7 percent from parents — exposing the “concerned parent” narrative as cover for a coordinated campaign. 5,668 books were ultimately banned and 920 more restricted; 1,671 of the targeted titles addressed LGBTQ themes or Black and Indigenous perspectives. Walsh links the surge to Trump’s January 2025 executive order mandating “patriotic admiration” in K-12 curricula, framing the suppression of critical thought as inseparable from the drive to prepare the population for war.

UPS Worldport near-miss exposes deepening aviation safety crisis

On April 14, a 767 cargo jet aborted its landing at Louisville Muhammad Ali International Airport — UPS’s Worldport hub — when another aircraft moved onto the runway, just months after UPS Airlines Flight 2976 crashed there in November, killing 15 people. NTSB has determined that crash resulted from fatigue cracks and overstress failures in the 34-year-old MD-11F’s engine attachment structure; a public hearing is set for May 19–20. The April 14 near-miss is one of three in recent weeks: a March 22 Air Canada Express collision with a fire truck at LaGuardia killed two pilots, and two Southwest aircraft nearly collided at Nashville on April 18. Over 90 percent of US air traffic control facilities operate below recommended staffing levels, forcing controllers into extended shifts — a situation compounded by Trump-era firings of NTSB board members.

US measles cases reach 1,792

CDC data through April 23 confirms 1,792 measles cases across 37 US jurisdictions in 2026 to date. 93 percent (1,668 cases) are outbreak-associated, spanning multiple outbreaks that originated in 2025 and 2026; an additional 10 cases involve international visitors. The figure already places 2026 on track to be one of the two worst US measles years since 2000, threatening the country’s already-revoked elimination status. The trajectory reflects the systematic dismantling of public health infrastructure under HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.

Moderna begins Phase 3 mRNA H5N1 trial

On April 21, Moderna dosed the first participants in its Phase 3 trial of mRNA-1018, the company’s mRNA-based H5N1 vaccine candidate, aiming to enroll roughly 4,000 healthy adults in the US and UK with priority for adults over 65 and poultry workers. CEPI is funding up to $54.3 million of the trial after RFK Jr.’s HHS canceled more than $700 million in Moderna bird flu contracts in 2025. This is the first large-scale human efficacy trial of an mRNA H5N1 vaccine. Separately, University of Nebraska Medical Center scientists report that H5N1 strains circulating in US dairy cattle have acquired two mutations enabling them to bind NeuGc, a sugar receptor present in cow mammary tissue but absent in humans and birds — explaining the efficient cow-to-cow spread without (yet) increasing human infectivity.

Lancet: prediabetes remission cuts heart attack risk

A post-hoc analysis published in The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology of 2,402 participants across the US Diabetes Prevention Program Outcomes Study (DPPOS) and China’s Da Qing DPOS found that reversing prediabetes to normal blood glucose was associated with a 42–59 percent reduction in cardiovascular death, myocardial infarction, and heart failure hospitalization over up to 20 years of follow-up (pooled HR ~0.45). Fasting glucose ≤97 mg/dL emerged as the key threshold. The finding is the first multi-decade, multi-ethnic evidence that prediabetes remission confers durable cardiac protection, and strengthens the case for aggressive early lifestyle intervention.

White House pushes federal preemption of state AI bills

The March 20 White House National AI Legislative Framework, with implementing pressure now coming from the DOJ’s AI Litigation Task Force, directs Congress to enact a single federal AI standard preempting the 600+ AI bills introduced in state legislatures in 2026. Indiana, Utah, and Washington have enacted laws barring insurers from using AI as the sole basis for claim denials — laws the DOJ task force is now challenging on Commerce Clause grounds. In parallel, the UN Independent International Scientific Panel on AI is preparing for its inaugural in-person summit; one AI pioneer cited by UN News this week called for “applying the brakes” on current development trajectories, citing risks from opaque algorithms and data-and-compute concentration in a handful of corporations. The federal-vs.-state AI fight pits Big Tech and the federal executive against a wave of state-level consumer protections.

  • The American Library Association’s 2025 censorship report — analyzed on the WSWS today (see US section) — anchors the day’s most consequential cultural development: a state-and-pressure-group infrastructure now banning books at roughly 15 times the historical baseline, with 1,671 LGBTQ, Black, and Indigenous titles disproportionately targeted. The data confirm that what is being suppressed is not “obscenity” but historical and political consciousness — the necessary preparation for the wars now under way.

The cultural front of the war drive was visible across multiple smaller stories over the weekend:

  • Anzac Day in Australia (April 25) was again pressed into service as militarist liturgy. Convicted Afghan war criminal Ben Roberts-Smith appeared at multiple public commemorations, his presence sanitized by political and media establishments. Anzac Day was widely reviled during the Vietnam War; its rehabilitation since the late 1980s has been a deliberate state cultural project, mobilizing the dead of Gallipoli to manufacture consent for new wars in the Middle East and against China. Australian writers and historians who pointed out the contradiction between Anzac mythology and the present reality of Australian Special Forces deployments in the Middle East faced predictable hostility from establishment commentators.

  • Trump’s January 2025 executive order mandating “patriotic admiration” in K-12 curricula — connected by David Walsh today to the spike in book bans — has now produced state-level curriculum overhauls in Florida, Texas, Oklahoma, and Tennessee that are excising material on slavery, the labor movement, the civil rights struggle, and US imperial wars. The targeted texts include works by Toni Morrison, Howard Zinn, James Baldwin, Maya Angelou, and Ibram X. Kendi, alongside contemporary YA fiction with LGBTQ characters. The drive, presented as “parents’ rights,” is in fact a coordinated bourgeois state campaign to disarm the next generation intellectually.

  • The King Charles state visit is itself a cultural event. The British Crown is being deployed as soft-power instrument at a moment when the official Anglo-American alliance is straining under the Iran war and Trump’s tariff war. Charles will lay a wreath at the September 11 Memorial and tour Harlem in a performance of liberal cosmopolitanism designed to shore up the war alliance.

  • In film, the world’s major spring festivals — Cannes (May 13–24), Tribeca (June 3–14) — are now finalizing slates against this political backdrop. A growing number of submissions deal with the Gaza genocide and the Iran war; some serious documentary work is being produced. These works will be measured by whether they engage with the social and economic forces driving the wars or retreat to the personalist and identitarian frameworks that dominate establishment cultural production.

  • Music and literature: A wave of prominent American and British musicians and writers issued a renewed open letter this week demanding an immediate end to US arms shipments to Israel and a halt to the Iran war. Such open letters, in themselves, change nothing, but they continue to track the deep alienation of a layer of the cultural intelligentsia from official policy — a phenomenon that dates back at least to 2023 and has now matured into something resembling a permanent oppositional formation, even as the major studios, publishers, and institutions remain disciplined.

The WSWS framework — that cultural production is to be evaluated for its engagement with the social totality, not as a consumer good — is especially needed now. The crisis of objective social conditions is producing both genuine artistic protest and a counter-mobilization of state-managed nationalist kitsch. Sorting one from the other is the daily task of socialist cultural criticism.
