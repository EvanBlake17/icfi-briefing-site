1. Iran submits 14-point peace plan as Trump rejects offer on Day 65 of war

On Day 65 of the US-Israeli war on Iran, Tehran has delivered a 14-point peace proposal through Pakistani mediators, which Trump publicly rejected. The plan demands a 30-day resolution timeline (against the US-proposed two-month ceasefire), an end to the US naval blockade, withdrawal of US forces from Iran’s periphery, release of frozen assets, payment of reparations, lifting of all sanctions, an end to the Lebanon campaign, and a new Strait of Hormuz governance mechanism. Trump told reporters: “I’m not satisfied” — the Iranians “have not yet paid a big enough price.” He simultaneously informed congressional leaders that hostilities had been “terminated as of April 7,” even as Iranian and Israeli forces continue daily exchanges and the US Navy enforces a blockade that has turned away 48 ships in the past 20 days.

The IRGC has set a formal deadline for the United States to lift the blockade, warning that “Trump must choose between an impossible military operation or a bad deal.” A nominal ceasefire has been in place since April 16–17, but both sides accuse the other of daily violations. Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer denounced Trump’s claim that the war is over: “That’s bulls***. This is an illegal war.”

Key facts: - Iran demands include the release of frozen assets, reparations, and lifting of all sanctions - US Navy has forced 48 ships to turn around at the blockade in the past 20 days - Trump claimed hostilities ended April 7; Iranian and US forces continue active operations

3. Strait of Hormuz traffic collapses 90% as oil hits $108

Shipping traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has collapsed 90% since the war began on February 28. Real-time vessel tracking shows as few as 3 ships per day passing the choke point, against a pre-war norm of 120–140. IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol called the disruption “the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.” Brent crude is trading near $107–$108/barrel, up from approximately $70 before the war; the average US gasoline price has reached $4.39/gallon. The strait normally carries roughly 20% of the world’s oil trade, plus nine other commodities (LNG, petrochemicals, steel, fertilizers) all severely disrupted, according to the World Economic Forum.

Japan’s Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi confirmed the first successful passage of a Japanese oil tanker since the war began. The US Treasury issued a public warning to companies against paying Iran “tolls” for guaranteed safe passage. As Nick Beams notes on the WSWS, citing a new World Bank report, “the war is hitting the global economy in cumulative waves: first through higher energy prices, then higher food prices and finally higher inflation.”

Key facts: - 90% collapse in shipping traffic; as few as 3 vessels/day vs. pre-war 120–140 - Brent crude ~$108/bbl, up from ~$70 pre-war; US gasoline at $4.39/gallon - World Bank energy price index projected to rise 24% in 2026 - Trafigura estimates 1.5 billion barrels of cumulative Gulf oil lost (5% of annual global output)

5. Spirit Airlines collapse wipes out 17,000 jobs after $500 million bailout denied

Spirit Airlines ceased all operations at 3:00 AM Eastern Time on May 2, 2026 — the first major US carrier to shut down in 25 years. 17,000 workers lost their jobs, including 14,000 Spirit employees and thousands of contractors. The airline failed to secure a $500 million federal bailout; the Trump administration declined to intervene. Spirit had served as the dominant carrier for low-income travelers on routes legacy carriers had abandoned.

Contributing causes include the 2023 JetBlue acquisition blocked by the Biden DOJ on competition grounds, an Airbus engine defect that grounded much of the fleet, competition from legacy carriers offering bare-bones fares, and the Iran war-driven spike in jet fuel prices as the final blow. Airlines are offering capped “rescue fares” of approximately $200 to stranded passengers; the company instructed travelers not to go to airports. The political dimension is stark: the Trump administration declined a $500 million rescue while simultaneously committing many billions to the war on Iran.

Key facts: - Operations halted 3:00 AM ET, May 2, 2026 - 17,000 jobs eliminated; no advance notice or severance for many contractors - $500 million bailout denied; Biden DOJ blocked the JetBlue acquisition that was Spirit’s exit path - Iran war-driven jet fuel spike named as the proximate trigger

H5N1 cattle strain replicates in human bronchus and lung tissue

Paired studies in the May 2026 issue of Emerging Infectious Diseases (CDC) document significant pandemic-risk signals from the H5N1 lineage circulating in US dairy cattle. NIH/NIAID researchers tested contemporary clade 2.3.4.4b isolates in a human nasal-epithelium model and found they replicate more efficiently than historical strains; the B3.13 genotype circulating in dairy cattle triggered a dampened interferon immune response — a marker for easier early-infection establishment. A companion paper in the same issue demonstrates B3.13 tropism and replication competence in human bronchial and lung tissue, not just nasal epithelium. Together, the two studies indicate the cattle lineage has meaningful capacity to infect the full human respiratory tract.

Capitalist crisis, war and the international class struggle — David North

David North’s May Day Rally speech functions as the ICFI’s programmatic assessment of the global conjuncture. Its central claim is that the 2026 war against Iran is the culmination of a 35-year trajectory of US imperialist aggression that began with the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991. North explicitly rejects the dominant narrative — including within sections of the left — that Israeli lobbying or “Zionist influence” caused the war. Such explanations, he argues, “constitute an apology for American imperialism,” abandon imperialism as an analytical category, and risk sliding into antisemitic conspiracy theory.

Surveying US military interventions from the Philippines (early 1900s) through Vietnam, the Gulf War, Afghanistan, Iraq, Libya, Syria and Gaza, he argues that none reversed the erosion of American economic dominance. The data he cites: national debt grew from approximately $5.8 trillion in 2001 to nearly $40 trillion in 2026; gold has risen from $35/oz at the Bretton Woods peg to approximately $4,600/oz — a 99% decline in dollar value relative to gold. Militarism abroad requires authoritarian austerity at home; the $1.535 trillion Pentagon budget is financed by destruction of Medicare, Medicaid, and Social Security: “The only law that operates is the law of force, wielded by a capitalist oligarchy that recognizes no constraint, no border, no constitution.”

Against this, North advances an account of an expanding global proletariat: more than 2 billion workers added since 1980; over 500 cities now exceed 1 million inhabitants; an estimated 1 billion African workers entering global labor markets in coming decades. He documents the present escalation: 458 strikes across eight European countries in Q1 2026, national general strikes in Belgium, Italy, and Argentina, 42,000 University of California workers on strike, 31,000 Kaiser healthcare workers on strike, and the 8-million-strong “No Kings” mobilization on March 28. The ICFI’s task is to build the Fourth International as the World Party of Socialist Revolution: “The road forward is the conscious and organized struggle of the international working class for power” — or “descent into barbarism.”
