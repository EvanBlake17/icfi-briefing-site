1. Iran-US war ceasefire on “life support” as nuclear talks collapse

The latest round of nuclear talks between the Trump administration and Iran collapsed Saturday after Tehran rejected a one-page US proposal demanding sharp enrichment restrictions and Iranian security guarantees for the Strait of Hormuz. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi denounced the document as “excessive and outrageous” and accused Trump of “lying about seeking peace.” Trump responded with renewed threats of bombing, warning Iran would have “a very bad time” if it failed to comply.

The war — launched February 28, 2026 by joint US-Israeli airstrikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and a dense layer of senior Iranian officials — has been frozen since March in an unstable, frequently violated halt. Iran held parallel talks with European powers in Istanbul on May 16, while Pakistan’s interior minister flew to Tehran in a would-be mediator role. Tehran warned that any reimposition of UN snapback sanctions could carry “irreversible” consequences.

The spillover into Lebanon continues to deepen. Combat between Israel and Hezbollah has now killed more than 2,000 combatants and civilians in what is being called the 2026 Lebanon war. As Andre Damon argued in yesterday’s WSWS Perspective, the Iran war was launched in part “to arrest a slide in US global dominance — yet it has already exposed severe limits,” with Washington now seeking Beijing’s assistance to manage fallout from a campaign that has not achieved its strategic objectives.

Key facts: - Trump’s proposal demanded enrichment caps below pre-JCPOA levels and US/allied freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz - Araghchi accused Washington of negotiating “with a gun to the table” while threatening renewed military action - Iranian officials reportedly told European interlocutors that any sanctions snapback would terminate diplomatic engagement entirely - The 2026 Lebanon war death toll has surpassed 2,000

2. Strait of Hormuz blockade enters fourth month, 1,550+ vessels stranded

Iran’s effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz — which carries approximately 20 percent of global daily oil and 20 percent of global LNG — has entered its fourth month with no end in sight. UNCTAD reports that more than 1,550 commercial vessels remain stranded in the region with an estimated 22,500 mariners trapped onboard as of early May. Oil flows through the Strait have collapsed to roughly 30 percent of pre-war volumes, and the IMF’s April World Economic Outlook now projects global growth slowing to 2.7 percent in 2026.

Trump’s “Operation Project Freedom,” launched May 4 to escort merchant shipping out of the chokepoint, was paused after only two days citing “great progress” on negotiations — progress that has now collapsed entirely with Saturday’s diplomatic breakdown (see story above). European governments are quietly initiating contingency planning for fuel rationing; Lufthansa has cut flights; and prices for oil, diesel, jet fuel, fertilizer, aluminum, and helium have surged sharply across world markets.

Key facts: - Brent crude closed Friday at ~$109.47/bbl, up 8.1 percent on the week - WTI traded in a $101.48–$106.00 range on Friday, finishing near $105.42 - IMF projects 2026 global growth at 2.7 percent, down from earlier 3.1 percent forecasts - Fertilizer and aluminum spot prices have hit multi-year highs on supply disruption

3. Israel kills Hamas Qassam Brigades chief al-Haddad in Gaza strike

Israeli airstrikes on the Remal neighborhood of Gaza City on the evening of May 15 killed Izz al-Din al-Haddad, commander of Hamas’s Qassam Brigades, in what Hamas has condemned as a flagrant breach of the October 2025 ceasefire. Al-Haddad, born 1970 and known by the nom de guerre “the Ghost,” had survived multiple prior assassination attempts and took command of the armed wing after Israel killed Mohammad Sinwar in May 2025. His wife, daughter, and at least four other Palestinian civilians — among them three women and a baby — were killed in the same strike, with 57 others wounded.

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, confirming the killing on May 16, called al-Haddad “one of the architects” of the October 7, 2023 attacks. The strike comes against a backdrop of continuous Israeli violations of the ceasefire: according to the Gaza Health Ministry, 870 Palestinians have been killed and 2,543 injured by Israeli attacks since the truce supposedly came into force in October 2025.

Key facts: - Strike hit a residential building in Remal at approximately 9:40 p.m. local time - Hamas described the attack as an “assassination” and breach of the ceasefire framework - Gaza ceasefire death toll: 870 killed, 2,543 wounded since October 2025 - Al-Haddad becomes the third successive Qassam commander killed by Israel in three years

4. Russia hits Ukraine with 300 bombs, 9,645 drones in 24 hours

Ukraine’s General Staff reported 234 combat engagements over the past 24 hours of fighting on day 1,544 of Russia’s invasion, with Russian forces launching 95 airstrikes, 300 guided aerial bombs, and a staggering 9,645 kamikaze drones across the line of contact. The heaviest fighting was concentrated in the Pokrovsk direction, where Ukrainian forces repelled 32 Russian assaults, and in the Huliaipole direction, where 24 attacks were recorded.

Ukraine conducted its own large-scale drone offensive against the Moscow region overnight May 16–17. Russia claimed to have shot down 279 of 287 incoming drones, with eight striking seven locations, though damage assessments remain incomplete. Ukraine’s General Staff put Russian losses for the 24-hour period at approximately 1,170 personnel, one tank, 82 artillery systems, and 2,131 UAVs destroyed. Cumulative Russian combat personnel losses since February 24, 2022 now stand at approximately 1,348,790 by Ukraine’s count.

Key facts: - 9,645 kamikaze drones in a single 24-hour window represents an order-of-magnitude escalation over winter 2025 - Pokrovsk remains the focal point of Russian assault operations after 11 consecutive months of attrition fighting - Cumulative Russian KIA/WIA figure of 1.35 million is published by Kyiv’s General Staff - Western military aid to Ukraine has been disrupted by the diversion of US logistics toward the Iran theater

5. State Department fires 250 diplomats mid-war

The US State Department on May 16 finalized the firing of nearly 250 foreign service officers via a brief, impersonal email — concluding reductions-in-force begun in July 2025 that have also eliminated more than 1,000 civil service positions. Entire offices that former officials say would otherwise have managed the US response to the Iran war have been wiped out. The American Foreign Service Association now estimates roughly 2,000 foreign service officers have left the department in the past year alone, and over 100 ambassador posts worldwide — including in the Middle East, Ukraine, and Russia — currently have no Senate-confirmed ambassador.

According to current and former officials cited by CNN and NPR, the Iran war negotiations and Ukraine diplomacy are now being run by Trump family members and business associates “often without teams of experienced diplomats with regional expertise.” The simultaneous evisceration of US diplomatic capacity during the most consequential military conflict since Iraq has alarmed even establishment voices, with one former under-secretary describing it as “unilateral disarmament of the State Department in the middle of a war.”

Key facts: - ~2,000 foreign service officers have departed State in 12 months — roughly 25 percent of the career corps - 100+ ambassador vacancies, including key Middle Eastern and Eastern European posts - Iran negotiations led by Trump family/business associates with no regional or language expertise - Civil service cuts have hit the Bureau of Near Eastern Affairs and the Bureau of European and Eurasian Affairs particularly hard

6. Indian army chief threatens to make Pakistan “history”

Indian Army Chief Gen. Upendra Dwivedi told a public event in Delhi on May 16 that Pakistan must “decide whether they want to be part of geography or history” — a thinly veiled threat of national elimination from a senior commander of a nuclear-armed state. The remark came during a session at which Dwivedi was asked how India would respond to a future repeat of the conditions that triggered Operation Sindoor, the May 2025 four-day aerial war between the two nuclear powers.

Pakistan’s military Inter-Services Public Relations directorate (ISPR) condemned the statement on May 17 as “provocative and escalatory,” reminding Delhi that “Pakistan is a declared nuclear power firmly rooted in South Asia’s geography.” Both nations remain on elevated military readiness one year after their last direct confrontation, and the Line of Control has seen sustained surveillance escalation. Pakistan over the past year has publicly inducted supersonic cruise missiles, loitering munitions, reconnaissance satellites, and a new Army Rocket Force Command — a doctrinal restructuring with clear nuclear implications.

Key facts: - Dwivedi’s remarks delivered at a public Delhi event, captured on video, and circulated widely on Indian state-aligned media - ISPR statement issued May 17 explicitly invoked Pakistan’s nuclear status - Operation Sindoor (May 2025) was the largest India-Pakistan aerial engagement in over five decades - South Asia hosts an estimated 340+ nuclear warheads between the two states

7. Sudan famine confirmed: 20 million face acute hunger

The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) on May 14 confirmed famine conditions in Al Fasher (North Darfur) and Kadugli (South Kordofan), with 20 additional areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan at imminent famine risk. Nearly 20 million Sudanese — roughly 38 percent of the country’s population — face acute food insecurity. More than 5 million are in emergency-level hunger, and approximately 135,000 people are now in catastrophic conditions. Over 800,000 children face severe acute malnutrition in 2026.

The crisis is now in its fourth year since fighting between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) erupted in April 2023. About 40 percent of health facilities remain non-functional; 17 million Sudanese lack safe water; 24 million lack adequate sanitation. The war has internally displaced nearly 9 million people, and 65 percent of Sudan’s 34 million people require urgent humanitarian assistance. UN and WFP officials have been blunt that funding shortfalls — exacerbated by Trump’s USAID dismantlement — have collapsed the international response.

Key facts: - 38 percent of Sudanese population in acute food insecurity (IPC-3 or worse) - 800,000+ children with severe acute malnutrition projected for 2026 - 9 million internally displaced, 65 percent of population needs humanitarian aid - Western governments have imposed no meaningful consequences on the UAE — the principal external sponsor of the RSF

8. Samsung chip union sets 18-day strike window

The National Samsung Electronics Union, representing approximately 30,000 chip workers, has set a formal strike window from May 21 through June 7, 2026 — an 18-day action that could rupture global semiconductor supply chains already under strain from AI demand and the Strait of Hormuz disruptions. Workers have rejected Samsung’s offered profit-sharing equal to ~13 percent of operating profit; the union is demanding 15 percent plus a 7 percent base wage increase. Samsung is the world’s largest memory chip maker, and China’s semiconductor imports hit a record $135 billion in the most recent quarter as Beijing accelerates its own AI buildout.

The strike comes amid a sharp Korean labor upsurge connected to the broader May Day 2026 mobilizations against the Iran war and surging living costs. The KOSPI’s 6 percent collapse on Friday reflected the convergence of Samsung labor action, US trade-war fallout, and oil-price shock. Korean union leaders have been publicly cautious, however, about authorizing the kind of cross-industry solidarity action that would be needed to actually defeat Samsung’s tier-system and contracting-out regime.

Key facts: - Strike window: May 21 – June 7, 2026 - Demands: 15% profit share + 7% base wage hike - Samsung’s 2025 operating profit on memory: $28 billion+ - KOSPI dropped 6%+ on Friday on convergence of trade and labor pressures

CIA director’s Havana visit signals Cuba regime-change preparation

CIA Director John Ratcliffe traveled to Havana in early May, meeting Cuban Interior Minister Lázaro Álvarez Casas and the island’s intelligence leadership — the highest-level publicly acknowledged US intelligence visit in over six decades. The trip, leaked through US media outlets, comes as Cuba endures 20–22 hour daily blackouts caused by the US-imposed fuel blockade, and as Trump administration officials have explicitly signaled that “Cuba is next” after Iran.

Mainstream outlets framed the meeting as diplomatic outreach. Analysts and Cuban officials, however, point to the historical pattern: similar publicized intelligence contacts preceded US-backed regime-change operations against Venezuela and, more recently, Iran. The deliberate broadcasting of the visit, in this reading, functions as preparatory legitimation — establishing a narrative that Havana is the obstructionist party to any future US intervention.

Sri Lanka extends draconian state of emergency

The JVP/NPP government of President Anura Kumara Dissanayake extended Sri Lanka’s state of emergency for the sixth consecutive month, granting the executive dictatorial powers to override laws, restrict assembly, and detain individuals at will. Essential Public Services regulations prohibit strikes in key sectors and impose jail terms on participating workers. Since the Iran war began in February, fuel prices have risen 40 percent, gas 31 percent, and electricity tariffs 32 percent; the cost-of-living index has jumped from 2.2 percent to 5.4 percent between March and April 2026.

The government claims the emergency is required for Cyclone Ditwah recovery operations. The political function — preempting a mass uprising on the scale of the 2022 revolt that toppled the Rajapaksa government — is increasingly transparent. Separate WSWS reporting also revealed that Dissanayake’s nominally “neutral” government secretly coordinated with US and Israeli diplomats over Iranian naval vessels, with the president himself admitting to coordinating with “relevant embassies.”

May Day strike wave links living costs to Iran war

TorchStone Global tracked 63 planned protest actions internationally around May 1, 2026, while the “May Day Strong” coalition called for coordinated work stoppages, school boycotts, and consumer boycotts in the United States. The World Federation of Trade Unions declared May Day 2026 a “milestone of struggle” against the rising energy and food prices tied to the Middle East war. Multi-sector work stoppages were reported in public transport, manufacturing, and service industries across Europe, Latin America, and Asia.

In the US, the “General Strike” movement (generalstrikeus.com) has gained significant traction in the context of anti-war and anti-austerity sentiment, though the AFL-CIO leadership has remained pointedly hostile to any move toward genuine cross-industry stoppages. The objective conditions for an international workers’ counteroffensive are sharper than at any point in three decades — but the trade union bureaucracies in every advanced country are working to channel the unrest into safer terrain.

Brazilian student strikes shake São Paulo

USP students demanding that PAPFE grants be raised from the current 885–330 reais to a livable 1,804 reais were subjected to a pre-dawn Military Police eviction on May 10. Proposed Bill 439/2026, now before the São Paulo Legislative Assembly, would expel striking students and ban them from any state university for 15 years. With 59 percent of Brazilians telling pollsters that Lula does not deserve another term, the student strikes have become a flashpoint that the PT and its pseudo-left satellites are working overtime to channel.

The strikes have spread from USP to multiple São Paulo high schools and to other Brazilian campuses, including in Rio Grande do Sul and Pernambuco. The Lula government, six months from the October elections, faces an open crisis of legitimacy — and is responding with police violence, not concessions.

German auto sector job cuts revised to 125,000 by 2035

A revised industry forecast now projects 125,000 German auto sector job losses by 2035, up from earlier estimates of 90,000. Volkswagen Group alone is cutting 50,000 positions; recent announcements at Mahle, Preh, TE Connectivity, and Porsche subsidiaries affect thousands more. The cuts come on top of the German government’s accelerated rearmament drive, which is being financed in significant part by reductions in social spending and the squeezing of industrial workers’ real wages.

IG Metall and the works councils have collaborated with employers through “co-management” structures rather than mobilizing against the cuts. As Peter Schwarz argued on the WSWS, workers will need to build rank-and-file action committees linked to the International Workers’ Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees if they are to defend jobs and living standards against the integrated assault by capital and the state.

$665 billion in Medicaid cuts begin hitting states

The Medicaid cuts contained in last summer’s “One Big Beautiful Bill Act” are now starting to land. State Medicaid budgets are projected to fall by a cumulative $665 billion over the next decade. New work requirements set to take effect this fall are expected to strip coverage from approximately 7.6 million Americans, according to projections compiled by Stateline. The Urban Institute estimates that roughly 3 in 10 young adults are now vulnerable to losing health-care access altogether.

The cuts are arriving simultaneously with a sharp acceleration in food and housing inflation tied to the Iran war, and at a moment when emergency rooms in the most affected states (Tennessee, Texas, West Virginia, Oklahoma) are already overwhelmed by the cumulative effects of the opioid epidemic, suicide and overdose deaths, and untreated chronic disease in the working-age population.

Nexteer auto workers move toward independent strike vote

Workers at Nexteer Automotive — supplier of steering systems to GM, Ford, and Stellantis — voted 73 percent to reject a second UAW-brokered tentative agreement, prompting the Nexteer Workers Rank-and-File Committee to call a Sunday membership meeting to vote for an immediate strike. Pulitzer winner Goldstone: low-wage job is “homelessness waiting to happen”

Pulitzer Prize winner Brian Goldstone, author of There Is No Place for Us: Working and Homeless in America, sat down with the WSWS to discuss the structural collapse of US housing affordability. Goldstone documents that 12 million renter households now spend over half their income on housing; rents have outpaced income gains by 325 percent since 1985; 93 percent of homeless families in Atlanta are Black; and the federal minimum wage has been frozen at $7.25 since 2016. His key formulation: “In America right now, a low-wage job … is homelessness waiting to happen.”

Goldstone advocates Finland-style social housing as an interim measure. The WSWS interview frames the structural problem more sharply: under capitalism, housing is treated as a financial asset, and the resulting catastrophe cannot be reformed away without breaking the grip of the property developers, the banks, and the institutional real-estate investors who now own a record share of American single-family homes.

US measles cases reach 1,893

The CDC and state health departments now confirm 1,893 US measles cases across 27 outbreaks as of May 14, 2026. South Carolina leads with 669 cases, followed by Utah (439), Texas (182), and Florida (135). Approximately 92 percent of cases involve unvaccinated individuals or those with unknown vaccination status. With four-and-a-half months still to run, 2026 is on pace to match or exceed 2025’s 2,288 full-year total — itself the worst year in over three decades — and the United States is on track to formally lose its WHO-certified measles elimination status. South Carolina’s health department has been criticized for delays in publishing case-level data, slowing local containment.

First documented human H5N5 infection in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine on May 7 published the first documented human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). A 75-year-old immunocompromised woman in Washington state developed severe acute hypoxemic respiratory failure following exposure to backyard birds; the upper-respiratory PCR was initially negative, exposing a serious diagnostic gap. The virus carried a transient mammalian-adaptive mutation that did not persist on serial sequencing, but the case expands the known zoonotic-spillover range of avian influenza subtypes.

WHO reports H5N1 child death and 13 zoonotic flu cases in Q1

WHO confirmed 13 avian and swine influenza infections in humans during Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child from Bangladesh. The detected subtypes include H5N1, H9N2 (5 cases), H10N3, and three variant influenza A strains. The breadth of novel influenza subtypes jumping the species barrier in a single quarter — combined with continued circulation of H5N1 in US dairy herds and the H5N5 case above — points to sustained and broadening pandemic risk from animal reservoirs.

White House moves toward pre-release AI vetting

The Trump administration is finalizing an executive order requiring government pre-deployment evaluation of frontier AI models, in a sharp reversal of the administration’s earlier deregulatory stance. The Commerce Department’s Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) has already signed agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI to evaluate models before public release. National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett has explicitly invoked the analogy of FDA drug approval. The move was triggered, according to Bloomberg and CNBC reporting, by national-security concerns over Anthropic’s Mythos model — specifically its demonstrated ability to identify and exploit novel cybersecurity vulnerabilities at scale. Industry sources signal panic over potential delays to scheduled model releases.

NEJM safety signal: AAV gene therapy linked to pediatric tumor

A separate NEJM paper published in May reports the development of a neuroepithelial tumor in a child previously treated with an AAV-based gene therapy for mucopolysaccharidosis type I. Genomic analysis identified integration of rearranged AAV vector elements into the PLAG1 oncogene — a rare but serious safety signal that will almost certainly prompt renewed scrutiny of integration-site risk across the pediatric gene-therapy clinical pipeline.

Cannes Film Festival mid-festival: war and dispossession dominate competition

The 79th Cannes Film Festival is now in its second week, with the Palme d’Or selection — running through May 23 — saturated with works on war, displacement, and the disintegration of the post-1991 international order. The dominance of these themes is itself an objective registration of the political moment: filmmakers from Iran, Ukraine, Palestine, Lebanon, Sudan, and the Sahel have all brought work into competition or the parallel sections that engages directly with the catastrophes of imperialist war and capitalist breakdown. The American films in competition this year, by contrast, remain — with isolated exceptions — preoccupied with interpersonal trauma, identity, and the genre conventions of the prestige drama, conspicuously avoiding the question of US imperial responsibility for the wars now reshaping the world.

This is itself an analytical fact, not a complaint. The institutional infrastructure of American cinema — its financing, distribution, marketing, and award systems — actively discourages the kind of unflinching engagement with imperialism that Iranian or Palestinian filmmakers produce out of direct social necessity. The work being shown at Cannes by directors from the war zones is, in a meaningful sense, the actual cinema of the present moment. American liberal critics will praise these films at the festival and forget them by autumn.

Cultural funding cuts deepen in Trump’s second budget

The Trump administration’s FY2027 budget request continues the assault on federal cultural funding: the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the Institute of Museum and Library Services are slated for effective elimination. PBS and NPR — already hollowed out by 2025 funding restrictions and the loss of corporate sponsorship — have announced further reductions to national news operations. Local public radio stations in the US South and rural Midwest, which were the last reliable source of accurate local journalism in much of the country, are closing at a rate of roughly one per week.

The defunding is a deliberate political project. It eliminates one of the few remaining institutional supports for non-commercial cultural production while clearing the field for the explicit promotion of “patriotic” content via the new National Patriotism Council and the Smithsonian’s restructured exhibition program. Liberal cultural institutions have offered no organized resistance; the leadership of the major museums and orchestras has spent the past year quietly preemptively complying.

Publishing industry roundup

The spring 2026 publishing season is dominated by titles registering the disintegration of the post-1991 order: a wave of new books on the New Cold War, the collapse of Pax Americana, the rise of the international far right, and the historical roots of the 2026 Iran war. Several major houses (Simon & Schuster, HarperCollins, Penguin Random House) have launched new “anti-woke” or “heterodox” imprints aimed at the conservative market, while quietly trimming their progressive nonfiction lists.

In music, the major labels’ aggressive licensing of artist back-catalogs to AI training datasets — concluded in confidential deals during winter 2025–26 — has begun producing the predictable result: a flood of synthetic music generated in the style of working musicians, with no royalty pathway back to the originals. The American Federation of Musicians has filed grievances; thus far, no major label has reversed course. The cultural and economic dispossession of musicians by their own labels is one of the most concentrated expressions of the wider attack on creative labor under late capitalism.

Perspective published: No Perspective was published today, May 17, 2026. The most recent Perspective on the site is “US-China summit brings no respite in global war” by Andre Damon, dated May 16, 2026. Its argument is summarized in full below.

US-China summit brings no respite in global war — Andre Damon (May 16 Perspective)

Damon opens with the observation that Trump’s state visit to Beijing — the first by a US president in nearly a decade — produced precisely nothing: no easing of the US blockade of Iran, no halt to arms transfers to Taiwan, no reduction of anti-China tariffs, and no joint communiqué. The diplomatic pageantry, the Perspective insists, conceals an inexorable structural drive toward great-power conflict rooted in the crisis of American capitalism itself. Trump’s approval rating has collapsed to 34 percent; the US national debt has reached 100 percent of GDP. The Iran war, launched less than three months before the summit, was initiated partly to arrest the slide in US global dominance — yet it has already exposed severe limits and forced Washington to seek Beijing’s assistance to manage the fallout.

The article’s central theoretical claim is that “American capitalism, crisis-ridden and indebted, cannot tolerate China’s continued economic growth.” This is presented not as the policy preference of any individual administration but as a systemic necessity flowing from capitalist competition on a world scale. The summit, in this reading, is less a pivot toward peaceful coexistence than the acknowledgment of a momentary tactical impasse: Washington cannot force Beijing into submission and must temporarily manage the contradiction while preserving the strategic posture that makes conflict inevitable. The US drive to seize “the commanding heights of the twenty-first century economy” — the framework underlying the trade war begun in 2018 — has demonstrably failed; China’s technological sector continues to advance in robotics and AI despite years of tariff pressure, export controls, and diplomatic isolation.

The political conclusion is sweeping: only the international working class, organized independently of all capitalist factions and on a socialist program, can prevent the slide into world war. The Perspective specifically warns against illusions in any section of the capitalist class — American, Chinese, or otherwise — as a force for peace, and calls for the building of rank-and-file committees and the strengthening of the International Committee of the Fourth International as the organizational instruments of this perspective. Trump’s domestic weakness is read not as a force for moderation but as a factor that may push the administration toward further military adventurism to shore up nationalist support.

What science knows about Andes hantavirus and why governments ignore it — Benjamin Mateus

Mateus’s unique contribution beyond the science covered above: he establishes that the deliberate dismantling of US and international public-health infrastructure during the active emergence of new zoonotic threats is not bureaucratic dysfunction but a class project. The Trump administration’s termination of the NIH’s CREID network, elimination of CDC Vessel Sanitation Program staff, and withdrawal from the WHO are presented as politically motivated transfers of public resources to financial and pharmaceutical interests. Mateus cites the recent MV Hondius cruise-ship hantavirus cluster as a concrete demonstration of the cost. The article’s distinct programmatic conclusion: only a science-based, internationally coordinated public health system freed from capitalist constraints can address the pandemic-class threat that Andes hantavirus poses given its R0 of 2.12 and 38–40 percent case-fatality rate.

South Asia, the Iran war and the bankruptcy of bourgeois nationalism — Deepal Jayasekera, Dilaxshan Mahalingam

This is the May Day speech delivered at the SEP (Sri Lanka) rally, now published in full. Beyond the Sri Lanka emergency facts cited above, the speech’s distinct contribution is a sustained historical analysis of South Asian bourgeois nationalism since 1947, arguing that Sinhala chauvinism, Tamil separatism, and Indian Hindutva are all incapable of opposing imperialism because each rests on a national-capitalist program that requires the suppression of its own working class. The authors present the JVP/NPP government’s secret coordination with US and Israeli diplomats over Iranian naval vessels — confirmed by Dissanayake’s own admission of “coordination with relevant embassies” — as the latest, sharpest demonstration of this bankruptcy. The fuel-price (40%), gas (31%), and electricity (32%) increases since February are the working-class price of this betrayal.

California DSA tells workers to vote for a billionaire Democrat in gubernatorial primaries — Jacob Crosse

The California DSA chapter has endorsed hedge-fund billionaire Tom Steyer in the Democratic gubernatorial primary. Steyer has spent roughly $546 million on political campaigns; his wealth derives from private-prison and coal investments. He currently polls at 17 percent, behind Xavier Becerra at 19 percent. Crosse argues the endorsement exposes the DSA as a Democratic Party auxiliary channeling working-class opposition behind wealthy capitalists. The political conclusion is that the DSA’s pretense of being a socialist organization has now been functionally abandoned and that workers in California require an independent class party — a perspective tied to the Socialist Equality Party’s intervention in the state. Mainstream California political coverage has ignored the class implications entirely.

Australian Labor’s “historic” housing policies are a fraud — Oscar Grenfell

Grenfell exposes the Australian Labor government’s budget housing reforms as cover for $63.8 billion in social spending cuts, including the removal of up to 300,000 disabled people from the National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS). The housing measures will help only approximately 7,500 homebuyers annually — 0.026 percent of the population — while the government’s own projections show overall housing availability decreasing by 35,000 units. Sydney median home prices now stand at 8.9 times annual income, versus 3.3 times in 1984. Grenfell’s distinct programmatic conclusion: resolving Australia’s housing crisis requires “a frontal assault on the wealth of the property developers, the billionaires and the banks” — i.e., the socialist reorganization of the housing sector itself.

Germany’s May 8 school strike against conscription — Joshua Seubert

Beyond the figure of approximately 45,000 students protesting across 150+ German cities on May 8, Seubert’s article provides the data: conscientious objector applications have already reached 2,656 in Q1 2026 alone (compared with 2,998 in all of 2024), and 28 percent of German 18-year-olds are simply ignoring the military questionnaire. The article’s distinct contribution is the polemic against SDAJ (the youth organization of the German Communist Party): SDAJ leadership has systematically avoided connecting conscription to capitalism and refuses to take a clear position on the Gaza, Ukraine, and Iran wars. The political conclusion: only a socialist internationalist program linking anti-war sentiment to the struggle against capitalism can give the anti-conscription movement an effective perspective against German militarism.

Jacobin / DSA

The Jacobin / DSA constellation continues its standard line of treating the Iran war as a uniquely Trumpian outrage rather than the latest expression of an integrated US imperialist drive that began under Clinton, accelerated under Bush, was sustained by Obama, and continued under Biden. Coverage of the Strait of Hormuz crisis emphasizes the suffering of US consumers from rising gas prices and the political cost to the Republican Party — categories drawn directly from the Democratic National Committee’s polling memos. The California DSA’s endorsement of hedge-fund billionaire Tom Steyer (covered in the WSWS section above) is the sharpest day-of expression of the underlying line: working-class opposition is to be channeled into the Democratic primary process, behind whichever capitalist candidate the local apparatus deems most “viable.”

The specific anti-Marxist content here is the systematic substitution of pressure-politics within the Democratic Party for any program of independent working-class political organization. The Iran war is treated as bad policy to be reformed away, not as a structural necessity of declining US capitalism. The DSA’s near-complete silence on the war’s class character — and its refusal to call for the formation of independent rank-and-file committees in the auto, logistics, and tech sectors now most affected by the war’s economic fallout — confirms its organic role as the left flank of the Democratic Party machine.

Left Voice

Left Voice, the US publication aligned with the Argentine PTS (Partido de los Trabajadores Socialistas), maintains a more militant rhetorical register on the Iran war and the Brazilian student strikes. The PTS / Frente de Izquierda current correctly identifies the imperialist character of the US assault on Iran and offers sharp critiques of bourgeois nationalism in Latin America. The persistent anti-Marxist content, however, lies in the Morenoite tradition’s “broad front” practice: the FIT-U in Argentina remains an electoral coalition organized for parliamentary participation under the Argentine bourgeois state, not a revolutionary party rooted in factory committees.

In coverage of the Brazilian USP student strikes, Left Voice tends to substitute militant phraseology for the patient construction of independent workers’ committees. The publication’s reluctance to undertake a sustained polemic against Lula’s Partido dos Trabalhadores — preferring instead to call on the PT-aligned trade union confederations to “rupture” with the government from above — reproduces the same problem that Trotsky identified with the centrist organizations of the 1930s.

PSL / Liberation News

The Party for Socialism and Liberation’s Liberation News maintains an anti-imperialist tone on the Iran war and the Israeli assault on Gaza, with comparatively sharp editorial denunciations of US and Israeli state violence. The decisive anti-Marxist content, however, is the PSL’s political adaptation to the Chinese and Russian state bureaucracies as supposed “anti-imperialist” forces. Coverage of the US-China summit framed Beijing’s posture as a defense of “multipolarity” against US hegemony, eliding the fact that the Chinese Communist Party regime presides over the brutal exploitation of hundreds of millions of Chinese workers and is itself a capitalist state pursuing its own great-power interests.

The PSL’s electoralist work (Claudia De la Cruz / Karina García-Vázquez campaign legacies, ongoing ballot-access drives) similarly substitutes parliamentary fronts for the construction of revolutionary cadre organizations. In the context of the Iran war, the PSL’s coverage emphasizes the “anti-war movement” understood as a coalition of left celebrities, NGOs, and progressive Democrats — precisely the formation that has demonstrably failed to halt any US war over the past quarter-century.

Socialist Alternative (US)

Socialist Alternative (US) — the American section of the International Socialist Alternative (formerly CWI) — continues to combine accurate reporting on the Medicaid cuts and the Nexteer strike with a politically muddled call for a “people’s party” or “workers’ party” defined primarily by its independence from corporate funding rather than by a defined revolutionary program. Coverage of the Iran war emphasizes “mass mobilization” as the strategic answer without specifying the political program that should animate it — a longstanding tendency in the CWI/ISA tradition.

The persistent anti-Marxist content is the substitution of organizational forms (a new party, a labor coalition) for the political clarification of class lines. Socialist Alternative has shown some openness to working with rank-and-file caucuses in the UAW and the Teamsters, but it has been unwilling to identify the union apparatus as a structural obstacle that must be displaced rather than reformed.

Socialist Worker (SWP UK)

The British Socialist Workers Party, through its Stop the War Coalition apparatus and its press, has continued to organize mobilizations against the Iran war and the Gaza assault, with the customary slogans calling for an end to British arms transfers to Israel and US bases on British soil. The persistent anti-Marxist content is the SWP’s longstanding strategic orientation toward the Labour Party “left” — a tendency that has been politically eviscerated under Starmer’s purges and now functions as a Westminster decoration with no organizational base.

The SWP’s reluctance to break with Stop the War Coalition’s coalition partners — including soft-left Labour MPs and a layer of liberal-pacifist NGOs — is the operational expression of the broader political problem. In conditions where Starmer’s Labour government is actively supplying weapons for the Israeli assault on Gaza and politically supporting the US position on Iran, the call to “pressure Labour” has become an alibi for refusing to build an independent revolutionary alternative.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The International Marxist Tendency, recently rebranded as the Revolutionary Communist International with a network of national “Revolutionary Communist Parties,” has continued to publicize claims of dramatic membership growth. The press — Socialist Appeal in the UK, communist.red internationally — combines rhetorical Marxism with a programmatic practice that adapts to the left-social-democratic milieu rather than confronting it. Coverage of the Iran war and the Brazilian strikes is largely propagandistic in form; the political program offered remains the construction of a “mass revolutionary party” defined by abstract slogans rather than the concrete struggle against the trade union bureaucracies and pseudo-left formations standing in the actual path of working-class mobilization.

The IMT/RCI’s refusal to undertake a serious accounting with the political record of its predecessor organizations (the Militant tendency in Labour, etc.) is the structural problem. The persistent failure to engage the historical question of Stalinism — and the political conclusions that flow from the ICFI’s analysis of it — leaves the RCI vulnerable to the same adaptations to social democracy and trade union bureaucracy that defined its predecessor organizations.
