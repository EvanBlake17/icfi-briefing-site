1. US-Iran war hits 60-day War Powers deadline as White House invokes April 7 ceasefire to bypass Congress

May 1 marked Day 62 of the US-Israeli war on Iran, blowing past the 60-day legal limit imposed by the 1973 War Powers Resolution. The White House argued no congressional authorization is required because the April 7 ceasefire “terminated hostilities” — even though a US naval blockade of Iranian ports remains in force, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, and the Pentagon continues to operate combat assets across the region.

The Senate on April 30 rejected a war-powers resolution by 47–50 — the sixth consecutive defeat for any congressional attempt to constrain the executive. Senator Susan Collins (R-ME) became the first Republican to vote yes; Senator John Fetterman (D-PA) was again the lone Democrat voting no. Trump posted: “Iran can’t get their act together. They don’t know how to sign a nonnuclear deal. They better get smart soon!” Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian responded that “any attempt to impose a maritime blockade or restrictions is contrary to international law…and is doomed to fail.”

Key facts: - Estimated US war cost: $25 billion in 60 days - Death tolls since hostilities began February 28: ~3,375 in Iran, ~2,509 in Lebanon, ~28 in Gulf states - The April 7 ceasefire was extended again on April 21, conditioned on Iran submitting “a unified proposal” - Both parties in Congress have now twice failed to assert any constitutional authority over the war

2. Strait of Hormuz dual blockade drives Brent to $119/barrel, the largest energy disruption since 1973

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since February 28, with a US naval blockade of Iranian ports added on April 13 to create what shipping analysts now call a “dual blockade.” Normal transit of roughly 20 million barrels of oil and LNG per day — about 20% of global seaborne oil trade — has collapsed to roughly 2 million barrels per day. UNCTAD warns the disruption has implications for global trade and development on a scale comparable to the 1973 oil shock.

Brent crude reached $119 per barrel as of May 1, the highest level since 2022; WTI traded above $105 (with intraday levels near $106). Wall Street analysts are now modeling potential $200/barrel scenarios. The US average gasoline price has hit $4.30/gallon — up $1.16 since the war began — and European diesel shortages are emerging. Qatar and UAE LNG flows are disrupted by 300+ million cubic meters per day; aluminum, fertilizer, and helium markets are also affected.

Iran has offered to reopen Hormuz on the condition that Washington lifts its blockade of Iranian ports. The US has refused. An adviser to Iran’s parliament speaker called the US blockade “no different from bombardment, and must be met with a military response.”

Bulgaria’s “Kremlin-friendly” Radev wins outright parliamentary majority

Bulgaria’s April 19 election — the country’s eighth snap election since 2021 — was won decisively by Progressive Bulgaria, led by former president Rumen Radev, with 44.6% of the vote and 131 of 240 National Assembly seats. The conservative pro-EU GERB-SDS collapsed to second place with 13.4% (39 seats); the liberal We Continue the Change-DB placed third at 12.6% (37 seats). Voter turnout rose to 48.8%, nearly 10 percentage points above October 2024.

Radev — a two-term president and former air force general — ran on an anti-oligarchy, anti-corruption platform. Western media universally describes him as “Kremlin-friendly” for opposing military aid to Ukraine and favoring Russian gas imports. The Washington Post headline framed his victory as installing “a new Russian ally in Europe.” But the result, set against five years of failed pro-EU coalitions, broken anti-corruption promises, and rising fuel prices from the Hormuz crisis, must be understood primarily as a working-class verdict on EU austerity and NATO war policy — a distinction the European bourgeois press has refused to draw.

US measles cases pass 1,792 with US elimination status at risk

As of April 23, the US has logged 1,792 confirmed measles cases across 30+ jurisdictions in 2026, with 22 active outbreaks; 93% of cases (1,668) are outbreak-associated. The South Carolina outbreak — the largest in decades — closed at nearly 1,000 cases and 21 hospitalizations. The Utah-Arizona border outbreak has exceeded 600 confirmed cases. The US is now on track to record its worst measles year since elimination was declared in 2000, with that status formally at risk should the case count exceed certain WHO criteria for sustained transmission.

Seasonal flu vaccines cut H5N1 mortality by 73% in animal studies

A Taiwan-US meta-analysis published April 15 in Emerging Microbes & Infections pooled nearly two decades of data from 35 controlled studies covering approximately 1,800 ferrets and found that seasonal flu vaccines containing the neuraminidase N1 component reduced H5N1-related mortality by approximately 73% versus unvaccinated controls. Vaccines lacking N1 showed little to no effect. The finding suggests that broadly deployed seasonal vaccines could provide critical cross-reactive protection during an H5N1 pandemic onset before targeted vaccines scale up — though human validation remains pending.

NEJM: pulsed field ablation beats drugs as first-line atrial fibrillation therapy

A randomized trial published in the April 30 issue of the New England Journal of Medicine found that pulsed field ablation (PFA) as first-line treatment for atrial fibrillation achieved a primary effectiveness rate of approximately 56% at 12 months versus approximately 30% for antiarrhythmic drug therapy, with a favorable safety profile. The result positions PFA as a credible first-line option rather than a rescue therapy and is likely to shift treatment guidelines.

COVID-19 status and WHO May vaccine meeting

CDC Rt estimates as of late April show COVID-19 declining or likely declining in 37 states and growing in only 1. Booster uptake remains very low — 9.4% of children/teens and 17.5% of adults 18+ received the 2025–26 shot. WHO’s TAG-CO-VAC will meet in May 2026 to set the next vaccine antigen composition. COVID has effectively settled into a seasonal respiratory pattern with outsized risk at the age extremes — but the persistently low vaccination coverage represents a continued failure of public health response, leaving vulnerable populations exposed.

600+ AI bills introduced in state legislatures

More than 600 AI bills imposing requirements on private entities have been introduced across US state legislatures in 2026 sessions. Colorado’s AI liability regulations take effect June 30, 2026, and proposed amendments to soften them remain unresolved. California faces a May 15 deadline for committee votes on multiple AI-regulation bills. The fragmented state-level legislative surge is creating compliance complexity ahead of any federal framework.

In a separate development, a Manhattan federal judge ruled that no attorney-client relationship “exists, or could exist” between a user and an AI platform like Claude, after a former CEO used an AI chatbot to prepare legal defense documents. US lawyers are now urgently advising clients that AI chatbot conversations are potentially discoverable in litigation — a precedent with significant implications for any worker who has used AI tools to prepare correspondence with counsel.

May Day’s artistic legacy is reasserted as a contested terrain

The 140th anniversary of the Haymarket affair is being commemorated this May Day across cultural venues as a still-contested political and artistic legacy. The 1886 Chicago general strike for the eight-hour day, the bombing in Haymarket Square, and the subsequent state execution of four labor leaders on November 11, 1887 produced one of the most enduring corpora of working-class art in American history — the protest songs, broadsheets, and Käthe Kollwitz-influenced graphic traditions that supplied the visual language of twentieth-century labor militancy. Rallies in Chicago, New York, and across European capitals are using projection mapping, mural restorations, and reissued archival prints to reactivate this material — a deliberate response to a publishing and museum environment that has, for decades, marginalized the Marxist content of the original tradition.

The political contestation over what May Day means has intensified as the day’s commemoration intersects with the largest US strike-action mobilization since the 1930s. Cultural producers face a choice between absorption into the Democratic Party–aligned “civic engagement” framing of the May Day Strong coalition and the harder task of reasserting the holiday’s revolutionary, internationalist, and explicitly anti-capitalist origins. The WSWS Editorial Board’s May Day rally — featuring statements from postal workers, autoworkers, teachers, and dockworkers — represents the latter pole; the dominant NGO infrastructure around the protests represents the former.

Sri Lankan state targets Tamil literary production

The Sri Lankan customs seizure of 360 copies of Pradeepan Deepachelvan’s novels and essays — Naduhal, Bayangaravadi, Cyanide, plus an essay collection and an interview volume — is the most significant act of state literary censorship in the country in over a decade. Deepachelvan, a teacher at Murugaananda College in Sri Lanka’s northern province, has published seven poetry collections engaging the country’s three-decade civil war; his work has been translated into multiple languages and is studied internationally in the post-conflict literature canon.

The pretext — “national security” and “national harmony” — exposes the Sri Lankan state’s ongoing fear of Tamil cultural production that engages honestly with the war’s history. As the SEP notes, Sinhala-chauvinist military propaganda continues to circulate freely at state-sponsored book fairs while Tamil novels examining the same conflict are confiscated. The pattern is consistent with a broader post-civil-war regime that has criminalized Tamil memory work — and it is taking place against a backdrop of accelerating austerity that compounds censorship with material immiseration.

Sabastian Sawe’s sub-2-hour London Marathon as cultural event

Kenyan runner Sabastian Sawe won the April 26 London Marathon in 1 hour, 59 minutes, 30 seconds — the first legal sub-2-hour marathon ever run under official World Athletics conditions. He improved on the late Kelvin Kiptum’s 2:00:35 world record by a full 1:05 and beat Eliud Kipchoge’s 2019 INEOS 1:59 Challenge time, which was aided by rotating pacemakers and did not count as an official record. Ethiopia’s Yomif Kejelcha, in his marathon debut, finished second in 1:59:41 — making the two men the only people in history to run the distance that fast under official conditions. Sawe ran a negative split (60:29 / 59:01); Tigst Assefa set a new women’s-only world record at 2:15:41.

The result is more than an athletic milestone. The economic structure of elite distance running — Kenyan and Ethiopian runners producing world records under Western shoe-company sponsorship contracts that capture the bulk of value — is one of the more visible expressions of late-imperialist sport. Sawe wore Adidas, breaking Nike’s long dominance in elite marathon footwear and reorienting tens of millions in marketing investment. The athletic achievement deserves recognition on its own terms; the structures within which it occurs deserve sustained socialist scrutiny.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — Mamdani’s first 100 days as NYC mayor framed as a “contradiction”; Democratic Party entrism normalized as the road to socialism
  • Left Voice — Most class-independent of the six; explicit call for general strike against ICE pressures union bureaucracy from the left
  • Liberation News / PSL — Anti-Iran-war content correct in form, but anti-imperialism remains structured around defense of bourgeois states
  • Socialist Alternative — Leading building work for May Day general strike; coalition with Indivisible/Sunrise still unaddressed
  • SWP UK / Socialist Worker — Stand Up To Racism front campaign directs anti-Reform vote toward Labour by omission
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — May Day rhetoric formally Trotskyist; Greens engagement and Russia-Ukraine campism warrant continued scrutiny

The defining story across all six tendencies today is the US May Day general strike mobilization. The central political failure across most of them is the absence of a clear program for what comes after the strike — particularly how to prevent it from being absorbed into Democratic Party midterm work in the US, or into anti-Reform electoralism in the UK.

Jacobin / DSA

“Zohran Mamdani and the Contradiction of Democratic Socialism” examines Mamdani at 100 days as NYC mayor, faulting his administration for failing to mobilize mass working-class participation. The headline framing of “contradiction” is a feint: Jacobin treats the DSA-to-mayor pipeline as the legitimate terrain of socialist politics, validating electoral capture by the Democratic Party as a viable road to power. “There’s a New Dark Money–Backed Democratic Machine” documents billionaire-backed infrastructure coordinating with Democratic Party networks for the 2026 midterms — framed as a warning about party elites vs. populists, but containing no call to break from the Democratic Party. “Democratic Governance of AI Is the Real Solution” argues for regulatory democratic oversight of AI rather than a moratorium, positioning state-managed capitalism as the answer. Reformist technocratic program with no challenge to the ownership of AI infrastructure by capital. Assessment: Jacobin continues to function as the intellectual organ of DSA’s Democratic Party entrism; the Mamdani coverage normalizes municipal governance within bourgeois institutions as a socialist achievement.

Left Voice

“Strikes, Student Walkouts, and Demonstrations Against ICE Continue Across the U.S.” provides live coverage of May Day mobilizations, emphasizing the independent working-class character of the movement and documenting concrete strike activity. “Why Aren’t the Largest U.S. Labor Unions Calling a General Strike Against ICE?” pressures AFL-CIO and major union bureaucracies to break with NGO-coalition timidity and call a genuine general strike — explicitly contrasting militant rank-and-file pressure with bureaucratic demobilization. “This May Day We Must Remember How Immigrants Built the Labor Movement” connects immigrant labor militancy historically to contemporary May Day action, arguing for full rights for all immigrants outside the framework of Democratic Party advocacy. Assessment: Left Voice (PTS/FT tendency) is the most consistently class-independent of the six. No visible support for bourgeois parties in current output. To watch: any softness on Democratic Party electoral coalitions as the 2026 midterms approach.

Liberation News / PSL

“PSL Editorial — The Meaning of May Day: We Need a Revolution!” calls for revolution in general terms; the rhetoric is genuine in register, but PSL’s actual practice consists of popular-front coalition politics and defense of bourgeois-nationalist regimes abroad. “PSL Statement: Stop Trump’s War on Iran!” opposes US military action against Iran — correct in form, but PSL’s anti-imperialism is structured around defense of the Iranian state rather than solidarity with the Iranian working class, which is itself under brutal IRI repression. “San Diego PSL Stands with Workers Everywhere on May Day” is local mobilization content. Assessment: PSL’s national-reformist program is consistent — anti-US imperialism translating in practice into support for Iranian, Venezuelan, Russian, and Chinese states as progressive forces. No independent working-class internationalism. The PSL also repeatedly enters popular fronts with the Democratic Party left.

“From Coast to Coast: Socialist Alternative Is Building for May Day Action” documents organizing in Madison, Chicago, Bay Area, etc. — SA is playing a leading role in building strike infrastructure and maintaining a socialist identity within the May Day Strong coalition. “Striking on May Day: An FAQ for Workers” provides practical agitational material on job protection, legality, and coordination — oriented toward rank-and-file action. “The Case for a Nationwide General Strike on May 1” makes the political argument for a general strike as the decisive tool against the Trump administration; formally correct, but the listed coalition partners (Indivisible, Sunrise, 50501) are aligned with the Democratic Party left flank. Coverage of the Strait of Hormuz crisis has focused almost entirely on US and European consumer prices.
