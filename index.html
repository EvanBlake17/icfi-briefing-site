1. Trump arrives in Beijing for first US presidential visit since 2017

President Trump landed in Beijing Wednesday for a three-day state visit (May 13–15) with Xi Jinping — the first US presidential visit to China since 2017, and the first direct Trump-Xi meeting since the October 2025 trade truce struck at Busan. The visit was originally scheduled for April but postponed by the eruption of the Iran war. Elon Musk, Apple CEO Tim Cook and more than a dozen other corporate executives are traveling with Trump on Air Force One.

The agenda is dominated by extending the fragile tariff truce — tariffs on Chinese imports currently sit at 47%, down from 57% after Busan and from a 2025 peak above 100% on some goods — alongside Taiwan arms sales, semiconductor export controls, rare earth minerals (China refines roughly 90% of global supply), AI governance, and stabilization of the Strait of Hormuz. The Jimmy Lai case has also been placed on the agenda.

Analysts uniformly expect limited deals rather than a comprehensive framework: a tariff pause, fresh Chinese purchase commitments for soybeans, beef and Boeing aircraft, possibly a rare-earths arrangement. The asymmetry created by the Iran war was bluntly stated by Gregory Poling of the Center for Strategic and International Studies: “It is not China being humiliated in the strait … It’s the US.” Salvador Santino Regilme of Leiden University described the relationship as “strategic rivalry and deep economic dependence” — the framework within which both sides are operating.

Key facts: - Trump publicly committed to “push Xi Jinping to open up China” — language that telegraphs continued US demands for unilateral concessions - China holds significant leverage in oil markets given its dominant position in Iranian crude purchases and its capacity to police or ignore Hormuz disruptions - The summit takes place against the backdrop of US-Pacific re-armament: Australia-Fiji “Vuvale Union” signed days earlier (see International section), Philippine basing expansion, and accelerating Japan-South Korea-US trilateral integration

2. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 75 as ceasefire is placed on “massive life support”

The US-Israel war on Iran entered its 75th day Wednesday with the Pentagon’s Jules Hurst confirming the conflict has now cost $29 billion — a $4 billion increase from late April, attributed to “updated repair and replacement of equipment and operational costs.” Trump publicly rejected Iran’s latest peace proposal during remarks before departing for Beijing, calling it “a piece of garbage” and admitting he did not finish reading it. He characterized the ceasefire as “on massive life support” and warned that Iran would be “decimated” if it did not accept US dictated terms, adding a veiled nuclear threat about “one big glow coming out of Iran.”

Iran’s proposal demands cessation of hostilities on all fronts (including Lebanon), removal of the US naval blockade, war reparations, sanctions relief, asset unfreezing, and recognition of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz — all rejected by Washington. As Kevin Reed wrote on the WSWS, Iran has now suffered “approximately 3,468 deaths including more than 1,700 civilians” against 13 US service member deaths, and the administration is moving aggressively to restrict press access and to characterize critical journalism as “virtual treason.”

Key facts: - Brent crude exceeded $104.50/barrel in Asian trading Tuesday, roughly 50% above pre-war levels; WTI sat near $101/barrel and jumped 3.87% on May 12 on supply concerns - Only three crude tankers exited the Strait of Hormuz in the past week, all with tracking systems disabled - US State Department is preparing two days of intensive Israel-Lebanon diplomatic talks May 14–15 to try to contain the regional spillover - Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan is conducting parallel talks in Qatar on Hormuz shipping safety

3. April CPI hits 3.8% as real wages fall for first time in three years

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported Tuesday that April consumer prices rose 3.8% year-over-year — the highest rate since May 2023 — and 0.6% month-over-month, beating consensus by 0.1 percentage point. The print was driven overwhelmingly by the Iran war’s oil shock, but core inflation also accelerated, suggesting the cost surge is broadening beyond energy.

Real average hourly wages fell 0.5% for the month and 0.3% on an annual basis — the first annual real-wage decline in three years. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York simultaneously reported rising rates of serious delinquency on US consumer loans, particularly student loans, and a deterioration in the share of households able to keep up with debt service. Markets responded by pricing out remaining 2026 rate cut expectations; odds of a rate hike by year-end rose to roughly 30% after the print.

Key facts: - Energy costs +17.9% year-over-year; gasoline +28.4%; fuel oil +54.3% - Core CPI +0.4% monthly / +2.8% annually (beat forecasts) - Shelter inflation +0.6% for the month — double March’s pace - Food prices +3.2% year-over-year - US average gasoline at $4.52/gallon

The convergence of accelerating headline inflation, eroding real wages, rising debt delinquency, and a Fed that is now openly contemplating hikes in 2027 amounts to a sharply deteriorating outlook for working-class households. The war’s costs are being passed directly onto wages and household balance sheets while corporate profits and Wall Street bonuses remain at records.

4. Starmer fights for political survival after Labour collapse in local elections

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer is fighting for political survival after Labour’s catastrophic performance in last week’s English local elections, where the party lost more than 1,400 council seats — roughly 60% of those contested — and lost control of 35 councils. Labour suffered parallel reverses in the Scottish and Welsh parliament elections. The collapse came less than two years after Starmer’s 2024 landslide.

More than 90 Labour MPs have now publicly called on Starmer to set a departure date. At least four junior ministers have resigned in days, including safeguarding minister Jess Phillips and, on Tuesday, health innovation and safety minister Zubir Ahmed. Two cabinet ministers are privately urging him to step down. Health Secretary Wes Streeting — widely seen as a leadership rival — is scheduled to meet Starmer Wednesday in a session that several British outlets have flagged as potentially decisive.

Starmer has publicly refused to resign and says he intends to remain and fight. Eurasia Group analysts now assess a 35% chance MPs force a leadership election by September, a 25% chance of an “orderly” Starmer departure, and a 20% chance of an immediate leadership contest. Labour’s losses have flowed primarily to Reform UK on the right and the Greens on the left — a pattern that mirrors the wider European disintegration of social democracy under conditions of austerity, militarism and complicity with imperialist war.

5. Gaza ceasefire fraying as aid drops 37% and famine spreads

The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs reports humanitarian aid flowing into Gaza dropped 37% in the first three months of 2026 against an already-inadequate 2025 baseline. Only ~10% of the funding required for critical 2026 operations has been secured. The IPC’s famine declaration covering Gaza Governorate from August 22, 2025 remains in force; at least 1.6 million Palestinians — 77% of Gaza’s population — now face “high levels of acute food insecurity.” Over 100,000 children and 37,000 pregnant or breastfeeding women are projected to suffer acute malnutrition through April 2026.

The structural collapse of aid is being driven by Israeli restrictions on generators, engine oil and spare parts — restrictions that translate directly into failures of health and sanitation systems — and by repeated killing of humanitarian personnel. Two NGO workers were killed in separate incidents in early May, triggering suspension of health and water services across multiple districts. The crossing closures have been deepened by regional disruptions tied to the Iran war.

Key facts: - Gaza food prices have soared further as Iran war disruptions raise shipping and fuel costs across the Eastern Mediterranean - The UN’s funding shortfall reflects deliberate cuts by the US and several European donors who continue to underwrite Israeli operations - The “ceasefire” period itself has produced sustained Israeli operations and ongoing displacement, with the population remaining concentrated in destroyed coastal zones

6. Global oil inventories at record drawdown as IEA warns of supply shock

The International Energy Agency’s May monthly oil market report states the world is “drawing oil inventories at a record pace as importing countries confront unprecedented disruptions to Middle Eastern supply.” Global stockpiles fell 246 million barrels between March and April alone — a single-month drawdown without recent precedent — leaving total inventories at 7.9 billion barrels.

Brent crude has risen approximately 50% from pre-war levels; officials and bank analysts expect prices to remain near $106–$107/barrel through June. WTI surged sharply on May 12 on fresh supply concerns. Deutsche Bank attributed Tuesday’s modest decline to “increased nervousness that a US-Iran deal looks further away” — the price is now whipsawing on every signal from the negotiations.

Key facts: - Only three tankers exited Hormuz last week with tracking disabled — effectively the lowest weekly count of the war - US average gasoline at $4.52/gallon, with NBC News reporting the biggest single-day jump since the April ceasefire announcement - Turkey’s Fidan in Doha for shipping safety talks; Saudi and UAE production at theoretical maximums; spare capacity is being exhausted

The structural picture — record inventory drawdown, near-empty spare capacity, no negotiated end in sight — is one in which any further escalation will produce a vertical price spike. The IEA’s framing implicitly acknowledges this: the agency is not modeling a continuation of current conditions but warning of a supply shock.

7. South Korea moves toward joining the war on Iran after HMM tanker incident

The South Korean government is openly weighing entry into the US-led war against Iran after a Panama-flagged, South Korean-operated HMM cargo vessel was struck in the Strait of Hormuz, sustaining a seven-meter gash from what the Lee government described as “two unidentified airborne objects.” Iran’s embassy in Seoul “firmly rejects and categorically denies” involvement in the strike.

As Ben McGrath reports on the WSWS, South Korea’s ruling class sees direct participation in the war as economically beneficial — securing Middle Eastern oil access and advancing President Lee’s stated goal of making South Korea the “fourth largest weapons exporter in the world.” Korean gasoline prices are already up 21.1% year-over-year, and a sustained Hormuz disruption would severely damage the South Korean economy, which imports nearly all its oil through that chokepoint.

Mainstream coverage of the HMM incident has accepted at face value the US-South Korean characterization of the strike as Iranian aggression. The pattern of unverifiable “incidents” producing pretexts for further imperialist participation — the WSWS raises the possibility of a US/Israeli false-flag provocation — recalls precedents from the Gulf of Tonkin through the 2019 Strait of Hormuz tanker attacks. Seoul’s possible entry would add a major US ally to the war coalition and would expose Korean conscripts to direct combat in a war neither parliament nor the Korean public has authorized.

German Defence Minister Boris Pistorius visited Kyiv Tuesday, announcing that Germany and Ukraine are jointly developing weapons systems with ranges up to 1,500 kilometers. One year after India-Pakistan war: Indus Waters Treaty still suspended

May 7, 2026 marked the first anniversary of India’s launch of Operation Sindoor — missile strikes on Pakistan in retaliation for the April 22, 2025 Pahalgam attack that killed 26 civilians. India’s suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty, announced April 23, 2025, remains in force. The treaty underpins irrigation for more than 240 million people and supplies over 80% of Pakistan’s agricultural water, according to World Bank figures.

Both governments are claiming strategic victory. Pakistani cities display military tribute banners; Indian officials insist on a decisive win. Analysts in The Diplomat and Al Jazeera warn that the familiar escalation pattern — unverified claims, inflammatory rhetoric, performative military posturing — is reemerging, with a next conflict assessed as “potentially more dangerous.” Western coverage of the treaty suspension and its slow-motion catastrophe for Pakistani agriculture has been minimal.

Eleven African nations join France-Mexico initiative to limit Security Council veto

Eleven African states formally joined the France- and Mexico-led “Veto Initiative” Tuesday, committing to call on the five permanent UN Security Council members to voluntarily refrain from using the veto when mass atrocities are under consideration. The signatures are explicitly motivated by the repeated US vetoes on Gaza and Lebanon resolutions and by the present US blockade of UN action on the Iran war. Iran’s ambassador to Beijing said that UN resolutions “ignoring the US blockade would be doomed to fail.” The initiative does not require Charter amendment — it relies entirely on political commitments — and is therefore symbolically significant but structurally weak.

Australia and Fiji sign “Vuvale Union” security pact aimed at China

Australia and Fiji formalized the “Vuvale Union” Tuesday — an upgraded security treaty explicitly aimed at countering Chinese influence in the Pacific. Australian Foreign Minister Penny Wong framed it as mutual strength in “a world that is more contested.” The agreement provides for enhanced maritime security, joint military exercises, and the embedding of Australian personnel in Fiji’s armed forces. It joins parallel Australian arrangements with Papua New Guinea, Nauru and Tuvalu — a steadily tightening neocolonial web that, as John Braddock writes on the WSWS, prepares the Pacific region for what could become “an even more catastrophic war.”

Germany launches sweeping legislative attack on basic rights

The German government has, over the past six months, advanced what the WSWS characterizes as “the most extensive rearmament programme since Hitler,” accompanied by a coordinated set of laws expanding state power and curtailing civil liberties. The Military Service Act (effective January 1, 2026) requires mandatory registration and medical examinations for men born in 2008 and later. The KRITIS Framework Act (March 17, 2026) establishes government control over critical infrastructure with statutory information blackouts. An Aviation Security Act amendment permits Bundeswehr deployment within German borders for drone defense. The MAD Act expands military intelligence operations abroad. And the implementation of the EU Terrorism Directive extends criminal liability to “preparatory” stages, potentially criminalizing protest activity. No mainstream German outlet has presented these as a coordinated assault on democratic rights — the synthesis itself is the WSWS contribution.

US measles cases top 1,842 in 2026

The CDC reported as of May 7, 2026 that 1,842 confirmed measles cases have been recorded across 39 US jurisdictions, with 93% (1,712) linked to outbreaks. South Carolina alone accounts for approximately 990 cases concentrated in Spartanburg County — a single ongoing outbreak that began in late 2025 and has continued to expand due to sustained community transmission in under-vaccinated populations. There have been 25 new outbreaks declared in 2026. The 2026 case count is on track to far exceed any year in the post-elimination era.

First human H5N5 avian influenza case reported in NEJM

A study published in the New England Journal of Medicine on May 7 documents the first known human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). The patient — a 75-year-old woman with Waldenström macroglobulinemia — developed acute hypoxemic respiratory failure after exposure to birds; bronchoalveolar lavage confirmed H5N5, a subtype distinct from the predominant H5N1. Notably, upper-respiratory testing missed the infection initially. Separately, the WHO tracked four human H5N1 cases in Q1 2026, including a fatal pediatric case in Bangladesh. The H5N5 spillover underscores that avian flu subtypes beyond H5N1 are capable of causing severe human disease.

Trump administration pivots to mandatory pre-release AI vetting

The White House is drafting an executive order requiring pre-deployment government vetting of frontier AI models — analogous to FDA drug review — following Anthropic’s release of its “Mythos” model, which demonstrated capability to identify and exploit software security vulnerabilities. On May 5, NIST’s Center for AI Standards and Innovation signed pre-deployment evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI. The pivot represents a striking policy reversal: the same administration that dismantled Biden-era AI safety executive orders is now contemplating the most expansive federal AI oversight framework yet proposed in the US. The framing — military and national-security concerns rather than worker displacement or social harm — defines the class character of the oversight being constructed.

Nature: hippocampus encodes language meaning in unconscious patients

Neural recordings from the hippocampi of anesthetized surgical patients, reported in Nature on May 11, showed that hippocampal neurons responded to auditory “oddballs,” encoded semantic properties of language, and could predict upcoming words in heard phrases — all without conscious awareness. The findings challenge the assumption that higher-order language processing requires consciousness and have implications for cognition in anesthesia and disorders of consciousness.

Alaska glacier landslide triggers major tsunami

A retreating glacier in Alaska destabilized a mountainside, triggering a massive landslide that unleashed a tsunami in a coastal inlet — early reports describe it as one of the largest landslide-generated tsunamis in Alaska in decades. The event is a direct consequence of accelerating glacial retreat driven by climate change, highlighting an underappreciated category of climate-linked geophysical hazard.

90 years of Chaplin’s Modern Times

May 2026 marks the 90th anniversary of Charlie Chaplin’s Modern Times (1936), and the WSWS uses the occasion to develop a fully political reading of the film. John Conrad’s retrospective argues that Modern Times retains its power because it depicts industrial workers ground down by capitalism and Depression-era poverty with documentary precision — a perspective rooted in Chaplin’s own impoverished London upbringing. The film earned $1.8 million domestically, was banned by Nazi propaganda minister Joseph Goebbels for “advocacy of communism,” and triggered an intensified FBI surveillance program that culminated, in 1952, in the US government’s barring of Chaplin’s reentry to the country during the McCarthyite purges. Conrad connects the film’s central conceit — machinery deployed purely for the extraction of profit — to contemporary capitalism, including AI-driven automation that is being weaponized rather than used to expand human freedom (a theme also developed in Evan Blake’s May Day rally speech on Socialism AI). The piece stands as a serious effort to reclaim Chaplin from the depoliticized “great clown” image that dominates mainstream tributes.

Cannes 2026 opens under shadow of Iran war and Gaza famine

The Cannes Film Festival opened its 79th edition this week against the most charged political backdrop in years. Programming decisions around Iranian and Israeli filmmakers have produced intense lobbying from both pro- and anti-war factions; several Iranian directors who could not travel to France have submitted recorded statements that have been circulated outside the official program. A wave of films explicitly engaging with the wars in Iran and Gaza — and with the European migrant question that has been radically reshaped by both conflicts — is competing for prizes. Industry coverage has accepted at face value the festival’s framing as a cultural “neutral ground,” even as Cannes’ choices on which voices to platform are deeply political.

Major museums absorb US federal funding cuts

US cultural institutions are absorbing the consequences of the Trump administration’s cuts to the National Endowment for the Humanities and the Institute of Museum and Library Services, with mid-sized regional museums reporting staff layoffs and program cancellations through the spring of 2026. The cuts compound earlier reductions and effectively defund the kind of public-facing, education-oriented programming that smaller institutions depend on. The shift transfers cultural patronage decisively to billionaire donors and corporate sponsors — a class realignment of cultural production that flies under the radar of most arts journalism.

Socialism AI and the political education of the working class — Evan Blake

In a May Day 2026 Online Rally speech, SEP National Committee member Evan Blake argues that artificial intelligence under capitalism is being weaponized — for war (notably the Middle East conflicts), for surveillance, and for the intensification of exploitation — while its potential as a tool of human liberation is systematically suppressed. The platform is positioned as a counterweight to AI tools that train and deploy workers as commodities. Blake urges workers and youth internationally to use the tool to build socialist movements that can take ownership of these productive forces. The speech is significant as both a theoretical intervention — restating Trotsky on technology and class — and a concrete political initiative.

Australia: Labor government’s pro-business budget targets the disabled and poor — Oscar Grenfell

Australia’s federal budget cuts $35–38 billion from the National Disability Insurance Scheme, removing up to 300,000 people from coverage over four years and slashing annual NDIS growth from approximately 10% to 2%. The budget also targets 28,000 public sector jobs for elimination, raises private health insurance costs for seniors by up to $640/year, and cuts $472 million from education. Defence spending remains at $60 billion annually with an additional $53 billion increase planned across the decade. There is no cost-of-living relief, and a proposed 25% levy on energy companies was explicitly rejected to protect their profits. Grenfell notes that the budget’s macroeconomic assumptions depend on the Iran war ending; should oil reach $200/barrel, inflation would exceed 7% and unemployment 5%, demolishing the budget framework.

Rising fertilizer prices, shortages threaten Sri Lankan farmers with disaster

The US-Israeli war on Iran has shut the Strait of Hormuz, driving world urea prices from approximately $400/tonne in 2025 to $857/tonne in April 2026 — devastating Sri Lanka, which imports over 90% of its fertilizer. The World Bank projects a further 60% increase for 2026. Individual farmers describe cultivation costs exceeding what paddy prices can recover; one farmer is carrying 600,000 rupees in debt. A November 2025 cyclone had already destroyed 20% of the main harvest ($814 million in losses). The article calls for nationalization of agribusiness under working-class control as the structural solution — counterposing this to the IMF-dictated framework that has dominated Sri Lankan agricultural policy since the 2022 collapse.

1. Indus Waters Treaty suspension — 240 million Pakistanis face agricultural catastrophe

India’s year-long suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty has left Pakistan’s agricultural sector — which feeds 240 million people and depends on treaty-governed river flows for over 80% of irrigation — in deep uncertainty. The issue has received almost no Western media coverage despite its scale. The slow-motion crisis demonstrates how national conflicts among bourgeois states impose devastating costs on working-class and peasant populations, and it directly connects to the WSWS’s analysis of nuclear war risk in South Asia.
