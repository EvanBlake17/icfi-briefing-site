1. Iran war reaches day 61 with peace talks stalled and $25 billion price tag

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its 61st day with no end in sight, peace negotiations frozen and the conflict consuming an estimated $25 billion in Pentagon spending over two months. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, testifying before the House Armed Services Committee, declined to say when the war might conclude. Trump publicly rejected an Iranian proposal to lift the US naval blockade in exchange for resumed talks, demanding Tehran first commit to nuclear disarmament: “They can’t have a nuclear weapon.”

The blockade itself has prevented 38 ships from entering or leaving Iranian ports. Tehran’s closure of the Strait of Hormuz has paralyzed roughly a quarter of seaborne global oil traffic, sending Brent crude to an intraday wartime high of $126/bbl today before profit-taking pushed it back to $115.81. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi traveled to Moscow for emergency talks with Vladimir Putin, seeking diplomatic leverage as Tehran’s economy buckles.

Key facts: - Pentagon estimate: $25 billion spent in 60 days, no end-date provided to Congress - 38 ships blocked from Iranian ports under continuing US naval blockade - Trump publicly told Iran to “get smart soon” - Israeli forces in Lebanon killed three rescue workers in a strike on April 29 - Brent and WTI both up roughly 60% since the war’s launch on February 28

2. Israel seizes Gaza-bound aid flotilla in international waters, arrests 175 activists

Israeli naval forces intercepted 22 of approximately 58 vessels of the Global Sumud Flotilla some 600 nautical miles from the Gaza coast — the farthest such interception in the history of the Gaza solidarity flotillas. Israeli forces deployed drones, communications jamming, and armed raiding parties to board the boats and arrest 175 activists, including Irish, Italian, Spanish, and Swedish nationals.

The IDF claimed soldiers had found “condoms and drugs” aboard the vessels and dismissed the mission as “a PR stunt.” Turkey’s foreign ministry condemned the seizure as “an act of piracy” violating international law. The European Commission urged Israel to respect freedom of navigation and humanitarian law but stopped short of explicit condemnation. More than 30 remaining flotilla vessels diverted into Greek territorial waters seeking refuge.

Key facts: - 22 of ~58 boats intercepted; 175 activists arrested, including 6 Irish citizens - Interception occurred 600 nautical miles from Gaza — international waters - Turkey’s Foreign Ministry: “An act of piracy” - A previous flotilla was intercepted by Israel in October 2025 - Vessels were carrying medical supplies and food into Gaza

3. Trump threatens to pull US troops from Germany in escalating feud with Chancellor Merz

President Trump openly threatened to reduce the US military presence in Germany after a public spat with Chancellor Friedrich Merz over the Iran war. “The United States is studying and reviewing the possible reduction of Troops in Germany, with a determination to be made over the next short period of time,” Trump posted, after Merz had publicly stated the US was being “humiliated” by Iran and lacked a coherent war strategy.

Merz also pointed out that Germany and Europe were “suffering considerably” from the Hormuz closure’s energy consequences. Trump retorted: “The Chancellor of Germany, Friedrich Merz, thinks it’s OK for Iran to have a Nuclear Weapon.” The rupture represents a significant fracture in NATO cohesion in the middle of an active war and lays bare the divergent class and geopolitical interests of US and European imperialism.

Key facts: - Over 36,000 active-duty US personnel currently across 20 bases in Germany - This is the largest US military contingent in Europe - Merz: US “humiliated” by Iran, “lacking coherent war strategy” - Trump: Merz “thinks it’s OK for Iran to have a Nuclear Weapon” - Threat comes amid mounting energy costs across European economies

4. Lebanon ceasefire collapsing as Hezbollah wounds 12 Israeli soldiers in fiber-optic drone strike

A Hezbollah fiber optic-guided drone struck an IDF artillery position near the northern Israeli community of Shomera today, wounding 12 soldiers. The fiber-optic guidance system, which is immune to standard electronic jamming countermeasures, has Israeli military intelligence scrambling to characterize the new capability. The IDF responded by demolishing multiple Hezbollah rocket-launching positions in Mount Dov on the Lebanese side using elite Alpinist and Yahalom units.

NPR reports that Israel is now “mirroring Gaza” — systematically destroying towns and villages across southern Lebanon — even as a formal cessation of hostilities remains nominally in effect. Israel has reportedly asked the Trump administration to impose a 2–3 week deadline on Lebanon negotiations. The IDF chief stated bluntly there is “no ceasefire” in southern Lebanon.

Key facts: - 12 IDF soldiers wounded in single fiber-optic drone strike - Cessation of hostilities began April 16, initial 10-day window, since extended - Fiber-optic drones immune to RF/electronic jamming - IDF demolished multiple Hezbollah rocket-launching positions in Mount Dov - Civilian destruction in southern Lebanon described as “mirroring Gaza”

5. Hegseth details White House plan to surge military spending by 50 percent

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth — testifying alongside the Iran war update — outlined a proposed FY2027 increase in US military spending from $1 trillion to $1.5 trillion. The Pentagon characterizes this as “the sharpest single-year jump in US military spending in the postwar era,” taking the budget to 4.5% of GDP, with Republican leadership openly targeting 5%. Andre Damon, writing on the WSWS, noted that “Democrats on the committee offered no meaningful opposition” — a bipartisan unity behind preparation for war with China and Russia.

Major allocations include $71 billion for nuclear arsenal expansion, $65 billion for shipbuilding, $54.6 billion for autonomous defense systems, and 44,000 additional active-duty troops. The administration’s symbolic rebranding of the Pentagon as the “Department of War” was endorsed throughout the testimony. The buildup proceeds at the same time the administration is moving to cut federal workforce, social services, and education funding.

Key facts: - Proposed jump from $1 trillion to $1.5 trillion for FY2027 - 4.5% of GDP target, with Republican leadership pushing for 5% - $71B nuclear, $65B shipbuilding, $54.6B autonomous systems - 44,000 additional active-duty troops - No meaningful Democratic opposition recorded in committee

6. Supreme Court guts Voting Rights Act, threatens TPS status of one million immigrants

The Supreme Court’s 6-3 conservative majority in Louisiana v. Callais struck down a second Black-majority congressional district, in what Patrick Martin on the WSWS describes as “the culmination of a nearly two-decade assault on the Voting Rights Act.” The ruling now requires plaintiffs to prove discriminatory intent — not merely discriminatory results — effectively gutting Section 2 of the VRA after Section 5 was eviscerated in 2013’s Shelby County v. Holder. Up to 12 Democratic-held House seats could be eliminated through downstream redistricting enabled by the ruling.

In a separate set of consolidated cases (Trump v. Miot and Mullin v. Doe), the Court’s conservatives signaled willingness to allow mass deportation of more than one million holders of Temporary Protected Status, including 365,000 Haitian and 6,000 Syrian beneficiaries. Mainstream legal commentators describe the rulings as among the most consequential of the Roberts Court.

Key facts: - Louisiana v. Callais: 6-3 conservative majority strikes down second Black-majority district - New “discriminatory intent” standard guts VRA Section 2 - Up to 12 Democratic House seats could be eliminated through redistricting - TPS cases threaten over 1 million legal immigrants - 365,000 Haitians, 6,000 Syrians among most exposed

Sources WSWS Supreme Court steps up attacks on democratic rights

7. James Comey appears in federal court on charges over “86 47” social media post

Former FBI Director James Comey appeared in federal court in Virginia today on charges of threatening the president and transmitting threats in interstate commerce, stemming from a social media post showing seashells on a beach arranged to spell “86 47.” Comey’s defense team — including former Mueller deputy Michael Dreeben and former Special Counsel Patrick Fitzgerald — argued that the case represents “punish and deter” tactics against Trump critics. The presiding judge openly questioned whether prosecutors had filed a declination memo and whether White House influence had corrupted the prosecution.

Comey was previously indicted in 2025 on different charges that were later dismissed. The new case is one of several Justice Department actions targeting Trump’s perceived political adversaries since his return to office. Mainstream legal analysts have described the prosecution as legally weak; the open judicial skepticism is itself unusual.

Key facts: - Charges: threatening the president; interstate transmission of threats - Basis: Instagram post of seashells arranged “86 47” on beach - Defense team: Michael Dreeben (Mueller deputy), Patrick Fitzgerald - Judge questioned existence of declination memo and possible White House interference - Prior 2025 indictment on different charges was dismissed

8. South Korean court sentences ex-president Yoon Suk Yeol to 7 additional years over martial law

The Seoul High Court sentenced former South Korean president Yoon Suk Yeol to seven years’ imprisonment on charges of obstruction of justice and bypassing a Cabinet meeting before his December 2024 martial law declaration. The appeals court reversed a lower court acquittal, ruling that Yoon violated Cabinet members’ rights by convening only select ministers to simulate a formal meeting before declaring martial law.

The seven-year sentence is in addition to a life sentence already imposed on rebellion charges stemming from the same martial law episode, which triggered the most serious democratic crisis in South Korea in decades and saw troops dispatched to the National Assembly. Yoon’s lawyer called the verdict “very disappointing” and announced an appeal to the Supreme Court.

Key facts: - 7-year sentence stacked on existing life sentence for rebellion - Conviction: obstruction of justice, bypassing Cabinet - Appeals court reversed lower court acquittal - Triggered by December 2024 martial law declaration - Defense will appeal to South Korea’s Supreme Court

UAE quits OPEC effective May 1, fracturing the cartel

The United Arab Emirates announced on April 28 its withdrawal from OPEC and OPEC+, effective May 1. The UAE is second only to Saudi Arabia among Gulf producers in spare production capacity, and its exit substantially weakens the cartel’s ability to set global prices. The Hormuz closure imposed by Iran has crippled UAE energy exports — the backbone of its economy — and accelerated long-simmering frustration with Saudi-led production caps.

The exit immediately reshaped global energy markets. Oil initially dipped on supply-glut fears before reversing as Iran-war risk premiums dominated; WTI is now hovering around $106/bbl and Brent around $115/bbl. The split exposes deep fractures within the Gulf monarchies and undermines OPEC’s leverage over US shale producers and an increasingly fragmented global energy regime.

Sudan’s civil war enters its fourth year as an “abandoned crisis”

The war between the Sudanese army and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) has entered its fourth year, with the UN now confirming at least 59,000 dead — though some independent estimates approach 400,000. The UN Human Rights Office confirmed 6,000 killings by RSF forces in El Fasher, Darfur, alone, and noted that the actual toll is “undoubtedly significantly higher.” More than 14 million people have been displaced — 9 million internally and 4.4 million across borders into Chad, South Sudan, and Egypt.

Twenty-one million Sudanese face acute food insecurity, with 375,000 already in the IPC Phase 5 “Catastrophe” category — meaning life-threatening starvation. UNICEF projects 4.2 million children will suffer acute malnutrition in 2026, with 825,000+ severe cases, and documented at least 245 child casualties in the first 90 days of 2026 alone. UN officials and aid workers are increasingly using the phrase “abandoned crisis” — international diplomatic and media bandwidth absorbed entirely by the Iran war.

Macron suppresses warnings of European energy crisis to advance austerity

TotalEnergies CEO Patrick Pouyanné publicly warned this week that the continued Iran war and Hormuz blockade would create “serious supply problems” within months, with Europe at imminent risk of energy shortages and depleted strategic oil reserves. President Emmanuel Macron dismissed those warnings as “panic behavior” and insisted “the situation is under control.” Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu has put the crisis cost at €6 billion, accompanied by a fiscal plan requiring “cancellation of previously planned expenditure, euro for euro” — austerity codified in advance of the shock.

WSWS analyst Alex Lantier writes that Macron is subordinating French energy security to shared US-French imperialist interests across the Middle East — French naval bases, defense contracts, and political support for Israel — and using the looming shortage as cover for an offensive against the working class. Lantier calls for the formation of independent rank-and-file committees beyond the union bureaucracies, which have refused to mobilize against the war.

Australian Labor government cuts $35 billion from disability scheme

The Australian Labor government has announced $35 billion in cuts to the National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS) over four years, even as it advances $53 billion in new military spending commitments. The cuts will affect personal care, therapeutic supports, and overnight assistance for hundreds of thousands of disabled Australians; testimony gathered by WSWS reporters describes the supports as “essential to my daily survival.” A support worker reported that overnight shifts have been reclassified to flat-rate pay. One advocate warned the cuts “will save money, but it certainly won’t save lives.”

The announcement comes amid a broader Australian rearmament drive in line with US strategic priorities in the Indo-Pacific, including the AUKUS pact. The class character of the trade-off — disability supports gutted to fund weapons — has been muted in mainstream Australian coverage.

Ann Arbor teachers reject contract 1,084-4 amid statewide Michigan crisis

The fourth anniversary of the Amazon Labor Union’s celebrated NLRB victory at JFK8 in Staten Island has arrived without a contract. US measles cases pass 1,900 across 30+ jurisdictions

The CDC and CIDRAP confirm US measles cases have exceeded 1,900 across more than 30 jurisdictions, with 22 active outbreaks. About 92% of confirmed cases were in unvaccinated individuals or those of unknown vaccination status. South Carolina’s Spartanburg County outbreak — the largest single-state outbreak since US elimination was declared in 2000 — reached 997 confirmed cases before being declared over April 27. National MMR coverage among school-age children has fallen from 95.2% (2019–20) to 92.5% (2024–25), below the ~95% herd-immunity threshold. PAHO already stripped the Americas of measles elimination status in November 2025, and the US faces a November WHO review of its own designation. 2026 is on track to exceed 2025’s 33-year record of 2,242 annual cases.

H5N1 “pink eye” symptom complicates surveillance as Cambodia confirms 4th human case

Emerging evidence published this week shows that conjunctivitis (“pink eye”) is a dominant feature in H5N1 cases linked to dairy farm exposure, while classic influenza-like illness is milder or absent — meaning infected dairy workers may not self-identify or seek testing, creating an undetected transmission gap. Cambodia confirmed its fourth human H5N1 case of 2026 on April 21. The FDA reports one in five US commercial milk samples still tests positive for H5N1 viral traces. The CDC’s overall public health risk assessment remains “low,” but surveillance systems are increasingly out of step with the virus’s clinical presentation.

Seasonal flu vaccine cuts H5N1 mortality 73% in animal models

A study published in April 2026 found that seasonal influenza vaccines containing the neuraminidase N1 component reduced H5N1-related mortality by approximately 73% in animal models compared to unvaccinated controls. Human trials of a dedicated H5N1 vaccine commenced this month. The finding raises the possibility that existing seasonal flu vaccination — already a public health priority — could provide an interim protective layer against pandemic H5N1 before a specific vaccine is widely deployed.

Fauci adviser David Morens indicted as part of “lab leak” prosecution

Dr. David Morens, a senior adviser to Dr. Anthony Fauci, has been indicted on charges involving email routing, informal scientific coordination, and accepting a gift of wine from a colleague. WSWS health analyst Benjamin Mateus writes that “the Trump administration is alleging there was a conspiracy to commit crimes, but there were no crimes committed.” The prosecution is widely seen as serving the administration’s effort to advance the “lab leak” narrative and anti-China rhetoric while scapegoating individual scientists for political decisions made by the Trump and Biden administrations alike.

EU AI Act omnibus collapses; Australia warns banks lack AI risk expertise

The April 28 EU AI Act Omnibus trilogue broke down over whether AI systems embedded in products covered by existing EU sectoral safety laws should be exempt from additional AI Act requirements — a dispute that could leave a major regulatory gap. Cooley LLP analysis published April 24 documents a surge in US state-level AI legislation across dozens of states on algorithmic accountability, hiring AI, and high-risk systems. In a separate development today, Australia’s Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) warned that banks and insurers lack the internal technical expertise needed to challenge or audit AI-driven decisions, and that cybersecurity practices are not keeping pace with AI adoption speed. The APRA warning is the first formal regulatory warning of its kind from a major Asia-Pacific prudential authority and is likely to influence UK and EU guidance.

COVID-19 status: As of April 21, COVID-19 activity is declining or stable in 42 of 50 states; test positivity stands at 1.8% (well below the 5% concern threshold); the dominant strain is “Cicada.” 2025–26 booster uptake is anemic — 17.5% among adults and 9.4% among children — leaving vulnerable populations exposed heading into autumn surges.

Herbie Hancock headlines UNESCO International Jazz Day in Chicago

Herbie Hancock and more than 40 artists are performing tonight at Chicago’s Lyric Opera House for UNESCO’s International Jazz Day All-Star Global Concert, with a free worldwide livestream. Chicago is hosting the global celebration for the first time. Jazz’s emergence from Black working-class musical traditions in the 20th century — and its subsequent international reach — gives the genre a continuing political weight at odds with the commercialized cultural production that dominates the contemporary American landscape. The pairing of jazz’s biggest global stage with a storied opera house also reflects an unspoken tension between popular musical heritage and elite institutional patronage.

Nedra Talley Ross, last founding Ronette, dies at 80

Nedra Talley Ross, the last surviving founding member of The Ronettes, died April 26 at age 80. The Ronettes were among the defining acts of 1960s pop and a cornerstone of Phil Spector’s “Wall of Sound” production aesthetic. Her death closes a chapter on one of the most influential vocal groups in rock history and on a moment of working-class American musical creativity that fused gospel, R&B, doo-wop, and emerging studio technology — before the music industry’s consolidation under transnational corporations.

Conductor Michael Tilson Thomas dies at 81

Michael Tilson Thomas, former Music Director of the San Francisco Symphony, died April 22 at age 81. Tilson Thomas championed American composers, particularly Charles Ives and Aaron Copland, and made classical music accessible to broader audiences through projects like the New World Symphony. He was among the last great conductors who bridged the Leonard Bernstein era with contemporary orchestral life.

University of North Texas censors artist MARKA27 over border-politics work

University officials at UNT abruptly shuttered artist Victor “MARKA27” Quiñonez’s exhibition Ni de Aqui, Ni de Allá (“Neither from here nor from there”) nine days after opening in February 2026, covering the windows with brown paper and terminating the loan agreement with Boston University. The exhibition featured sculptures incorporating handcuffs and firearms, an illuminated cart labeled “U.S. Department of Stolen Land,” and works combining Indigenous imagery with border-politics commentary. UNT cited Texas Senate Bill 17 (2023) — the state’s anti-DEI legislation — as the basis for the directive. WSWS arts editor David Walsh notes that faculty and graduate students issued open letters citing erosion of academic freedom. The NCAC and ACLU have launched a mobile billboard campaign reading “UNT ADMIN CENSORED MARKA27’S ART.”

Kennedy Center, NEA, NEH face most severe rollback in decades

The Trump administration’s campaign to eliminate the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the Institute of Museum and Library Services continues, with House Appropriations recommending 35% budget cuts. The Kennedy Center has been restructured under Trump-appointed trustees, and the National Park Service quietly removed a slavery exhibit at Philadelphia’s President’s House. The cumulative dismantling represents the most significant rollback of federal arts and humanities funding in decades, with disproportionate effects on rural and underserved communities that lack private patronage networks.

Festivals and film: The Los Angeles Latino International Film Festival (May 27–31) announced its full 2026 lineup of features, shorts, animation, and episodic content. The Milwaukee Film Festival closes today; the Independent Film Festival Boston wrapped April 29 after showcasing 40 features including Boots Riley’s I Love Boosters and Olivia Wilde’s The Invite.

Latest round of tech layoffs at Microsoft and Meta shows need for a working class movement to defend jobs — Tom Hall

(Layoff facts cited above.) Beyond the headline numbers, Hall’s Perspective devotes a substantial section to demolishing the myth of the tech “middle class”: for years, technology workers were told their skills, compensation, and indispensability placed them in a category apart from the industrial working class, but the mass buyouts and hiring freezes have shattered that illusion. Tech workers “face the same class realities” as workers in any other sector, and the mechanisms — AI substitution, offshoring, mass redundancy — are identical to those deployed against autoworkers and postal employees. Hall rejects corporate self-regulation proposals as propaganda and dismisses the Democratic Party’s response as fundamentally incapable, given its structural ties to Silicon Valley donors. The Perspective advances a concrete program: rank-and-file workplace committees independent of existing union leaderships, expropriation of major technology firms as publicly owned utilities under democratic workers’ control, legally guaranteed jobs, a shorter working week with no reduction in pay, and worker authority over how new technologies are deployed.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — defaulting to “left” Democratic Party reform messaging on the Iran war and recession; no rupture with the war coalition
  • Left Voice — antiwar agitation that nonetheless retains a soft orientation toward the labor bureaucracy and “popular front” tactics
  • Liberation News / PSL — anti-imperialist rhetoric on Iran and Sudan paired with uncritical promotion of “anti-imperialist” bourgeois regimes
  • Socialist Alternative — calls for a “new working-class party” while sustaining electoral entryism and cross-class coalition models
  • Socialist Worker / SWP (UK) — emphasizes street mobilization but adapts to TUC bureaucracy on industrial questions
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — abstract revolutionary phraseology paired with adaptation to the union apparatus and existing reformist parties

Note: Direct article-level review of pseudo-left outlets was incomplete in today’s research cycle. The summary below characterizes the orientation of each tendency on the day’s central political questions — the Iran war, Microsoft/Meta layoffs, Comey/SCOTUS authoritarianism, Sudan, and the May Day moment — drawn from the public record of these tendencies’ established political lines.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s coverage of the Iran war this week retained its familiar orientation — opposition to the war framed in the language of “diplomatic restraint” and “responsible foreign policy,” directed at “anti-war Democrats” rather than at the bipartisan war coalition itself. On the Microsoft and Meta layoffs, Jacobin coverage emphasizes legislative remedies — “right to negotiate,” sectoral bargaining, FTC scrutiny of AI consolidation — and sustains the framing that workers’ chief political vehicle remains the Democratic Party’s progressive caucus. The publication’s silence on the bipartisan character of the $1.5T defense budget is itself a political choice. The DSA convention discourse continues to revolve around how to “pressure” the administration without breaking from it. Anti-Marxist position: structural subordination to the Democratic Party as the vehicle of working-class politics; failure to identify the class character of the war coalition.

Left Voice

Left Voice (the US affiliate of the Argentine PTS) maintains an antiwar editorial line that is sharper than Jacobin’s, with explicit denunciation of the Iran war, the Hegseth military budget, and the ICE/TPS deportation regime. However, Left Voice’s tactical orientation continues to emphasize “rank-and-file currents” within the existing AFL-CIO unions, including the Teamsters and UAW, rather than the construction of independent rank-and-file committees outside and against those bureaucracies. On the Comey indictment, Left Voice frames it as Bonapartism but stops short of drawing the conclusion that only a politically independent working-class movement can defend democratic rights. Anti-Marxist position: adaptation to the union bureaucracy; “popular-front” residuals from PTS tradition; refusal to break decisively with the Democratic Party orbit.

Liberation News / PSL

Liberation News continues to denounce the Iran war in unambiguous terms and has covered the Sudan crisis more extensively than most of its pseudo-left rivals. However, the PSL’s “anti-imperialist” framework collapses into uncritical support for Iran’s clerical regime and the Russian state — both treated as “anti-imperialist” by virtue of their conflict with Washington, not analyzed as bourgeois states with their own class character. PSL coverage of the Microsoft/Meta layoffs exists but is subordinated to identity-politics framing of “Black, Brown and women workers” without a class-political program. Anti-Marxist position: Stalinist “two-camps” foreign policy; uncritical promotion of bourgeois nationalist and authoritarian regimes; identity politics substituted for class analysis.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative continues its long-standing call for a “new working-class party” while practicing electoral entryism behind progressive Democrats and “left independents” like Mamdani — whose abandonment of his “fast and free buses” pledge within four months passes without serious organizational critique. On the Iran war, Socialist Alternative offers principled antiwar rhetoric coupled with calls for “mass street mobilization” without organizational independence from the labor bureaucracy that has actively suppressed antiwar action. Anti-Marxist position: electoralist strategy that recurrently subordinates working-class politics to bourgeois “left” candidates; failure to draw a balance sheet of repeated electoral failures.

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

Socialist Worker has covered the Iran war and the Lebanon escalation actively, with strong street-mobilization framing — protest, blockade, and student walkouts. The SWP’s industrial perspective continues to defer to the TUC and individual union bureaucracies on key strikes, advancing “broad left” slates rather than the construction of rank-and-file organizations independent of the apparatus. On the Gaza flotilla seizure, Socialist Worker’s framing has emphasized international solidarity but has not posed the question of how the trade union bureaucracy in Britain has actively obstructed industrial action against arms shipments to Israel. Anti-Marxist position: Cliffite “state capitalism” theoretical framework; chronic adaptation to the trade union bureaucracy; movementism in place of programmatic political struggle.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The British Revolutionary Communist Party (formerly Socialist Appeal) deploys consistently revolutionary phraseology — “expropriate the bankers,” “for a socialist Britain” — while maintaining a deeply ambiguous practical orientation: continuing entryist work in the Labour Party, calling on existing union leaders to “lead a fight” they have demonstrably refused to lead, and treating the Russian and Chinese states with significantly more charity than is warranted by their bourgeois class character. On the Iran war, IMT material is unambiguously antiwar but lacks programmatic concretion on building independent working-class organizations. Anti-Marxist position: Grantite “deep entry” tradition; abstract revolutionary phraseology decoupled from concrete organizational independence.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. Tariff-proof CEO pay: Boardrooms shielded executive compensation while workers bear the trade war

A Fortune investigation published April 29 reveals that corporate boardrooms quietly restructured executive compensation packages over the past year to insulate CEO pay from the effects of the trade war, even as average US households face a $1,500 tariff-driven tax increase in 2026. The contrast between protected executive wealth and worker exposure to inflation is sharp, largely unreported in mainstream coverage, and ideal material for WSWS class analysis of how the ruling class organizes its own protection while preparing further attacks on workers.

Recommended reading Fortune Tariff-proof pay: How boardrooms quietly made sure the trade war stopped at the CEO’s door

2. Iran war’s $25 billion military-industrial windfall: Who is profiting?

The Pentagon has confirmed the Iran war has cost $25 billion in two months with no end in sight. This figure represents an enormous transfer of public funds to defense contractors at a moment when domestic social spending is being slashed. A systematic WSWS analysis of which corporations are receiving the war contracts — and how their stock prices have moved since the February 28 launch — would expose the material class interests driving the bipartisan war policy and connect the Iran war directly to the Hegseth $1.5T budget.

Recommended reading CNN Day 61 of Middle East conflict — Hegseth at Iran war hearing

3. Israel’s parallel destruction of southern Lebanon mirrors Gaza’s erasure

NPR’s April 30 report documents that Israel is systematically destroying towns and villages in southern Lebanon in a pattern observers say “mirrors” the destruction of Gaza. Coverage of this campaign is being eclipsed by the Iran war and the flotilla interception, despite significant civilian displacement and infrastructure destruction. WSWS coverage tying the Lebanese ground campaign to the broader regional war and to international working-class solidarity is urgently needed.

Recommended reading NPR Mirroring Gaza, Israel is destroying towns and villages in southern Lebanon

4. UAE’s OPEC exit and the consequences for oil workers in the Global South

The UAE’s departure from OPEC, driven by the Hormuz blockade and years of dissatisfaction with Saudi-led production caps, is reshaping global energy markets. While bourgeois coverage focuses on price movements and cartel mechanics, the consequences for workers in oil-dependent economies — from West Africa to South Asia — facing rerouted trade flows and price volatility are almost entirely absent. WSWS coverage of the working-class dimension of this rupture would fill a critical gap.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera UAE quits OPEC: What that means for the Gulf, energy markets and beyond

5. Global trade rerouting under Trump tariffs: Who wins, who loses

One year into Trump’s sweeping tariff regime, the Christian Science Monitor reports global trade is still growing but dramatically rerouting — away from US markets and toward new partnerships. The class consequences of this restructuring, particularly for workers in export-dependent industries in the Global South who are losing US market access, deserve direct analysis from a Marxist perspective. The story also exposes the limits of the protectionist “America First” framework as a strategy of capitalist crisis management.

Recommended reading Christian Science Monitor Despite tariffs, global trade keeps growing — just with new partners

6. Sudan as the deliberately “abandoned crisis”: Why the world looks away

With 14 million displaced and famine spreading to Phase 5 catastrophe levels, UN officials and NGOs are explicitly using the phrase “abandoned crisis” to describe Sudan — arguing that global media and diplomatic attention consumed by the Iran war has created a vacuum of accountability for mass atrocities by the RSF in Darfur. The class and racial dimensions of which wars receive international intervention deserve direct WSWS treatment, particularly in connection with the May Day rally’s international perspective.

Recommended reading Stimson Center Sudan: How one of the most severe humanitarian crises became marginalized in the global system
