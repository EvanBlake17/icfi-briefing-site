1. Russia kills 24 in Kyiv apartment strike, including three children, in 1,567-projectile barrage

Russia launched what Ukrainian officials described as the largest combined missile-and-drone barrage since the start of the invasion. Between May 13 and May 14, Moscow fired 1,567 total projectiles at Ukrainian targets — 675 one-way attack drones, 29 Kh-101 cruise missiles, 12 Iskander/S-400 missiles, plus reconnaissance drones. A cruise missile struck a nine-story apartment block in Kyiv’s Darnytskyi district, killing 24 people, including three children born in 2008, 2010 and 2013, and wounding 48 others. Kyiv held an official day of mourning on Friday. The escalation followed by days the expiry of a three-day, US-brokered Victory Day “ceasefire” that had no enforcement mechanism, monitoring body, or dispute resolution process — a structural defect analysts had flagged in advance.

Key facts:

  • Ukrainian air defense intercepted 652 drones (94% rate) but only 7% of incoming missiles; DTEK energy infrastructure and a UAV manufacturer were also hit
  • Zelenskyy stated the recovered cruise missile was built in the second quarter of 2026, telling officials this proves “Russia is still importing components and resources for missile production in circumvention of global sanctions”; he ordered the military to “prepare possible formats for our response”
  • 180+ sites across Ukraine were damaged since Wednesday, including 50+ residential buildings
  • In the week between the ceasefire’s collapse and the Kyiv attack, Russia struck an oil refinery in Ryazan and Ukraine struck Russian gas facilities and residential buildings; Putin’s May 10 offer of “direct talks with Zelenskyy in Moscow or a neutral country” was not accepted by Kyiv

2. Trump sends Iran nuclear proposal via Pakistan as ceasefire on “massive life support”

The US-Iran war — formally renamed and “concluded” with the end of “Operation Epic Fury” on May 6 — is again on the verge of resumption. The April 8 ceasefire that ended large-scale combat remains nominally in place, but Trump publicly described it on May 11 as on “massive life support.” On May 16, the administration transmitted a new nuclear framework to Tehran via Pakistani mediators demanding a complete moratorium on uranium enrichment. Ali Shamkhani, adviser to Supreme Leader Khamenei, replied that Iran would sign a deal in exchange for the “quick removal of all financial sanctions” but added pointedly: “He speaks of an olive branch, but we see only barbed wire.”

Key facts:

  • Iran is holding parallel nuclear talks with the European E3 in Istanbul on May 15–16, hedging Tehran’s options against US escalation
  • Key sticking points: uranium enrichment rights, freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz, sanctions relief sequencing, and Lebanon/Hezbollah ceasefire terms
  • The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since Iran’s March 4 announcement following the February 28 US-Israeli air campaign that killed Supreme Leader Khamenei; roughly 25% of global seaborne oil transits through it
  • American consumers have absorbed a $37 billion hit from spiking fuel prices since the war began — more than $284 per household
  • The US House voted 212–212 on May 15, rejecting a war powers resolution to end Trump’s Iran attacks; the Pentagon is reportedly considering renaming “Operation Epic Fury” to circumvent the 60-day War Powers Act clock — a maneuver that would effectively nullify constitutional congressional oversight of war
  • At a May 15 briefing, US Central Command commander Admiral Brad Cooper denied any “corroborated” civilian casualties from US strikes — directly contradicting Senator Kirsten Gillibrand, who cited 22 bombed schools on the Senate floor

3. Trump-Xi Beijing summit ends with no agreements on Taiwan, Iran, or Hormuz

Trump’s three-day state visit to Beijing concluded on May 15 with no agreements on the central issues raised — Taiwan, the Iran war, or the Strait of Hormuz — and notably no joint communiqué, the standard diplomatic instrument for recording shared positions between heads of state. Analysts described the outcome as “underwhelming” and “meagre.” Xi Jinping opened the summit with a public warning that mishandling Taiwan could cause “clashes and even conflicts.” Trump told reporters en route to Alaska he had made “no commitment either way” on a pending $14 billion arms sale to Taiwan; the decision is now expected within days of his return to Washington.

In his perspective in today’s WSWS, Andre Damon argues that the ceremonial trappings of the summit masked a comprehensive diplomatic failure rooted in deeper structural contradictions: “No diplomatic framework can resolve the underlying contradiction between US imperialism and the rise of China within the capitalist system.” Trump’s approval rating stood at 34 percent at the time of the visit; the US national debt has reached 100 percent of GDP after three credit downgrades.

Key facts:

  • Trade concessions: China agreed to purchase 200 Boeing aircraft, US soybeans, oil and LNG; the earlier US-China tariff truce (US tariffs at 30%, China at 10%) remains in place
  • Xi’s foreign ministry called the summit “historical” and said Xi will visit the United States in autumn 2026
  • Trump said Xi offered to “help” on Iran but Trump stated he “didn’t ask” Xi to pressure Tehran on the Strait of Hormuz
  • Following the recent visit of KMT opposition leader to Beijing, China announced new economic incentives for Taiwan including resumed direct flights and eased tourism — part of a parallel “soft” track to military pressure

4. Bolivia paralyzed by 11-day COB general strike as fuel prices triple

Bolivia’s Central Obrera Boliviana (COB) labor federation has now entered the eleventh day of an indefinite general strike, with indigenous groups, miners, teachers and farmers joining and at least 70 roadblocks erected across the country. La Paz, El Alto, Cochabamba, Oruro and Sucre have all been brought to a halt. The trigger was the new government of President Rodrigo Paz — successor to Evo Morales’s MAS — ending fuel subsidies, which sent diesel from 3.72 bolivianos to 9.80 bolivianos per liter (approximately $5.40/gallon), a 163% overnight increase, with premium petrol jumping similarly.

COB Secretary-General Mario Argollo declared: “Starting today, a general, indefinite and active strike is declared, until the government understands the people’s demands.” Demands include a 20% minimum wage increase (current floor: 3,300 bolivianos, or $477/month), restoration of fuel subsidies, and the resignation of President Paz.

Key facts:

  • Bolivia is experiencing its worst economic crisis in 40 years, including a severe currency shortage rooted in collapsed gas exports and IMF-style austerity demands
  • The Paz government — a “left-wing” successor to MAS — is now facing demands for resignation from the very social movements that historically backed the left, exposing what the WSWS calls the bankruptcy of national-reformist alternatives to imperialism
  • Major regional press is treating the protests as a domestic political crisis; almost none have framed the events as a working-class response to IMF-backed austerity

5. Supreme Court preserves mifepristone telehealth and mail access 7-2

The US Supreme Court ruled on May 14 by a 7-2 margin that mifepristone — the drug used in the majority of US abortions — can continue to be prescribed via telehealth and distributed by mail, blocking a Fifth Circuit appeals court order that would have reverted prescriptions to in-person-only rules nationwide effective immediately. The decision is described as preserving access “for now”; the underlying Louisiana v. FDA case remains active and the legal architecture for further restriction has not been dismantled.

Justices Clarence Thomas and Samuel Alito dissented. Thomas argued in his dissent that mailing abortion medications violates the 1873 Comstock Act, an anti-obscenity statute that has been effectively unenforced for decades but that the Christian right and the Heritage Foundation have explicitly targeted as a vehicle for a future federal abortion ban without need for new legislation. The dissent itself functions as a public roadmap for that strategy.

Key facts:

  • Medication abortion now accounts for the majority of all US abortions; telehealth and mail-order distribution dramatically expanded after the Dobbs decision
  • The ruling concerns only the procedural posture of the case (whether the appeals court order could take effect during litigation), not the merits, which remain pending
  • The Trump administration’s Justice Department had filed conflicting briefs in earlier stages of the litigation

7. Hungary’s Tisza party forming government with two-thirds supermajority after Orbán defeat

Hungary’s post-Orbán transition is now in its government-formation stage. Péter Magyar’s Tisza party won 141 of 199 parliamentary seats (53.6% of the vote) in the April 12 election, while Viktor Orbán’s Fidesz collapsed to 55 seats (37.8%), ending 16 years of nationalist, pro-Russia rule. Voter turnout was 79.6%, the highest since Hungary’s first post-communist election in 1990. Tisza’s two-thirds supermajority enables constitutional amendments — both to undo Fidesz-era restrictions and, potentially, to install new ones.

Magyar told supporters on election night the victory was “visible from the moon and every window in Hungary.” European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen declared: “Hungary has chosen Europe.” Magyar has pledged to reset EU relations, deepen Central European ties, and reverse Orbán-era democratic backsliding.

Key facts:

  • The Tisza party draws from a broadly pro-EU, market-oriented voter base; its electoral program centers on rule-of-law restoration and re-integration with Brussels rather than any reversal of the social inequalities entrenched under Orbán
  • Analysts at Chatham House and elsewhere warn that “Orbánism” — the institutional and legal infrastructure of one-party rule, including captured judiciary, media-ownership concentration, and electoral district gerrymandering — may survive the change in government
  • The Magyar government’s first foreign policy gesture is expected to be alignment with EU posture on Ukraine; this represents a sharp reorientation of Hungary’s NATO/Russia stance

CIA Director John Ratcliffe traveled to Havana on May 14 and publicly met with Cuban Interior Minister Lázaro Álvarez Casas and intelligence service leaders — the first publicly documented visit of a CIA director to Cuba. Sri Lanka extends draconian state of emergency for sixth consecutive month

The Sri Lankan parliament has approved a sixth consecutive monthly extension of the state of emergency — originally declared after Cyclone Ditwah in November 2025 — alongside Essential Public Services regulations that criminalize public-sector strikes with jail sentences. Five months after the cyclone, displaced persons remain in camps and compensation has not been paid. The real function of the extensions, as the WSWS notes, is the suppression of working-class opposition to IMF austerity under the Dissanayake government. Since the US-Israeli war on Iran began, fuel tariffs in Sri Lanka have risen 40%, gas 31%, and electricity 32%; the cost-of-living index jumped from 2.2% to 5.4% in April. The country has reimposed fuel rationing and declared Wednesdays a public holiday to conserve diesel. Opposition parties either abstained from or supported the extensions, and trade unions pledged non-interference.

Harvey Weinstein rape retrial ends in mistrial after jury deadlocks

Harvey Weinstein’s rape retrial in New York ended on May 15 in a mistrial after jurors deadlocked, unable to reach a unanimous verdict. The retrial followed the 2024 overturning of his original 2020 conviction by New York’s top court on procedural grounds — specifically, the trial court’s admission of testimony about prior bad acts not charged in the indictment. Prosecutors have not yet announced whether they will seek a third trial. Weinstein remains imprisoned in California on a separate 2023 rape conviction; the New York case involves separate charges. The outcome is a setback for an early central case of the #MeToo movement and reopens questions about how the bourgeois legal system handles cases turning on power, dependence, and the conditions of consent within labor relationships.

Nexteer auto workers in Saginaw demand strike vote after UAW contract rejected 96.2%

US measles cases reach 1,884 across 40 jurisdictions

As of the CDC’s latest weekly update, 1,884 confirmed measles cases have been reported by 40 US jurisdictions in 2026, with 27 new outbreaks logged this year. 93% of cases (1,761) are outbreak-associated. South Carolina’s Spartanburg County cluster alone accounts for roughly 990 cases — more than the entire 2024 total. The 2026 cumulative count is already on pace to surpass the full-year 2025 figure of 2,288 cases, which itself was the highest annual count in decades. The resurgence is driven by sustained, ideologically organized vaccine hesitancy and the dismantling of public-health infrastructure — both intensified under the Trump administration’s RFK Jr. HHS.

First human H5N5 infection reported in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine has published the first documented human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). The patient, a 75-year-old woman with Waldenström macroglobulinemia, developed severe acute respiratory distress syndrome after exposure to birds; genomic analysis confirmed Eurasian lineage clade 6 with a transient, low-frequency mammalian-associated mutation. H5N5 is a neuraminidase subtype distinct from the more common H5N1. The case is significant because it raises concern that immunocompromised patients may be especially vulnerable to novel avian influenza spillovers even when upper-respiratory testing is initially negative — a diagnostic gap with implications for surveillance.

WHO logs 13 zoonotic flu cases in Q1 2026 including fatal H5N1 in Bangladesh child

The World Health Organization tracked 13 human cases of avian and swine influenza in Q1 2026. Among them was a previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child from Bangladesh, alongside three additional H5N1 cases in Cambodia. There remains no evidence of sustained human-to-human transmission, but the slow drip of cases across Asia keeps pandemic-preparedness concerns elevated in a moment when, as Benjamin Mateus argues in today’s WSWS, capitalist governments are systematically dismantling the surveillance, vaccine, and research infrastructure built up over previous decades.

BA.3.2 “Cicada” COVID variant in 31 states with vaccine-evasion signal

The heavily mutated BA.3.2 (“Cicada”) COVID-19 variant has been detected in at least 31 US states. CDC ensemble modeling as of May 12 shows COVID declining in 20 states and growing in 1, with ED visits projected at roughly 0.12% nationally for the week ending May 16. In lab studies, BA.3.2 shows reduced susceptibility to the current JN.1-targeting 2025–2026 vaccines, prompting WHO’s TAG-CO-VAC to convene this month to deliberate on next-season vaccine antigen composition. The variant represents the kind of recurrent immune-escape evolution that an actually functioning public-health system would address through universal, free, regularly updated boosters — and that the current US system, oriented around individual purchase, will not.

White House reverses, will require pre-deployment review of frontier AI models

The White House announced on May 15 that the US government will evaluate cutting-edge AI models before public deployment — a sharp reversal of the administration’s earlier hands-off posture. Microsoft, Google DeepMind, and xAI have committed to sharing their latest state-of-the-art systems with the government for national security testing. The policy marks the first formal pre-deployment review mandate from the current administration and could materially affect how frontier labs schedule their model releases. The move clearly reflects the integration of AI into the war planning apparatus exposed by the Pentagon’s recent ultimatum to Anthropic over safety guardrails; the “review” framework is being constructed around military-intelligence access, not democratic oversight.

Cerebras Systems surges 68% on Nasdaq debut to $95 billion valuation

Wafer-scale AI chipmaker Cerebras Systems surged 68% on its Nasdaq debut, reaching a market capitalization of approximately $95 billion — one of the largest US tech IPOs in recent years. The listing validates Cerebras’s wafer-scale chip architecture as a serious competitor to Nvidia’s GPU-dominated AI compute market and signals that investor appetite for AI infrastructure plays remains robust even as the broader equity market wobbles.

Gallup: 71% of Americans oppose AI data centers in their communities

A new Gallup survey finds that 71% of US adults oppose siting an AI data center in their local area, with 48% strongly opposed, citing energy, water, and noise concerns. The polling reflects a growing local-resistance movement that stands to complicate the multi-hundred-billion-dollar US data center build-out announced by hyperscalers and the federal government. The numbers suggest the political coalition for AI expansion is concentrated in capital and the national security state; the population it is being imposed on is overwhelmingly hostile.

Cannes 2026 in full swing under Park Chan-wook jury, Travolta and Jackson honorary Palme d’Or

The 79th Cannes Film Festival continued through May 15 with politics, AI, and the retreat of major Hollywood studios as defining themes. Jury president Park Chan-wook used his opening press conference to reject the depoliticization of the festival: “Art and politics are not concepts that are in conflict with each other. One cannot disqualify a film on the pretext that it has a political message. Just as one cannot reject a film because it would not be political enough.” Honorary Palme d’Or awards have gone to Peter Jackson (announced earlier) and, on May 15, to John Travolta, ahead of the world premiere of his directorial debut Propeller One-Way Night Coach (Apple TV release May 29). The decision to honor Travolta — a Hollywood figure of middling artistic substance and well-known association with Scientology — sits awkwardly with the festival’s political framing.

Several films in competition engage directly with the present world crisis. Pegah Ahangarani’s Rehearsals for a Revolution — a film about political repression in Iran — has assumed unusually urgent significance given the US-Israeli war on Iran and the ongoing repression by the Tehran regime. Two large French productions position themselves within the country’s contested historical memory: a Charles de Gaulle biopic by former diplomat Antonin Baudry, and a film on Resistance leader Jean Moulin by Laszlo Nemes (of Son of Saul fame). Each tests how French national mythology is mobilized at a moment when European militarization is being legitimated by appeals to that same mythology.

Pawlikowski’s “Fatherland” lands as Cannes critics’ centerpiece

Polish director Paweł Pawlikowski’s Fatherland — set in the summer of 1949 during the early Cold War, following a father and daughter on a journey through the divisions of postwar Germany — has emerged as a critics’ centerpiece in early Cannes coverage. Pawlikowski’s previous films (Ida, Cold War) have shown a serious engagement with how the political settlement of mid-twentieth-century Europe shaped individual fates; the appearance of Fatherland at a moment of renewed German rearmament and the rehabilitation of Cold War frameworks in mainstream European politics gives the work an unintended contemporary urgency.

Pulitzer winner Brian Goldstone calls homelessness the product of capitalism’s “success”

Brian Goldstone — winner of the 2026 Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction for There Is No Place for Us: Working and Homeless in America — gave an extended interview to the WSWS in which he argued that family homelessness in the United States is not a product of economic failure but of economic “success” — cities’ prosperity actively displacing the low-wage workers who built it. His Atlanta-based reporting found “entire business models designed to profit from their hardship,” including Blackstone-owned extended-stay hotels charging double previous apartment rents to displaced families. Federal minimum wage has stagnated at $7.25 since 2016; rents have outpaced income by 325% since 1985; 12 million renter households now spend more than half their income on housing. In Atlanta, 93% of homeless families are Black. Goldstone advocates “social housing” — permanently removing housing from speculative markets, with rents tied to income, citing Finland and Vienna as models.

Death of Claudine Longet, 84

The French-American singer and actress Claudine Longet has died at 84. Longet’s career as a chanteuse in the 1960s — recordings with A&M Records, appearances on the Andy Williams Show and films including Blake Edwards’s The Party — was largely eclipsed in public memory by the 1976 trial following the fatal shooting of her partner, Olympic skier Spider Sabich. The trial’s outcome — a conviction only for negligent homicide and a 30-day sentence — became a recurring point of reference in cultural commentary on the unequal application of criminal law. Longet’s musical work, regardless of the biographical controversy, occupies a small but distinctive corner of the post-bossa-nova vocal idiom of the late 1960s.

CIA director’s trip to Havana paves way for regime change operation — Andrea Lobo

Lobo argues that Ratcliffe’s unprecedented public visit to Havana was not a diplomatic overture but staged political theater designed to establish that “Cuba bears responsibility for whatever comes next.” The DOJ’s signaling of plans to indict Raúl Castro over the 1996 Brothers to the Rescue shootdown, timed to within hours of Ratcliffe’s departure, confirms the regime-change architecture. The article sharply criticizes Latin American left governments — Lula, Sheinbaum, Petro — for inaction in the face of the intensified US blockade, and criticizes the Cuban government itself for welcoming the CIA director while signaling willingness to surrender what remains of its economic sovereignty. The article’s framing of the US humanitarian gesture ($100 million through Catholic charities = ~$10 per Cuban) as propaganda preparing the population for “rescue” by regime change is the strongest analytical contribution unavailable in bourgeois coverage.

Sri Lankan government extends draconian state of emergency — Saman Gunadasa

Beyond the summary above, Gunadasa’s piece is significant for documenting the role of opposition parties and the trade unions in the extension. The SJB and other opposition formations either abstained or supported the extensions; trade unions pledged non-interference with the government. The article calls for independent workplace committees and socialist organization in concrete connection to the strike wave the emergency was designed to preempt. The role of the JVP-led NPP government — formerly an “anti-establishment” formation — in deploying the security architecture of its predecessors is treated as a textbook case of pseudo-left integration into the bourgeois state apparatus.

Germany’s May 8 school strike underscores the necessity of a socialist perspective — Joshua Seubert

The article documents the third nationwide German school strike against conscription and militarization since December 2025: ~45,000 students across 150+ cities on May 8 (the 81st anniversary of Nazi Germany’s defeat), with 10,000 in Berlin alone. Concrete resistance data: 28% of 18-year-old men ignored mandatory pre-muster questionnaires since January 2026; conscientious objector applications jumped from 2,998 (2024) to 3,867 (2025), with 2,656 submitted in Q1 2026 alone. The article’s polemical core is its critique of the Stalinist SDAJ for refusing to connect German conscription resistance to the specific wars (Gaza, Ukraine, Iran) German militarism is preparing for — a refusal that, the article argues, leaves the youth movement disarmed in front of the very war drive it is mobilizing against.

California DSA tells workers to vote for a billionaire Democrat — Jacob Crosse

The California DSA endorsed hedge fund billionaire Tom Steyer in the Democratic gubernatorial primary while explicitly acknowledging that his wealth derived from worker exploitation, including investments in private prisons and Whitehaven Coal (through Farallon Capital). The DSA endorsement called Steyer “the most progressive of the current viable candidates” while simultaneously identifying him as a “conscious representative of the capitalist class” — a formulation that captures the DSA’s role as a Democratic Party pressure valve channeling working-class opposition toward billionaire candidates. Steyer has spent approximately $546 million across prior campaigns; his platform centers on “reinventing capitalism.” He entered the race following Eric Swalwell’s withdrawal after sexual assault allegations.

Australian study reveals tunnelling workers’ concerns over silica dust exposure — Jenny Campbell

A University of Sydney study of 290 tunnelling workers found 62.5% face barriers to dust control practices, in an industry where previous research estimated 20-30 of every 2,000 Queensland tunnel workers would develop lung cancer and 200-300 silicosis. Workers describe a “culture of fear and hostility” — raising safety concerns risks blacklisting, air-quality monitors are placed in “clean areas” or removed during dusty operations, and inspectors give advance notice so companies can prepare facades. Queensland authorities withheld dust monitoring data from 2015-2023 citing potential damage to corporate reputation. The article criticizes the AWU and CFMEU for channeling concerns into parliamentary lobbying rather than supporting worker action.

The fraud of Labor’s “historic” housing policies in Australian budget — Oscar Grenfell

Grenfell shows the Albanese government’s flagship housing measure — aid to 75,000 additional homebuyers over a decade — amounts to ~7,500 annually, or 0.026% of Australia’s 28 million population. Treasury modeling itself projects that the changes may reduce available housing by 35,000 properties while slowing price increases only marginally (6% to 4% annually). The same budget includes $63.8 billion in cuts over four years, more than half targeting disability support programs affecting up to 300,000 people, while preserving negative gearing substantially intact and directing $10.2 billion annually in reduced regulatory costs to business.

What science knows about Andes hantavirus and why governments ignore it — Benjamin Mateus

Mateus uses the 2026 MV Hondius cruise ship outbreak — the first documented shipborne Andes virus event — as a case study in deliberate government defunding of pandemic infrastructure. Andes virus is the only hantavirus with confirmed person-to-person transmission; the 2018-19 Epuyén outbreak had an R-number of 2.12; the case fatality rate is 38-40% with no vaccine or antiviral. The NIH terminated the CREID network studying Andes virus in June 2025; the Trump administration eliminated the STOP Spillover program; the CDC’s Vessel Sanitation Program was gutted in April 2025; the US withdrew from WHO in January 2025. The article’s conclusion — that capitalist governments have “systematically subordinated objective science to the profit interests of financial oligarchy” — is a useful frame for the broader public-health crisis.

South Asia, the Iran war and the bankruptcy of bourgeois nationalism — Deepal Jayasekera and Dilaxshan Mahalingam

The 2026 May Day Online Rally speech argues South Asia is “being dragged into World War III” through governments’ complicity with US-Israeli operations against Iran. Sri Lanka’s claimed neutrality is exposed: President Dissanayake coordinated with US and Israeli embassies regarding Iranian vessels, with one captured after a US submarine attack killing 100+ sailors. Since late February, Sri Lankan fuel prices rose 26-33%, kerosene 30%, bus fares 12%; fuel rationing was reimposed and Wednesdays declared public holidays. The opposition SJB calls for condemnation of “both sides”; Tamil parties support the war effort; Modi aligns with Washington. All variants of bourgeois nationalism are exposed.

The role of Australian imperialism in the war on Iran and China — Cheryl Crisp

Crisp’s May Day speech documents Australia’s central integration into US military operations through Pine Gap and other joint facilities providing targeting and intelligence for strikes on Iran. Nearly 70% of Australians oppose Gaza operations and 72% oppose Iran strikes, yet Labor governs in lockstep with Washington while cutting $35 billion from the National Disability Insurance Scheme and increasing military spending from $48 billion to $60 billion annually, with the $368 billion submarine program continuing. Both major parties received historic low vote shares in 2025.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s three most significant May 14 features illustrate the publication’s reliably pro-Democratic orientation refracted through a thin “democratic socialist” gloss. Chris Rabb, “Philly Wants to Send a Socialist to Congress” (jacobin.com) is a self-promoting platform piece for Rabb’s Pennsylvania congressional run on the Democratic ballot line. The “socialism” advanced is the AOC-Sanders variant: rhetorical denunciation of “both parties” coupled with active operation inside one of them, the very party currently underwriting the Iran war and the Trump administration’s economic agenda. Jivan Sobrinho-Wheeler, “Corporate Democrats Still Don’t Understand Housing” (jacobin.com) advances a market-friendly “build more housing” line whose horizon is the regulation of capital, not the decommodification advocated by Goldstone in the WSWS interview noted above. The article presents the housing crisis as a problem of insufficiently enlightened Democratic policy rather than as a structural product of housing’s status as a commodity. Nicholas Liu, “How Mohammed Mossadegh’s Liberal Anti-Imperialism Collapsed” (jacobin.com) is the most theoretically substantive of the three: a useful historical narrative of the 1953 coup and its aftermath, but one that pointedly avoids drawing the obvious lesson for the present US-Iran war — namely, that liberal-bourgeois anti-imperialism is incapable of withstanding imperialist counter-revolution and that only an independent working-class movement can. The omission is structural: Jacobin’s editorial framework cannot draw that conclusion without indicting its own electoral project. Assessment: business as usual — historical material as ornament around an unbroken commitment to working through the Democratic Party.

Liberation News / PSL

PSL’s central output of the period is its Nakba Day statement: “On the 78th anniversary of the Nakba, we commit to intensifying the struggle to end US aid to Israel” (liberationnews.org). The article correctly identifies the 1948 ethnic cleansing as the founding act of the Israeli state and Palestinian resistance as a continuous historical thread. What it does not contain is any orientation toward independent working-class politics, any critique of the Democratic Party’s role in arming the genocide, or any class analysis of the layer of “progressive” Democratic politicians around whom PSL’s protest coalitions cluster. The earlier-week piece “Imperialist-backed forces are biggest threat to Mali’s peace and stability” repackages Russian and Burkinabé junta talking points as anti-imperialism without analyzing the class character of any of the warring factions. Assessment: moral denunciation of imperialism without any program for working-class power — a campist orientation toward “anti-Western” regimes regardless of their actual class basis, combined in the US with protest politics oriented around mobilizing Democratic Party constituencies.

Socialist Worker / SWP UK

The Socialist Workers Party’s daily output reflects the IST’s standard division of labor between anti-racism mobilization and trade-union work. “Expert says ‘government rhetoric feeds Islamophobia and racism’” (socialistworker.co.uk) treats the Labour government’s rhetoric as an aberration to be corrected by pressure, rather than as the predictable orientation of British imperialism in a period of intensified war and immigration crackdown. “Lambeth council workers fight cuts to vital health services” (socialistworker.co.uk) is a sympathetic report on London council austerity resistance that channels worker anger back into the existing union machinery (UNISON, GMB) rather than building rank-and-file organization independent of the bureaucracy that has enforced cuts in previous rounds. “Meet the people at the sharp end of the housing crisis” (socialistworker.co.uk) is human-interest reporting without programmatic content. Assessment: unbroken accommodation to the Labour Party and the existing trade-union apparatus, dressed up in militant rhetoric. The SWP’s silence on the Iran war during this 24-hour window — given Britain’s direct integration via Cyprus bases — is itself significant.

Communist Red / RCP-IMT

The newly relaunched IMT-aligned site continues its centenary coverage of the 1926 General Strike: “The General Strike in Scotland” (Nish Gora) and “The General Strike in London” (Simi Ojo) appeared on May 15 and 16 respectively. The historical material is competent reportage, but the political function is to displace contemporary working-class struggle into safely distant historical memory. The contemporary intervention of note is “Greens surge in London: What next?” (Tommaso Ugo Verga and Khaled Malachi, May 14, communist.red), which gives a soft endorsement to working with Green left-currents — a tactical adaptation to an electoral formation that combines middle-class environmentalism with rhetorical hostility to Labour and no break from bourgeois property relations. Assessment: combination of romantic labour-movement nostalgia with a search for “movement of the moment” electoral vehicles — precisely the orientation Ted Grant’s tradition has long mistaken for revolutionary entrism.
