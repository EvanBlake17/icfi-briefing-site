1. Ebola declared a global health emergency as outbreak spreads to Goma, Bunia and Kampala

The World Health Organization’s declaration of a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) on May 16 has been followed by rapid escalation. The outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo’s Ituri Province — the 17th in the DRC, arriving just five months after the previous one ended — has now seeded cases in the cities of Bunia, Goma and Kampala, Uganda. WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus has voiced alarm at the “scale and speed” of transmission.

The pathogen is the Bundibugyo ebolavirus, a rare strain for which no approved vaccine or specific treatment exists — existing vaccines target only the Zaire strain. The virus killed between 30 and 50 percent of those infected in prior outbreaks. WHO officials concede a candidate vaccine is six to nine months away. The epicenter, destabilized by Islamic State-aligned and other armed groups, hosts 1.9 million people already in need of humanitarian aid, preventing effective contact tracing.

This is the subject of today’s WSWS Perspective by Evan Blake, who applies Engels’s concept of “social murder,” arguing the catastrophe is the product of imperialist plunder and the Trump administration’s destruction of global disease surveillance — including the $100 million STOP Spillover program — which allowed the virus to circulate undetected in Ituri for roughly six weeks.

Key facts: - As of May 19, official figures stood at 543 suspected cases and at least 131 deaths; the WSWS, citing more recent data, reports over 650 suspected cases and more than 150 deaths - Africa CDC Director-General Jean Kaseya said he was in “panic mode,” citing the absence of medicines, PPE and manufacturing capacity: “We don’t have manufacturing for PPE” - MSF and the US CDC have deployed teams; health workers report being underprotected and undertrained - The Trump administration has pledged “roughly $13 million” toward the response while invoking Title 42 to bar non-citizens from the DRC, Uganda and South Sudan

2. Famine officially confirmed in Gaza as children at risk of death triple to 43,400

The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) has officially confirmed famine in Gaza for the first time — the first confirmed famine in the Middle East. The World Food Programme and UN humanitarian agencies report that conditions have collapsed since the prior IPC analysis, with the systematic restriction of food, fuel and medical supplies driving mass starvation in a population subjected to more than two years of Israeli military assault.

The figures mark a catastrophic deterioration over a matter of weeks. The number of children at severe risk of death from malnutrition by the end of June has tripled, from 14,100 to 43,400. The number of pregnant and breastfeeding women facing dangerous malnutrition has likewise tripled, from 17,000 to 55,000 by mid-2026.

Key facts: - Funding for humanitarian operations in Gaza stands at less than 10 percent of the resources required for 2026 - NGO workers have been killed in incidents that triggered the suspension of health and water services - Restrictions on generators, engine oil and spare parts are causing widespread failures of water, sanitation and medical systems - Israel continues to control more than 50 percent of Gaza while conducting simultaneous operations on the Lebanese and Iranian fronts

The 21-hour Islamabad Talks of April 11–12 between Vice President JD Vance and Iranian officials failed to produce an agreement; Trump has publicly described Iran as “dying to make a deal,” a claim Iran denies, insisting talks are not even occurring.

6. Sudan’s war enters fourth year as the world’s largest humanitarian catastrophe

Sudan’s civil war, now in its fourth year, is the largest humanitarian catastrophe on the planet, with an estimated 400,000+ dead and 14 million displaced. The conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces has shattered the country’s infrastructure and produced famine conditions across multiple regions, yet receives a fraction of the international attention given to Ukraine or Gaza.

The war is fueled by external weapons flows — from the UAE, Egypt, Russia and others — with no serious international intervention. Both the RSF commander and the army chief have publicly signaled readiness to fight for decades, with conflict-resolution estimates ranging from 2033 to 2040.

Key facts: - 29 million people — 62% of the population — lack sufficient food; 61.7% are acutely food insecure - An estimated 70–80% of health facilities in conflict zones are non-functional; 65% of the population lacks adequate medical care - 17 million women and girls need humanitarian assistance amid systematic sexual violence; the WFP projects 33.7 million needing aid in 2026

Trump shelves AI safety executive order after tech industry lobbying

President Trump postponed a planned executive order that would have required pre-release government security testing of frontier AI models, following 11th-hour lobbying by Elon Musk, Mark Zuckerberg and former AI czar David Sacks, according to the Washington Post. The reversal demonstrates the degree to which the most powerful technology oligarchs — several of them personally aligned with Trump — are able to dictate federal regulatory policy directly.

US measles cases surpass 1,950, on pace to break 2025 record

As of May 21, the United States has recorded 1,952 confirmed measles cases across 29 outbreaks, with 93% outbreak-associated and 92% of patients unvaccinated or of unknown vaccination status. With the full-year 2025 total at 2,288 cases, the country is on pace to exceed last year’s record with seven months remaining. The US elimination status, declared in 2000, is now under serious threat if transmission continues at this rate.

The resurgence is the predictable outcome of collapsing vaccination coverage and the official sanction given to anti-vaccine politics within the federal public-health apparatus — a domestic counterpart to the dismantling of global disease surveillance that produced the Ebola crisis abroad.

Arts and culture

The dominant cultural event of the past 24 hours is the spectacle of a single Christie’s evening sale generating $1.1 billion, a stark illustration of the financialization of art in an era of mass starvation and war. The auction set a record $181.2 million for a Jackson Pollock and established new records for works by Constantin Brancusi, much of it drawn from the collection of the late billionaire media mogul S.I. Newhouse, whose net worth was estimated at $10 billion.

In a commentary published today, the WSWS’s David Walsh subjects the event to a Marxist analysis, treating the art market not as a vehicle of human culture but as an investment instrument that offers tax advantages to the ultra-wealthy. He invokes Marx’s observation that when artists prioritize money they compromise their artistic integrity, and notes that the global art market — valued at $1.7 trillion and growing 4% in 2025 to a turnover of $59.6 billion — increasingly functions to remove great works from public access and sequester them in private hands.

The episode crystallizes a central WSWS theme: under capitalism, the products of human creative labor are converted into financial assets, while access to art is determined by wealth rather than cultivation. The Pollock canvas — produced by an artist whose work emerged from a specific mid-20th-century social and aesthetic ferment — becomes, in the auction room, a mere store of value, its content irrelevant to the transaction. Walsh advances the conception of a society in which the relationship to art is mediated by human development, not the market.

Notes on the broader cultural moment:

  • The art market as a barometer of inequality: Record auction totals are recurring against a backdrop of confirmed famine in Gaza and the world’s largest displacement crisis in Sudan — the same global order that cannot fund 10% of Gaza’s humanitarian needs channels billions into the private hoarding of canvases. This juxtaposition is itself a damning indictment of the social system.
  • Estate sales and the concentration of culture: The Newhouse collection’s dispersal exemplifies how major works circulate among a tiny stratum of collectors and institutions tied to finance capital, rather than entering the cultural commons.
  • Artistic achievement and its appropriation: A consistent WSWS principle holds in evaluating such works — genuine artistic achievement (Pollock’s, Brancusi’s) must be recognized on its own terms, even as the market mechanisms that price and privatize it are subjected to criticism. The task is to distinguish the work from the commodity it has been made into.

WSWS coverage — last 24 hours

Perspective published: The Ebola epidemic, imperialism and the political economy of social murder by Evan Blake — confirmed dated May 23, 2026 (read in full on wsws.org).

Blake opens with Engels’s 1845 formulation of “social murder” — that when a ruling class knowingly maintains conditions producing mass death, that constitutes murder. He disputes the framing of epidemics as “natural disasters”: a zoonotic spillover may begin as a natural event, but modern science possesses the tools (detection, tracing, isolation, treatment) to prevent any spillover from becoming a regional catastrophe; that these tools exist but go undeployed transforms the outcome into a political question.

He identifies two sets of causal crimes. The first is structural: Eastern Congo has endured 30 years of war, 6 million deaths, 7 million internally displaced and 27 million facing acute hunger — conditions rooted in over a century of imperialist plunder, from Leopold II’s regime (up to 10 million killed) and the CIA’s murder of Lumumba and installation of Mobutu, to the Kivu wars over the coltan and gold that flow into the supply chains of Glencore and the technology giants. The second is the Trump administration’s destruction since January 2025 of the $100 million STOP Spillover program, USAID, PEPFAR surveillance, and US support for the WHO, plus a gag order severing the CDC from international partners — letting the virus circulate undetected for six weeks. He calls the diversion of an Air France flight over a single asymptomatic Congolese passenger “nativist theater.” The article closes with a four-point emergency program — restoring global surveillance, deploying personnel and PPE, free vaccines and treatments (including the immediate release of the US-owned antibody therapy MBP134), and the expropriation of mining and tech wealth — to be fought for only by the international working class through the IWA-RFC.

The crisis of public health in the era of COVID-19: an interview with Arijit Chakravarty — Benjamin Mateus

Dr. Chakravarty links the concurrent emergencies — Bundibugyo Ebola, Andes hantavirus spreading from the cruise ship MV Hondius, circulating H5N1, and measles resurgence — as interconnected failures flowing from the mismanagement of COVID-19. He identifies three cascading consequences: institutions now treat communication as a substitute for containment; repeated infections and collapsed vaccination have impaired population immunity; and behavioral erosion has made the public unwilling to mask or quarantine. He argues the gutting of US CDC capacity since January 2025 leaves the Ebola response without the institutional backbone present during the 2014–15 outbreaks, and calls for permanent indoor air-quality infrastructure, honest risk communication, and early contact tracing.

NATO summit in Sweden exposes sharpening inter-imperialist antagonisms — Jordan Shilton

NATO foreign ministers met in Helsingborg, Sweden to prepare the Ankara summit, with the meeting dominated by open US-European antagonisms. Rubio arrived “disappointed” by European responses to the war on Iran; Trump deployed 5,000 troops to Poland while drawing down 5,000 from Germany; and Washington suspended Canada-US cooperation by pulling out of the Permanent Joint Board of Defense (in place since 1940) over Canada’s delayed F-35 purchase. Shilton ties these fractures to the breakdown of the post-WWII equilibrium, traces European rearmament (Germany’s €1 trillion authorization, the 5%-of-GDP commitment by 2035), and argues the austerity required to fund militarism will intensify class struggle.

Four weeks of general strike in Bolivia: US imperialism and its regional allies move to crush an uprising — Tomas Castanheira

Castanheira traces Paz’s Decree 5503 to IMF and Inter-American Development Bank conditions and a Harvard Growth Lab roadmap drafted before Paz was elected. Milei’s Argentina sent a C-130 of “humanitarian” supplies and is alleged to be supplying arms; Lula stayed silent as the USS Nimitz exercised in Guanabara Bay. He concludes only an internationally coordinated working-class offensive can answer the imperialist apparatus being mobilized.

Pro-independence Parti Québécois strengthens its ties with the far right — Laurent Lafrance

PQ leader Paul St-Pierre Plamondon appeared on Rebel News — ruled by the Quebec Press Council an “activist organization with ties to far-right circles” — to discuss healthcare privatization, immigration restriction and “state secularism,” promoting the “Great Replacement” theory. Lafrance traces the PQ’s far-right drift since 2020, including its cultivation of Trump-aligned Alberta separatists and support for Bill 21 (banning religious garb for public workers while protecting Catholic symbols), and concludes the PQ is a capitalist, pro-austerity party incompatible with working-class interests.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — channels antiwar sentiment over Iran and Gaza into Congressional war-powers maneuvers and pressure on Democrats, evading the call for an independent movement against imperialism
  • Left Voice — militant rhetoric on Bolivia and Gaza coupled with adaptation to left-populist and union formations and the FIT-U electoral bloc
  • PSL / Liberation News — campist defense of the Iranian and other bourgeois-nationalist states as “anti-imperialist,” subordinating the working class to capitalist regimes
  • Socialist Alternative — reformist “tax the rich” and new-party appeals that in practice tail Democratic progressives
  • SWP (UK) — builds Stop the War popular-front mobilizations allied with Labour lefts and Islamists rather than a revolutionary party
  • IMT / RCP — abstract “are you a communist?” recruitment sloganeering and adaptation to Stalinist prestige

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s coverage of the Iran war and Gaza famine continues to frame the central task as one of pressuring the US Congress — reviving war-powers resolutions and demanding individual Democrats break with the administration — rather than mobilizing the working class independently against imperialist war. Its labor coverage praises union officialdom’s “wins” while obscuring the role of the bureaucracy in suppressing strikes. The underlying line subordinates the antiwar and labor movements to the Democratic Party and its periphery, treating the capitalist state as a terrain to be reformed rather than overthrown. The class interest served is that of a privileged middle-class layer seeking influence within the existing order.

Left Voice

Left Voice (US co-thinkers of Argentina’s PTS) gives sympathetic and often militant coverage to the Bolivian general strike and the Gaza solidarity movement, correctly identifying US imperialism’s hand. But its perspective remains bound to the FIT-U electoral bloc and to “united front” formulas with reformist and left-populist forces, reflecting the Morenoite tradition’s vacillation between revolutionary phrases and opportunist practice. On Bolivia it elevates the COB leadership and radical-nationalist currents rather than warning workers against the trade-union and Stalinist apparatuses that have repeatedly betrayed them. The result is centrist adaptation dressed in Trotskyist language.

PSL / Liberation News

Liberation News frames the Iran war as US-Israeli aggression — accurate as far as it goes — but couples this with a campist defense of the Iranian bourgeois state as an “anti-imperialist” bulwark, downplaying or ignoring the working-class protest movement and the regime’s 13-week internet blackout used to suppress it. The same logic governs its treatment of China, Russia and other capitalist states. By subordinating the Iranian and regional working class to one camp of capitalist rulers against another, the PSL abandons the independent class standpoint and the perspective of permanent revolution.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s output centers on “tax the rich” demands, calls for a new working-class party, and support for ballot-box pressure campaigns that in practice tail Democratic progressives such as the “Squad.” On the Iran war it issues general antiwar appeals without a program to break the working class from the bourgeois parties, and its labor coverage appeals to union leaderships to fight rather than building rank-and-file organizations against them. This is reformism — the conception that capitalism can be incrementally pressured into serving workers’ needs.

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

The SWP continues to channel opposition to the Gaza genocide and Iran war into Stop the War Coalition mobilizations built on a popular-front basis with Labour “lefts,” trade-union officials and Islamist organizations. Its theoretical heritage — Cliffite “state capitalism” — denies the imperialist character of the conflicts’ great-power dimensions and substitutes protest spectacle for the construction of a revolutionary party. The political line keeps the antiwar movement safely within the orbit of the Labour Party and the union bureaucracy.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The recently rebranded Revolutionary Communist Party (IMT) saturates its coverage of Bolivia, Iran and Gaza with maximalist calls for revolution and “are you a communist?” recruitment appeals, while its actual practice combines abstract propagandism with adaptation to the prestige of Stalinism and to left-reformist formations. Its sloganeering substitutes declarations of revolutionary intent for the patient theoretical and programmatic struggle to build a genuine internationalist leadership. Across all six tendencies, the common thread is the refusal to fight for the political independence of the working class from the bourgeoisie and its agencies — the dividing line the ICFI defends.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. 12 million to lose Medicaid, 40 million face SNAP cuts under the “Big Beautiful Bill”

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, enacted the largest cuts to basic-needs programs in US history to fund $4.5 trillion in tax cuts. The CBO projects 12 million will lose Medicaid by 2034, while new work requirements taking effect in December 2026 could strip over 40 million from SNAP. This is the central domestic act of class war by the Trump administration and warrants sustained socialist analysis as deliberate upward redistribution, not mere fiscal policy.

Recommended reading Center for American Progress The truth about the OBBBA’s cuts to Medicaid and Medicare

2. The Strait of Hormuz blockade and the global working-class energy burden

The dual blockade of the world’s most critical oil-transit point has driven Brent crude above $100/barrel and severely curtailed LNG supplies, raising inflation globally and threatening recession in import-dependent economies. The burden falls on working-class households while energy corporations profit. A class analysis of who benefits from artificially elevated energy prices — and how this constitutes warfare mediated by military conflict — is largely absent from mainstream coverage.

Recommended reading Bloomberg Iran war, Hormuz closure and the oil shock

3. Sudan’s genocide and the silence of imperialism

Sudan’s war — 400,000+ dead, 14 million displaced, 29 million food insecure — receives a fraction of the attention given to Ukraine or Gaza, even as it is fueled by weapons flows from the UAE, Egypt and Russia. A socialist analysis would situate the RSF’s atrocities within post-colonial resource extraction and great-power rivalry over Sudan’s oil and strategic position, and contrast the world’s indifference with the resources lavished on other theaters.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera The crisis in Sudan is much worse than acknowledged

4. Tech oligarchs kill Trump’s AI safety order — self-regulation triumphs

The shelving of the AI security-testing executive order after direct lobbying by Musk, Zuckerberg and Sacks is a vivid demonstration of the subordination of the state to the technology oligarchy. With Anthropic nearing a $900 billion valuation, the question of who controls — and who is accountable for — increasingly powerful AI systems is being decided in the interests of private capital. This warrants analysis as a question of class power over a transformative technology.

Recommended reading Washington Post Tech CEOs blocked executive order

5. US measles surge threatens elimination status as anti-vaccine politics take hold

With 1,952 confirmed cases across 29 outbreaks and 92% of patients unvaccinated, the US is on pace to break its 2025 record and lose the elimination status it has held since 2000. The resurgence is the domestic face of the same political assault on public health that gutted global disease surveillance and produced the Ebola catastrophe abroad — a connection that a socialist publication is uniquely positioned to draw.

Recommended reading U.S. News Tracking the 2026 US measles outbreaks

6. Pakistan’s military rearms against India while mediating the Iran war

Asim Munir’s simultaneous role as Iran-US mediator and architect of Pakistan’s accelerated military modernization — supersonic missiles, satellite systems, a new Rocket Force — illustrates how the military commands of postcolonial nuclear states operate autonomously from their populations. While Munir meets world leaders in Tehran, Pakistani workers bear the costs of a build-up framed as national pride. A class analysis of South Asian militarism is missing from Western coverage.

Recommended reading Quwa How Pakistan’s military rearmed in 12 months
