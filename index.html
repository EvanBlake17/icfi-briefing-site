1. Putin arrives in Beijing for Xi summit days after Trump

Russian President Vladimir Putin landed in Beijing today for a two-day summit with Xi Jinping, beginning just days after US President Donald Trump concluded his own visit to the Chinese capital. Chinese state media described the back-to-back superpower visits as “extremely rare in the post-Cold War era.” The summit also marks the 25th anniversary of the 2001 Treaty of Good-Neighborliness, Friendship and Cooperation between Russia and China.

The agenda is dominated by energy. Russia, hemorrhaging revenue under the dual pressures of the Ukraine war and Western sanctions, requires Chinese demand at discounted prices; China is seeking deeper integration of Russian gas and oil supply along with continued access to Russian dual-use drone and weapons technology. Officials in both capitals briefed reporters on a “major new gas and oil agreement” expected to be announced during the summit.

Beijing’s choreography of the two visits projects a posture of equidistance. Trump’s summit produced claims of “broad trade deals” but no progress on Taiwan or US demands for Chinese pressure on Iran. China explicitly refused to lean on Tehran. On Ukraine, Chinese officials said they would advocate for “peaceful negotiations” but would not pressure Russia toward any specific outcome. As one regional analyst quoted by Al Jazeera summarized: “China has all the cards.”

Key facts: - Putin’s two-day summit runs May 19–20; Trump departed Beijing only days earlier - Chinese state media called the back-to-back visits unprecedented in the post-Cold War era - China has never condemned Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, even as it deepens military-technological cooperation with Moscow - The summit follows the Russian drone strike on a Chinese-flagged cargo ship in the Black Sea (see story 3), which Beijing did not publicly protest

2. Trump threatens new Iran strikes as G7 emergency summit opens in Paris

With the US Navy maintaining its blockade of the Strait of Hormuz and global oil prices oscillating between $100 and $110 per barrel, President Trump declared on social media that Iran was “dying to sign” a deal while simultaneously posting images of nuclear buttons. Trump claimed he had scheduled a new military strike against Iran but had postponed it “two or three days.” Iranian negotiators rejected the latest US terms outright; Pakistani officials mediating the talks told reporters Washington “keeps moving their goal posts.”

G7 finance ministers convened an emergency session in Paris today to address the accelerating global economic damage. Member governments have collectively drawn roughly 400 million barrels from strategic reserves to dampen the price spike; inflation has surged across the eurozone and the United States; and the bond market has entered severe stress (see World economy section). The 2026 Iran war began February 28; a Pakistan-brokered ceasefire has been violated repeatedly since taking effect April 7–8. As Alex Lantier wrote on the WSWS, “Ruling circles across the G7 offer no meaningful opposition to military aggression” — the meeting was framed not as a brake on war but as crisis management for a war the financial bourgeoisie is determined to continue.

3. Ukraine launches largest drone strike of the war; Russian Shahed hits Chinese cargo ship

Russia’s Ministry of Defense reported intercepting or destroying more than 550 Ukrainian drones overnight May 17–18, in what is being described as Ukraine’s largest single drone assault of the entire war. Strikes hit more than a dozen Russian regions. At least four people were killed and a dozen wounded; Moscow’s mayor confirmed drones struck a checkpoint near the Moscow oil refinery and hit residential buildings in Moscow suburbs. President Volodymyr Zelenskyy called the strikes “entirely justified.”

Separately, a Russian Shahed drone struck the KSL Deyang, a Chinese-owned cargo vessel with a Chinese crew sailing under a Marshall Islands flag, in the Black Sea on May 18. The vessel was en route to Pivdennyi port to load iron ore. One side of the ship was “partially charred,” though all Chinese crew survived uninjured. A second vessel under a Guinea-Bissau flag was also struck. Zelenskyy stated: “The Russians could not have been unaware of what vessel was at sea.” The strike came less than 24 hours before Putin departed for Beijing — yet China made no public condemnation.

Key facts: - 550+ Ukrainian drones launched, the largest single attack since the war began - At least 4 dead, a dozen wounded inside Russia - KSL Deyang’s hull “partially charred”; all Chinese crew survived - Beijing remained silent ahead of the Putin summit

4. Democratic Party and union apparatus shut down LIRR strike in secret deal

The three-day strike by approximately 3,500 Long Island Rail Road workers — the largest commuter rail action in the New York region in decades — was ordered ended over the weekend after Governor Kathy Hochul brokered a settlement in secret with the five LIRR unions. No contract terms were disclosed to workers before they were sent back to their jobs. Reporting indicates the framework provides a 9.5 percent raise over three years — measurable against an 18 percent rise in regional living costs since 2022, median rents above $3,800 per month, and food prices up nearly 25 percent.

The strike demonstrated immense working-class leverage. The MTA’s strikebreaking shuttle-bus operation, scaled for 13,000 daily seats, filled only 2,100 — meaning the overwhelming majority of the 298,000 daily commuters refused to use scab transport. The contract covering 40,000 New York City bus and subway workers in TWU Local 100 expired the same Saturday the LIRR strike began; a joint walkout, the WSWS notes, “would have shut down the financial capital of American capitalism.” Newly inaugurated DSA-backed Mayor Zohran Mamdani, elected last November on a platform of free buses and taxing the rich, gave no public support to strikers and did not visit picket lines.

Hochul publicly slandered strikers as “reckless.” Bus drivers interviewed by the WSWS at the East New York depot described management offering 68 cents in raises while seeking to eliminate healthcare; one driver of 26 years declared “it is well needed for transit workers to join the LIRR strike.”

5. Bolivian national strike faces military repression as four killed in pre-dawn crackdown

A two-week-old indefinite national strike against Decree 5503 — which eliminated fuel subsidies overnight, doubling gas prices — has expanded to include manufacturing workers, miners, teachers, peasants, and indigenous groups, with more than 67 roadblocks across eight of Bolivia’s nine departments. President Rodrigo Paz, who replaced the MAS government last fall, deployed 3,500 troops in a May 17 pre-dawn crackdown that killed at least four people and arrested 57. The government has issued arrest warrants against COB leader Mario Argollo and former president Evo Morales.

The Iran war’s disruption of fuel markets is a major driver of Bolivian fiscal collapse: with global crude above $100/bbl, the cost of maintaining domestic fuel subsidies became unsustainable in the eyes of finance capital, which has been pressing La Paz for “structural adjustment.” As Andrea Lobo notes on the WSWS, the COB’s historical role since the 1952 revolution has been to function as a capitalist stabilizing institution — channeling the 2000–2005 uprisings toward Morales’s electoral victory rather than socialist transformation, and again attempting to limit the present movement to negotiation with the Paz government.

Key facts: - 67+ roadblocks across eight departments; movement is multi-class but worker-led - 3,500 troops deployed May 17; at least 4 killed, 57 arrested - Arrest warrants issued against COB leader and against Evo Morales - Fuel subsidy elimination doubled gasoline prices overnight

7. WHO opens annual assembly amid deepening Ebola crisis in Central Africa

The 79th World Health Assembly opens in Geneva as a Bundibugyo-strain Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda has killed at least 118 people. Médecins Sans Frontières revealed today that the virus circulated undetected for five to six weeks before the first lab-confirmed case on May 14. One hospital alone is treating 390 suspected cases. Confirmed cases have spread to Bunia, Goma, Kampala, and Butembo.

The surveillance failure is a direct product of the Trump administration’s gutting of US humanitarian funding for the DRC, slashing it from over $900 million to roughly $179 million — nearly 80 percent — and closing both the USAID DRC mission and the NIH viral hemorrhagic fever research lab in Kinshasa. An infected American physician was evacuated to Germany rather than the United States. The CDC has imposed a Title 42 travel ban on non-US citizens arriving from affected countries while refusing to answer questions about whether the aid cuts contributed to the crisis. Africa CDC Director Jean Kaseya told reporters he was in “panic mode.” The WHO’s own budget has been cut by 21 percent — roughly $1 billion — over the past year, with the United States as the principal driver.

8. Italy general strike and Israeli seizure of Sumud Flotilla

The Italian rank-and-file unions USB, USI, and FI-SI led a 24-hour general strike across Italy on May 18, disrupting transport, schools, and public services, in opposition to the genocide in Gaza and Italian support for the Iran war. The strike coincided with Israeli naval forces seizing approximately 25 vessels from the Global Sumud Flotilla in international waters west of Cyprus — including several Italian-flagged ships — detaining seven to eight Italian citizens. Opposition parties described the operation as “piracy.”

Italy’s major confederations — CGIL, CISL, and UIL — abstained from the strike, the fourth time they have done so in nine months. Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni’s government issued only minimal diplomatic protest over the seizure of its nationals. As Marc Wells writes on the WSWS, the base unions hold strategic logistical power (Italy is a key NATO transit hub for arms shipments) but achieving fundamental change requires international coordination rather than appeals to the Meloni or EU institutions — hence the WSWS call to build an Italian section of the ICFI.

Sudan: 20 million face acute hunger in world’s worst displacement crisis

The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) reports that 20 million Sudanese — roughly 40 percent of the population — face acute hunger three years into the war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces. 34 million people, or 65 percent of the country, need urgent humanitarian assistance. Famine has been formally confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli, with risk of famine in 20 additional areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan. Fourteen million people have been displaced, the largest displacement crisis on Earth. Families are reportedly eating leaves, animal feed, and animal skin to survive. The World Food Programme requires $610 million by August to continue operations. The approaching rainy season (July onward) is expected to cut humanitarian access. The US, UAE, and Saudi Arabia are deeply implicated in arming the warring factions, yet Sudan receives only a fraction of the media coverage given to other conflicts.

Israel accelerates de facto annexation of West Bank through administrative takeover

Israel’s security cabinet in February transferred West Bank administrative powers from the IDF and Civil Administration to Israeli civilian ministries — the Israeli Ministry of Justice now controls land registration in Area C. The cabinet has allocated over 244 million shekels (roughly $66 million) to establish a mechanism facilitating Israeli land registration in the occupied territory. New rules allow Israelis to directly purchase land from Palestinians in the West Bank with significantly reduced legal safeguards. A UN Special Rapporteur condemned these as “deliberate, incremental steps toward permanent annexation, advanced piece by piece, in broad daylight, and with total impunity.” Tens of thousands of Palestinians have been forcibly displaced from Area C as settlement expansion accelerates. Israel’s neighbors have characterized the moves as the “de facto beginning of annexation.”

Gaza: 77% of population still acutely food insecure six months after ceasefire

The May UN OCHA situation report confirms that six months into the Gaza ceasefire, 1.6 million people — 77 percent of the population — still face high levels of acute food insecurity. Over 100,000 children and 37,000 pregnant and breastfeeding women are projected to suffer acute malnutrition through April 2027. Since the ceasefire took effect, UN agencies and partners have collected 293,600 metric tons of aid — a 72 percent increase versus the prior period — and the denial rate for coordinated humanitarian missions has fallen from 31 percent to 11 percent. But OCHA warns that without sustained large-scale expansion of food, health, and livelihood assistance, “hundreds of thousands could rapidly slip back into famine.” Massive infrastructure destruction means recovery is expected to take decades.

Australia Labor government’s bill guts disability supports for 760,000

The NDIS Amendment Bill 2026, tabled by Anthony Albanese’s Labor government two days after a federal budget that slashed $35–38 billion from the National Disability Insurance Scheme, introduces “functional assessments” designed to remove up to 300,000 participants from the 760,000-person scheme — the largest single program cut in Australian history. The bill grants the Health Minister unilateral power to cut funding to whole disability categories by up to 99 percent with no judicial review, and explicitly rewrites the NDIS’s founding purpose to be contingent on “financial sustainability of the scheme.” The structure closely mirrors the automated payment reductions of the Robodebt scandal that ended the previous Coalition government. The cuts are inseparable from Labor’s concurrent surge in military spending and its diplomatic support for the US assault on Iran.

Australia: Victorian teachers face fourth AEU sellout

The Australian Education Union has negotiated a four-year “in-principle agreement” offering compounded annual increases of roughly 6.7–7 percent against officially recorded inflation of 4.6 percent and forward projections approaching 6 percent, with no enforceable class-size caps, no guaranteed planning time, no mechanism to recover real wage losses since 2021 — and stripping teachers of strike rights until 2030. Sri Lanka prisoner death raises questions about 2022 uprising convictions

Supun Tharindu Sunil Rathna — one of twelve sentenced to death for the killing of MP Amarakeerthi Athukorala during Sri Lanka’s mass 2022 anti-government uprising — died at Welikada Prison on May 11 after an alleged fall from an upper floor. The post-mortem produced an open verdict and samples were sent for poison analysis; nonetheless, police immediately declared a “strong suspicion of suicide.” His mother stated bluntly: “it is hard to believe that he simply fell from there.” The original conviction rested on circumstantial evidence, with one of three judges dissenting and calling for acquittal. The Socialist Equality Party characterizes the prosecution as deliberate class retribution against participants in the 2022 uprising that brought down the Rajapaksa regime, and demands the release of the remaining convicted.

State Department forces out 250 career diplomats as multiple wars converge

The State Department has finalized the dismissal of nearly 250 career foreign service officers, leaving only 7 percent of US ambassadors as career diplomats — a historic low. Embassies without confirmed ambassadors lose access to senior foreign government officials, severely degrading US diplomatic capacity at a moment when Washington is managing the Iran war, the Ukraine war, the Sudan crisis, the Strait of Hormuz blockade, and a ceasefire breakdown simultaneously. Former diplomats told CNN that the loss of energy security expertise is particularly damaging given the Hormuz crisis. FAA cuts air traffic controller hiring target by 2,000 amid record-low staffing

The FAA has reduced its staffing target for certified professional air traffic controllers from 14,633 to 12,563 — a cut of more than 2,000. Yet current staffing of approximately 11,000 remains below even this lowered target. This is the lowest air traffic controller workforce since Ronald Reagan fired 11,000 PATCO strikers in 1981. Those 11,000 controllers managed 14,200 daily flights; today’s 11,000 manage 44,000 daily flights. Research cited in the WSWS article finds that fatigue contributes to up to 20 percent of aircraft mishaps; working 18+ hours impairs cognitive performance to the equivalent of 0.05% blood alcohol. DOGE has eliminated 400 FAA support staff (safety assistants, radar technicians, charting specialists). The NATCA union locked controllers into a 13-year concession contract without a membership vote.

US measles cases hit 1,893; 2026 on pace to surpass 2025 total

The CDC reports 1,893 confirmed measles cases in the United States in 2026 as of May 14, with 27 new outbreaks declared this year. 93 percent of cases (1,761) are outbreak-associated. The full-year 2025 total was 2,288 cases — 2026 is on pace to surpass it before mid-year. Declining kindergarten vaccination coverage, now at 92.5 percent (down from 95.2 percent in 2019–20), leaves approximately 286,000 incoming kindergartners unprotected, sustaining outbreak conditions. The collapse of measles control is occurring under the leadership of HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., whose decades of anti-vaccine activism have been amplified through federal public health channels.

First confirmed H5N5 human death documented in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine published a case letter documenting a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman in Washington state who contracted highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5) in October 2025 and died on illness day 28. This is the first confirmed human H5N5 infection in the medical literature. H5N5 is a newly recognized strain capable of human infection; its emergence alongside ongoing H5N1 activity broadens the spectrum of zoonotic-risk avian influenzas. The Ebola outbreak in DRC/Uganda (see Top stories) further illustrates the consequences of dismantled global surveillance.

Trump administration shifts to pre-deployment AI vetting

The Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI/NIST) announced pre-deployment evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and Elon Musk’s xAI, expanding on prior deals with OpenAI and Anthropic. The White House is consulting experts on formalizing a mandatory government review process for frontier AI models. This marks a notable shift away from the administration’s earlier light-touch posture, driven partly by cybersecurity concerns raised by Anthropic’s latest model. The architecture is designed to give the executive branch — and specifically the military and intelligence apparatus — first-mover access to capabilities developed at private companies, while privately owned IP and profits remain untouched. The Pentagon previously demanded that Anthropic drop AI safeguards or face blacklisting.

WHO confirms LP.8.1 as fall 2026 COVID vaccine antigen

The WHO’s Technical Advisory Group on COVID-19 Vaccine Composition (TAG-CO-VAC) confirmed monovalent LP.8.1 as the recommended antigen for upcoming COVID-19 vaccines, continuing the recommendation first issued in December 2025. The dominant circulating variant is XFG.1.1 (32% of cases); US test positivity stands at approximately 1 percent, the lowest in years, though wastewater surveillance has been gutted under the Kennedy HHS.

Arts and culture

India’s CBFC blocks Oscar-nominated The Voice of Hind Rajab

The Central Board of Film Certification has blocked the theatrical release in India of The Voice of Hind Rajab — the Venice Silver Lion winner and Oscar-nominated docudrama by Kaouther Ben Hania about the killing of five-year-old Palestinian Hind Rajab by Israeli tank fire on January 29, 2024. The censorship decision, taken under the Modi government, violates the 1952 Cinematograph Act on procedural grounds: no Gazette publication of the decision has been made. More than 90 international artists and filmmakers — including writers, directors, and Academy Award winners — signed an open letter denouncing the ban as “a plainly unlawful attack on freedom of expression.”

The CBFC’s action is inseparable from the strategic partnership between Hindutva nationalism and the Israeli state. India imports Israeli military technology and policing methods; Modi’s government applies a similar apartheid logic to Kashmir and to Muslim minorities domestically. India has separately denied visas to anti-Zionist Israeli theater artists performing works about Gaza’s destruction. As Kranti Kumara writes on the WSWS, the ban demonstrates that the Hindu nationalist regime experiences any artistic engagement with Palestinian suffering as a direct political threat. The Voice of Hind Rajab — which centers on the 70-minute audio recording of Hind’s voice as she pleaded for rescue while trapped in a vehicle with her dead family members — is precisely the kind of work that forces audiences to confront the human reality of a genocide that bourgeois media has spent two years deliberately blurring.

Genuine artistic achievement — work that addresses the contradictions of capitalism, imperial violence, and the conditions of human life — finds itself simultaneously threatened by direct censorship, by funding cuts, and by the AI-driven commodification of creative labor.

The political line is consistent with Jacobin’s longstanding adaptation to the Democratic Party. The total silence on the LIRR betrayal is not accidental but structural.
