1. US-Iran war: tentative deal awaits Trump and Khamenei sign-off

A tentative memorandum of understanding has been negotiated to halt the US-Iran war, but neither Donald Trump nor Iran’s supreme leader has signed off, and military operations continued through the night even as diplomats spoke of a breakthrough. The draft would commit Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz within 30 days, trigger a 60-day ceasefire extension, reaffirm that Iran will never develop nuclear weapons, and dispose of its enriched-uranium stockpile under an agreed mechanism. WSWS analyst Andre Damon argues the diplomatic process is functioning as cover for expanding imperial dominance rather than as a genuine restraint, noting the draft also includes a $300 billion reconstruction fund.

Key facts:

  • US CENTCOM conducted overnight “self-defense strikes” on Iranian missile launch sites and on boats said to be attempting to lay mines; Iran’s Revolutionary Guard claimed it downed an MQ-9 drone.
  • Iran’s Foreign Ministry spokesman said the negotiations face “problems and obstacles” caused by “frequent changes” in US positions.
  • Trump said he wants “a great and meaningful pact or there will be no deal.” Secretary of State Marco Rubio: “The straits have to be open — they’re going to be open one way or the other.”
  • Iran insists any deal halt all fronts, including Lebanon — a central sticking point.
  • More than 3,100 have been killed in Israeli strikes since early March; over 1 million are displaced in Lebanon.

2. Israel escalates Lebanon war, kills 29 in 24 hours

Israel intensified its bombardment of Lebanon over the past 24 hours, with the Lebanese health ministry reporting at least 29 killed and 58 wounded. Netanyahu formally authorized escalation, ordering “even greater acceleration of our operations” to “crush” Hezbollah. The expansion of the Lebanon front directly threatens the US-Iran negotiations, since Tehran is demanding that any ceasefire cover all theaters.

Key facts:

  • An Israeli strike on a building in Tyre killed 13 members of a single family, including a woman, wounded 35, and left 15 missing.
  • Israel struck Beirut for the first time in weeks, targeting a Hezbollah missile-unit commander.
  • The total Lebanese death toll since the war began on March 2, 2026 has reached 2,196, including 172 children.
  • Israel has declared roughly 15% of Lebanon a combat zone and ordered the evacuation of Tyre, while violating a ceasefire nearly daily without ever having withdrawn under an earlier November 2024 agreement.

Sources Al Jazeera Israel intensifies attacks on southern Lebanon · Wikipedia 2026 Lebanon war · WSWS Under cover of US-Iran negotiations, Israel steps up effort to annex Gaza

3. Israel moves to annex Gaza as Hamas military chief killed

Even as US-Iran talks proceeded, Netanyahu ordered Israeli forces to seize 70% of Gaza, confining 2.1 million Palestinians to less than a third of the territory. Defense Minister Israel Katz openly described the policy as “voluntary emigration” — language the WSWS identifies as a euphemism for ethnic cleansing. The annexation drive coincided with the killing of Mohammed Odeh, head of Hamas’s Qassam Brigades, in a May 26 strike on a residential building in Gaza City that also killed his wife and two sons.

Key facts:

  • Odeh was the second Qassam Brigades commander killed this month, having succeeded Izz al-Din al-Haddad, killed earlier in May.
  • Gaza’s Health Ministry reports the cumulative death toll since October 7, 2023 stands at 72,803.
  • Approximately 906 Palestinians have been killed since the October 11 ceasefire took effect — over 900 by the WSWS count of the October 10, 2025 truce — including fighters and civilians.
  • The pattern of killing family members in “targeted” strikes underscores the systematic character of the assault on civilians.

Sources Al Jazeera Hamas confirms Israel killed its military wing chief · Wikipedia Casualties of the Gaza war · WSWS Under cover of US-Iran negotiations, Israel steps up effort to annex Gaza

4. Eleven workers killed in Longview, Washington mill disaster

A 900,000-gallon tank of “white liquor” — a highly caustic solution of sodium hydroxide and sodium sulfide — ruptured early on May 26 at the Nippon Dynawave Packaging mill in Longview, Washington, killing 11 workers. Approximately 570,000 gallons spilled across the facility and into the Columbia River, partly through dilution attempts by emergency crews. Democratic Governor Bob Ferguson said authorities were “bracing ourselves for this being the deadliest industrial tragedy in modern Washington state history.” The disaster is the subject of today’s WSWS Perspective by Jacob Crosse, who frames it as the predictable product of a system that subordinates worker safety to profit.

Key facts:

  • The mill is owned by Nippon Paper Industries, which bought it from Weyerhaeuser in 2016 for $285 million; it employs roughly 1,000 workers.
  • State regulators issued four citations against the company between 2019 and 2025; two inspections were already underway at the time of the rupture — one over an aqua ammonia tank valve, another over a failed drain that caused a sinkhole beneath the facility.
  • The white liquor causes second- and third-degree burns on skin contact; corrosive chemicals hampered recovery and contaminated the river.
  • Per the AFL-CIO’s Death on the Job report, 5,283 US workers were killed on the job in 2024 — more than 15 a day — with OSHA’s 1,651 inspectors covering 12 million workplaces.

5. Ebola declared a global emergency in DRC: 1,205 cases, 264 dead

The Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo continues to expand, with the World Health Organization having declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) on May 17 — just two days after the outbreak was confirmed. As of May 27, the DRC has recorded 1,205 suspected and confirmed cases and at least 264 deaths; Uganda has confirmed seven cases including one death, with the disease reaching the capital, Kampala.

Key facts:

  • The outbreak is caused by Bundibugyo ebolavirus, a rarer strain for which existing treatments — developed against the Zaire strain — are not approved.
  • It began in Mongbwalu, a gold-mining town of 130,000 in Ituri province, and has since spread to North Kivu, South Kivu, and Uganda.
  • Health workers responding to the outbreak have faced violent attacks from local residents, complicating containment.
  • The epicenter’s conditions — artisanal mining poverty, collapsed public health infrastructure, foreign corporate extraction — illustrate the social roots of the crisis. No pharmaceutical firm has prioritized a Bundibugyo vaccine.

6. Putin and Xi issue anti-US communiqué, sign 40 agreements

In the wake of their Beijing summit, Vladimir Putin and Xi Jinping issued a joint declaration condemning the “failure of neocolonial hegemony” and calling for “a new era of international relations” and a multipolar world. Roughly 40 intergovernmental, interagency, and corporate documents were signed. The communiqué is a marker of the deepening inter-imperialist realignment around the US-Iran war and the Trump administration’s unilateralism.

Key facts:

  • Bilateral trade reached nearly $240 billion in 2025 and grew 20% in the first four months of 2026.
  • The leaders jointly condemned the US “Golden Dome” missile-defense system, which would place a new missile field in the American Midwest.
  • Both warned against a global return to the “law of the jungle,” a direct reference to Trump-era unilateralism.
  • The summit came as Russia continues to lose ground in Ukraine while holding roughly 12% of the country’s territory.

Sudan: 19.5 million face acute hunger as war enters its third year

A joint WFP/FAO/UNICEF report finds that 19.5 million people — roughly two in five Sudanese — are experiencing crisis-level food insecurity or worse as the civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces enters its third year. The catastrophe, enabled by years of Western support for the military government and the RSF’s Gulf backers, receives a fraction of the coverage afforded other conflicts.

  • 5 million people face emergency-level hunger; 135,000 are in catastrophic conditions with heightened risk of death.
  • 800,000 children are at risk of severe malnutrition in 2026.
  • Nearly 9 million people have been displaced since April 2023.
  • The Strait of Hormuz closure has worsened a global fertilizer shortage that the FAO’s chief economist warns could trigger a severe food crisis within 6–12 months; 318 million people globally face crisis-level hunger.

Sources UNICEF Risk of famine persists; nearly 19.5 million face acute food insecurity · NPR Severe global food crisis could come within the year, UN agency says

Iran ends 88-day internet blackout, the longest in modern history

President Masoud Pezeshkian ordered Iran’s nationwide internet blackout lifted on May 25; partial restoration began May 26 after 88 consecutive days of shutdown — the longest nationwide internet disruption ever recorded, according to NetBlocks. The blackout, imposed by the Islamic Republic on its own population as US bombs fell, functioned as a form of collective punishment.

  • The first shutdown began January 8 during protests; a second began February 28 as US strikes escalated.
  • At peak restoration, traffic reached only 40% of 2026’s maximum; WhatsApp is newly restricted, and YouTube and Instagram remain heavily blocked.
  • Thousands of Iranians lost their jobs during the blackout, and small businesses dependent on digital platforms were destroyed.
  • NetBlocks warned “it is unclear if the restoration will be sustained.”

Sources The National Internet restored to tens of millions in Iran after three-month blackout · UPI Iran internet restored after 88 days

Russia-Ukraine war: Russia retreats but Ukraine’s grid is in ruins

Russia registered a net loss of 29 square miles of Ukrainian territory in the week of May 12–19 — its largest single-week retreat in months — even as it retains roughly 29,083 square miles, about 12% of Ukraine. The military stalemate is unfolding against the backdrop of a collapsing civilian infrastructure.

  • Ukraine has lost roughly 70% of its electricity generation capacity; most civilians receive only 3–4 hours of power a day.
  • An estimated 600,000 people have left Kyiv due to power shortages and ongoing strikes.
  • Russian attacks on Ukraine’s rail network have caused $5.8 billion in damage since the invasion began.

UK records hottest May day in history — twice in two days

Britain broke its all-time May temperature record on two consecutive days, with Kew Gardens recording 35.1°C on May 26 after 34.8°C the day before; the prior record of 32.8°C had stood since 1922. Wales also broke its May record. A government-backed climate report released this week concluded the UK is “built for a climate that no longer exists” — but says nothing about who profits from the fossil-fuel industry driving the emergency, or about the class divide in heat resilience between air-conditioned wealthy households and workers in Victorian-era housing.

  • The UK Health Security Agency issued multiple Amber and Yellow Heat Health Alerts.
  • London hit 35°C (95°F) for two consecutive days; heat-related mortality disproportionately affects the elderly, the unhoused, and the poor.

US stocks at record highs as ECB warns of geoeconomic shock

DOJ opens retaliatory criminal probe into Trump accuser E. Jean Carroll

The Justice Department has launched a criminal perjury investigation into E. Jean Carroll, the writer who won a $5 million sexual-abuse verdict against Donald Trump in May 2023 and an $83 million defamation award in January 2024, both upheld on appeal. Prosecutors are examining whether Carroll lied in a 2022 deposition when she said no one else was paying her legal fees, before later disclosing funding from a nonprofit linked to LinkedIn co-founder Reid Hoffman — a point federal appellate courts already deemed immaterial, finding she “plausibly represented that she had forgotten about the limited outside funding.” Senior DOJ leaders referred the matter to federal prosecutors in Chicago, though the US attorney there denies opening an investigation. As WSWS analyst Patrick Martin notes, the probe is naked retaliation against an 82-year-old woman, with conflicts of interest running through the department: Acting AG Todd Blanche, who worked on Trump’s Carroll appeals, has been recused.

Nexteer workers denounce third UAW sellout contract

The 1,300 Nexteer Automotive workers in Saginaw, Michigan are confronting a third proposed contract after rejecting two by margins of 96.2% and 73% and authorizing a strike with 86% approval — only for UAW Local 699 to extend the contract unilaterally without a vote. The new agreement offers $27/hour by 2030, identical in real terms to the plant’s 2005 wage (which inflation-adjusted would require over $45 today), while raising healthcare costs for post-2021 hires and lowering the discharge threshold from 18 to 12 attendance points.

US measles cases hit 1,952 — worst outbreak since elimination

As of May 21 (updated May 22), the US has confirmed 1,952 measles cases across 40 jurisdictions, with 93% (1,815) linked to 29 active outbreaks. Cases span 39 states plus Washington DC, including Texas, New York, Florida, and California. This is the largest US measles resurgence since elimination was declared in 2000, driven overwhelmingly by unvaccinated communities — a public health collapse unfolding alongside the gutting of federal vaccine infrastructure.

First human H5N5 bird flu case published in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine published (May 7) the first globally reported human case of influenza A(H5N5): a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman in Washington state, infected in October 2025, who died on illness day 28. No human-to-human transmission was identified, but the case marks a new H5 subtype capable of infecting humans, expanding the range of strains under pandemic surveillance.

Roundup:

  • H5N1: The US has recorded 71 total human A(H5) cases since February 2024. WHO separately reported a fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh among 13 avian/swine flu human cases tracked in Q1 2026. (CDC, CIDRAP)
  • MASH drug: In a 160-person Phase IIb trial published in The Lancet (~May 26), the DGAT2-blocking drug ION224 (monthly injections, 51 weeks) improved liver inflammation and fibrosis in ~60% of patients at the highest dose, without requiring weight loss — among the strongest results yet for a disease affecting tens of millions with no approved therapy. (ScienceDaily)
  • COVID-19: Test positivity is ~0.83% (week ending May 16), hospitalizations 0.2 per 100,000, and national wastewater the lowest since spring 2021. Dominant variant is XFG.1.1 (32%); the BA.3.2 “Cicada” subvariant shows slow Northeast growth as southern states see an early uptick. (The Sick Times)
  • EU AI Act: On May 7, the Council and Parliament agreed to push high-risk AI compliance deadlines to December 2027–August 2028 and reduce burdens on SMEs, while adding an immediate ban (from December 2, 2026) on AI systems generating non-consensual intimate imagery and CSAM — a pivot toward competitiveness over strict timelines. (EU Council)
  • Nanotech: Researchers demonstrated atomically precise carbon mechanosynthesis on hydrogenated silicon using inverted-mode scanning tunneling microscopy — a foundational step toward molecular manufacturing. (ScienceDaily)

CBS shuts down The Late Show with Stephen Colbert

The most significant cultural event of the past 24 hours is the conclusion of CBS’s cancellation of The Late Show with Stephen Colbert, ending the 33-year Late Show franchise. The May 21 finale drew 6.74 million viewers — Colbert’s largest-ever CBS audience — demonstrating that the decision was political, not commercial, despite the network’s claim of a $40 million annual loss. The cancellation followed direct pressure from Trump (who celebrated by sharing an AI-generated video of himself throwing Colbert into a dumpster) and came as Paramount sought administration approval for its $8 billion merger with Skydance, owned by David Ellison, son of Trump donor Larry Ellison. The episode is a textbook case of corporate censorship achieved through media consolidation rather than formal government mandate — what WSWS analyst Kevin Reed frames as part of an “orchestrated attack on fundamental democratic rights.” It marks a qualitative narrowing of the space for even mild liberal satire in the corporate media, with the disciplining of one of the country’s most-watched broadcasters serving as a warning to the entire industry.

Anti-Zionist art and the centenary of the 1926 General Strike

Two cultural threads in the past day deserve note for engaging seriously with war and historical memory:

  • “Drawings Against Genocide”: Artist Matthew Collings’s anti-Zionist drawing project, profiled this week, represents a growing current of visual art responding directly to the destruction of Gaza — the kind of socially engaged work the WSWS framework values for confronting imperialist violence rather than retreating into apolitical aestheticism.
  • 1926 General Strike centenary: Cultural and historical commemorations of the 1926 British General Strike — the betrayal of which by the TUC leadership and the Anglo-Soviet Trade Union Committee is central to Trotsky’s critique of the Comintern — are circulating widely on its 100th anniversary, a reminder of the enduring relevance of the question of revolutionary leadership.

The juxtaposition is instructive: genuine artistic engagement with genocide and class struggle coexists with the corporate suppression of dissent exemplified by the Colbert affair. The retreat of the major broadcasters from political content cedes ground to independent and explicitly political cultural production, even as it impoverishes mass culture.

WSWS coverage — last 24 hours

Perspective published: “Industrial slaughter in Longview: 11 workers killed in Washington’s deadliest workplace disaster in nearly 100 years” by Jacob Crosse — read in full on wsws.org. (Summarized as Top Story #4; the unique programmatic content follows below.)

Industrial slaughter in Longview — Jacob Crosse

Beyond the facts reported above, the Perspective embeds the disaster in a pattern of simultaneous industrial carnage: a methyl methacrylate tank emergency in Garden Grove, California; a lumber mill explosion in Maine injuring twelve; a January 2026 toxic gas exposure that killed two; and the explosion of a Shanxi province coal mine in China that killed 82. It refuses to treat any as isolated accidents, locating them in a unified capitalist logic. The political indictment is specific: Governor Bob Ferguson had years as state attorney general to pursue Nippon Dynawave and did nothing; Democratic Congresswoman Marie Gluesenkamp Perez praised “working-class jobs” before all bodies were recovered. Crosse notes that an estimated 135,000 US workers die annually from occupational diseases, and that at current rates OSHA could inspect each workplace once every 191 years. The programmatic conclusion is a call for rank-and-file safety committees in every workplace, independent of management, government, and the union officialdom that sits on joint “safety” committees — citing the WSWS Postal Workers Rank-and-File Safety Committee as a model.

OSHA fines USPS only $26,481 over death of postal worker Nick Acker — Kathleen Martin

Federal regulators fined the USPS $26,481 — reduced from $66,200 — following the November 2025 death of 36-year-old Nick Acker, crushed inside a mail sorting machine at Detroit’s Network Distribution Center and found six to eight hours after his death. OSHA issued five citations for Lockout/Tagout failures. The fine equals roughly ten seconds of USPS annual revenue. Acker’s fiancée Stephanie Jaszcz called it “disgusting and infuriating”; his father said witnesses refused to testify despite telling OSHA “no one was following the rules.” The American Postal Workers Union issued no public statement. The article argues regulators, management, and the union bureaucracy collectively shield employers from accountability — a direct companion to the Longview Perspective.

Bolivian government authorizes military deployment against working-class uprising — Tomas Castanheira

President Rodrigo Paz signed legislation repealing Law 1341 — which since 2020 had restricted military deployment against civilians — passed by over two-thirds of both chambers within 48 hours under emergency procedures. By May 28, authorities counted 66 active road blockades across six departments as transport workers struck indefinitely over fuel shortages. During a May 25 operation, 24-year-old Víctor Cruz Quispe was shot dead; the Ombudsman registered seven dead, 23 wounded, and 321 arrested. Government deputies labeled workers “terrorists.” Castanheira argues MAS factions and other bourgeois organizations cannot represent workers’ interests.

Mexican government bows to Washington over cartel-link prosecutions — Don Knowland

The US has indicted ten high-level Mexican officials, including Sinaloa Governor Rubén Rocha Moya, on cartel-protection charges. President Sheinbaum’s approval has dropped from 75% to 68% since March, and CNN reported CIA involvement in a targeted assassination inside Mexico that Sheinbaum denied. Despite “mutual respect” rhetoric, the Morena government has frozen suspects’ accounts, enabled extraditions, and approved US intelligence operations. Knowland cites suggestions from Morena’s own founders that former President AMLO could face US prosecution, concluding that Sheinbaum’s capitulation mirrors the subordination of all Latin American states to Washington.

Syrian refugees fear deportation from Germany — Lena Sokoll

Following a March 2026 state visit by Syrian leader Ahmed al-Sharaa — formerly head of the UN-designated terrorist al-Nusra Front — Chancellor Merz declared that “around 80 percent” of the roughly one million Syrians in Germany should return within three years; single men’s asylum claims have been rejected as “manifestly unfounded” since September 2025. Germany’s own Foreign Minister acknowledged in 2025 that Syria’s devastated infrastructure makes dignified return impossible. Sokoll argues the policy provides a scapegoat while the government pursues rearmament and austerity.

New Zealand government introduces anti-transgender legislation — Tom Peters

NZ First MP Jenny Marcroft introduced the “Legislation (Definitions of Woman and Man) Amendment Bill,” backed by the National Party and ACT, encoding “biological female/male” definitions across all NZ law — timed one week before a budget cutting thousands of public-sector jobs and raising university fees. Peters cites a University of Waikato survey finding nearly one in five transgender and non-binary people have been threatened with violence and more than half have considered suicide, connecting the bill to its function as a distraction from economic attacks.

ISIS-linked families return to Australia, but mother and child blocked — Mike Head

Nineteen Australian women and children returned from Syria’s Al Roj camp facing intensive police interrogation, while Hodan Abby and her nine-year-old daughter — who has shrapnel wounds needing care — remain barred under a Temporary Exclusion Order the Labor government can renew indefinitely on ministerial “reasonable suspicion” alone. Head calls the TEO regime “a historic attack on the core democratic right of citizenship” and traces ISIS recruitment to CIA support for Islamist groups in Syria from 2011 and to the social conditions facing working-class immigrant communities.

Australian High Court to hear limited challenge to public housing demolitions — Margaret Rees

The High Court granted leave in Mallard v Homes Victoria on only one of two grounds — procedural fairness under the Housing Act 1984 — rejecting the human rights dimension. Even a successful ruling would merely compel consultation, with no obligation to halt demolitions. Homes Victoria has used the legal process to accelerate “decanting” — emptying towers despite injunctions. Rees concludes only independent working-class organization can stop the demolition of 44 public housing towers.

Sri Lanka’s opposition leader urges another IMF program — Rohantha De Silva

With the rupee down 14% over twelve months and the IMF program requiring $14.2 billion in reserves by March 2027 against an actual $7 billion, SJB opposition leader Sajith Premadasa called for successor IMF negotiations, stating: “What I am proposing is not a retreat from fiscal discipline.” De Silva argues this cross-party austerity consensus is designed to suppress the kind of uprising that produced the 2022 Aragala protests.

The politics of Hasan Piker: radical rhetoric in service of the Democratic Party — David Rye

Piker (1.88 million YouTube subscribers, a $2.7 million home) steers Gaza-radicalized followers toward Democratic Party reform — backing AOC, Zohran Mamdani, and “replace bad Democrats” while calling himself “a reformist.” The article accuses him of misappropriating Lenin’s Left-Wing Communism to justify working within bourgeois institutions, of dismissing the American working class as having “no class consciousness,” and of advancing a “Hasanabi Doctrine” excluding working-class agency. It characterizes Piker as a “safety valve” containing radical energy within the Democratic Party.

Democratic Socialist Chris Rabb wins Pennsylvania primary — Landon Gourov

DSA member Chris Rabb won the Democratic primary in PA’s 3rd District with 44.7% against establishment-backed Sharif Street (29.2%) in a safe seat. The article notes Rabb voted for increased police funding in 2022, was endorsed by the Philadelphia Inquirer and backed by “Patriotic Millionaires,” and opposes the Iran war only “on budgetary grounds.” Gourov concludes the Democratic Party cannot be reformed from within, holding up the Philadelphia Workers Rank-and-File Strike Committee as an alternative.

CPM-Kenya defends counter-revolutionary Maoist strategy against Trotskyism — Part 5 — Kipchumba Ochieng

The concluding installment (May 28) argues the National Democratic Revolution strategy functions as a counterrevolutionary mechanism subordinating workers to bourgeois nationalism. Drawing on FRELIMO, the MPLA, SWAPO, and the ANC — all of which preserved capitalist property and deployed state violence against workers — it presents post-apartheid South Africa (40%+ official unemployment, the richest 10% holding over 80% of wealth) as the NDR’s “most brutal outcome.” Joe Slovo’s “sunset clauses” are presented as the paradigmatic betrayal, with continent-wide protests framed as requiring ICFI sections rather than Stalinist-Maoist parties.

  • “Tony Blair Is a Demon the British Left Needs to Exorcize” — Correct in targeting Blair’s alliance with tech billionaires, but the piece serves to manage Labour-left expectations, attacking the most discredited figures while preserving faith in Labourism as a vehicle.
  • “Scottish Nationalists Hang On to Power, Without Authority” — Critiques SNP corruption (ex-chief executive Peter Murrell’s £400k embezzlement) but offers no class-independent perspective on the national question; the implicit audience is the social-democratic left.

Assessment: Jacobin’s consistent orientation is channeling working-class anger through the Labour/Democratic Party left. No break with the parliamentary framework.

Left Voice

  • “The War Against Iran Shows the Failure of Western ‘Liberation’” — Critiques the imperialist framing of the Iran war as liberation, maintaining a clearer anti-war line than Jacobin and a more explicit break with the Democrats.
  • “U.S. Offensive Against Cuba: CPR-FI Condemns Military Aggression” — Condemns US threats against Cuba and calls for an end to the embargo, but characterizes Cuba as a deformed workers’ state without consistently calling for political revolution against the bureaucracy.

Assessment: Among the relative strengths of the pseudo-left on the war and on independence from the Democrats, but their FT-CI tradition remains a centrist current outside the ICFI, weak on permanent revolution and the class nature of Stalinist states.

PSL / Liberation News

  • “PSL Statement: Baseless Indictment of Raúl Castro a Pretext for Another War” — Defends the Cuban state apparatus and Raúl Castro against US pressure, collapsing the distinction between the Cuban working class and its bureaucratic ruling caste.
  • “Capitalism: The Real Cause of ‘Super El Niño’ Climate Catastrophes” — Names capitalism in PSL’s standard agitprop register but offers no perspective on independent working-class environmental struggle.

Assessment: A classic national-reformist program — uncritical defense of states it labels “anti-imperialist” (Cuba, Venezuela, Russia) regardless of class character, substituting statist campism for Marxist class analysis.

Socialist Alternative

  • “The Democrats Are Not the Answer to Fight the Right” — Rhetorically breaks with the Democrats, in tension with the ISA’s long record of subordinating independent politics to pressure on Democratic figures.

Assessment: More militant rhetoric than Jacobin, but the ISA/CWI tradition is built on entryism and popular-frontism, channeling movements through union bureaucracies and electoral pressure.

SWP (UK)

  • “Palestine Solidarity Wins Big in New York Boycott Vote” — Reports a Brooklyn co-op’s BDS endorsement as a major victory; SWP’s Palestine work is significant but systematically subordinated to Labour-left pressure politics.
  • “Children Abandoned as Private Firms Cash In on Care Crisis” — Anti-privatization agitation with no program beyond pressure on the Labour government.
  • “UCU Union Congress: Members Pledge to Fight Fascism and Defend Trans+ Rights” — A bureaucratic-pressure model, building resolutions within existing structures rather than mobilizing the rank and file independently of the TUC.

Assessment: The IST’s parent body, whose Stop the War work historically channeled anti-war sentiment into pressure on Labour rather than independent political action.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

  • “The 1926 General Strike: Britain’s Revolution Betrayed” — Marks the centenary, arguing the TUC leadership betrayed a revolutionary situation — a legitimate lesson central to Trotsky’s critique of the Comintern, though framed to serve the IMT’s own mass-recruitment project.

  • “Interview with Anti-Zionist Artist Matthew Collings on ‘Drawings Against Genocide’” — Consistent, clear opposition to the Gaza genocide without the Labour-management of the SWP.

Assessment: The RCP/IMT engages seriously with leadership betrayal and is firm on Palestine, but offers no reckoning with the ICFI’s analysis of Pabloism and the postwar liquidation of the Fourth International.
