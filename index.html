1. US-Iran peace talks collapse as Trump cancels Pakistan delegation

Mediated talks in Islamabad between Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and Pakistani leadership collapsed without result Friday, prompting Trump to cancel a planned 18-hour flight by envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner to Pakistan. Within 10 minutes of the cancellation, Iran submitted a new proposal that Trump publicly described as “much better,” leaving the negotiations in flux as the ceasefire is set to expire Wednesday evening Washington time — Day 58 of the US-Israel war on Iran.

Trump declared: “We have all the cards. They can call us anytime they want, but you’re not going to be making any more 18-hour flights to sit around talking about nothing.” Araghchi denounced the US naval blockade as “an act of war and thus a violation of the ceasefire,” threatening to close the Strait of Hormuz and strike regional energy and desalination infrastructure. As Andre Damon noted in his WSWS analysis, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has now declared the Iran blockade is “going global,” with the Treasury sanctioning a major Chinese oil refinery and 40 shipping companies for purchasing Iranian crude — escalating the war into a direct economic confrontation with China.

Key facts: - US naval blockade has redirected at least 33 ships since April 13; 26 “shadow fleet” vessels have breached the blockade line - Hegseth says blockade is now a “global operation” targeting Iranian-linked shipping in any ocean - Treasury sanctioned a Chinese refinery and 40 shipping firms over Iranian crude purchases - Brent crude jumped above $100/bbl for the first time in two weeks on the news

President Zelensky stated: “Russia’s tactics remain unchanged — attack drones, cruise missiles, and a significant number of ballistic missiles. Most targets are civilian infrastructure in cities.”

The strike pattern — overwhelmingly targeting civilian infrastructure in cities — continues a campaign explicitly designed to degrade Ukrainian morale and energy capacity ahead of the next phase of NATO escalation. The barrage came as Western governments continue to refuse direct negotiations with Moscow while pumping Ukraine full of long-range weaponry.

6. Ukraine marks 40th anniversary of Chernobyl under wartime conditions

Residents of Slavutych — the city built to house displaced Chernobyl workers — gathered in the central square at midnight for a candlelit vigil, defying wartime curfews and official warnings against mass gatherings to mark the 40th anniversary of the world’s worst nuclear disaster. Mourners laid candles on a radiation hazard symbol on the pavement. The explosion at Reactor No. 4 occurred on April 26, 1986; approximately 600,000 “liquidators” were mobilized across the Soviet Union to contain the disaster, many exposed to lethal radiation doses without adequate protection.

Bulgaria’s Kremlin-friendly ex-president Radev wins landslide

Former President Rumen Radev’s Progressive Bulgaria coalition won 43.9% of the vote in the snap parliamentary election held April 19–20, on 51.2% turnout — a landslide sufficient to form a unilateral government, the first such government to emerge in Bulgaria since the country fell into political deadlock in 2021. This was the eighth snap election in five years. Radev has previously called EU sanctions on Russia “counterproductive,” opposed sending weapons to Ukraine, and advocated resuming Russian gas imports. He received congratulatory messages from EU and Russian officials simultaneously and pledged a “pragmatic foreign policy.” Government formation continued through April 26.

Russia is described in European Council on Foreign Relations analysis as having “regained leverage inside the European Union” through the result — Bulgaria is both an EU and NATO member. The outcome reflects political disillusionment with EU austerity and the war drive, and signals a major Eurosceptic and pro-Russian breakthrough on the EU’s eastern flank.

US measles cases reach 1,792 — second-worst year since 2000

The CDC reports 1,792 confirmed measles cases across 22 outbreaks as of April 23, 2026, with 93% outbreak-associated. State leaders: South Carolina (667), Utah (408), Texas (176), Florida (129). The weekly growth rate has slowed sharply to ~0.4%, down from ~6% earlier in the year, but 2026 is already the second-worst measles year since 2000, trailing only 2025’s full-year total of 2,288 cases. The collapse of childhood vaccination rates in the wake of the Trump administration’s restructuring of CDC and HHS continues to threaten US measles elimination status — a designation the country has held since 2000.

H5N1 acquires cattle-specific NeuGc-binding mutations

Newly identified H5N1 mutations, reported through UNMC’s transmission tracking project, allow the virus to bind NeuGc, a sugar receptor found in cattle mammary glands but absent in humans and birds. The adaptation helps explain the virus’s persistence in dairy herds. The US has now recorded 71 total human A(H5) cases since February 2024, including one confirmed case of likely mammal-to-human transmission in Texas in early April. The cattle-specific adaptation is not expected to increase human transmissibility, but ongoing viral evolution in mammals remains the key pandemic risk.

“Cicada” BA.3.2 COVID variant in 31 US states

The heavily mutated BA.3.2 “Cicada” variant has now been detected in 31 US states and 33 countries, accounting for ~7% of national wastewater samples. CDC projections for the week ending April 25 show ~1,400 new COVID hospitalizations (range 750–3,100); the test-positivity rate as of early April was 1.8%, with COVID declining or likely declining in 37 states. Despite the novel variant’s spread, overall COVID burden remains very low — but the heavy mutation load warrants continued immune-evasion monitoring.

NEJM: Paxlovid fails in vaccinated patients; once-monthly obesity drug succeeds

The New England Journal of Medicine published two open-label trials this week showing nirmatrelvir–ritonavir (Paxlovid) did not reduce hospitalization or death among vaccinated, higher-risk COVID patients — a significant reassessment that challenges current prescribing guidelines for vaccinated populations. Separately, a Phase 2 trial of once-monthly maridebart cafraglutide (a GLP-1 class agent) demonstrated significant weight reduction on a monthly injection schedule — a major convenience advance over current weekly GLP-1 protocols.

DeepSeek V4 releases as Meta announces 8,000 layoffs

Chinese AI lab DeepSeek released its V4 model this week, claiming state-of-the-art open-source performance in reasoning and agentic tasks — sustaining pressure on US labs to keep flagship open-weight models competitive. The release came as Meta announced approximately 8,000 job cuts while simultaneously increasing AI infrastructure investment. Meanwhile, the bipartisan CHAT Act (SB 1700) passed its state legislature 31–0 in the Senate and 90–0 in the House, becoming one of the most broadly bipartisan AI safety bills enacted in 2026. The UN’s first Independent International Scientific Panel on AI is convening its inaugural in-person summit; Nobel laureate Geoffrey Hinton publicly described AI as “a fast car with no steering wheel.”

Michael Tilson Thomas (1944–2026)

The most significant cultural loss of the past 24 hours is the death of American conductor Michael Tilson Thomas at age 81. Tilson Thomas, a protégé of Leonard Bernstein, served as music director of the San Francisco Symphony from 1995 to 2020 and helped re-establish the Bay Area as a major center of American orchestral life. He was also founder of the New World Symphony in Miami Beach, an academy that trained an entire generation of American musicians.

Tilson Thomas’s significance lies less in any single recording than in his lifelong commitment to a democratic approach to concert culture. He worked deliberately to widen audiences — addressing them directly from the podium, championing accessible programming, and producing television and online documentaries that introduced classical repertoire to viewers far outside concert-hall demographics. His advocacy for Charles Ives in particular — a composer historically marginalized as eccentric — embodied a serious effort to recover the indigenous American tradition in classical music as an artistic patrimony belonging to working people, not merely to the donor class. As Fred Mazelis wrote in his WSWS obituary, Thomas’s career exemplified the broader humanist musical culture “under threat from commercial and political pressures.”

His death comes at a moment when American orchestras and arts institutions are confronting massive funding cuts under the Trump administration’s restructuring of the National Endowment for the Arts, the Kennedy Center, and federal cultural programs more broadly — a context in which Tilson Thomas’s model of public-facing serious music feels at once more urgent and more precarious.

Chernobyl at 40 — the cultural reckoning

Beyond the Slavutych vigil (see Top Stories), the 40th anniversary has produced a small but noteworthy wave of cultural projects. Chornobyl Children International opened an exhibition in Cork displaying artwork created by children of the affected region — a direct response to the elision of liquidator and survivor experience in the dominant Western narrative of Chernobyl as a tale of Soviet incompetence. The persistent Western dramatization of the disaster — most prominently in HBO’s 2019 Chernobyl — was almost entirely silent on the 600,000 working-class men who were drafted from across the USSR and exposed to radiation doses without warning, and on the systematic abandonment of those liquidators by post-Soviet states. The class-inflected truth of the disaster is one that anniversary coverage in 2026 has continued to bury.

State pageantry and cultural diplomacy

The King Charles state visit (see Top Stories) is itself a cultural event — and one that should be read with skepticism. The British monarchy is being deployed as a soft-power instrument to stabilize an Atlantic alliance fractured by Trump’s open hostility to Starmer over the Iran war. The deliberate scheduling around the Semiquincentennial ties American republican mythology to British monarchic ceremony in a way that reflects the deep ideological stagnation of the late-imperial Atlantic order — and the absence of any cultural form within either liberal democracy or the monarchic tradition that can credibly speak to the social crisis tearing both countries apart.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s Iran-war coverage continues to reflect the magazine’s established orientation: opposition to the war framed as a critique of “neoconservative excess” and a call for congressional pressure on the Democratic Party caucus, particularly Sanders, Khanna, and the Squad. Recent pieces have praised Senate war-powers resolutions and presented them as a viable mechanism to constrain the executive — a line that systematically obscures the Democratic Party’s actual role as a co-architect of the war (the Biden administration’s 2024–25 weapons transfers to Israel that prepared the ground for the present conflict, and current Democratic votes to fund DHS and continue military aid). DSA municipal endorsements continue to flow to candidates running on the Democratic ballot line. The political function: channeling anti-war sentiment back into the very party organizing the war.

Left Voice / Fraction of Trotskyists (PTS)

Left Voice has produced sharper coverage of the Iran war and the El Gamal case than Jacobin, correctly identifying both as expressions of US imperialism rather than departures from it. Recent pieces have called for student strikes against ICE and union action against weapons shipments. The political limitation: Left Voice’s framework remains a “self-organization of the oppressed” model that treats existing union leaderships as objects to be pressured rather than as organic components of the capitalist state apparatus that must be politically broken. There is no programmatic call for independent rank-and-file committees nor a serious analysis of the trade-union bureaucracy’s class function. The convergence with Jacobin appears in shared invocations of “the left” as if it were a single subject.

Liberation News / PSL

PSL coverage is dominated by call-outs for mass anti-war demonstrations — important and broadly correct in their immediate demands. The political content, however, is shaped by the PSL’s Stalinist heritage: the Islamic Republic of Iran is presented as a national-anti-imperialist state, the Russian Federation under Putin is treated as an objective counter-weight to NATO, and the class character of these regimes is systematically obscured. Recent articles have characterized Iranian opposition figures as “imperialist agents” without distinguishing between bourgeois-liberal opposition and the Iranian working class, which has its own independent reasons to oppose the regime. The framework converges with Russian and Chinese state-media talking points and offers no orientation for the Iranian or Russian working class.

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s output continues to push a “fighting trade-unionism” line that locates the strategic problem in individual union officials — Shawn Fain, Sean O’Brien — rather than in the bureaucracy as a social layer. Recent articles have endorsed AOC’s anti-war statements and Bernie Sanders’s town-hall tour as evidence of an emerging “working-class left,” collapsing the distinction between bourgeois-liberal critics of the war and an independent socialist movement. This is the longstanding ISA orientation: a “left” inside both the Democratic Party and the AFL-CIO, reformable from within. The political function is identical to Jacobin’s, though the rhetoric is more militant.

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

The SWP’s recent output has emphasized the Starmer Labour government’s refusal to join the US war on Iran as a positive development to be defended, rather than situating it within Labour’s broader project of austerity, anti-immigrant policy, and military rearmament. The convergence with the Labour Party’s left-wing parliamentary formations is explicit. The SWP has been notably silent on Labour’s £30 billion welfare cut and on the Reform UK breakthrough at the local level, treating the Iran-war stance as if it could be politically separated from the rest of the Starmer program.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The IMT’s split into the rebranded Revolutionary Communist Party continues to drive an aggressive recruitment line. Recent pieces use the Iran war and the Lebanon strikes as recruitment hooks, with the demand for “communism” deployed as a marketing slogan rather than a programmatic distinction. The IMT continues to refuse a serious accounting of its own historical orientation — particularly Alan Woods’s defenses of Hugo Chávez, Nicolás Maduro, and the post-Soviet Communist Party of the Russian Federation — that distinguish it sharply from a Trotskyist position. The combination of recruitment maximalism and political ambiguity remains the IMT’s hallmark.

1. Sudan famine and RSF drone strikes on hospitals

The collapse of food security across Sudan — 25 million in severe insecurity, 770,000 children at imminent risk of death — is being deliberately accelerated by an RSF drone-warfare campaign aimed at hospitals, markets, and residential areas. Western coverage relative to the Middle East war is negligible. The class dimension is acute: this is mass starvation in the world’s poorest country, weaponized by a paramilitary faction with documented Gulf-state and Wagner Group support, while the UN response is paralyzed by the same great-power deadlock that produced the Iran war.

2. Myanmar civil war: junta loses territorial control as air war escalates

The junta now controls fewer than 40% of townships but retains air superiority and is bombing hospitals, schools, and markets as a counter-insurgency tactic. ACLED’s 96,000 death toll since the 2021 coup makes this one of the most violent conflicts in Asia, yet WSWS coverage in 2026 has been thin. The story bears directly on the question of imperialist great-power competition in Southeast Asia and the role of ASEAN governments and Chinese state policy in propping up the regime.

Peru’s defense and foreign affairs ministers have resigned over the secretly negotiated Lockheed F-16 procurement; impeachment proceedings against Boluarte are now underway. The episode demonstrates how US arms-industry contracts are themselves a vector of political destabilization across Latin America — and Boluarte’s possible removal would mark a renewed cycle of crisis in a country where the SEP’s Latin American sister organizations have a developed perspective.

4. Bulgaria’s Radev landslide and the EU eastern flank

The April 19–20 election produced a 43.9% landslide for the pro-Russian Progressive Bulgaria coalition, ending years of political deadlock and producing the first single-party government since 2021. The result reflects mass disillusionment with the EU austerity project and the Ukraine war, and represents a major Eurosceptic-pro-Russian breakthrough inside both NATO and the EU. The WSWS analysis of the election would clarify the working-class basis of disillusionment without conceding ground to bourgeois-nationalist orientation.

5. Chernobyl liquidators 40 years on — abandoned by Soviet and post-Soviet states

The 40th anniversary coverage has overwhelmingly centered on wildlife in the exclusion zone or on the dramatic Western narrative of Soviet incompetence. Many surviving liquidators face chronic illness and inadequate state compensation. This is a deeply class-inflected story and one in which the WSWS’s distinctive analytical framework — the Stalinist degeneration of the Soviet workers’ state and the post-Soviet abandonment of social provision under capitalist restoration — would produce the only coherent account.

Coming alongside the Treasury sanctions on Chinese shipping companies and Hegseth’s “global blockade” doctrine, the carrier teaser represents the convergence of economic, financial, and military instruments of inter-imperialist conflict that the WSWS has consistently analyzed.
