1. Iran halts US negotiations and vows to fully block the Strait of Hormuz

Iran announced on June 1 that it is ending all communication with the United States through intermediaries and will move to completely seal the Strait of Hormuz, citing Israel’s expanding military operations in Lebanon and continued ceasefire violations as the trigger. The decision detonates a tentative diplomatic framework that had taken shape over the preceding days and threatens a renewed oil-supply crisis.

A tentative 60-day memorandum of understanding had reportedly been reached. Under its terms, Iran would remove all mines from the Strait within 30 days, guarantee “unrestricted” shipping, and commit to not pursuing a nuclear weapon, in exchange for US sanctions waivers permitting Iran to sell oil freely. Trump never signed it. A US official said “the president relayed to the mediators that he wants a couple of days to think about it,” and Trump reportedly altered the deal’s language over the weekend on enriched-uranium terms and on the Strait of Hormuz provisions.

Key facts:

  • Oil prices jumped more than 7% within hours of Iran’s announcement.
  • The Strait of Hormuz has been largely blocked since February 28, 2026, when the US and Israel launched the air war against Iran and assassinated Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei.
  • Iran cited Israel’s seizure of Beaufort Castle and its push beyond the Litani as among the reasons for breaking off talks.
  • The April 12 ceasefire between the US and Iran remains nominally in effect despite repeated armed exchanges.

The collapse confirms the WSWS analysis that the “negotiations” were never aimed at a stable settlement but at managing a war of imperialist plunder against Iran. Washington’s insistence on rewriting terms even as a deal was within reach reflects a ruling class that treats the lives of millions in the region — and the global working class facing an energy shock — as bargaining chips.

2. US carries out fresh strikes on Iran as IRGC fires ballistic missiles at US forces in Kuwait

US fighter jets struck Iranian air defenses, a ground control station, and two one-way attack drones over the weekend of May 25–26 at Goruk and on Qeshm Island, following a May 25 strike on Iranian missile launch sites and boats laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz. The strikes, the Pentagon said, were triggered by Iran shooting down a US MQ-1 drone over international waters.

Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps responded by launching two ballistic missiles at US forces in Kuwait on Sunday evening; both were intercepted by US air defenses. The IRGC separately claimed its aerospace force struck a US telecommunications tower on Sirik Island in Hormozgan province.

Key facts:

  • US CENTCOM described its strikes as “measured and deliberate”; no US troops were reported injured.
  • The exchanges occurred while the April 12 ceasefire remained formally in place.
  • The cycle of “self-defense” strikes and Iranian retaliation underscores that the ceasefire exists only on paper.

The pattern of escalating “self-defense” justifications echoes the open-ended legal framework Washington has used across the Middle East for two decades to normalize permanent war.

3. Israel seizes Crusader-era Beaufort Castle and drives its offensive beyond the Litani River

Israeli forces captured the 12th-century Beaufort Castle (Qal’at al-Shaqif) near Nabatiyeh in southern Lebanon on May 31 — roughly 14.5 km from the Israeli border — and raised the Israeli flag over it. Netanyahu called the seizure “a dramatic change” in the operation and ordered troops to push deeper, beyond the Litani River, in open violation of the April ceasefire.

This is Israel’s deepest military push into Lebanon in 26 years; Israel previously held the castle during its 18-year occupation that ended in 2000. UNESCO classifies Beaufort as “one of the best-preserved examples of medieval castles in the Near East.”

Key facts:

  • France issued a “strong condemnation” of the operation.
  • The escalation is among the key triggers Iran cited for breaking off negotiations with the US.
  • More than 2,000 civilians and combatants have been killed in the Lebanon war; the offensive has destroyed a swathe of villages and signals an extended Israeli occupation.

The drive beyond the Litani reflects the Netanyahu government’s strategy — backed materially by Washington — of redrawing the regional map by force.

4. Gaza death toll passes 75,800 as post-ceasefire killings mount

The cumulative death toll from the assault on Gaza and the wider conflict reached at least 75,811 as of early May — more than 73,770 Palestinians and over 2,039 Israelis since October 2023, according to the Gaza Health Ministry and the Israeli foreign ministry. The October 2025 ceasefire left more than half of Gaza’s territory under Israeli military control, with Hamas controlling the remainder.

Key facts:

  • At least 738 Palestinians have been killed since the October 2025 ceasefire took effect, including at least 200 since February 28, 2026.
  • UN Human Rights Chief Volker Türk: “The unrelenting pattern of killings reflects continuing disregard for Palestinian lives, enabled by sweeping impunity.”
  • Israeli airstrikes, shelling, and drone attacks continue daily across Gaza.
  • A UN Commission of Inquiry noted that the wider regional war has “eclipsed” but not ended the surge in violations against Palestinians.

The aggregate figure — rarely presented as a whole by Western outlets, which report incident by incident — is the measure of a genocide carried out with the direct material and diplomatic backing of the United States and its allies.

5. US-imposed Ukraine peace deadline passes with no deal

The Trump administration’s June 2026 deadline for Ukraine and Russia to reach a peace agreement has arrived with no settlement. US-brokered trilateral talks in Abu Dhabi yielded no breakthrough on the core sticking points: territory, ceasefire lines, control of the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant, and security guarantees against future Russian invasion.

Key facts:

  • Russia demanded Ukraine withdraw from the Donbas — a condition Kyiv says it will never accept — and presented Washington with a “$12 trillion economic proposal” (the “Dmitriev package”).
  • A prisoner exchange was completed: Russia returned 157 captured Ukrainian service members; Ukraine returned 150 Russian POWs.
  • Russia carried out its largest single aerial barrage of the war in mid-May after a brief ceasefire expired, killing at least two people in Kyiv.
  • Zelenskyy confirmed in February that “the Americans are proposing the parties end the war by the beginning of this summer.”

The deadlock raises the prospect of Washington imposing a territorial capitulation on Kyiv or abandoning its proxy outright — with the consequences, as always, borne by Ukrainian and Russian workers.

6. Ethiopia votes amid armed conflict and the exclusion of Tigray

Ethiopia held its general election on June 1, with more than 50.5 million registered voters across Africa’s second-most-populous nation. Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed’s Prosperity Party, which won 410 of 484 parliamentary seats in 2021, is expected to dominate again; results are expected by June 11.

Key facts:

  • No voting was held in the entire Tigray region or in 30 constituencies in Amhara, with the National Electoral Board citing “unfavourable conditions” due to continued armed conflict.
  • The Oromo Liberation Army is fighting in Oromia; the Fano armed group has controlled Amhara countryside since 2023.
  • Observers from the African Union Election Observation Mission are present.
  • Critics describe the vote as “not a vote — it is a verdict on a collapsing state,” citing opposition suppression and Abiy’s reported plans to shift to a presidential system.

The exercise functions less as a democratic contest than as a legitimizing spectacle for a state at war with large parts of its own population.

New Zealand budget deepens austerity to fund rearmament

New Zealand Finance Minister Nicola Willis’s May 28 budget delivers a 1.3% operating spending increase — well below the 3.1% and rising inflation rate — while cutting roughly 8,700 public-sector jobs by mid-2029 to save $2.4 billion, raising tertiary fees, and imposing a real freeze on healthcare funding. The budget reveals that 17.8% of children live in post-housing-cost poverty, with material hardship rising from 10.6% to 14.3% since 2022. Labour leader Chris Hipkins said the government “has given New Zealanders nothing to ease the pressure they are under.”

At the same time, Wellington committed to doubling the military budget to 2% of GDP — a figure US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared insufficient, demanding 3.5%. As Tom Peters noted on the WSWS, both the Labour opposition and the Greens, themselves recently in government, have offered no credible alternative to making the working class pay for rearmament.

Germany’s economic experts demand a “U-turn” on care, health and pensions

Germany’s Council of Economic Experts spring report, published May 27, calls for reforming long-term care insurance, eliminating benefit supplements for residents who cannot afford care homes, and imposing stricter asset-depletion requirements before insurance assistance begins. Council chair Monika Schnitzer argued care insurance “is intended to prevent social hardship, but not to relieve people of their responsibility to contribute their own funds.” The total social-insurance contribution rate stands at 42.3% in 2026 and could approach 50% by 2040 under current law.

The WSWS juxtaposes this against a Boston Consulting Group finding that Germany’s 5,000 ultra-wealthy individuals (assets above €86 million) now control 27.3% of the country’s €10.6 trillion in financial wealth, with annual wealth growth of roughly €1.5 trillion — enough to fund long-term care insurance for two decades. The DGB unions have avoided major strikes for more than 20 years.

Bondi attacker was on ASIO radar three years before Hanukkah massacre

As Australia’s Royal Commission into Antisemitism and Social Cohesion resumed, ASIO chief Mike Burgess confirmed that Naveed Akram — one of the attackers in the December 14 Bondi Beach Hanukkah attack that killed 15 — had been placed in a “Known Entity Management Framework” database in 2022, three years before the attack, following a 2019 investigation tied to his association with an Islamic State supporter. Burgess conceded in a written statement that he should have noted the agency’s general concern about terrorism and antisemitic conduct; ASIO’s December summer threat assessment did not address warnings about Jewish celebrations as targets.

The Akrams traveled to Uzbekistan in 2022, a trip with possible IS-K proximity, and Sajid Akram obtained a gun licence in 2023 despite a three-year delay. The WSWS raises whether continuous monitoring served intelligence-gathering rather than threat-prevention purposes — a question with disturbing parallels to numerous prior terror cases in which perpetrators were known to security agencies.

Federal judge orders Trump’s name off the Kennedy Center

US District Judge Christopher Cooper ruled May 29 that adding Trump’s name to the Kennedy Center violated a 1964 federal law establishing the institution as “The John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts.” Cooper wrote: “Congress gave the Kennedy Center its name, and only Congress can change it,” ordering the name removed within two weeks. He also blocked a planned two-year closure for renovations that critics said would have effectively shuttered the institution under executive control.

The lawsuit was filed by Representative Joyce Beatty. Interior Secretary Doug Burgum said Trump’s name “won’t be coming off,” signaling defiance, while Trump claimed he has “no interest” in the overhaul and would return control to Congress.

More than 10,000 federal lawyers — one in five — have left since 2024

Roughly one in five lawyers who worked for the federal government at the end of 2024 had departed by March 2026 — an exodus exceeding 10,000 attorneys spanning the DOJ, regulatory agencies, and executive departments. The pace represents an unprecedented hollowing-out of the legal infrastructure underpinning federal enforcement and regulation.

The walkout is attributed to political pressure, ideological purges, and reassignments under the administration’s restructuring of the executive branch. Critics warn it strips agencies of the expertise required to enforce environmental, civil rights, labor, antitrust, and consumer protection law — precisely the agencies that exist to constrain corporate power. No official breakdown by agency or cause has been released.

US measles cases near 2,000 as elimination status hangs by a thread

As of May 28, the US has recorded 1,983 confirmed measles cases across 40 jurisdictions, with 93% (1,847 cases) linked to active outbreaks and 30 new outbreaks declared this year. The 2026 total is on pace to exceed 2025’s full-year count of 2,288 — itself a 30-year high — placing the country at serious risk of losing the CDC-certified elimination status it has held since 2000. H5N1 bird flu has now produced 71 confirmed US human cases since 2024, with two deaths; a new review concludes infections can be asymptomatic and that low-level human-to-human spread is “likely,” raising fears of undercounting.

Sources CDC Measles data and research · CIDRAP H5N1 cases can be asymptomatic, spread likely

First documented human H5N5 infection reported in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine published a correspondence documenting the world’s first confirmed human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). The patient was a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman who presented with fever and diarrhea; standard influenza A RT-PCR tests came back negative despite a high viral load in the lower respiratory tract, exposing a serious diagnostic gap. A novel H5 subtype crossing into humans — and undetectable by standard rapid tests — underscores the pandemic risk posed by the expanding H5 clade.

Sources NEJM Human infection with avian influenza A(H5N5)

Finland opens Onkalo, the world’s first permanent nuclear waste repository

Finland’s Onkalo repository — “cave” in Finnish — began operational licensing on June 1 after the Finnish Radiation and Nuclear Safety Authority (STUK) issued its final assessment. Blasted 430 meters into 1.9-billion-year-old stable bedrock on the island of Olkiluoto, the €1 billion facility is the first permanent geological disposal site for high-level nuclear waste anywhere in the world. Spent fuel will be deposited for up to 100 years, then sealed for at least 100,000 years — longer than recorded human civilization has existed.

Arts and culture

  • The Smithsonian and Kennedy Center battles crystallize a broader executive assault on cultural institutions.
  • A wave of American art-school closures lays bare the subordination of culture to profit.

The clearest cultural story of the day is the deepening political battle over America’s public cultural institutions. The federal court ruling restoring the John F. Kennedy Center’s name (covered above) coincides with the Smithsonian’s release of “American Aspirations,” a major exhibition co-curated by Secretary Lonnie G. Bunch III as the institution faces sustained federal pressure over the content of its displays. Taken together, these episodes mark an attempt by the executive branch to bend national cultural memory to a nationalist program — and the resistance, however legalistic, of institutions and the public to that effort.

The WSWS today connects this attack to the structural crisis of arts education in “Why are art schools disappearing in America?” The article documents a wave of closures — the Oregon College of Art and Craft (founded 1907, closed 2019), Memphis College of Art (1936–2020), the San Francisco Art Institute (1871–2022), the University of the Arts Philadelphia (2024), and the end of degree programs at the Pennsylvania Academy of Fine Arts (2025). It traces these to the financialization of higher education, administrative bloat (administrative positions up 369% from 1976–2011 versus 23% for tenure-track faculty), the collapse of public funding from roughly 80% to 50% of university revenue, and federal loan-eligibility rules that punish degrees with lower graduate earnings. Culture, the authors conclude, is being abandoned wherever it cannot be made to turn a profit.

US murder spree in Latin American waters moves beyond 200 killed — Andrea Lobo

The Perspective documents the latest escalation of a US military campaign that began September 2, 2025 in the Caribbean, expanded to the Eastern Pacific in October, and has now killed more than 205 people across roughly 60 destroyed vessels. In the most recent strike — the fourth in a single week — the US military released video of a small boat erupting in flames in the Eastern Pacific, killing three men.

Lobo’s central argument is that, under the cover of counter-narcotics operations, the Trump administration has routinized extrajudicial mass killing as settled imperialist policy — “mass murder turned into a daily, bureaucratic operation.” No victim has been named, no evidence of alleged crimes released, and no legal framework offered. Survivors and relatives identify many of the dead as ordinary fishermen and migrant laborers; entire fishing communities along the coasts of Colombia and Ecuador have abandoned their livelihoods. The article details a documented “double-tap” strike in which two survivors clinging to debris were deliberately killed in a second strike — a war crime under the Fourth Geneva Convention — reportedly on Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth’s personal order that no survivors be permitted.

Lobo situates the campaign within three US imperial objectives: reasserting Monroe Doctrine dominance over Latin America after years of Chinese infrastructure investment; intimidating governments contemplating closer ties to Beijing; and targeting the corridor around Peru’s Chancay port, a Chinese-developed Pacific hub. The uniform silence of Latin American governments — right-wing and nominally “Pink Tide” alike — confirms, she argues, Trotsky’s Theory of Permanent Revolution: only the independent international working class, not any section of the regional bourgeoisie, can defend democratic and national rights against imperialism.

The WSWS profiles four anniversaries. 25 years ago: the June 1, 2001 massacre of Nepal’s royal family by Crown Prince Dipendra, set against King Birendra’s own record of killing pro-democracy protesters in 1990. 50 years ago: the June 5, 1976 collapse of Idaho’s Teton Dam, built — over US Geological Survey warnings — on unsuitable fractured volcanic rock; 11 people died and communities were destroyed, with no criminal charges filed. 75 years ago: the June 4, 1951 Supreme Court ruling in Dennis v. United States upholding Smith Act convictions of 11 CPUSA leaders for studying Marxist texts, with Justices Black and Douglas dissenting against unconstitutional prior censorship. 100 years ago: the June 1, 1926 assumption of the Polish presidency by Ignacy Mościcki as figurehead for Piłsudski’s authoritarian “Sanacja” coup — a putsch facilitated by the Polish Communist Party’s disastrous Stalinist support.

Pseudo-left press review

  • Jacobin / DSA — frames the crisis as “political capitalism” and rehabilitates the CPUSA, evading the question of working-class political independence.
  • Left Voice — sharply criticizes Mamdani from the left while still orienting workers toward defending the “Mamdani moment.”
  • Liberation News / PSL — anti-imperialist phrasemongering coupled with uncritical promotion of bourgeois-nationalist regimes.
  • Socialist Worker / SWP (UK) — channels industrial militancy back into pressure on the Labour government and the union apparatus.
  • Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT) — abstract “communist” propaganda detached from a concrete strategy in the unions.

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin leads with “The USA Is Living Under Political Capitalism,” arguing that contemporary capitalism prioritizes wealth extraction through “political maneuvering” and monopoly rent rather than competitive markets. The framing of “political capitalism” as a distinct phase substitutes a moralizing critique of “rent-seeking” for an analysis of the capitalist mode of production itself — implying the problem is bad-faith manipulation rather than the profit system, and pointing toward regulatory reform rather than expropriation.

“American Communists Did a Lot Right and a Lot Wrong” offers a soft rehabilitation of the CPUSA, treating its members as “more complicated than the stereotypes.” Tellingly, the piece elides the central historical lesson — the CPUSA’s Stalinist subordination of the working class to the Roosevelt Democrats and the Popular Front, the very orientation the DSA reproduces today. “Emmanuel Macron Has Boosted France’s Corporate Welfare State” correctly skewers Macron’s largesse to corporate “rentiers” but, consistent with Jacobin’s house line, frames the remedy as a fairer distribution of state subsidies rather than a struggle against the capitalist state.

Assessment: Jacobin’s output reinforces its function as the theoretical workshop of the DSA’s left-Democratic project — recasting the crisis of capitalism as a problem of policy capture, soluble within the existing order.

Left Voice

Left Voice continues its pointed criticism of Zohran Mamdani in “Why Is Zohran Mamdani Endorsing Strikebreaker Kathy Hochul?” and “No, Zohran, Jessica Tisch Has Not Made New York Safer for the City’s Working Class,” attacking his endorsement of the governor and his retention of a billionaire police commissioner. These pieces are sharper than anything in the DSA press. Yet “Fight the Attacks on Mamdani and All Immigrants with Mobilizations and Class Struggle” reveals the contradiction: having exposed Mamdani’s pro-capitalist trajectory, Left Voice still calls to defend him against the right, channeling workers back toward the “Mamdani moment” rather than toward a decisive political break with the Democratic Party.

Assessment: Left Voice oscillates between trenchant criticism and a centrist refusal to draw the organizational conclusion — that the DSA’s project is a trap, requiring an independent revolutionary party, not pressure on its left wing.

Liberation News / PSL

The PSL’s recent output marks the 78th anniversary of the Nakba with a pledge to “intensify efforts to end U.S. aid to Israel,” argues that capitalism is the cause of climate catastrophe, and issued a statement on Raúl Castro alongside reflections from a Pan-Africanism summit in Nairobi. The anti-imperialist rhetoric is consistent, but the PSL’s method substitutes cheerleading for bourgeois-nationalist and Stalinist regimes for a perspective of independent working-class struggle — presenting capitalist states as anti-imperialist allies rather than as obstacles to the international unity of workers.

Assessment: The PSL’s campism leads it to subordinate the working class to one camp of bourgeois states against another, the opposite of an internationalist socialist strategy.

Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

The SWP highlights “College workers back national fight over pay and workloads,” “Labour’s dystopian AI plan to clamp down on refugees,” and “Green council backs shoebox flats in Bristol housing scandal.” The reporting captures genuine working-class grievances and Labour’s reactionary turn on immigration. But the SWP’s perspective routinely terminates in calls for more militant pressure on the union leaders and the Labour “left,” rather than in building an independent leadership against the bureaucracy that contains these struggles.

Assessment: The SWP documents the militancy of the rank and file while leaving intact the apparatus that strangles it.

Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The IMT’s “The ‘Epstein class’ exposed: how the ruling class rules,” “A tough pill to swallow: the decline of Britain’s pharmaceuticals sector,” and “The trade union leaders’ pitiful response to the centenary of the General Strike” marry sweeping denunciations of the ruling class with abstract calls for “communism.” The General Strike piece correctly indicts the TUC’s 1926 betrayal but offers no concrete strategy for the present struggle in the unions, reflecting the RCP’s substitution of recruitment-oriented propaganda for intervention in the actual class struggle.

Assessment: The IMT’s relaunched “communist” branding remains detached from a worked-out program in the workplaces and unions.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The exodus of 10,000 federal lawyers and the dismantling of enforcement capacity

More than one in five federal attorneys have left government since 2024, hollowing out the agencies that enforce labor, civil rights, environmental, and antitrust law. Framed by the press as a “brain drain,” this is in substance the deliberate removal of legal constraints on capital — a structural dimension of the authoritarian transformation of the state that warrants Marxist analysis.

Recommended reading Just Security Early Edition: June 1, 2026

2. Ethiopia’s election as a legitimizing spectacle for a state at war with its people

Uniformly reported as a democratic exercise, Ethiopia’s June 1 vote excluded all of Tigray and 30 Amhara constituencies amid active wars with the Oromo Liberation Army and Fano. A socialist analysis would examine whom the ballot legitimizes, whom it silences, and Abiy’s reported maneuvering to consolidate a presidential system.

Recommended reading Ethiopia Panorama Ethiopia’s June 1 election is not a vote — it is a verdict on a collapsing state

3. The working-class price of the Iran war: fuel, heat, and food

Beyond Brent benchmarks, the energy shock is devastating workers worldwide — Asian and European gas up 59–94% year-on-year, fertilizer disruption driving food inflation, real wages eaten away while energy majors book windfalls. The class question of who pays and who profits is absent from mainstream coverage.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera Oil prices soar on fears of long supply disruption

4. Onkalo and the 100,000-year liability capitalism hands to future generations

The opening of the world’s first permanent nuclear waste repository is celebrated as a technological triumph, but it privatizes decades of nuclear-power profit while socializing a 100,000-year stewardship burden onto the Finnish state and future generations — a case study in who bears the costs of capitalist energy production.

Recommended reading France 24 In Finland, radioactive spent nuclear fuel soon to be buried underground

5. First human H5N5 case and the pandemic risk of a hollowed-out public health system

The first documented human H5N5 infection — undetectable by standard influenza tests — arrives as US measles nears 2,000 cases and surveillance budgets are slashed. The convergence of an expanding H5 clade, diagnostic gaps, and the destruction of public health infrastructure poses a danger the WSWS is uniquely positioned to analyze.

Recommended reading NEJM Human infection with avian influenza A(H5N5)

6. The executive assault on the Smithsonian and the battle over cultural memory

The Smithsonian’s “American Aspirations” exhibition, released under federal pressure, alongside the Kennedy Center renaming fight, signals a coordinated drive to subordinate national cultural institutions to a nationalist program. This belongs in the same frame as the WSWS analysis of disappearing art schools.

Recommended reading Smithsonian Magazine Smart News: Arts and Culture
