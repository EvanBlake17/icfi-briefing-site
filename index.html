1. Trump rejects Tehran peace proposal as Iran war enters 64th day

Sixty-four days after the United States and Israel launched the February 28 surprise strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and other senior Iranian officials, President Donald Trump rejected Tehran’s latest peace proposal on May 1, telling reporters that Iran’s offer contained things “I can’t agree to.” Iran’s military responded that it is “fully prepared for any new adventures or foolishness from the Americans,” and Israeli strikes continued on Lebanon’s Habboush area.

The war has now produced preliminary death tolls of approximately 3,375 killed in Iran (including 376 children), roughly 2,509 in Lebanon on the Hezbollah-Israel front, and 28 in Gulf states. The Pentagon has priced the conflict at $25 billion to date. The US national average gasoline price hit $4.30/gallon, a four-year high, as the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed.

Key facts: - Trump has called those who say the United States is not winning the war “treasonous” - The IDF has reported dropping more than 12,000 bombs across Iran and 3,600 on Tehran alone, including a March 9 strike on a residential building that killed 40–50 people - As David North argued in his May Day Rally speech (see WSWS coverage), the war flows from “capitalism’s inherent contradictions between private ownership and social production, and between global economic integration and nation-states” — not from any single faction’s lobbying power - Johannes Stern, in a parallel Humboldt University lecture, warned that the Berlin government has used the war as cover to accelerate a 64% increase in German military spending between 2022 and 2028

Sources Al Jazeera Iran war live: Trump says no early end to war, unhappy with Tehran offer · CNN Iran war Day 63 live updates · PBS NewsHour May 1 full episode · WSWS Capitalist crisis, war and the international class struggle · WSWS Stop the war against Iran!

2. Senate blocks Iran war powers resolution for the sixth time, blowing past the 60-day deadline

The Senate voted 47–50 on May 1 to reject S.J.Res.59, the war powers resolution that would have directed the removal of US forces from unauthorized hostilities against Iran. It was the sixth such defeat since the war began, and it landed on the exact 60-day deadline established by the 1973 War Powers Resolution, which legally requires the president to obtain congressional authorization for sustained hostilities.

Only two Republicans broke ranks: Susan Collins (Maine), voting yes for the first time, and Rand Paul (Kentucky). Legal scholars across the political spectrum now openly state that the administration is conducting the war without lawful authority. Trump has nevertheless escalated his rhetoric, denouncing critics as “treasonous.”

Key facts: - The 1973 statute was passed over Nixon’s veto in the wake of the secret bombing of Cambodia, precisely to prevent indefinite executive war-making - The Senate’s failure to assert its constitutional prerogative comes alongside continued Democratic procedural cooperation — the same Democratic caucus, as Jacob Crosse documents on the WSWS, structured the DHS funding deal so Republicans can deliver $70 billion to ICE and CBP via reconciliation - The vote effectively cedes the executive branch unbounded authority to wage open-ended foreign war

Sources Democracy Now! Senate Republicans block Iran war powers resolution for sixth time · Time Senate rejects measure to curb Iran war hours before key legal deadline · The Hill Iran war powers resolution rejected

3. UN warns of global recession as Strait of Hormuz transits collapse 90 percent

UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned on May 1 that the closure of the Strait of Hormuz is “strangling the global economy,” with ship transits down more than 90 percent since late February and Brent crude hovering around $118 a barrel. In a medium-disruption scenario, Guterres projected 32 million people will be pushed into poverty and 45 million more will face extreme hunger this year. In the worst-case scenario — sustained disruptions through year-end — the UN warns of a full global recession with “dramatic impacts on people, economy, and political stability.”

The IMF baseline now projects global growth of only 3.1 percent in 2026 against headline inflation of 4.4 percent. The World Bank, as Nick Beams details on the WSWS, has documented a 24 percent jump in its energy price index this year (40 percent above January forecasts), urea fertilizer prices surging 50 percent to post-2022 highs, and aluminum, copper, and tin reaching all-time highs for a third consecutive year. The Strait normally carries 34 percent of global seaborne crude oil, 50 percent of sulfur, and 29 percent of LPG.

Key facts: - Southeast and South Asia are bearing the initial burden; energy shortages have now spread into Europe - World Bank Chief Economist Indermit Gill: an extended conflict with oil above $100/barrel “could push up to 45 million more people into acute food insecurity this year” - The class character of the crisis is unmistakable: the working class of the Global South is being asked to absorb the price of US imperialism’s decision to launch this war

Sources UN News Guterres warns Hormuz crisis is strangling global economy · IMF War darkens global economic outlook and reshapes policy priorities · WSWS World Bank details Iran war global commodity shock

4. Hegseth orders 5,000 US troops withdrawn from Germany after Merz criticizes Iran war

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth on May 1 ordered the withdrawal of approximately 5,000 US troops from Germany over the next 6–12 months — one brigade combat team will depart, and a long-range fires battalion previously scheduled to deploy to Germany will be reassigned. Germany currently hosts roughly 35,000 active-duty US personnel, more than any other European country.

The decision came in direct retaliation for German Chancellor Friedrich Merz’s public statement that the United States was being “humiliated” by Iran and his criticism of Washington’s lack of a coherent war strategy. Trump had threatened the withdrawal earlier in the week, framing it as a personal punishment of Berlin.

Key facts: - The withdrawal undoes much of the eastern-flank buildup put in place after Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine - Germany is concurrently raising defense spending 64 percent between 2022 and 2028, partly justified by the very security vacuum Washington is now creating - The episode marks one of the sharpest open ruptures inside NATO since the alliance’s founding, exposing the imperialist rivalries among the great powers that the alliance has long sought to paper over

5. Ukrainian drones hit Tuapse refinery for fourth time in 17 days; Russian capacity at 16-year low

Ukrainian drones struck Russia’s Tuapse oil refinery on May 1, the fourth strike in 17 days following hits on April 16, April 20, and April 28. Russia’s average refinery processing capacity has now fallen to 4.69 million barrels per day — the lowest level since December 2009, a 16-year low. No casualties were reported in the May 1 strike, and local officials confirmed drone origin.

NBC News, citing on-the-ground reporting from the Black Sea port, described an environmental catastrophe: boiling oil products spilled onto streets after the third strike, damaging vehicles, with residents observing “black rain” downwind. Russia’s Emergency Situations Ministry had claimed fires were extinguished on April 29 — only for fresh strikes on April 30 and May 1 to reignite them.

Key facts: - The strikes are part of a sustained Ukrainian campaign aimed at degrading Russia’s oil-export economy - Workers and civilians in Tuapse — a town of roughly 60,000 — are bearing the toxic public-health consequences of an inter-imperialist proxy war whose architects sit in Washington, Brussels, Kyiv, and Moscow - Russia’s refining shortfall is one factor pushing global oil prices higher even as the Strait of Hormuz disruption dominates headlines

6. Trump signs bill ending record 75-day DHS shutdown — but ICE and CBP funding deferred to reconciliation

Trump signed legislation on April 30 ending the longest partial government shutdown in US history at 75 days. The bill funds the Transportation Security Administration, FEMA, the Coast Guard, and Secret Service through September. It explicitly excludes Immigration and Customs Enforcement and Customs and Border Protection — a structural choice, not an oversight.

The Coast Guard had been operating in what its leadership called “crisis mode,” with halted pay, reduced power, and strained overseas missions. Airports across the country experienced prolonged TSA security delays; staffing remains depleted. The Republican infighting that drove the impasse centered on whether to fund immigration enforcement separately from the rest of DHS.

As Jacob Crosse reports on the WSWS, Senate Democrats led by Chuck Schumer deliberately structured the deal so that ICE and CBP funding will move via the budget reconciliation process. Republicans intend to deliver approximately $70 billion to immigration enforcement over three years (on top of $170 billion previously allocated), while Democrats can claim procedural distance from that vote — political theater masking genuine bipartisan partnership in building the deportation state.

Key facts: - Tens of thousands of federal workers — many lower-paid Coast Guard, TSA, and FEMA personnel — went 75 days without pay - The bill funds standard DHS operations through September; immigration enforcement now becomes a separate political fight, exactly as Republicans wanted - The shutdown’s resolution comes just weeks after high-profile killings by ICE officers in Minneapolis triggered renewed mass anti-deportation protests

7. Supreme Court strikes down Louisiana’s second majority-Black congressional district

The Supreme Court on May 1 struck down Louisiana’s second majority-Black congressional district as an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. The district had been created under court order following prior Voting Rights Act litigation, and the ruling effectively voids that earlier remedy. Multiple legal analysts described the decision as a “significant blow” to the Voting Rights Act and as continuing the current majority’s pattern of narrowing Section 2 enforcement.

Key facts: - Louisiana’s Black population is approximately 33 percent of the state - The decision deepens a multi-year pattern of judicial rollback of voting rights, alongside the gutting of Section 5 preclearance in Shelby County v. Holder (2013) and the Court’s 2023 Section 2 jurisprudence - The ruling lands amid an executive branch claim of unbounded war power abroad and an unprecedented domestic deportation apparatus — a combined assault on the formal mechanisms of bourgeois democracy

8. Cole Tomas Allen charged with attempted assassination of Trump after Correspondents’ Dinner attack

Cole Tomas Allen, 31, of Torrance, California, was arraigned May 1 on charges of attempted assassination of the president, interstate transport of firearms with intent to commit a felony, and discharging a firearm during a crime of violence. The April 25 attack at the White House Correspondents’ Dinner saw Allen, armed with guns and knives, breach security and shoot a Secret Service officer in the chest. The officer’s ballistic vest absorbed the round; the officer returned fire and wounded Allen, who was taken into custody. Allen will remain jailed pending trial and faces up to life in prison.

Key facts: - Federal prosecutors have released surveillance video showing Allen running through security - A message attributed to Allen described himself as a “Friendly Federal Assassin” and cited grievances against Trump administration policies - Allen worked as a part-time tutor and amateur game developer - This is the second known assassination attempt against Trump in the post-2024 period; the rapid emergence of such acts in a polarized, militarized political climate signals deeper instability in the bourgeois state

Aung San Suu Kyi transferred from prison to house arrest in Myanmar

Myanmar state television announced May 1 that Aung San Suu Kyi has been transferred from prison to house arrest. The 80-year-old former leader has been imprisoned since the February 2021 military coup — over five years. Senior General Min Aung Hlaing, who led the coup and assumed the presidency through a military-organized election held amid the ongoing civil war, commuted her “remaining sentence to be served at the designated residence.” Her original sentence was reduced by one-sixth to 18 years and 9 months; she still has more than 13 years to serve.

The move was timed to coincide with the Full Moon Day of Kasone, a major Buddhist holiday. Analysts read it as a public-relations gesture by a junta seeking domestic and international legitimacy while resistance forces continue to hold large parts of the country. Her son Kim Aris stated bluntly: “Moving her is not freeing her.” Her lawyers have not been able to confirm the transfer or contact her.

Sweden’s prime minister commits to a coalition with the fascist Sweden Democrats

Prime Minister Ulf Kristersson announced that a victory by his right-wing bloc in the September 13 parliamentary election would bring the Sweden Democrats — a party with neo-Nazi origins — directly into cabinet, with what he termed “major policy influence and important ministerial posts in migration and integration.” This is a qualitative escalation from the SD’s current outside-support arrangement. The right-wing bloc’s combined support has fallen from 49.5 percent in 2022 to 44.7 percent, while the opposition leads at 53.4 percent. The Liberals — who abandoned their longstanding SD veto — have collapsed to 2.2 percent, below the 4 percent parliamentary threshold.

As Jon Andersson notes on the WSWS, the Left Party has facilitated this trajectory through parliamentary abstentions, support for NATO membership, and acquiescence to a 64 percent rise in Swedish defense spending between 2022 and 2028. The political mechanism by which a fascist party reaches cabinet is constructed from the failures of the official “left.”

Brazil’s PSTU president sentenced to two years for criticizing Israel

A São Paulo court has sentenced José Maria de Almeida (“Zé Maria”), president of the Brazilian Workers’ Party (PSTU), to two years in prison for remarks made at an October 2023 protest. Almeida had stated that “every act of force, every act of violence by the Palestinian people against Zionism is legitimate” and called for a secular democratic Palestine “from the river to the sea.” Judge Massimo Palazzolo ruled the speech contained “degrading, generalizing and prejudicial content” under Brazil’s racism statute, following complaints from the Israelite Confederation of Brazil.

The Socialist Equality Group (Brazil) statement situates the conviction in a global pattern of criminalizing anti-war and anti-Zionist speech under antisemitism pretexts: US visa revocations of foreign students, UK proscriptions of Palestine solidarity organizations, Australian arrests, Argentine prosecutions, and the spread of the IHRA definition that conflates criticism of Israel with antisemitism. Roger Waters’ Brazil tour faced attempted censorship, and journalist Breno Altman is under investigation. The PSTU is a Morenoite tendency with significant political differences from the ICFI, but a conviction targeting basic political speech requires unconditional opposition.

Youth homelessness in Germany has nearly tripled since 2022

The Federal Association for Assistance to the Homeless (BAG W) reports that homeless minors in Germany rose from approximately 47,200 to over 137,000 between early 2022 and early 2025, while homelessness among young adults under 26 rose from 18,800 to 55,700. Total youth homelessness now exceeds 190,000. In Berlin, total homelessness rose 85 percent between 2016 and 2024, from 30,000 to over 55,000.

Germany’s social housing stock has collapsed from 3.9 million units in 1987 to 1.6 million in 2010, and apartment rents have risen nearly 10 percent over five years. Housing giant Vonovia posted €4.19 billion in profits in 2025 — nearly double its 2020 earnings. The crisis is unfolding alongside a 64 percent rise in military spending.

Australian inflation hits two-and-a-half year high as Iran war drives fuel prices up 32.8 percent

Australia’s Consumer Price Index surged to 4.6 percent in the 12 months to March 2026 — a two-and-a-half year high. The primary driver was a 32.8 percent spike in automotive fuel (diesel up 41 percent), directly attributable to the Iran war. Electricity prices rose 25.4 percent, and food inflation is in the double digits. Real wages have fallen to 15-year lows: nominal wage growth of 3.1 percent against 3.8 percent inflation. Housing costs have surged 137 percent since 2010, against 50 percent wage growth. Deloitte projects Australian inflation could hit 7.5 percent if oil reaches $175/barrel.

The Albanese Labor government has paired this with $35 billion in cuts to the National Disability Insurance Scheme over four years — the largest single program cut in Australian history — alongside a record $60 billion annual military budget.

Global May Day protests place the Iran war and inflation at the center of working-class demands

Activists rallied across Europe, Asia, Latin America, and North America on May 1, with peace, wages, and worker protections as central demands. The unifying theme of the 2026 demonstrations was opposition to the US-Israel war on Iran, which workers identified as the immediate driver of rising energy and food costs. Protests were notably large in Germany, France, South Korea, Indonesia, and Argentina. In the United States, organizers highlighted the $4.30/gallon gasoline average and the $25 billion war cost against ongoing cuts to domestic programs.

Real wages have declined across multiple economies as energy-driven inflation outpaces nominal wage growth. The mass character of the May Day mobilization — and its open political character, with the Iran war as the central question — signals what David North in his keynote address to the WSWS’s 13th annual International May Day Online Rally called “a clear and measurable escalation of class conflict beyond 2025 levels.”

Stocks at record highs as Nasdaq and S&P 500 hit all-time peaks

US equities closed mixed but extended gains on May 1, with the Nasdaq up 0.89 percent to a record 25,114.44 and the S&P 500 climbing 0.29 percent to a fresh record 7,230.12. Apple was a notable upside driver. The Dow Jones lagged, falling 154 points or 0.31 percent to 49,499.27. The April 29 session had already seen the Dow surge ~800 points and the S&P post its first close above 7,200.

The rally is occurring against the backdrop of war abroad, a 75-day government shutdown just ended, and the highest inflation reading in years — a textbook example of equity markets and the real economy moving in opposite directions, with the gains accruing to large shareholders while workers absorb the inflation.

Q1 GDP misses at +2.0 percent annualized as PCE deflator surges to 4.5 percent

The BEA’s advance estimate, released April 30, put real GDP growth at +2.0 percent annualized for Q1 2026, missing consensus expectations of +2.3 percent. The reading is a significant rebound from Q4 2025’s +0.5 percent print. The PCE deflator surged to approximately 4.5 percent, raising open stagflation concerns. Consumer spending was led by healthcare services.

FOMC holds rates with unprecedented 8–4 dissent

The Federal Open Market Committee held the federal funds rate at 3.50–3.75 percent on April 29 — but with an extraordinary 8–4 dissent. One governor wanted a cut; three members signaled the next move could be a hike. The last time four FOMC members dissented was October 1992. The Fed statement noted: “Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase in global energy prices.” This was likely Chair Jerome Powell’s final meeting; a leadership transition is imminent.

Oil pulls back from highs; gold off ~2 percent over week

WTI crude eased to roughly $101.69/barrel, down ~3.2 percent from the previous close of ~$105.07. Brent slipped to roughly $111. Gold pulled back to ~$4,614–$4,638/oz, off about 2 percent over seven days but still historically elevated. Bitcoin traded near $78,178 and Ethereum near $2,284 on May 1, both modestly higher day-on-day.

ISM Manufacturing PMI holds at 52.7 percent but employment contracts and prices paid surge

The April ISM Manufacturing PMI came in at 52.7 percent, unchanged from March and the fourth straight month of expansion. New Orders rose 0.6 points to 54.1. The Employment sub-index dropped 2.3 points to 46.4 — back into contraction. Prices Paid surged 6.3 points to 84.6 percent, signaling significant tariff pass-through. M&A activity continued, with Abbott’s ~$21 billion acquisition of Exact Sciences and Sun Pharma’s $11.75 billion acquisition of Organon among the largest. QVC Group filed Chapter 11 in April; Q1 2026 business filings are the second-highest since 2010.

LAUSD service workers vote on SEIU contract amid looming district budget cuts

Approximately 30,000 Los Angeles Unified School District classified workers — bus drivers, custodians, special education assistants, cafeteria workers — are voting through May 8 on an SEIU Local 99 tentative agreement. The advertised “24 percent over three years” obscures staggered increases that fail to address the average SEIU salary of approximately $35,501, which qualifies as “extremely low income” in LA County. As Marc Wells argues on the WSWS, the May 8 vote conclusion is timed days before the district releases a new budget expected to announce massive cuts — replicating the pattern in Oakland, San Francisco, Chicago, and Philadelphia where contracts preceded layoffs. Vague subcontracting language and tiered hour increases — special education assistants get immediate improvements while other units wait until 2027 — are additional concerns.

UIC graduate workers launch third strike since 2019

Over 1,500 members of the Graduate Educators Organization at the University of Illinois Chicago launched their third strike since 2019, targeting wages that have not kept pace with Chicago’s cost of living. The 2022 contract minimum of $24,200 per year is now untenable against Chicago average one-bedroom rents of $2,300/month — meaning rent alone consumes more than 100 percent of base graduate stipends. UIC reported $490.7 million in research funding (up 27 percent since 2019), and top administrators collectively earn over $6 million annually.

The pattern of union bureaucracy undermining worker leverage — the AFT blocked faculty sympathy actions during the 2022 strike, and at Columbia and UC campuses 91–93 percent strike authorization votes were either refused or overridden — points to the need for democratically elected rank-and-file committees independent of the apparatus.

Disney rolls out facial recognition across Disneyland Resort

Under new CEO Josh D’Amaro, Disney implemented facial recognition across Disneyland Resort on April 28. Guest faces are converted into numerical identifiers matched with ticket data; while technically optional, the system defaults to biometric scanning, with manual verification rendered deliberately slower. The rollout coincides with approximately 1,000 job eliminations as part of a $7.5 billion cost-reduction effort: facial recognition reduces front-line staffing.

Approximately 8–10 percent of Los Angeles and Orange County residents are undocumented; precedent — Motel 6 sharing guest data with ICE, data brokers selling location data to federal agencies without warrants — leaves the system trivially weaponizable for deportation enforcement. UC Irvine law professor Ari Waldman: “The normalization of facial surveillance is really problematic. We can’t go around life hiding our faces, so this isn’t just next step in surveillance; it’s qualitatively different.”

Texas and Florida execute prisoners maintaining their innocence

Texas executed James Broadnax (37) and Florida executed James Ernest Hitchcock (70) on April 30, within 35 minutes of each other. Both prisoners maintained their innocence to the end. Broadnax’s case is particularly disturbing: in March 2026 his cousin Demarius Cummings filed a sworn affidavit and video stating, “I was the killer,” with DNA corroboration — Broadnax’s DNA appeared nowhere on the weapon or victims, while Cummings’s DNA was found on both. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals rejected the evidence on procedural grounds. Broadnax’s final statement: “I’m innocent, the facts of my case should speak for itself.”

Hitchcock spent nearly 50 years on death row after a 1977 conviction; 1996 witness testimony implicating his brother was never heard by jurors. Florida conducted 19 executions in 2025 alone — the leading edge of an accelerating national execution pattern marked by racial bias, prosecutorial misconduct, and inadequate counsel for poor defendants.

US national debt crosses 100 percent of GDP for first peacetime year since 1946

Andre Damon reports on the WSWS that US debt held by the public has crossed 100 percent of GDP for the first peacetime year since 1946 — $31.27 trillion in debt against $31.22 trillion in GDP. Total federal debt exceeds $39 trillion, approximately 125 percent of GDP. Annual interest payments have crossed $1 trillion. The 2025 tax cut delivered $4.1 trillion in benefits to the wealthy. The CBO projects the debt-to-GDP ratio will reach 156 percent by 2055. All three major credit agencies have now downgraded the United States from AAA status.

The Trump administration is simultaneously demanding the largest military budget in modern history — $1.5 trillion proposed for fiscal year 2027 — while the 2025 reconciliation bill imposes $880 billion in Medicaid cuts, $186 billion in SNAP cuts, and $536 billion in Medicare cuts over ten years. The class arithmetic is explicit.

Sources WSWS US national debt surpasses size of the economy

Maine governor exits Senate primary as Platner surges with labor backing

Maine Governor Janet Mills suspended her US Senate primary campaign after polls showed her losing decisively to Graham Platner, a progressive candidate backed by labor unions and endorsed by Senators Elizabeth Warren and Bernie Sanders. The collapse of an incumbent Democratic governor’s Senate bid in a working-class state has received almost no national coverage amid war headlines. The Platner candidacy reflects the same working-class radicalization that drove the millions of anti-ICE protesters into the streets and that surfaces at every May Day rally — but channeled, once again, into the Democratic Party primary structure that has historically buried such impulses.

US measles cases hit 1,814 in 2026, on pace to exceed 2025 total

The CDC has confirmed 1,814 measles cases across 37 US jurisdictions as of April 30, with 93 percent of cases outbreak-associated across 24 active outbreaks. The full-year 2025 total was 2,288; 2026 is on pace to exceed it. The South Carolina outbreak alone reached approximately 990 cases by early March. The trajectory reflects post-pandemic erosion of MMR vaccination coverage and the entrenchment of vaccine refusal in segments of the political establishment.

Pentagon awards classified AI contracts to 8 firms — Anthropic excluded and labeled “supply chain risk”

The Department of Defense on May 1 formalized contracts with Amazon Web Services, Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA, OpenAI, SpaceX, Reflection, and Oracle to deploy frontier AI models on classified military networks. Anthropic was excluded after refusing to permit use for autonomous weapons and domestic mass surveillance. The Pentagon then labeled Anthropic a “supply chain risk” — a designation previously reserved for foreign adversary-linked firms. A federal judge in California has blocked the designation pending litigation. This is the sharpest public fracture between the US defense establishment and an AI safety-focused lab to date, and it foreshadows the broader integration of frontier AI into the kill chain.

BA.3.2 “Cicada” COVID variant in 29+ US states

BA.3.2 — nicknamed “Cicada,” a heavily mutated Omicron descendant detected in at least 33 countries — has now been identified in 29-plus US states as of late April. It carries spike protein mutations suggesting potential immune escape, but no data indicate increased hospitalizations or deaths. Nationally, COVID is declining or stable in 37 of 44 states tracked by the CDC. The WHO is gathering Cicada data to inform its May 2026 vaccine antigen composition decision.

A separate final analysis in the same issue confirms that fixed-duration venetoclax-based regimens are effective in previously untreated chronic lymphocytic leukemia, supporting a time-limited treatment paradigm that could substantially reduce patient burden and drug costs versus continuous targeted therapy.

Arts and culture

The most politically resonant cultural item of the past 24 hours is the San Francisco International Film Festival screening of Amir Azizi’s Inside Amir (Daroon-e Amir), reviewed on the WSWS by David Walsh. Filmed in Tehran in 2025 without official government approval, the film follows a young courier named Amir through the streets and neighborhoods of the city as he prepares to emigrate to Italy to join his girlfriend. What the filmmakers could not have known is that the city they were documenting — its hospitals, its Pasteur Institute (founded 1920), its working-class apartment blocks, its 10 million residents, its 17 million in the metropolitan area — was about to become the target of an industrial-scale bombing campaign. The IDF reported dropping more than 12,000 bombs across Iran and 3,600 on Tehran alone, including a March 9 strike on a residential building that killed 40–50 people. Walsh frames the film as acquiring a devastating retrospective meaning: an unintended record of urban normalcy on the eve of destruction. This is precisely the kind of cultural object the WSWS is best placed to recognize — not because of its politics, but because of its honest engagement with the human reality the bombs were aimed at.

Perspective published: “Capitalist crisis, war and the international class struggle” by David North — keynote address delivered to the 2026 International May Day Online Rally. Read in full on wsws.org.

The Perspective is the centerpiece of the WSWS’s 13th annual May Day Rally and merits attention in full. Beyond the points already integrated above, North’s speech opens with a series of solidarity statements — for Bogdan Syrotiuk, the imprisoned Ukrainian socialist; for Turkish labor leaders facing state prosecution; for the people of Gaza; for Venezuelan workers whose president Maduro was kidnapped by Washington; and for immigrants facing mass detention and deportation in the United States. These are positioned not as a list of charitable concerns but as elements of a single global counter-revolutionary process.

The analytical core of the speech is a refusal of the “Israel-centric” narrative now circulating in parts of the left — the idea that Israel drives US foreign policy rather than the other way around. North argues this represents a superficial reading that mistakes form for content. He situates the Iran war within “a distinct 35-year period” inaugurated by the USSR’s dissolution in 1991, during which US imperialism, unable to recover its postwar economic dominance through productive means, has repeatedly attempted to reassert global hegemony through military force — from Yugoslavia to Iraq, Afghanistan, Libya, Syria, and now Iran.

Two arresting data points: since 1944, gold has risen from $35 per ounce to roughly $4,600, demonstrating that “the value of the dollar relative to gold has declined by more than 99 percent”; and the international working class has grown by two billion people since 1980. The speech documents 458 strikes across just eight European countries in Q1 2026 alone, including five general strikes — what North calls “a clear and measurable escalation of class conflict beyond 2025 levels.”

The closing section concerns organizational instruments: the World Socialist Web Site (now in its 28th year), the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees, the International Youth and Students for Social Equality, and a newly launched initiative called “Socialism AI.” The final formulation: the epoch presents a choice between “war or peace, dictatorship or democracy, socialism or barbarism.”

Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin’s May Day output emphasizes labor revival narratives — graduate worker organizing, public-sector strikes, the “post-Starbucks” union resurgence — and an emerging “abundance liberalism” framing that treats supply-side reform as a left horizon. On the Iran war, the magazine’s line frames the conflict primarily as the project of a Trump-Netanyahu-neoconservative “elite” rather than as the product of structural contradictions in US capitalism. This conflicts directly with the analysis North developed in the WSWS Perspective: it permits the conclusion that defeating Trump electorally — preferably through a Democratic primary — would arrest the war drive. DSA’s electoral arm continues to back progressive Democratic candidates, with Maine’s Graham Platner among the magazine’s recent profile subjects. The political consequence is to steer working-class anger into the Democratic primary apparatus that has historically buried such impulses. There is no programmatic rupture with the Democratic Party.

Source Jacobin May Day coverage and labor commentary

Left Voice

Left Voice (US affiliate of the Trotskyist Fraction–Fourth International / PTS Argentina) has run May Day pieces emphasizing Latin American May Day traditions and student-worker solidarity, with sharper anti-imperialist content than Jacobin. On the Iran war the line is correctly opposed to US-Israeli aggression. The decisive political problem is the tendency’s continued orientation toward “broad front” formations and its substantial adaptation to identity-political frames in its US output. The Morenoist programmatic heritage — independent of Stalinism, but historically committed to forms of “democratic” anti-imperialism rather than the construction of independent working-class parties on a Trotskyist program — produces a formal radicalism that does not break, in practice, with the orientation toward bourgeois-democratic regroupments. Left Voice does not share the ICFI’s analysis of the trade union bureaucracy as a counter-revolutionary force requiring rank-and-file rupture, instead promoting “class-struggle” caucus work within the existing apparatus.

Source Left Voice May Day coverage

Liberation News / PSL

Liberation News (Party for Socialism and Liberation) coverage centers global May Day mobilizations, anti-Iran-war coalitions, and ANSWER Coalition demonstrations. The political line combines correct opposition to US imperialism with a politics of anti-war coalition-building that has historically included alignments with bourgeois forces — Workers World/PSL’s longstanding orientation toward Stalinist regimes (China, North Korea, Cuba) and the “axis of resistance” frame for Iran reflects a national-defensist rather than internationalist program. There is no independent perspective for the Iranian working class against both US imperialism and the Iranian bourgeois regime; opposition to US war is treated as equivalent to support for the existing Iranian state. This is the textbook anti-Trotskyist position North polemicized against in the May Day speech.

Source Liberation News May Day and Iran war coverage

Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative’s US site features May Day calls for one-day general strikes and “people’s tribunals” against ICE, alongside “tax the rich” municipal demands. The orientation, inherited from the broader CWI/ISA tradition, treats general strikes as pressure tactics on existing bourgeois institutions rather than as instruments for the conquest of power. The tendency continues to back select Democratic candidates and remains committed to running independent socialist candidates in a manner that does not require a programmatic break with the Democratic Party as such. There is no analysis of the trade union bureaucracy as an independent social layer; the strategy is to “elect socialists to union office” within existing structures.

Source Socialist Alternative May Day commentary

Socialist Worker / SWP UK

The Socialist Workers Party (UK) and its International Socialist Tendency continue to operate primarily through Stop the War Coalition mobilizations and broad anti-war fronts that include left-Labour MPs. May Day output emphasizes Palestine and Iran solidarity demonstrations, but without a perspective for breaking the British working class from the Labour Party. The IST’s foundational state-capitalist theorization of the USSR (Tony Cliff’s tradition) translates programmatically into a perpetual orientation toward “movementism” — the view that the construction of a revolutionary party can be substituted by participation in mass movements as such. There is no orientation toward rank-and-file committees independent of the existing trade union bureaucracy.

Source Socialist Worker May Day and Iran coverage

Socialist Appeal / RCP IMT

The International Marxist Tendency completed its 2024 rebrand to “Revolutionary Communist Party” under “Are you a communist?” branding, which has produced significant recruitment among radicalizing youth. May Day output emphasizes recruitment, the “communist” identity, and the IMT’s calls for nationalization. The programmatic content remains national-reformist: the tendency has historically adapted to Labourite formations and to bourgeois nationalist movements (Bolivarianism, Corbynism). On Iran, the line correctly opposes US-Israeli aggression but does not develop the perspective of permanent revolution — the necessity of overthrowing both imperialism and the Iranian bourgeoisie. The “Are you a communist?” recruitment frame draws workers and youth into an organization whose program, despite the militant aesthetic, lies within the historical orbit of left-reformism rather than Trotskyism.

What the WSWS should cover today

1. The class character of the 75-day federal worker pay freeze

While the DHS shutdown’s resolution is being covered as a Beltway political victory, there has been almost no examination of what the 75-day pay freeze meant materially for tens of thousands of Coast Guard, TSA, and FEMA workers — many lower-paid, with no savings cushion. CBS News reported that the Coast Guard cut power and halted overseas missions during the impasse. A WSWS investigation into how working-class federal employees and their families absorbed the shutdown — including emergency food bank use, missed rent, and forced reliance on payday lenders — would expose what the bipartisan establishment treats as a procedural footnote.

Recommended reading CBS News Coast Guard crisis: DHS shutdown halts pay, cuts power, strains missions

2. The Tuapse environmental catastrophe as a workers’ health story

Four Ukrainian drone strikes in 17 days have turned Russia’s Black Sea port of Tuapse into what reporters are calling a toxic environmental disaster zone — boiling oil products spilled onto streets, “black rain” downwind, residents and refinery workers left without coordinated medical response. Mainstream coverage has framed this as a war story or an oil-market story; the public-health and class consequences for the town’s workers and civilians have been almost entirely absent. A WSWS piece could expose how an inter-imperialist proxy war between NATO and Russia is being paid for by Russian working-class communities downwind of strategic infrastructure.

Recommended reading Al Jazeera “It’s all very toxic”: environmental catastrophe at Tuapse

3. The Maine Senate primary: Mills exit, Platner surge, and the limits of progressive Democratic insurgency

Maine Governor Janet Mills suspended her US Senate primary campaign after polls showed her losing decisively to Graham Platner, a labor-backed progressive endorsed by Warren and Sanders. The story is receiving almost no national coverage but has direct programmatic significance: it represents both a real expression of working-class anger and the latest attempt to channel that anger through the Democratic Party. The WSWS could provide the only serious analysis of why such “left” candidacies have historically produced no break with the bourgeois state.

Recommended reading NPR News roundup for May 1 (Maine Senate race)

4. The Pentagon-Anthropic fracture and the militarization of frontier AI

The Pentagon’s exclusion of Anthropic from its $X-billion classified AI contracts — and its retaliatory designation of the company as a “supply chain risk” — is the sharpest open conflict yet between the US war machine and an AI company over kill-chain integration and domestic surveillance applications. The story has received tech-press coverage but no Marxist analysis. A WSWS piece could situate it within the broader incorporation of frontier AI into US imperialism’s military operations, the role of the Trump administration’s national security apparatus in dictating “research” directions, and the class implications of an AI-driven mass surveillance infrastructure.

Recommended reading DefenseScoop DoD expands classified AI work with 8 companies, excluding Anthropic

5. The 32-45 million driven into poverty and hunger by the Hormuz crisis

Guterres’s UN warning — that 32 million people will be pushed into poverty and 45 million more will face extreme hunger in a medium-disruption scenario — is being covered in financial press as an “economic indicator” story. It is, in fact, the largest mass humanitarian consequence of the US-Israel war on Iran, and the costs are being absorbed disproportionately by the working class of South Asia, Southeast Asia, and sub-Saharan Africa. A WSWS analysis could reframe the war debate around the millions of working-class lives whose conditions of existence are being destroyed for the geopolitical objectives of Washington and Tel Aviv.

Recommended reading UN News Guterres warns Hormuz crisis is strangling global economy

6. The $25 billion Iran war contract economy

The Pentagon has put a $25 billion price tag on the Iran war so far, against the backdrop of the DHS shutdown, $880 billion in Medicaid cuts, and $4.30/gallon gas hitting working-class drivers. This spending — combined with a 60-day legal deadline being blown past by Congress — raises the question of which defense contractors are receiving these contracts, what political networks they sit within, and how the financial returns from this war are being distributed. Mainstream press is not following the money; the WSWS is well-placed to do so.
