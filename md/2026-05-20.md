### 1. Trump cancels planned Iran strike, says he was "an hour away"

Donald Trump announced on Truth Social that he had cancelled a strike on Iran scheduled for Tuesday, May 19, claiming he was "an hour away" from authorization before Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates intervened. According to Trump, the Gulf monarchies asked Washington to "hold off" because "serious negotiations are now taking place." The two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan that began April 8 immediately fractured; the US imposed a naval blockade on April 13 after talks collapsed.

The war that began February 28 has produced a dual blockade: US warships block Iranian shipping, while Iran restricts the Strait of Hormuz and charges tolls exceeding $1 million per vessel transiting the chokepoint. ABC News reports Trump has issued a "series of unenforced deadlines" through the conflict. The current US 10-point framework demands an Iranian nuclear freeze and reopening of Hormuz; Tehran's counter-proposal demands war reparations and lifting of all sanctions.

In a Perspective published today on the WSWS, Jordan Shilton wrote that the G7 finance ministers' communiqué in Paris "echoed" Trump's threats by demanding "a swift return to free and safe transit through the Strait of Hormuz" while making "no mention" of the February 28 bombing campaign that, per the article, killed thousands of Iranian civilians.

Key facts:
- Trump on Truth Social: cancellation came at request of Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE.
- US naval blockade since April 13; Iran charges over $1 million per ship at Hormuz.
- IEA projects a Q2 global oil demand contraction of ~1.5 million bpd, the sharpest since COVID.
- IMF April WEO cuts 2026 global growth to 3.1%, citing the Iran war.

### 2. Putin and Xi sign roughly 40 agreements in Beijing

Vladimir Putin arrived in Beijing May 19 for a two-day state visit — his 25th to China — five days after Donald Trump's own summit with Xi Jinping. The two sides planned to sign approximately 40 agreements spanning energy, tourism, education, and economic cooperation. Russia's oil exports to China grew 35% in Q1 2026, and Russia is now one of China's largest natural gas suppliers. Talks on the Power of Siberia 2 pipeline reached "basic understanding" on route but no construction timeline.

Both leaders publicly criticized Trump's $175 billion "Golden Dome" missile defense program and opposed the expiration of US-Russia arms control treaties. Xi called a "comprehensive ceasefire" on Iran "of utmost urgency." Putin said: "Even against the backdrop of unfavourable external factors, our interaction and economic cooperation demonstrate strong momentum." Xi: "We have been able to continuously deepen our political mutual trust and strategic coordination with a resilience that remains unyielding."

Today's WSWS Perspective frames Washington's separate diplomatic outreach to Beijing as a tactical pause rather than a thaw — the US is seeking near-term economic stability with China while preparing the ground for longer-term confrontation.

### 3. WHO declares Ebola Bundibugyo a global health emergency

The World Health Organization on May 17 declared the Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda a Public Health Emergency of International Concern — its highest alert level. As of May 19, more than 500 suspected cases and 130 deaths have been reported; only 30 of those cases have been laboratory-confirmed, and significant undercounting is expected. The outbreak is centered in DRC's Ituri Province, with one case in Goma and two confirmed in Kampala, Uganda.

The pathogen is Bundibugyo virus, an orthoebolavirus for which **no licensed vaccine or specific therapeutic exists** — unlike Ebola Zaire, against which two vaccines have been licensed since the West African epidemic of 2014. Hospitals in Bunia, Ituri's capital, are overwhelmed; field hospitals are being established. The two Kampala cases have no apparent epidemiological link to each other, raising the possibility of unobserved chains of transmission.

Key facts:
- 500+ suspected cases, 130 deaths; only 30 confirmed.
- Bundibugyo strain: no licensed vaccine, no antiviral.
- Two unrelated confirmed cases in Kampala on May 15–16.
- Field hospitals being stood up in Bunia.

### 4. Bolivia under siege as labor-led blockades besiege La Paz

President Rodrigo Paz, in office less than six months, faces what analysts call Bolivia's worst economic crisis in 40 years. Year-on-year inflation reached 14% in April. The immediate trigger was Paz's removal of fuel subsidies, which spiked gasoline and diesel prices, followed by the government's import of low-quality gasoline that damaged transport vehicles — the "junk gasoline" scandal that ignited a wave of strikes.

Two weeks of road blockades by the Bolivian Workers' Central (COB), peasant unions, and miners have emptied La Paz markets and depleted hospital oxygen reserves. At least three people died after emergency vehicles were blocked from reaching medical centers. Each constituency has its own demands: COB wants wage increases; peasant unions want fuel supply restored; miners demand new concessions; teachers want salary raises. Eight Latin American governments — most of them aligned with Washington — released a joint statement rejecting "any action aimed at destabilizing the democratic order," framing organized working-class resistance as a coup threat.

### 5. UK government crisis deepens; 95+ MPs demand Starmer go

Labour lost approximately 1,500 council seats in English local elections, while the hard-right Reform UK party won 1,454 seats. More than 95 Labour MPs have formally called on Prime Minister Keir Starmer to resign or set a departure timetable. Ministerial resignations include Health Secretary Wes Streeting, Home Office Minister Jess Phillips, and at least two other junior ministers and four ministerial aides. Streeting has confirmed he will stand in any leadership election; Starmer has insisted he, too, will stand in any challenge, describing his government as a "10-year project of renewal."

The Eurasia Group says Starmer is "unlikely to last the year." The collapse is occurring less than two years after Labour's historic 2024 majority, and the simultaneous Reform UK surge points to the danger of the far right filling the vacuum opened by Labour's continuation of austerity, welfare cuts, and complicity in the Israeli war on Gaza.

### 6. Six US state primaries reshape midterm landscape

### 7. Philippines Senate opens impeachment trial of VP Sara Duterte

The Philippine Senate formally convened as an impeachment court on May 18; Vice President Sara Duterte has 10 days to respond to the charges. The House voted 257–25, with 9 abstentions, to impeach her on May 11 — her second impeachment, making her the first official in Philippine history to be impeached twice. The articles include misuse of confidential funds, unexplained wealth, and alleged threats against President Bongbong Marcos, First Lady Liza Araneta Marcos, and former House Speaker Martin Romualdez. Senate President Alan Peter Cayetano declared the trial open without setting a date for full hearings. The proceedings deepen the rupture between the Marcos and Duterte political dynasties, formerly allied on the 2022 "Uniteam" ticket.

### India: Modi demands "sacrifices" as Iran war shocks economy

Narendra Modi has appealed for Indians to "reduce the burden on the country's resources" as the Iran war ruptures Indian energy supply. India imports over 80% of its oil, more than half of it formerly sourced from Gulf states. The rupee has fallen from 91 to 96.5 against the dollar since February 28; foreign portfolio investment outflows have totaled roughly $23 billion. Fuel prices have risen 3 rupees per liter — the first hike in four years. Food delivery workers, dependent on LPG, have seen incomes cut by two-thirds. More than 40% of Indians depend on agriculture that relies on imported fertilizers now disrupted by the conflict. Nomura economists noted Modi's rhetoric signals "the burden of adjustment may be incrementally shared with consumers." On the WSWS, Wasantha Rupasinghe characterizes the appeal as a class-war assault on workers.

### Australia: Labor vetoes 13 university research projects

Education Minister Jason Clare has vetoed 13 university research projects on vague "security, defence or international relations" grounds. New legislation will impose "threshold standards" that could trigger deregistration of universities whose academics breach national security rules. Blocked research includes drone, cybersecurity, and alternative-energy collaborations. Pentagon funding for Australian university research has grown from $1.7 million in 2007 to $60 million annually by 2022, with military contracts exceeding $200 million since late 2024. The Safeguarding Australia's Military Secrets Act provides for up to 20 years in prison; the Defence Trade Controls Amendment Act adds up to 10 years plus nearly $800,000 in fines. On the WSWS, Mike Head argues the measures create a "wartime-style atmosphere" integrating Australian universities into the US war machine against China and Russia.

### New Zealand: bipartisan push to raise retirement age

New Zealand's ruling National-led government and the Labour opposition are converging on plans to raise the retirement age from 65 to 67, phased in 2037–2040, and to potentially introduce means-testing of the current NZ$647/week individual benefit. Treasury projects annual superannuation costs will reach NZ$30 billion by decade's end, up from NZ$15.5 billion in 2020, as the over-65 population exceeds one million by 2030. Prime Minister Christopher Luxon has pledged to campaign on the change; Labour leader Chris Hipkins says he is "open to a conversation." Some business-aligned advocates are pushing the retirement age as high as 72–73. On the WSWS, John Braddock frames the cuts as a wealth transfer protecting capital after decades of wage stagnation.

### US measles outbreak hits 1,893 cases; on pace to exceed 2025 total

The CDC reports 1,893 confirmed measles cases across 40 jurisdictions as of May 14 — already approaching the 2,288 cases recorded for all of 2025. Some 93% of 2026 cases (1,761) are outbreak-associated, spanning 27 active outbreaks. The scale of sustained transmission is the largest since measles elimination was declared in the United States in 2000, threatening that status. The outbreak is unfolding against deep cuts to the CDC, defunding of state public health departments, and an explicit federal embrace of anti-vaccine politics under HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.

### First documented human H5N5 case reported in NEJM

The *New England Journal of Medicine* published the first documented human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5) — a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman with Waldenström macroglobulinemia and prior rituximab therapy who developed severe illness in October 2025. Initial nasal swab RT-PCR for influenza A was negative, delaying diagnosis. H5N5 now joins H5N1, H5N2, and H5N6 as confirmed human spillovers from the H5 clade, underscoring the breadth of pandemic-relevant zoonotic threats.

### WHO logs 13 zoonotic flu cases in humans in Q1 2026

WHO confirmed 13 zoonotic influenza human cases in Q1 2026: 4 H5N1 (including a previously unreported fatal pediatric case in Bangladesh), 5 H9N2, and individual detections of H10N3, H1N1v, H1N2v, and H3N2v. No sustained human-to-human transmission was detected, but the simultaneous diversity of subtypes crossing into humans is at a historically elevated level.

### "Cicada" BA.3.2 COVID variant carries ~70–75 spike mutations

The XFG.1.1 SARS-CoV-2 sub-lineage now accounts for 32% of US cases as of mid-May. The BA.3.2 "Cicada" variant — carrying ~70–75 spike mutations — is co-circulating but has not driven any rise in severe disease or hospitalizations. As of May 12, COVID is growing in only 1 state and declining in 20; test positivity stands at 1%. The high mutational divergence of BA.3.2 warrants continued vaccine-strain monitoring even as case curves remain low.

### EU agrees AI Act "omnibus simplification"; US patchwork grows

EU member states reached political agreement on May 7 to simplify the AI Act's compliance requirements in the name of boosting "innovation," while also formally banning AI "nudification" apps. In the United States, Connecticut became the latest state to pass broad AI legislation (SB 5), even as Colorado's AI Act remains stayed by a federal court. The US now has over 1,200 active state-level AI bills with no unified federal framework. The UK's Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) published findings that employers using AI recruitment tools must impose more substantive human review of automated decisions; public consultation runs until May 29.

## Arts and culture

### Whitney Biennial 2026 reveals an art world unable to face the catastrophe

WSWS arts critic Clare Hurley argues in a major review of the 2026 Whitney Biennial that the exhibition exposes contemporary American art's incapacity to engage meaningfully with the present epoch — fascism, genocide, oligarchy, imperialist war. The generation of exhibiting artists, shaped by postmodernism and identity politics, retreats into abstraction and self-referential conceptualism rather than confronting material conditions. The Whitney's board of billionaires and defense contractors, Hurley writes, enforces a soft institutional censorship on overtly political work critical of Trump, US imperialism, or Palestinian solidarity. A handful of pieces partially escape that framework: Mao Ishikawa's photographs of Okinawan working-class life, Ali Eyal's paintings of an Iraqi childhood under occupation, and a Palestinian video installation by Basel Abbas and Ruanne Abou-Rahme. Hurley concludes that genuinely revolutionary art will emerge only "as society moves toward revolution."

### Taiwan Travelogue wins the 2026 International Booker Prize

Yáng Shuāng-zǐ's *Taiwan Travelogue*, translated from Chinese by Lin King, won the 2026 International Booker Prize at a ceremony at London's Tate Modern on May 19. The novel is a formally inventive work set in 1930s Japanese-occupied Taiwan that uses the conceit of a Japanese travel writer's memoir to interrogate questions of empire, language, and historical memory. It is a rare win for Taiwanese literature on the world stage, arriving at a moment of acute geopolitical attention to the island and the broader question of US-China war.

### Cannes 2026 enters its final days

The 79th Cannes Film Festival (May 12–23), with South Korean auteur Park Chan-wook presiding over the jury, enters its closing week today as the Marché du Film shutters. Twenty-one films from three continents are in main competition, including Jane Schoenbrun's *Teenage Sex and Death at Camp Miasma*. A record 2,541 features were submitted this year; five women directors are in main competition. Cannes remains the world's preeminent venue for politically serious cinema — though, as with most major festivals, the gap between the catastrophe of the world and what reaches the screen remains stark.
