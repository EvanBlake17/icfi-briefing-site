### 1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 20 as $200 billion funding request reveals invasion plans

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its 20th day on March 20 with no diplomatic offramp in sight, as the scale of Washington's military ambitions became clearer through a $200 billion supplemental spending request submitted to Congress. The request, confirmed by Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth — who stated "it takes money to kill bad guys" — dwarfs the $51 billion Congress initially appropriated for the 2003 Iraq invasion and approaches the $144 billion annual peak of the Iraq surge, suggesting plans for a protracted ground occupation rather than continued aerial operations alone.

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, the spending request reveals that "the administration is planning the most endless of all endless wars — an open-ended invasion aimed at subjugating or destroying a country of 90 million people." Combined with the proposed FY 2027 military budget of $1.5 trillion, the war funding would require devastating cuts to domestic programs: $1 trillion from Medicaid, $536 billion from Medicare, and $186 billion through SNAP elimination.

- Total regional death toll exceeds **2,300**; Iran's Red Crescent reports **18,000+ civilian injuries** inside Iran; the deadliest single strike hit an elementary girls' school in Minab, killing **more than 170 schoolgirls**
- The US has struck **more than 7,000 targets** in Iran since February 28
- Israel struck Iran's **South Pars gas field** — the world's largest — prompting Iranian retaliation against Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG hub, which Qatar says will take **five years to repair**
- Brent crude surged to **$115–$118/barrel**, up ~50% since the war began; the IEA released 400 million barrels of strategic reserves but **failed to move prices**
- The UN nuclear watchdog confirmed **a projectile struck Iran's Bushehr nuclear plant** site
- Trump claimed no advance knowledge of the South Pars strike, but Israeli and US officials told Axios it was **coordinated with and approved by the White House**
- Saudi Arabia intercepted a missile over Al Jouf on March 20, indicating widening regional fallout

### 2. Pakistan's airstrike on Kabul rehabilitation hospital kills up to 408 civilians

Pakistan's air force struck the Omid Addiction Treatment Hospital in Kabul on March 16 as part of "Operation Ghazab Lil Haq," destroying a 1,000-bed facility that was operating at approximately 3,000 capacity. The death toll remains disputed: Afghan authorities report 408 killed and 265 injured, while UNAMA confirmed at least 143 killed and 119 injured after an on-site investigation that found "complete destruction" of a block housing adolescent drug patients.

The strike represents one of the deadliest single attacks on a medical facility since the 2015 US bombing of the MSF hospital in Kunduz. Pakistan has declared itself in "open war" with Afghanistan — a dangerous escalation between two nuclear-armed neighbors (Pakistan possesses nuclear weapons; Afghanistan borders nuclear-armed Pakistan and sits near India's nuclear umbrella). A five-day ceasefire was announced March 18 at the request of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkey for Eid al-Fitr, but fundamental tensions remain unresolved.

Key facts:
- UN mission officer-in-charge Georgette Gagnon confirmed the facility was "a well-known rehabilitation center" run by the Taliban interior ministry
- Amnesty International stated the site is "well-documented" as a civilian rehab center since 2016
- Pakistan's Information Minister denied civilian targeting, claiming secondary explosions indicated an ammunition depot
- The conflict is receiving disproportionately little international attention relative to its scale, overshadowed by the Iran war

### 5. BP locks out 900 Whiting refinery workers after 98% contract rejection

BP locked out nearly 900 skilled refinery workers at its Whiting, Indiana facility on March 19 after workers rejected the company's "last, best and final" contract offer by 98.3% with 94% turnout. The Whiting refinery is the largest in the Midwest, producing 440,000 barrels per day, and the lockout comes as US oil companies stand to earn an additional $63.4 billion if crude averages above $100/barrel — a threshold already surpassed amid the Iran war.

The rejected contract demanded $8–$10/hour wage cuts, elimination of 100 union positions, expanded use of contract labor, closure of the environmental department, and AI implementation without job protections. BP's only concession in a counteroffer was reducing a signing bonus from $7,500 to $2,500 while maintaining all other demands.

Key facts:
- The lockout affects **USW Local 7-1** members — skilled operators and maintenance workers
- BP is deploying management and contract workers to maintain operations during the lockout
- The WSWS noted that the lockout strategy is designed to force "labor peace" while oil companies pocket war-driven windfall profits

### 6. Sudan drone strikes hit hospital as world's largest humanitarian crisis deepens

A drone strike hit Dilling General Hospital in Sudan's Nuba Mountains, killing at least four patients, while the city of El Obeid endured drone attacks over five consecutive days. The UN recorded more than 200 civilians killed by drone strikes in White Nile State and Kordofan between March 4–12 alone, including at least 152 victims of Sudanese Armed Forces strikes in West Kordofan.

Key facts:
- UN Spokesperson Stéphane Dujarric warned "humanitarian access is also becoming increasingly restrained" as supply routes face ongoing threats
- The conflict receives a fraction of the international attention directed at the Iran war despite comparable civilian suffering

### US measles cases approach 1,400 as herd immunity erodes

The US measles outbreak continues to worsen, with 1,362 confirmed cases reported across 31 jurisdictions as of March 12 — on pace to be the worst year since measles elimination was declared in 2000. South Carolina leads with 632 cases, followed by Utah (117) and Florida (64). An alarming 93% of patients are unvaccinated or have unknown vaccine status, and 4% have required hospitalization.

Only 10 states now maintain the 95% MMR vaccination threshold required for herd immunity, with 14 active outbreaks underway. PAHO has delayed its decision on whether the United States loses its measles elimination status until November — a potential public health milestone that would mark the reversal of a quarter-century achievement. The erosion of vaccination rates reflects both the anti-scientific propaganda promoted by figures like Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and the systematic defunding of public health infrastructure.
