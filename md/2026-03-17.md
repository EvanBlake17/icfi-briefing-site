### 1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day 17 with over 2,300 dead across the region

### 3. Iran war triggers largest oil supply shock in decades as Hormuz traffic collapses

The war has produced the most severe oil supply disruption in decades, with tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20 million barrels per day normally transit — reduced to a trickle. The IEA confirmed global supply has fallen by approximately 8 million barrels per day, and its 32 member countries announced plans to release 400 million barrels from strategic reserves over 120 days beginning next week.

Brent crude settled at just over $102 per barrel on Monday, up roughly 40% from $72 on February 27. LNG prices have surged nearly 60% since the war began. Goldman Sachs projected the war will reduce global GDP by 0.3% and push headline inflation up 0.5–0.6 percentage points.

Key facts:
- Gulf states have cut oil production by at least **10 mb/d**
- Global stocks down **5.5%** since February 28; Tokyo Nikkei down **11%**, Saudi Tadawul down **9.6%**

### 6. Cuba protests erupt as demonstrators torch Communist Party headquarters in Morón

### 7. DHS partial shutdown triggers mass TSA resignations and hour-long airport security lines

The Department of Homeland Security partial shutdown, which began on February 14 after Congress failed to agree on funding, has produced cascading effects on airport security. More than 300 TSA officers have resigned since mid-February, and unscheduled absences have jumped from 2% to 6%. Over 100,000 DHS workers — including TSA, FEMA, and CISA personnel — missed their first full paycheck.

Wait times exceeded one hour at multiple airports, with New Orleans advising travelers to arrive three hours early. Similar conditions were reported at Fort Lauderdale, Philadelphia, Austin, and Chicago airports. Airline CEOs from American, Delta, JetBlue, Southwest, and United wrote jointly to Congress demanding resolution.

Key facts:
- Workers forced to withdraw **$1,850–$3,700** each from retirement savings for rent, food, and medical co-pays
- TSA officer Johnny Jones: "The TSA officers are tired of being used as political pawns in the game"
- DHS blames "Democrats' reckless shutdown"; Democrats blame Republicans for blocking bipartisan funding bills
- Shutdown is fundamentally a labor rights crisis — over 100,000 workers denied wages for political leverage

### NEJM: CAR T-cell therapy achieves drug-free remission in refractory autoimmune disease

A study published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* (Vol. 394, No. 11) reported that in 11 patients with multirefractory autoimmune hemolytic anemia, CD19 CAR T-cell therapy produced a complete response and drug-free remission lasting a median of 11.5 months, with expected but manageable toxicity. ### *One Battle After Another* sweeps the 98th Academy Awards

Paul Thomas Anderson's *One Battle After Another* — a time-hopping drama depicting ICE raids and state repression — swept the 98th Academy Awards on March 15–16, winning six Oscars including Best Picture, Best Director, and Best Adapted Screenplay. These were Anderson's first career Oscar wins. Ryan Coogler's *Sinners* also made history with four wins, including Michael B. Jordan for Best Actor and Autumn Durald becoming the first woman to win Best Cinematography.

The ceremony's most politically significant moment came when Javier Bardem declared "No to war. Free Palestine" before an audience of roughly 20 million viewers. Palestinian actor Motaz Malhees, barred from attending due to his citizenship, sent a statement read aloud: "You can block a passport. You cannot block a voice." These interventions represented rare instances of direct political speech at a ceremony that has increasingly policed such expression.

As WSWS arts editor David Walsh noted, the ceremony reflects a tentative but significant shift in cultural consciousness away from identity politics toward broader opposition to state violence and imperial war — though he cautioned against overstating the depth of this shift. Anderson's choice of subject matter — the machinery of immigration enforcement as a system of state terror — represents a noteworthy departure from the safely biographical or nostalgic films that have dominated recent Best Picture winners.

## What the WSWS should cover today

### 1. Cuban workers under a double siege — US oil embargo meets decade-long infrastructure collapse

The Morón protests are being framed by Western media as anti-communist uprising and by Cuban state media as foreign-directed vandalism. Neither framing addresses the material foundation: Trump's January 2026 executive order has cut off all oil supply for three months, collapsing Cuba's thermoelectric grid. A WSWS article could analyze the protests as an expression of working-class desperation — not a US-backed color revolution — while also examining the Cuban government's failure to develop energy alternatives over six decades and its deployment of security forces against protesting workers.

### 4. Asian workers bear the heaviest burden of the Hormuz oil shock

84% of Hormuz oil exports transit to Asia. While coverage focuses on US and European economic projections, the real human cost falls on working-class people in Cambodia (+68% petrol), Vietnam (+50%), Nigeria (+35%), and India, where the rupee is under severe pressure and subsidized fuel programs are strained. The WSWS could report on workers who cannot afford to get to their jobs and farmers who cannot afford fertilizer for the coming planting season — the class dimensions of the oil shock that IMF forecasts obscure.

### 5. The FCC's wartime censorship threat and the collapse of press independence

FCC Chair Brendan Carr's threat to revoke broadcast licenses over Iran war coverage warrants sustained WSWS analysis as a qualitative escalation in state management of wartime information — comparable to the Espionage Act prosecutions of World War I or post-9/11 pressure on networks to air Pentagon-approved coverage. Even Republican Senator Ron Johnson has objected, but the structural power of the FCC over broadcast licenses means the threat is real and represents a new stage in the erosion of democratic rights under conditions of war.
