### 1. Iran halts US negotiations and vows to fully block the Strait of Hormuz

Iran announced on June 1 that it is ending all communication with the United States through intermediaries and will move to completely seal the Strait of Hormuz, citing Israel's expanding military operations in Lebanon and continued ceasefire violations as the trigger. The decision detonates a tentative diplomatic framework that had taken shape over the preceding days and threatens a renewed oil-supply crisis.

A tentative 60-day memorandum of understanding had reportedly been reached. Under its terms, Iran would remove all mines from the Strait within 30 days, guarantee "unrestricted" shipping, and commit to not pursuing a nuclear weapon, in exchange for US sanctions waivers permitting Iran to sell oil freely. Trump never signed it. A US official said "the president relayed to the mediators that he wants a couple of days to think about it," and Trump reportedly altered the deal's language over the weekend on enriched-uranium terms and on the Strait of Hormuz provisions.

Key facts:

- Oil prices jumped more than 7% within hours of Iran's announcement.
- The Strait of Hormuz has been largely blocked since February 28, 2026, when the US and Israel launched the air war against Iran and assassinated Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei.
- Iran cited Israel's seizure of Beaufort Castle and its push beyond the Litani as among the reasons for breaking off talks.
- The April 12 ceasefire between the US and Iran remains nominally in effect despite repeated armed exchanges.

The collapse confirms the WSWS analysis that the "negotiations" were never aimed at a stable settlement but at managing a war of imperialist plunder against Iran. Washington's insistence on rewriting terms even as a deal was within reach reflects a ruling class that treats the lives of millions in the region — and the global working class facing an energy shock — as bargaining chips.

### 2. US carries out fresh strikes on Iran as IRGC fires ballistic missiles at US forces in Kuwait

US fighter jets struck Iranian air defenses, a ground control station, and two one-way attack drones over the weekend of May 25–26 at Goruk and on Qeshm Island, following a May 25 strike on Iranian missile launch sites and boats laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz. ### 3. Israel seizes Crusader-era Beaufort Castle and drives its offensive beyond the Litani River

Israeli forces captured the 12th-century Beaufort Castle (Qal'at al-Shaqif) near Nabatiyeh in southern Lebanon on May 31 — roughly 14.5 km from the Israeli border — and raised the Israeli flag over it. Netanyahu called the seizure "a dramatic change" in the operation and ordered troops to push deeper, beyond the Litani River, in open violation of the April ceasefire.

This is Israel's deepest military push into Lebanon in 26 years; Israel previously held the castle during its 18-year occupation that ended in 2000. UNESCO classifies Beaufort as "one of the best-preserved examples of medieval castles in the Near East."

Key facts:

- France issued a "strong condemnation" of the operation.
- The escalation is among the key triggers Iran cited for breaking off negotiations with the US.
- More than 2,000 civilians and combatants have been killed in the Lebanon war; the offensive has destroyed a swathe of villages and signals an extended Israeli occupation.

The drive beyond the Litani reflects the Netanyahu government's strategy — backed materially by Washington — of redrawing the regional map by force.

### Germany's economic experts demand a "U-turn" on care, health and pensions

Germany's Council of Economic Experts spring report, published May 27, calls for reforming long-term care insurance, eliminating benefit supplements for residents who cannot afford care homes, and imposing stricter asset-depletion requirements before insurance assistance begins. Council chair Monika Schnitzer argued care insurance "is intended to prevent social hardship, but not to relieve people of their responsibility to contribute their own funds." The total social-insurance contribution rate stands at 42.3% in 2026 and could approach 50% by 2040 under current law.

The WSWS juxtaposes this against a Boston Consulting Group finding that Germany's 5,000 ultra-wealthy individuals (assets above €86 million) now control 27.3% of the country's €10.6 trillion in financial wealth, with annual wealth growth of roughly €1.5 trillion — enough to fund long-term care insurance for two decades. The DGB unions have avoided major strikes for more than 20 years.

### US measles cases near 2,000 as elimination status hangs by a thread

As of May 28, the US has recorded 1,983 confirmed measles cases across 40 jurisdictions, with 93% (1,847 cases) linked to active outbreaks and 30 new outbreaks declared this year. The 2026 total is on pace to exceed 2025's full-year count of 2,288 — itself a 30-year high — placing the country at serious risk of losing the CDC-certified elimination status it has held since 2000. H5N1 bird flu has now produced 71 confirmed US human cases since 2024, with two deaths; a new review concludes infections can be asymptomatic and that low-level human-to-human spread is "likely," raising fears of undercounting.

### First documented human H5N5 infection reported in NEJM

The New England Journal of Medicine published a correspondence documenting the world's first confirmed human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). The patient was a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman who presented with fever and diarrhea; standard influenza A RT-PCR tests came back negative despite a high viral load in the lower respiratory tract, exposing a serious diagnostic gap. A novel H5 subtype crossing into humans — and undetectable by standard rapid tests — underscores the pandemic risk posed by the expanding H5 clade.

### Finland opens Onkalo, the world's first permanent nuclear waste repository

Finland's Onkalo repository — "cave" in Finnish — began operational licensing on June 1 after the Finnish Radiation and Nuclear Safety Authority (STUK) issued its final assessment. Blasted 430 meters into 1.9-billion-year-old stable bedrock on the island of Olkiluoto, the €1 billion facility is the first permanent geological disposal site for high-level nuclear waste anywhere in the world. Spent fuel will be deposited for up to 100 years, then sealed for at least 100,000 years — longer than recorded human civilization has existed.

### US murder spree in Latin American waters moves beyond 200 killed — Andrea Lobo

The Perspective documents the latest escalation of a US military campaign that began September 2, 2025 in the Caribbean, expanded to the Eastern Pacific in October, and has now killed more than 205 people across roughly 60 destroyed vessels. In the most recent strike — the fourth in a single week — the US military released video of a small boat erupting in flames in the Eastern Pacific, killing three men.

Lobo's central argument is that, under the cover of counter-narcotics operations, the Trump administration has routinized extrajudicial mass killing as settled imperialist policy — "mass murder turned into a daily, bureaucratic operation." No victim has been named, no evidence of alleged crimes released, and no legal framework offered. Survivors and relatives identify many of the dead as ordinary fishermen and migrant laborers; entire fishing communities along the coasts of Colombia and Ecuador have abandoned their livelihoods. The article details a documented "double-tap" strike in which two survivors clinging to debris were deliberately killed in a second strike — a war crime under the Fourth Geneva Convention — reportedly on Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's personal order that no survivors be permitted.

Lobo situates the campaign within three US imperial objectives: reasserting Monroe Doctrine dominance over Latin America after years of Chinese infrastructure investment; intimidating governments contemplating closer ties to Beijing; and targeting the corridor around Peru's Chancay port, a Chinese-developed Pacific hub. The uniform silence of Latin American governments — right-wing and nominally "Pink Tide" alike — confirms, she argues, Trotsky's Theory of Permanent Revolution: only the independent international working class, not any section of the regional bourgeoisie, can defend democratic and national rights against imperialism.

### Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin leads with **"The USA Is Living Under Political Capitalism,"** arguing that contemporary capitalism prioritizes wealth extraction through "political maneuvering" and monopoly rent rather than competitive markets. The framing of "political capitalism" as a distinct phase substitutes a moralizing critique of "rent-seeking" for an analysis of the capitalist mode of production itself — implying the problem is bad-faith manipulation rather than the profit system, and pointing toward regulatory reform rather than expropriation.

**"American Communists Did a Lot Right and a Lot Wrong"** offers a soft rehabilitation of the CPUSA, treating its members as "more complicated than the stereotypes." Tellingly, the piece elides the central historical lesson — the CPUSA's Stalinist subordination of the working class to the Roosevelt Democrats and the Popular Front, the very orientation the DSA reproduces today. **"Emmanuel Macron Has Boosted France's Corporate Welfare State"** correctly skewers Macron's largesse to corporate "rentiers" but, consistent with Jacobin's house line, frames the remedy as a fairer distribution of state subsidies rather than a struggle against the capitalist state.

*Assessment:* Jacobin's output reinforces its function as the theoretical workshop of the DSA's left-Democratic project — recasting the crisis of capitalism as a problem of policy capture, soluble within the existing order.

### Left Voice

Left Voice continues its pointed criticism of Zohran Mamdani in **"Why Is Zohran Mamdani Endorsing Strikebreaker Kathy Hochul?"** and **"No, Zohran, Jessica Tisch Has Not Made New York Safer for the City's Working Class,"** attacking his endorsement of the governor and his retention of a billionaire police commissioner. These pieces are sharper than anything in the DSA press. Yet **"Fight the Attacks on Mamdani and All Immigrants with Mobilizations and Class Struggle"** reveals the contradiction: having exposed Mamdani's pro-capitalist trajectory, Left Voice still calls to defend him against the right, channeling workers back toward the "Mamdani moment" rather than toward a decisive political break with the Democratic Party.

*Assessment:* Left Voice oscillates between trenchant criticism and a centrist refusal to draw the organizational conclusion — that the DSA's project is a trap, requiring an independent revolutionary party, not pressure on its left wing.

### Liberation News / PSL

The PSL's recent output marks the 78th anniversary of the Nakba with a pledge to "intensify efforts to end U.S. aid to Israel," argues that capitalism is the cause of climate catastrophe, and issued a statement on Raúl Castro alongside reflections from a Pan-Africanism summit in Nairobi. The anti-imperialist rhetoric is consistent, but the PSL's method substitutes cheerleading for bourgeois-nationalist and Stalinist regimes for a perspective of independent working-class struggle — presenting capitalist states as anti-imperialist allies rather than as obstacles to the international unity of workers.

*Assessment:* The PSL's campism leads it to subordinate the working class to one camp of bourgeois states against another, the opposite of an internationalist socialist strategy.
