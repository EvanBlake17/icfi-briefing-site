### 2. Strait of Hormuz closure triggers worst oil supply disruption in history

Iran confirmed closure of the Strait of Hormuz on March 2, and tanker traffic has dropped approximately 70%, approaching zero. Over 150 ships are anchored outside the strait to avoid risk. The strait normally handles roughly 20% of global daily oil supply and major LNG volumes. Iraq, the UAE, and Kuwait have all cut production due to the waterway closure.

Brent crude peaked at $119/barrel — the first time oil crossed $100 since Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. US gasoline jumped from approximately $2.98 to $3.45/gallon in one week, with a $4 national average projected this week. In Michigan, gas has already hit $3.58/gallon; in California, prices exceed $5.

Key facts:
- **QatarEnergy**, the world's largest LNG exporter, declared force majeure on March 4 after Iranian drone attacks on its facilities; all LNG production halted March 2
- Qatar's Energy Minister Saad al-Kaabi warned that all Gulf producers could soon halt exports and that prices could hit **$150/barrel**
- Top energy analysts describe this as the **biggest oil output disruption in history**, surpassing the 1973 Arab oil embargo
- The IMF calculates that every sustained 10% oil price rise produces a **0.4% inflation increase** and a **0.15% reduction** in global economic growth
- Economists warn of **stagflation** — simultaneous inflation spike and growth slowdown — hitting the global South hardest, where countries face dollar-denominated debts, oil import dependency, and no welfare buffers
- Oil seesawed between $119 and sub-$90 in a single 24-hour session on March 9–10 after Trump signaled the conflict "could be over soon"

## Pseudo-left press review

- [Jacobin / DSA](#jacobin--dsa) — promoting the line that "the US is fighting Israel's war," channeling antiwar sentiment into pressure on Democratic Party leaders rather than independent working-class action
- [Left Voice](#left-voice) — correctly opposing imperialism but orienting toward educators' union structures and maintaining the framework of "defense of Iran" without a clear program for independent working-class intervention
- [Socialist Alternative](#socialist-alternative) — describing a "regional war spiraling out of control" with emphasis on the humanitarian catastrophe but no strategic perspective for how the working class can stop it
- [Liberation News / PSL](#liberation-news--psl) — focused on anti-imperialist denunciation of US aggression while adapting to campist frameworks that treat the Iranian regime as a legitimate resistance force

### Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin's Iran war coverage has been prolific, with at least eight articles published in the past week, but the political line is consistent: channeling antiwar sentiment back into the Democratic Party framework.

- **"The US Is Fighting Israel's War on Iran"** (Branko Marcetic) argues that the US was forced into the war by Israel, citing administration officials who acknowledged Israel "was determined to act with or without the US." This is precisely the line David North rebutted at the WSWS webinar: "The tail may wag very vigorously, but it's the dog that's running the show. Israel does not drive American policy." By absolving US imperialism of primary agency, Marcetic's framing obscures the 35-year bipartisan drive for regime change in Iran — from the 1953 coup to Trump's withdrawal from the nuclear accord — and redirects criticism toward the Israel lobby rather than the capitalist system.

- **"The Iran War Shows Why It's Time for Chuck Schumer to Go"** (Liza Featherstone) notes that 89% of Democratic voters oppose the war while Schumer offered only weak procedural complaints. The article's political conclusion — replace Schumer with a more credible antiwar Democrat — epitomizes Jacobin's role: identifying real grievances only to channel them back into the Democratic Party. The demand is for better leadership within an imperialist party, not independent working-class political action.

- **"Defense Contractors Stand to Profit Off the Iran War"** documents how Lockheed Martin's stock jumped 3.4% on the war's opening — useful reporting, but the political framework remains one of corporate influence on policy rather than the systemic character of imperialist war.

The DSA's role was directly exposed when NYC Mayor Zohran Mamdani visited the White House to shake Trump's hand on the eve of the bombing — an event the WSWS webinar identified as "exposing the absolute political bankruptcy" of the DSA current. Jacobin has not addressed Mamdani's visit.

**Overall orientation:** Jacobin functions as a pressure valve, acknowledging mass antiwar sentiment while systematically redirecting it toward Democratic Party reform — the opposite of the independent working-class political mobilization that the crisis demands.

### Left Voice

Left Voice has published extensively on the Iran war, with the Trotskyist Fraction (FT/PTS) international network producing statements from multiple countries.

- **"Defend Iran, but Don't Support the Theocracy"** articulates the FT's characteristic "third camp" position: opposing US imperialism while distancing from the Iranian government. While the anti-imperialist instinct is correct, the formulation obscures the central question: who will stop the war? Left Voice offers no concrete strategy for mobilizing working-class social power — the actual lever that could halt arms production and military logistics.

- **"Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran"** calls on teachers' unions to pass antiwar resolutions and organize workplace actions. This orientation toward existing union structures — controlled by bureaucracies that have supported every US war — substitutes institutional pressure for independent working-class political organization. The article does not address the AFL-CIO's endorsement of the "No Kings" protests, which channel worker anger into the framework of Democratic Party-aligned coalition politics.

- **"Down With U.S. Imperialist Aggression Against Iran!"** and the South Korean socialist statement both correctly identify the war's imperialist character but remain at the level of denunciation without a program for building the international working-class movement necessary to stop it.

**Overall orientation:** Left Voice produces more radical-sounding rhetoric than Jacobin but shares the fundamental limitation: no strategy for building an independent political movement of the working class against war, substituting union resolutions and campus protests for the revolutionary mobilization that the situation demands.

Millions of migrant workers from South Asia, Africa, and Southeast Asia living in Qatar, UAE, Bahrain, and Saudi Arabia face Iranian missile attacks, forced factory shutdowns, and no evacuation plans. Their precarious labor conditions and total vulnerability are almost entirely absent from Western war coverage. The kafala system that binds these workers to employers means they cannot leave even as bombs fall. This is a story about the class structure of the Gulf states revealed by war.
