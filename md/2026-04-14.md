### 7. Peru presidential election in chaos as ballots fail to reach 63,000 voters

Peru's April 12 general election — the country's ninth presidential vote in less than a decade — was extended by a day after logistical failures left approximately 63,000 voters without ballots, ballot boxes, or voting materials. The election features the largest field of presidential candidates in Peruvian history, reflecting deep political fragmentation.

With 62% of ballots counted, right-wing candidate Keiko Fujimori led with 16.88%; far-right candidate Rafael López Aliaga was second at 13.88%. A June 7 runoff is virtually certain as no candidate is expected to clear the 50% threshold. The absence of any credible left-wing candidate — after the impeachment and imprisonment of Pedro Castillo — is itself a significant political fact. As the WSWS noted, the far right's dominance reflects "the depth of social despair and the absence of any independent political alternative for the working class."

Key facts:
- Approximately 211 polling tables failed to open, disenfranchising 63,000 voters
- Nine out of ten Peruvians disapprove of Congress
- Final results remained unknown as of Monday night; the chaos drew criticism of Peru's electoral authority (ONPE)

### Russia's Supreme Court declares Memorial an "extremist" organization

Russia's Supreme Court ruled on April 9 that International Memorial — one of Russia's oldest and most respected human rights organizations, winner of the 2022 Nobel Peace Prize — constitutes an "extremist" group. The designation results in the shutdown of Memorial's database documenting over three million victims of Stalin's Great Terror, including shooting lists signed personally by Stalin and other Politburo members.

The Justice Ministry filed the lawsuit on March 27. The case concluded in a single closed-door hearing, the case file was classified "top secret," and Memorial's attorneys were not allowed to participate. The designation entails a ban on all Memorial activities under threat of lengthy prison sentences; individuals suspected of involvement can be placed on Russia's "list of extremists" with their bank accounts frozen.

Key facts:
- Memorial was previously ordered liquidated in 2021 but continued operations; the "extremist" designation is a qualitative escalation carrying criminal penalties
- Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and NBC News all reported on the ruling
- Memorial denounced the verdict as "unlawful and unfounded" and pledged to continue its work

### 2,200 Nova Scotia long-term care workers strike

More than 2,200 long-term care workers at 22 facilities across Nova Scotia walked off the job on Monday after being without a contract since October 2023. The striking CUPE members — licensed practical nurses, physiotherapists, continuing-care assistants, and housekeeping staff — earn under $19/hour, compared to workers in comparable roles in Prince Edward Island who earn over $10/hour more.

The Nova Scotia government offered a four-year deal with raises of 12–24%, retroactive pay to 2023, and a 70% increase in shift and weekend premiums, but the union rejected it as inadequate. CUPE represents approximately 5,000 long-term care workers provincewide and expects additional facilities to join the strike in coming days.

IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva stated: "Even our most hopeful scenario involves a growth downgrade," citing infrastructure damage, supply disruptions, and loss of confidence.

The average effective US tariff rate on imported goods stands at 10.3% as of early 2026 — the highest since 1947, up from 2.4% in 2024. A Supreme Court ruling in February struck down IEEPA-based tariffs 6–3; the administration responded with Section 122 Trade Act authority imposing a blanket 10% tariff. The Tax Foundation estimates the current tariff regime amounts to an additional tax burden of $1,500 per US household. ### Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin's output this week consistently channels analysis toward what is useful for the Democratic Party's left flank. **"The Iran Ceasefire Is a Stunning Defeat for Militarism"** frames the ceasefire as proof that anti-war pressure works and that "the American empire has limits" — but the takeaway is aimed at liberal audiences celebrating a diplomatic outcome rather than opposing the imperial system itself. There is no critique of the Democrats who enabled the war buildup and no independent working-class perspective on the conflict.

**"The Fight Against Trumpism Can Reinvigorate Labor"** explicitly channels union energy into defending bourgeois "democracy" against Trump rather than independent class politics. The framing positions labor as a prop for the anti-Trump coalition — classic social-democratism that subordinates the workers' movement to the Democratic Party's electoral terrain.

**"New Yorkers Want to Tax the Rich. Julie Menin Doesn't Care"** functions as implicit campaigning for Zohran Mamdani's Democratic primary bid, framing the political horizon as a progressive tax policy within the capitalist state, promoted through a Democratic candidate. The overall orientation: suppressing independent working-class politics beneath the Democratic Party umbrella while maintaining socialist branding.

### Left Voice

Left Voice published a sharper-than-average critique of AOC in **"Promises, Promises: AOC, Israel, and the DSA,"** identifying her April 1 reversal on military aid to Israel as an opportunist manoeuvre timed to secure DSA endorsement and noting DSA's complicity in allowing it. This is relatively principled compared to most pseudo-left publications.

However, the same week's launch of **"Introducing Left Voice Magazine: The Mamdani Moment and the Fight Against the Far Right"** directly contradicts this stance by framing a Democratic Party candidate's primary campaign as a strategic moment for the socialist left. This repeats the pattern of the PTS/FT sections internationally: subordinating the independent party to popular-front logic. Criticizing AOC with one hand while platform-building for another Democratic candidate with the other reveals the fundamental incoherence of the FT's electoral orientation.

Left Voice also published **"Millions of Workers and Peasants Are Shutting Down India"** on India's February general strike — straightforwardly internationalist in content but lacking a revolutionary party programme beyond sympathy for rank-and-file action.

### Liberation News / PSL

PSL's **"Why Trump Failed in His Criminal War Against Iran"** provides correct anti-imperialist framing on the war itself but exhibits characteristic campism: Iran's theocratic state is implicitly cast as a victim-nation without any class analysis of its own ruling class or the interests of Iranian workers distinct from those of the regime. The anti-war conclusion directs supporters toward "keeping up the pressure" rather than building independent working-class opposition to imperialism.

More revealing is **"Mamdani's Victory and the Movement for Socialism,"** in which the PSL explicitly celebrates a Democratic Party candidate's primary victory as a step toward socialism. This is national-reformism in its purest form: a self-described socialist organization abandoning independent class politics to tail a bourgeois-party candidate. The PSL has no independent electoral strategy; it channels socialist energy into the Democrats when a sufficiently "progressive" candidate appears.

### Socialist Worker / SWP (UK)

Issue 3000's front page — **"Thousands Slaughtered by U.S. and Israel"** — is one of the stronger anti-war covers in recent SWP output, correctly implicating Starmer and the Labour government via British military bases. The line on Starmer is sharper than most of the UK left.

**"How Should We Engage with AI in the Workplace?"** offers trade-union-focused analysis of algorithmic management and AI surveillance but limits its programme to GDPR protections and union negotiation — an economistic framework that never challenges capital's ownership of the technology itself.

The SWP's engagement with "Your Party" — evidenced by coverage of the **"Your Party Leadership Votes to Ban Dual Membership"** — deserves scrutiny as possible entryist adaptation to a populist formation whose political character remains ambiguous. The IST tradition's pattern of entry into non-socialist formations to recruit from their periphery has repeatedly led to political adaptation.

### Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

The RCP (formerly Socialist Appeal) published no notable statement on the Iran ceasefire, Gaza, the Hormuz blockade, or any current international struggle this week — a politically significant silence for an organization claiming the mantle of revolutionary communism during the largest US military operation since Iraq. Their output consists of the Italian PCR congress report (215 delegates from 23 regions — organizational growth data) and **"History Is Moving in Our Direction,"** a Central Committee statement that is pure propaganda — organizational optimism without specific political intervention on any current event.

The IMT/RCP's consistent limitation: strong on Marxist theory in the abstract, weak on concrete tactics for the current conjuncture. An organization that cannot articulate a position on the Iran war in real time is not functioning as a revolutionary leadership.

## What the WSWS should cover today

### 1. The Iranian working class under 45 days of bombardment and naval blockade

Coverage of the Iran war has focused almost entirely on military assets, diplomatic manoeuvres, and oil markets. The material conditions of Iranian workers, civilians, and the poor — subject to ongoing bombardment, a naval blockade cutting off imports, and economic collapse — have been almost entirely absent from Western and WSWS reporting alike. Iran's oil export revenue of approximately $45 billion/year (13% of GDP) is now being strangled. The impact on wages, food prices, medicine, and public services — and the political response of the Iranian working class to both the US assault and their own government — demands direct attention from a socialist publication.

### 2. Global energy price shock and its class impact — who pays for $100+ oil?

While oil prices and GDP projections dominate financial media, the distributional impact — on working-class energy bills, transport costs, food prices (from supply chain disruption), and public budgets in the Global South — has received virtually no coverage. The IMF's own April 2026 WEO warns that "smaller, less diversified economies" face "revenue losses and fiscal strain" with "limited capacity to absorb higher costs." The WSWS should lead on the class dimensions of the energy shock: who benefits and who pays.

### 3. Haiti's Citadelle stampede as a product of state collapse

The deaths of at least 30 Haitians at the Citadelle Laferrière are being framed as a crowd-control failure. The deeper story is Haiti's decade of deliberate state dismantlement — by the Haitian oligarchy, successive US-backed governments, and international "reconstruction" operations — that has left the country without functioning emergency services, safety regulation, or public infrastructure. The WSWS has covered Haiti's political crisis extensively; this disaster is a direct product of those conditions.

### 4. Nova Scotia long-term care workers' strike and the crisis of elder care

The strike of 2,200 long-term care workers — earning under $19/hour, without a contract for over two years, with potential expansion to 5,000 workers — exposes the crisis of elder care across Canada and internationally. The low wages and understaffing that drove the strike are inseparable from the for-profit model of long-term care that produced catastrophic death tolls during COVID-19. This connects directly to the WSWS's pandemic coverage and its analysis of the commodification of health care.

### 5. ProPublica strike: first US newsroom walkout over AI protections

Approximately 150 ProPublica journalists staged a 24-hour strike on April 9 — the first major US newsroom walkout driven by demands for AI protections. With 92% strike authorization, workers demanded restrictions on AI replacing jobs, standard seniority protections in layoffs, and wage increases. The strike raises fundamental questions about the ownership and control of AI technology and the defence of journalism as a profession — questions the WSWS is uniquely positioned to address from a socialist perspective.

### 6. The Trump administration's assault on arts funding and cultural censorship

The proposed elimination of the NEA, NEH, and IMLS — alongside the cancellation of grants based on artists' racial and ethnic identities and the acceleration of book bans to 23,000+ — constitutes the most aggressive federal attack on arts and culture in American history. This is happening simultaneously with a 44% increase in the defense budget. The WSWS's arts criticism tradition provides the framework to analyse this as a deliberate effort to eliminate critical cultural production during wartime.

### 7. Peru's election chaos and the bankruptcy of the political establishment

Peru is electing its ninth president in less than a decade, with the largest field of candidates in history and 63,000 people disenfranchised by logistical collapse. The leading candidates are Keiko Fujimori (whose father is a convicted authoritarian) and Rafael López Aliaga (far right). The absence of a credible left — after the impeachment and imprisonment of Pedro Castillo — is itself the central story, warranting deeper WSWS treatment than the initial dispatch provided.
