### 1. Trump departs for Beijing state visit amid global eruption of imperialist war

President Trump leaves Tuesday evening for a three-day state visit to Beijing (May 13–15) at Xi Jinping's invitation, the first US presidential visit to China in roughly nine years. The trip occurs against the backdrop of an unraveling US-Iran ceasefire, a collapsed Russia-Ukraine truce, and an active US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. The summit agenda includes a bilateral trade management board, an investment forum, and industry-level agreements spanning aerospace, agriculture and energy. The administration's top priority is securing access to rare earths critical to semiconductors; possible Chinese purchases of US soybeans and other agricultural products that Beijing boycotted during the trade war are also on the table.

Trump travels with an unprecedented corporate delegation: Tim Cook (Apple), Elon Musk (Tesla), Larry Fink (BlackRock), Stephen Schwarzman (Blackstone), David Solomon (Goldman Sachs), Jane Fraser (Citigroup), plus representatives from Boeing, Meta, Micron, Qualcomm, Visa, Mastercard and Cargill. Taiwan, the Iran war and AI governance loom over the talks. A regional source told CNN that Iran ceasefire negotiations are "unlikely to make significant progress until Trump meets Xi."

Analysts are broadly skeptical of US leverage. The Council on Foreign Relations assessed that "China will have the upper hand"; Time's pre-summit headline read "Why Trump's China Trip Is Set Up to Fail." As Andre Damon wrote on the WSWS, the summit is taking place during a "six-month arc of relentless US imperialist escalation" — including the kidnapping of Venezuela's president and the war on Iran — that constitutes "the opening clashes of a global conflict targeting China itself." Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent stated explicitly on Fox News: "I would urge the Chinese to join us in supporting this military operation. We have absolute control of the strait."

Key facts:
- First US presidential visit to China since 2017; Trump's first since returning to office
- Wall Street Journal editorial board urges "passing a $1.5 trillion defense budget to rearm" and warns Trump risks becoming the "chief dove" of his own administration
- BYD outsold Tesla globally in 2025 by 600,000+ vehicles, shipping 2.26 million fully electric cars; kept out of US market by 247.5% tariff
- Chinese firms produce roughly four out of every five humanoid robots manufactured worldwide; Unitree alone targets 20,000 units in 2026
- Federal debt approaching $40 trillion; the New York Times notes "concerns about America's mounting debt load and its weaponized use of sanctions" are "raising doubts about the safety of the dollar"

### 2. US-Iran ceasefire on "massive life support" as Trump rejects counterproposal

Trump on Monday called Iran's ceasefire counterproposal "a piece of garbage" and "totally unacceptable," declaring the truce "on massive life support." His aides are now "more seriously considering a resumption of major combat operations," US officials told CNN. The war began February 28, 2026 with a joint US-Israeli operation; the Lebanon front opened March 2 after Hezbollah missile launches. American forces have killed more than 3,000 Iranians; on April 7, Trump publicly threatened that "a whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again."

The administration imposed new sanctions Monday on 12 entities facilitating Iranian oil sales to China — a direct signal to Beijing on the eve of Trump's state visit. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu stated that Iran's "enriched uranium has to be taken out." Iran's parliament speaker shot back: "The longer they drag their feet, the more American taxpayers will pay."

The economic shock is now historic. Saudi Aramco's CEO called it "the largest energy supply shock the world has ever experienced." Brent crude hit $104.21/barrel on Monday. US gasoline has surged from $2.98/gallon at the war's start to $4.52, costing American households an estimated $284 each — a $37 billion aggregate transfer to the oil sector. Death tolls now stand at 2,846+ killed in Lebanon (450+ since the April 17 ceasefire), 3,468+ total in Iran, and 18 Israeli military personnel since combat began. A single Saturday strike killed eight family members in Lebanon, including a six-month-old.

### 3. Russia-Ukraine ceasefire expires as fighting resumes

The three-day Russia-Ukraine ceasefire brokered by Trump (May 9–11) — paired with an exchange of 1,000 prisoners — expired Monday with both sides immediately trading blame for violations. At least three people were killed in Russian drone strikes near the front line on Monday alone, and Ukrainian military tallies recorded more than 200 battlefield clashes since Saturday morning. Russian forces have suffered an estimated 1,342,030 personnel casualties since February 2022, including 920 in the past 24 hours according to Ukrainian figures.

Putin suggested the war may be "coming to an end" and signaled willingness to meet Zelensky in a third country if a deal is near completion — but maintains a hard maximalist demand for control of all Donbas, territory his forces have not fully captured. ### 4. US Justice Department seeks stay on tariff ruling, prepares Supreme Court appeal

The Justice Department on Monday asked the US Court of International Trade to pause its May 7 ruling that struck down Trump's 10% universal tariffs while the administration appeals to the US Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. If the court refuses, the administration is prepared to seek emergency relief at the Supreme Court. The original ruling — handed down 2-1 last Wednesday — found that Trump exceeded his statutory authority under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 in imposing the levies, which are distinct from the earlier IEEPA-based tariffs the Supreme Court rejected.

The Section 122 tariffs currently remain in force for nearly all importers; the ruling presently blocks the tariffs only for two private importers and the State of Washington, which were the named plaintiffs. The tariffs are scheduled to expire in July 2026 regardless, but the administration is fighting the precedent. Tax Foundation analysis finds Trump's tariffs collectively represent the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, averaging $1,500 per household in 2026.

Key facts:
- Court of International Trade ruled 2-1 on May 7; refund portal already opened for the two plaintiffs and Washington State
- US opens tariff refund portal for affected entities; checks expected this summer per Yahoo Finance tariff tracker
- Tariffs scheduled to lapse in July regardless of the appellate outcome
- Administration prepared to escalate to the Supreme Court if a stay is denied at the Federal Circuit

### Sudan famine engulfs Darfur as 34 million face acute crisis

Nearly 34 million people — 65% of Sudan's population — now need urgent humanitarian assistance, and 19 million face high levels of acute food insecurity, according to UN agencies. Famine is confirmed in Al Fasher and Kadugli, with new famine zones declared in Um Baru and Kernoi; an additional 20 areas across Greater Darfur and Greater Kordofan are at risk. UNICEF reports that 4.2 million young children and pregnant or breastfeeding women require malnutrition treatment, and children have endured "1,000 days of agony." Almost 12 million people are internally displaced — the world's largest displacement crisis. The World Food Programme says it urgently requires $610 million to continue Sudan operations through August 2026.

The army and the Rapid Support Forces each control large territories; the conflict is grinding toward de facto partition, with arms flows from outside powers fueling both sides. Western corporate media coverage is minimal, reflecting the geopolitical calculus that Sudan does not advance any major imperialist agenda.

### India-Pakistan: one year after the war, Indus Waters Treaty still suspended

May 7, 2026 marked one year since India launched Operation Sindoor — missile strikes on Pakistan in retaliation for the April 22, 2025 Pahalgam attack that killed 26 civilians. The four-day conflict killed more than 70 and was the worst India-Pakistan fighting since Kargil in 1999. India suspended the Indus Waters Treaty on April 23, 2025 and has not reinstated it. The treaty governs irrigation water for 240 million people and supplies 80% of Pakistan's agricultural water, per World Bank figures. Analysts warn a new armed confrontation is possible in 2026 because "the drivers of conflict, particularly terrorism emanating from Pakistan, remain intact." Both governments held commemorative events on May 7 claiming strategic success. Al Jazeera's assessment: the two countries are "technically at peace — in the way that two people who once threw furniture at each other are technically peaceful roommates."

### France charges suspect in alleged Louvre, Paris Jewish community plot

A 27-year-old Tunisian national living in France irregularly was arrested May 7 by the DGSI (French domestic intelligence) and on Monday was charged by a Paris anti-terrorism judge with criminal conspiracy to commit terrorist acts. The named targets were the Louvre museum and the Jewish community in Paris's 16th arrondissement; the DGSI described preparations as already "at an advanced stage" at the time of arrest. The suspect was also said to be contemplating travel to join Islamic State in Syria or Mozambique. He has been remanded in pre-trial detention. The case is being politically deployed by the Macron government to justify ongoing expansions of surveillance powers and accelerated deportations of irregular migrants — a pattern across European capitals as governments respond to the Iran war by tightening domestic emergency measures.

### MV Hondius hantavirus outbreak spreads to four countries

A rapidly worsening hantavirus (Andes virus) outbreak aboard the Antarctic expedition cruise ship MV Hondius has killed three passengers — a Dutch couple and a German woman — with confirmed and probable cases now spread to the United States, France, Switzerland, the Netherlands and Spain following government-organized repatriation flights that effectively seeded the pathogen internationally. The Andes strain is the only hantavirus known to transmit human-to-human; there is no vaccine, no specific treatment, and a fatality rate of roughly 40% among severely ill patients. US Acting CDC Director Jay Bhattacharya — co-author of the Great Barrington Declaration — publicly argued against contact tracing on the repatriation flights, claiming the virus cannot spread before symptom onset, a position that WSWS public health analysts Alex Lantier and Benjamin Mateus describe as "completely unsubstantiated." Officials simultaneously used the National Quarantine Unit at the University of Nebraska Medical Center and biocontainment aircraft protocols that contradict their public reassurances. The outbreak is the first major test of the post-pandemic public health architecture under the new HHS leadership.

### US measles cases approach 1,850 — elimination status at risk

As of May 7, the CDC has confirmed 1,842 measles cases in 2026 across 39 jurisdictions, with 93% (1,712) outbreak-associated. Austin reported its first new case this week — an unvaccinated adult exposed while traveling internationally. Texas's South Plains region remains a focal outbreak zone. CIDRAP analysts say the United States is now "highly likely" to lose its measles elimination status this fall, with 2026 ranking among the two worst years since the year 2000.

### Lancet: dual antiplatelet therapy after thrombolysis improves stroke outcomes

The TAPIS trial, published May 8 in *The Lancet*, found that initiating dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) within 6 hours of IV thrombolysis in moderate ischemic stroke patients significantly improved excellent functional outcomes at 90 days, without a statistically significant increase in symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage. The finding challenges the conventional caution around combining antiplatelet agents with thrombolytics and may shift acute stroke management protocols globally.

### Meta's 2026 AI capex jumps to $145 billion

Meta raised its full-year 2026 capex guidance from $115–135 billion to **$125–145 billion** after Q1 earnings, citing higher component costs and expanded data center commitments. Infrastructure contract commitments jumped $107 billion in a single quarter. Meta stock fell roughly 9% in the session following the announcement. Meta's 2026 AI infrastructure spend alone now exceeds its combined spending for all of 2024 and 2025, underscoring the capital arms race among frontier AI labs.

## Arts and culture

The major arts story of the past 24 hours is the **61st Venice Biennale**, which opened amid coordinated strikes and demonstrations by cultural workers and artists protesting Israel's ongoing genocide in Gaza. The Art Not Genocide Alliance issued the call for industry-wide action, and walkouts swept the opening events. Demonstrators demanded that cultural institutions sever ties with Israeli state sponsorship, including the bilateral funding agreements that placed Israel's national pavilion in operation despite the ongoing destruction of Gaza's universities, museums and cultural heritage. As WSWS arts editor Stefan Steinberg reported, the actions go beyond symbolic gestures: cultural workers identified specific institutional complicities — board memberships, sponsorship arrangements, official state cooperation agreements — and called for their termination. Major outlets including *The Guardian*, *ARTnews* and *Artforum* covered the protests, though largely as a curiosity rather than as a serious challenge to the political economy of state-sponsored art.

The protests represent a continuation of the labor unrest that has rippled through major cultural institutions over the past two years — film festivals, opera houses, museum staff unions — as workers respond to imperialist war and to the censorship of pro-Palestine speech inside the institutions that employ them. Underlying the conflict is a structural fact: the major biennales, festivals and museums function as instruments of state cultural diplomacy. When the cultural workers who actually make these institutions function refuse to participate in normalization, the institutions face a legitimacy crisis they cannot resolve through public relations alone.

The Venice protests intersect with the broader pattern of arts and culture under siege documented in recent months:

- **Censorship and defunding:** US federal arts agencies continue to operate under a directive prohibiting funding for projects "in conflict with administration priorities," a vague standard already used to defund projects engaging with Palestine, climate change, transgender history and labor history
- **Institutional capture:** Several major US museums have quietly added "national security review" requirements to international exhibition planning, reflecting the deepening militarization of cultural policy
- **Cultural workers organizing:** Beyond the Biennale, unionization drives continue at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the Whitney, and several smaller institutions, often in conflict with management's political alignments
- **Genre under pressure:** War-themed prestige film and television production has surged, with multiple Iran-war-themed projects already in development less than three months into the conflict — a development worth tracking for its propaganda content
- **The German rearmament cultural front:** As Christoph Vandreier's May Day speech noted, German cultural institutions are being mobilized in service of the largest rearmament program since Hitler; programming on "European security" and "transatlantic resilience" has expanded sharply across publicly-funded venues, often crowding out anti-war voices

The most politically significant development remains the willingness of cultural workers — performers, technicians, curators, writers — to engage in industrial action over imperialist war. This represents the kind of class-conscious response to genocide that the official institutions of social democracy and the trade union bureaucracies have refused to provide. The Venice walkouts are a model of the working-class political solidarity that the WSWS and ICFI have called for, and they exist in sharp contradiction to the institutional silence of governing center-left parties across Europe.

### 1. US households absorb $37 billion gas price shock from Iran war

US consumers have absorbed a $37 billion increase in gasoline and diesel costs since the Iran war began, averaging $284 per household, even as Saudi Aramco's CEO calls it "the largest energy supply shock the world has ever experienced." ### 2. Pakistan's agricultural water crisis — 240 million people threatened by Indus Waters Treaty suspension

India's suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty, in place since 1960, threatens the water supply for 80% of Pakistan's agriculture and the livelihoods of 240 million people. This is a slow-motion humanitarian crisis rooted in the colonial partition of South Asia and the unresolved Kashmir conflict — a story Western media frames solely as a bilateral security issue, when in reality it is a class question affecting hundreds of millions of agricultural workers and peasants. A WSWS analysis connecting the water suspension to the broader regional war drive and to working-class organization across the subcontinent would fill a significant gap.

### 3. Sudan famine — 34 million in crisis while world looks away

Sudan is the world's largest humanitarian crisis and largest internal displacement crisis. ### 4. Trump's tariffs and the class dimension of who pays, who profits

The legal battle over Trump's 10% global tariffs is being covered as a separation-of-powers question, but the underlying reality is a $1,500-per-household tax on US workers, while large corporations receive exemptions and the administration fights in court to preserve the levies. The Tax Foundation finds this is the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993. A WSWS analysis breaking down who pays and who profits — by income decile, by industry, by trading partner — would expose the class structure of the trade-war regime.
