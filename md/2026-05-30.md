### 1. US-Iran ceasefire extension stalls as Trump withholds approval

US and Iranian negotiators reached a tentative agreement to extend the three-month-old ceasefire by 60 days and open fresh nuclear talks, but President Trump's Situation Room meeting on May 29 ended without a decision. Under the draft terms, Iran would clear all mines from the Strait of Hormuz within 30 days; in return, Washington would gradually lift its naval blockade on Iranian ports and relax sanctions to permit increased Iranian oil exports.

The central obstacle remains Iran's stockpile of **440.9 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60 percent**. Trump opposes transferring the material to either China or Russia, and no third-party custodian has been agreed. Tehran further demands that any deal halt Israeli military operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon — a condition Washington has not accepted.

Key facts:

- Vice President JD Vance: *"It's hard to say exactly when or if the president's going to sign."*
- Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, speaking at the Shangri-La Dialogue in Singapore on May 30, said the US is *"more than capable"* of resuming strikes.
- An adviser to Iran's supreme leader accused Trump of *"betraying diplomacy for the third time"* by maintaining the blockade while negotiating.
- Iranian forces fired ballistic missiles at Kuwait and launched drones toward the Strait on May 29, even as talks continued.

The WSWS has tracked the US-Israel war on Iran since February as a calculated imperialist war drive, not a misadventure — the negotiation posture is a tactic within that drive, not a departure from it.

### 4. Ebola epidemic accelerates across central Africa; Kenyan court blocks US offshore camp

A rare-strain Ebola epidemic is accelerating across central Africa, with the WHO Director-General traveling to Kinshasa on May 30 to oversee the response. The outbreak, caused by the **Bundibugyo strain** — for which existing vaccines are ineffective — has reached over **1,200 suspected and confirmed cases and roughly 250 deaths** as of late May, already the third-largest Ebola outbreak in history.

In a major development, Kenya's High Court froze a Trump administration plan to establish a 50-bed offshore Ebola facility at Laikipia Air Base (~125 miles north of Nairobi), barring the government from "establishing, operationalizing, facilitating, approving or permitting" any such arrangement pending a June 2 hearing.

Key facts:

- The Trump administration has cut USAID funding by 90 percent over 15 months and withdrawn from the WHO.
- The DRC confirmed its FIFA World Cup delegation is in compliance with US Ebola protocols as the US co-hosts the 2026 tournament.

### India and US sign critical minerals framework

India and the United States signed a framework cooperation agreement on critical minerals and rare earths in New Delhi, covering mining, processing and recycling of materials vital to defense and green-technology supply chains. The partnership explicitly aims to diversify supply chains **away from China**, which dominates global rare-earth processing at roughly 85–90 percent of capacity.

The deal follows the May 14–15 Trump-Xi Beijing summit, where China agreed to expand purchases of US goods including at least **$17 billion per year in US agricultural products** across 2026–2028. The minerals — used heavily in weapons systems, drones and military electronics — make the agreement a militarized supply-chain realignment that locks India deeper into the US-China rivalry.

### US measles cases approach 2,000, elimination status at risk

As of May 28, the CDC reported **1,983 confirmed measles cases** across 40 jurisdictions, with 30 active outbreaks; 93 percent of cases were outbreak-associated and roughly 92 percent occurred in unvaccinated individuals or those of unknown status. The US risks losing its official measles elimination status, to be reviewed in November 2026, as MMR coverage in children has slipped from 95.2 percent (2019–20) to **92.5 percent (2024–25)**. The crisis is mirrored internationally: Peru declared a 90-day emergency after 301 confirmed and 1,403 suspected cases, with Puno region vaccination collapsing from 70 percent to just 16 percent — a failure the WSWS attributes to IMF/World Bank austerity gutting public health.

### Phase 3 trial: bepirovirsen "functionally cures" 1-in-5 hepatitis B patients

In the global B-Well Phase 3 trial (1,834 participants, 29 countries) published in the **NEJM**, **19–20 percent of patients** achieved a functional cure (sustained HBsAg loss plus undetectable viral DNA) after 24 weeks of the RNA-targeting drug bepirovirsen, versus **0 percent on placebo** and ~3 percent with the current decade-long standard of care. It is the first Phase 3 evidence that fixed-duration therapy can durably suppress hepatitis B, which chronically infects ~300 million people worldwide. An FDA priority-review decision is expected in Q3 2026.

### H5N1 fatal child case in Bangladesh; 71 US human cases

The WHO tracked 13 avian/swine influenza human cases in Q1 2026, including a previously unreported **fatal H5N1 case in a child in Bangladesh**. The US has logged **71 total A(H5) human cases** since February 2024, concentrated among dairy and poultry workers, with no sustained human-to-human transmission detected. Scientists warn the virus is circulating in more species and continents than at any prior point, raising pandemic risk if it adapts to efficient human spread.

### COVID wastewater at lowest since spring 2021

SARS-CoV-2 wastewater concentration is at its lowest recorded level since spring 2021, with national test positivity at **0.83 percent** and hospitalizations near 0.2 per 100,000. However, cases are growing in Georgia, Florida and Louisiana as heat pushes people indoors; the dominant variant is **XFG.1.1** (32 percent of sequences), while the emerging BA.3.2 ("Cicada") sub-variant shows slow growth in the Northeast.

The EU's "Digital Omnibus" package — the first amendments to the AI Act since June 2024 — pushes high-risk AI system compliance deadlines from August 2026 to **December 2027** (a 16-month deferral) and adds two new prohibited practices: AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) and child sexual abuse material (CSAM). Separately, the Trump administration is now considering formal oversight of frontier AI models — a policy it previously rejected — driven by national-security concerns over Anthropic's new "Mythos" model.

## WSWS coverage — last 24 hours

**Perspective published:** *The Nexteer rebellion: The working class vs the trade union apparatus* by the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees (URL confirms today's date, /2026/05/30/) — read in full on wsws.org.

The Perspective treats the contract rebellion at Nexteer Automotive in Saginaw, Michigan as a signal event in the broader conflict between the working class and the trade union apparatus. Workers rejected three successive tentative agreements — by **96.2 percent** (April 2), **73 percent** (May 14), and **55–45 percent** (May 28–29, production workers at 59 percent) — and authorized a strike by **86 percent** on May 20–21, yet the UAW has refused to call any work stoppage. The IWA-RFC argues the apparatus functions as "labor police," citing UAW officials collecting **$3.2 million in 2024**, with president Shawn Fain receiving $274,407, and a court-appointed monitor's documentation of officials' threats of violence. At a May 18 meeting, International servicing representative **Jason Tuck** allegedly "cursed workers and threatened plant closure if they struck," then walked out. The piece draws parallels to a 41-day New York nurses' strike and a three-week Colorado meatpacking strike, both shut down without contracts workers could review. It sharply criticizes the DSA and Jacobin for promoting union leadership despite this record, and calls for rank-and-file committees independent of the apparatus, coordination with American Axle workers, and transparent access to contract language.

### After third contract rejection, Nexteer workers denounce UAW-management collusion — By our reporter

On-the-ground interviews add worker testimony to the Perspective's analysis. A 16-year veteran called the five-year deal's $3.00 raise "a slap in the face." A younger worker reported intensified management harassment after each rejection and asked, "What are we paying the union for, if they let management" violate contract language? A 20-year veteran called for solidarity with American Axle workers, noting a strike "would shut down GM and Stellantis for sure." UAW Local 699 warned members to keep working and threatened arbitration against unauthorized action — concrete confirmation of the bureaucracy's role as enforcer against its own members.

### Sri Lankan court case continues over violent attack on SEP members — Our reporters

Moratuwa Magistrate's Court continued proceedings over a November 2023 assault on SEP members Dehin Wasantha and Lakshman Fernando, attacked with wooden poles while distributing leaflets for meetings commemorating the centenary of Trotskyism. The ACTU praised the Labor government's federal budget as "fairer for workers," citing property tax and capital gains tweaks the WSWS shows are cosmetic: the impact is only 7,500 additional home purchases annually (0.026% of the population), and $250 in promised tax relief won't arrive until mid-2028. The article connects the repression to Labor's simultaneous NDIS cuts — the same state apparatus silencing dissent and disciplining workers.

### Students protest against New Zealand budget cuts — Tom Peters

Thousands of students protested across New Zealand on May 28 against a coalition budget eliminating "fees-free" third-year tuition (worth up to $12,000) and authorizing a **6 percent fee increase**. ### Workers Struggles: Asia, Australia and the Pacific

A regional digest of labor actions. **India:** Kerala transport unions protested fuel prices; Punjab sanitation workers ended a 16-day strike after talks on a 26,000-rupee minimum wage; Shimla sanitation workers won a 10 percent pay restoration; **1.2 million** chemists and pharmaceutical distributors struck nationally against illegal online medicine sales. **Bangladesh:** police attacked Chaity Composite garment workers with tear gas and rubber bullets over unpaid wages; Energypac workers blocked a highway over April wage nonpayment. **Australia:** 20,000 early childhood educators face a 4–15 percent "pay cliff" when $3.6 billion in funding expires in November; ACT teachers held their first strike in 15 years; 350 ACT hospital doctors struck over a 3 percent offer against a 6 percent demand; Parks Victoria workers struck for the first time since 2012; DXC Technology workers staged a third 72-hour strike after the company admitted underpaying its 5,000 Australian workers since 2017.

### Miles Davis at 100, a complex figure reflecting a complex time — John Andrews

Marking the centenary of Davis's birth, Andrews traces the trumpeter from bebop with Charlie Parker through the "Birth of the Cool" Capitol sessions with Gil Evans, the modal revolution of *Kind of Blue* (1959, the best-selling jazz album in history), and the electrified fusion of the late 1960s–70s. Davis's dictum — "Don't play what's there, play what's not there" — captures his emphasis on attitude over technical execution. The piece does not evade his contradictions: his physical abuse of first wife Frances Taylor, his aloof persona while colleagues struggled financially, and his political conventionality (performing at a 1961 benefit for an imperialist-aligned African relief foundation that Max Roach protested). Andrews argues Davis's artistic peak coincided with postwar labor militancy and the civil rights movement, and that his later retreat mirrored broader cultural decline through the Reagan era — closing with the suggestion that a renewed leftward movement could again generate conditions for transformative art.
