### 3. Strait of Hormuz closure triggers largest oil supply disruption in history

The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz since March 2 has produced what IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol called "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Tanker traffic through the strait has fallen to less than 10% of pre-crisis flows, taking an estimated 10 million barrels per day of Gulf oil production offline. On March 11, the IEA released an unprecedented 400 million barrels of emergency reserves — a measure that benefits OECD member states while leaving much of Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia exposed to unmitigated price shocks.

The humanitarian consequences extend well beyond energy. Seventy percent of Gulf food imports have been disrupted, causing 40–120% price spikes in consumer staples across the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Saudi Arabia. European gas storage stands at just 30% after a harsh winter, with the Dutch TTF gas benchmark nearly doubling to over €60/MWh. Pakistan brokered a limited deal allowing 20 Pakistani-flagged vessels to transit the strait — two ships per day — as a confidence-building measure, but this represents a fraction of normal traffic.

Key facts:
- Brent crude peaked at **$126/barrel**; currently ~$115/barrel, more than 55% above pre-war level
- At least **40 energy assets across 9 countries** severely damaged
- The Ras Laffan LNG facility in Qatar — the world's largest — has been offline since March 2
- First time oil exceeded $100/barrel in four years was March 8

### Trump administration allows CDC director vacancy to lapse, transferring powers to RFK Jr.

The Trump administration allowed the 210-day Federal Vacancies Reform Act deadline to pass on March 25 without nominating a CDC director, causing non-delegable statutory powers to revert directly to HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. The WSWS's Benjamin Mateus argued this was the intended outcome, not an oversight — part of a systematic dismantling of the nation's public health infrastructure.

The CDC has lost roughly a quarter of its workforce through layoffs and attrition. Disease surveillance capabilities are "severely disrupted." Approximately 20 Kennedy-aligned political appointees now micromanage budgets and communications across the agency. On March 16, a federal judge blocked Kennedy's dismantling of the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) and rollbacks of childhood vaccination schedules.

Key facts:
- The administration is pursuing ICD-10 diagnostic codes for "Post-Acute Covid-19 Vaccination Syndrome" (PACVS) — a move that could provide legal scaffolding to sue vaccine manufacturers and potentially eliminate liability protections
- If liability protections are removed, manufacturers would likely withdraw vaccines from the US market
- No nominee for CDC director has been announced or is reportedly under consideration

### H5N1 bird flu shifts toward domestic poultry in the Americas

A PAHO/WHO epidemiological update reports 75 confirmed human H5N1 infections and 2 deaths across 5 countries in the Americas since April 2022, with the virus detected in 37 mammalian and 94 bird species. While no new human cases have been reported since November 2025, a significant epidemiological shift is underway: detections in wild birds are declining while outbreaks in domestic poultry are increasing. This pivot from wildlife to farm flocks raises the risk profile for agricultural workers and complicates containment heading into spring poultry season — precisely when larger numbers of workers are in close proximity to birds in enclosed facilities.

### Medical research roundup

- **Lancet PRO-TAVI trial** (*The Lancet*, Vol. 407, Issue 10535, March 28): In patients with coronary artery disease undergoing transcatheter aortic valve implantation, deferring percutaneous coronary intervention was non-inferior to upfront PCI at 1 year for the composite of all-cause mortality, MI, stroke, and major bleeding. This supports a "watchful waiting" approach that could reduce procedural burden for a large patient population.
- **NEJM COVID-19 vaccine effectiveness** (*NEJM*, NEJMoa2510226): A large observational study in US Veterans assessed the 2024–2025 updated COVID-19 vaccine's association with COVID-19 outcomes. This represents one of the first real-world effectiveness analyses of the updated formulation across a large, older, high-comorbidity population — data that is politically contested given the administration's anti-vaccine posture.

## Arts and culture

The arts world this week is marked by three developments of genuine cultural significance: the 50th anniversary of South Africa's Market Theatre, a political testing ground during apartheid; the launch of *Saturday Night Live UK*, a transatlantic cultural export at a moment of acute political tension; and the continued reverberations of the 98th Academy Awards, where Paul Thomas Anderson's *One Battle After Another* dominated with six Oscars.

### Market Theatre celebrates 50 years as apartheid-era cultural landmark

Johannesburg's Market Theatre is marking its 50th anniversary — a milestone for the institution that became one of the most consequential cultural spaces of the anti-apartheid struggle. Opened in an old fruit and vegetable market just three days after the 1976 Soweto uprising in which hundreds of protesting students were killed, the theater hosted work by Athol Fugard and Tony Award-winner John Kani at a time when multiracial audiences were considered subversive.

Current artistic director Greg Homann recalled that "the theater was often raided. Actors were sometimes in some kind of danger" — apartheid censors would walk onstage during performances. The Market's history embodies the relationship between artistic expression and political resistance that the WSWS has consistently emphasized. Yet the celebration also invites sober reflection: South Africa's post-apartheid ANC government has presided over deepening inequality, mass unemployment, and the degradation of public services. The cultural space that once challenged state power now operates in a society where the promises of liberation remain unfulfilled for the working class. Issues of access to education, corruption, and gender-based violence are being tackled on its stage as the Market turns 50.

## What the WSWS should cover today

### 1. The global food crisis inside Gulf states from the Hormuz blockade

Seventy percent of food imports to Gulf Cooperation Council states — which rely on the Strait of Hormuz for over 80% of their caloric intake — have been disrupted, causing 40–120% price spikes in consumer staples by mid-March. This is a humanitarian crisis affecting tens of millions of migrant workers and low-income residents in the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Saudi Arabia. These workers, predominantly from South Asia and Southeast Asia, have no political rights and no recourse. Western media has focused almost exclusively on oil prices and energy markets while ignoring the food dimension and its class character.

### 4. Who controls global oil reserves? The IEA emergency release and the Global South

The IEA's unprecedented release of 400 million barrels of emergency reserves on March 11 raises fundamental questions about which institutions govern global energy resources. Countries outside the IEA framework — most of Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia — have no access to this emergency buffer and face unmitigated price shocks. The class dimension is stark: wealthy OECD states cushion their populations (partially) while the poorest countries absorb the full impact of a war they had no role in launching.

### 5. The school strike in Minab and the accountability vacuum for civilian casualties in Iran

The February 28 strike on the Shajareh Tayyebeh girls' elementary school in Minab, with unconfirmed reports of 175 killed mostly children, has received almost no sustained Western media coverage. A dedicated investigation connecting this to the broader pattern of targeting civilian infrastructure — universities, hospitals, schools — would serve as a powerful indictment of the war's character.
