### 1. US-Iran war: Iran seizes ships, peace talks collapse, oil surges past $100

### 5. DOJ indicts Southern Poverty Law Center on 11 federal counts

A federal grand jury in Montgomery, Alabama, on April 21 returned a 14-page indictment against the Southern Poverty Law Center on six counts of wire fraud, four counts of false statements to a federally insured bank, and one count of conspiracy to commit concealment money laundering. DOJ alleges the SPLC funneled more than $3 million in donor funds between 2014 and 2023 to paid informants inside the KKK, Aryan Nations, and National Socialist Party of America, using shell entities such as "Fox Photography" and "Rare Books Warehouse." FBI Director **Kash Patel** announced the charges personally.

The case is widely read as a Trump administration move to dismantle the country's pre-eminent civil rights monitoring organization. As Jacob Crosse notes on the WSWS, when pressed, Acting Attorney General **Todd Blanche** acknowledged that the money did not actually benefit extremist groups — the charge concerns disclosure of informant payments, a routine law enforcement practice.

Key facts:

- SPLC Interim CEO **Bryan Fair** called the charges "false allegations," saying the group has "stood as a beacon of hope fighting white supremacy for 55 years"
- The indictment comes as figures previously tracked by the SPLC — including Stephen Miller and Jack Posobiec — occupy positions of power in the second Trump administration
- SPLC's Intelligence Project publishes the "Hatewatch" database used by journalists and researchers globally; its survival as an active monitor is now in question

### Progressive Bulgaria wins absolute majority as GERB collapses

Progressive Bulgaria — a coalition formed in March 2026 and formally unified on April 17 under President **Rumen Radev** — won 43.91% of the vote and 130 of 240 National Assembly seats in snap elections, enough to govern without coalition partners. The previously dominant GERB party's vote collapsed from 25.52% to 13.18%, and the Bulgarian Socialist Party fell below the 4% threshold for the first time since the restoration of capitalism in 1989.

The elections were triggered by mass December protests against the outgoing government's military-heavy 2026 budget. Two-thirds of previous non-voters and 34% of voters aged 18–30 backed Radev. Despite his anti-war rhetoric, Radev is a former US military-trained officer whose presidency has overseen continued Bulgarian arms shipments to Ukraine and one of the EU's worst COVID death tolls (548 deaths per 100,000, as the WSWS documents).

### US measles cases reach 1,748 across 33 jurisdictions

As of April 16, the US has reported **1,748 confirmed measles cases** across 33 states and jurisdictions in 2026, with **94%** (1,637 cases) outbreak-associated. Weekly growth has slowed to just **0.40%** — seven new cases in the most recent week, the slowest rate since the surge began. South Carolina alone accounts for **997 cases**, centered in Spartanburg County, where state officials have been accused of suppressing case-level data. 2026 is already on track to be one of the two worst measles years since 2000, threatening the country's endemic-elimination status for the first time in decades.

### Cambodia confirms fourth human H5N1 case of 2026

Cambodia's National Institute of Public Health on April 21 confirmed H5N1 avian influenza in a **66-year-old woman** from Svay Rieng province, now in isolation in intensive care. Investigators found sick and dead backyard chickens at her home from April 2–13 that had been used for cooking; no human-to-human transmission has been identified. Four Cambodian human cases in roughly 90 days reinforces scientific concern that H5N1 continues to spill over from poultry to humans at a persistent low-level rate in Southeast Asia.

### Stanford AI Index: US private investment hits $285.9 billion, public trust collapses

Stanford HAI's 2026 AI Index, released this week, documents US private AI investment at **$285.9 billion** in 2025 — roughly **23 times** China's $12.4 billion. AI coding benchmarks hit near-100% on SWE-bench Verified (up from 60% in one year), and frontier models now exceed human baselines on PhD-level science questions. Simultaneously, only **31% of Americans** trust their government to regulate AI — last among surveyed nations — and Foundation Model Transparency Index scores fell from 58 to 40. Organizational adoption has hit 88%; 80% of university students now use AI tools.

### OpenAI's "Superintelligence New Deal" reignites federal vs. state AI preemption fight

OpenAI's 13-page "Superintelligence New Deal" blueprint, published April 5–6 under CEO **Sam Altman**'s byline, proposes higher capital-gains and corporate taxes on AI-driven returns, a nationally managed public wealth fund seeded by AI companies, a four-day workweek with no pay cut, and federal guardrails on government AI use. It explicitly warns that superintelligence may arrive "soon" and that advanced AI could enable a "world-shaking cyberattack" as early as 2026. Critics — including AI governance researchers — argue the proposals are strategically vague in ways that favor regulatory capture by frontier labs. Over **100 state AI laws** were enacted in 2025 and **600+ new state bills** are active this session.

### COVID at near-record low, NIST opens agentic AI standards process

COVID-19 activity is at a near-record low in the US. As of April 14, transmission is declining or stable in **45 of 46 states** monitored; test positivity was **1.8%** for the week ending April 4; COVID accounted for just **0.3%** of all emergency department visits as of April 17. The anticipated spring wave has not materialized.

NIST's Center for AI Standards and Innovation has issued a Request for Information on secure development of agentic AI systems and published a concept paper on "agentic identity standards" — a new domain focused on how autonomous AI agents authenticate and authorize actions. The push signals that regulators see a qualitative risk shift as AI moves from answering questions to autonomously taking actions.

### Two independent films on the incarcerated and the working poor tested against Belinsky

The most substantive arts commentary of the 24-hour window came from Joanne Laurier's WSWS review of two independent American films — *Ricky*, directed by Rashad Frett, following a 30-year-old formerly incarcerated Connecticut man after 15 years in prison, and *Mad Bills to Pay (or Destiny, dile que no soy malo)*, directed by Joel Alfonso Vargas, tracking a 19-year-old Dominican American navigating poverty and early fatherhood in the Bronx.

Laurier finds both films worthy in intent but insufficient in execution. *Ricky* flattens social reality into what she calls "grey monotone"; *Mad Bills to Pay* remains claustrophobically individual, failing to indicate "any objectively existing impulse for social breakthrough." Invoking the 19th-century Russian critic Belinsky — "Society is also something real, and its essence is made up not only of costumes and hairstyles, but also of customs, habits, concepts, relations" — Laurier argues that genuine realism must depict the structural forces (mass incarceration as a systemic phenomenon involving "approximately two million people," the labor market, the housing crisis) that shape individual lives, not merely the surface details of those lives. The review is a polemic against the contemporary indie tradition's retreat from the social totality into lifestyle documentation.

### Fuqua's Michael Jackson biopic arrives amid legacy debates

Antoine Fuqua's long-awaited biographical film *Michael*, with Jaafar Jackson in the title role, arrives in theaters April 24 after years of production and public controversy over estate involvement and casting. The release comes alongside the *Beaches: A New Musical* stage adaptation (opened April 22 at the Majestic), Netflix's *Stranger Things: Tales From '85* spin-off (April 23), the opening of the OutShine LGBTQ+ Film Festival in Miami (April 23–May 10), and a new Charlize Theron Australian-wilderness thriller for Netflix debuting April 24. The OutShine Festival, by contrast, continues to program independent queer cinema at a moment of intensifying political attack on LGBTQ+ cultural institutions in the United States.
