### 1. Iran re-closes Strait of Hormuz, citing US port blockade

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps announced April 18 that the Strait of Hormuz has "returned to its previous state" of tight control, reversing a brief reopening during the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire. In a formal statement, the IRGC declared: "Until the US restores full freedom of navigation for vessels travelling from Iran to their destinations and back, the status of the Strait of Hormuz will remain tightly controlled." The move came after US Central Command turned back approximately 21 vessels at Iranian ports, though at least eight oil and gas tankers had already transited before the closure was reimposed.

As Alex Lantier noted on the WSWS, Iran's foreign minister had earlier attempted to declare the strait "completely open for the remaining period of ceasefire," but Trump immediately countered that the US naval blockade of Iranian ports would "remain in full force" — exposing the reopening as conditional and fragile from the outset. Trump threatened escalation: "Maybe I won't extend it… So you'll have a blockade, and unfortunately we'll have to start dropping bombs again."

Key facts:
- 20% of globally traded oil transits the Strait of Hormuz; approximately 11 million barrels per day of Iranian production has been disrupted since "Operation Epic Fury" launched February 28
- The IEA has called the disruption "the greatest energy security threat in history"
- Brent crude fell 9.07% to $90.38/barrel on ceasefire optimism despite the closure; analysts warn prices could surge to $200/barrel if the blockade persists
- USS Gerald R. Ford carrier group now operating in the Red Sea; USS Miguel Keith is steaming toward the Strait of Malacca
- Cumulative Iran war casualties per WSWS reporting: 1,500 killed, 18,551 wounded, 81,000+ civilian structures damaged, 3.2 million internally displaced
- Next round of US-Iran negotiations scheduled for Monday in Pakistan

### 4. Sudan civil war enters fourth year; 34 million need aid

The war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) entered its fourth year on April 15, with 34 million people — roughly 65% of Sudan's population — requiring urgent humanitarian assistance. UN Emergency Relief Coordinator Tom Fletcher marked the anniversary with a statement that "this grim and chastening anniversary marks another year when the world has failed" Sudan.

The nearly $3 billion humanitarian response plan is only 16% funded. A Berlin donors conference in mid-April pledged $1.5 billion — still far below requirements. Drone warfare has emerged as the defining feature of the war's current phase: roughly 700 civilians were killed by drones in the first three months of 2026 alone, with 80% of documented child casualties attributed to drone strikes.

Key facts:
- Estimated 400,000 dead (higher estimates exist); 14 million displaced — 9 million internally, 4.4 million to Chad, Egypt, and South Sudan
- 4,300+ children killed or maimed since the war began in April 2023
- 12.7 million people require support for sexual and gender-based violence, up from 3.1 million in 2023
- El Fasher, the last SAF-held city in Darfur, remains under RSF siege

### 5. Gaza ceasefire failing at six months; all crossings closed

### 6. Canada: Carney Liberals secure historic majority

Prime Minister Mark Carney's Liberal Party won all three federal byelections held April 14, securing what Canadian political analysts are calling the first minority-to-majority transition between general elections in Canadian history. Winning candidates were Danielle Martin in University-Rosedale, Doly Begum in Scarborough Southwest, and Tatiana Auguste in Terrebonne (a narrow result). Combined with five floor-crossers from opposition parties since November 2025, the Liberals now hold 171+ seats in the House of Commons.

The government is now highly unlikely to fall on a confidence vote, with the next general election potentially deferred until October 2029. Carney's government has been defined by managing Canada-US trade tensions under Trump's tariff regime and absorbing the domestic impact of the Iran war's oil shock — exactly the crises the Canadian bourgeoisie hired the former central banker to manage.

Key facts:
- The three byelection wins were contested without the NDP recovering any of its 2025 election collapse
- Conservative leader Pierre Poilievre now faces pressure inside the Conservative caucus after the byelection sweep
- Ontario and Quebec voters continued to consolidate behind the Liberals on anti-tariff and "national unity" grounds

### 7. US tariff regime in disarray after Supreme Court ruling

Trump's tariff regime has been thrown into legal disarray following the Supreme Court's February 20 ruling that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not authorize tariffs. Chief Justice Roberts wrote the 6-3 majority opinion, joined by Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, Gorsuch, Barrett, and Jackson. More than $160 billion in tariffs deemed illegally collected now represents a major fiscal exposure for the Treasury.

Trump immediately invoked Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, imposing a 10% blanket tariff — a mechanism that expires automatically in 150 days unless Congress acts. USTR has simultaneously launched 76 new Section 301 investigations as an alternate legal basis. The Tax Foundation reports that Trump's tariffs have produced the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, averaging $1,500 per household in 2026.

Key facts:
- Procter & Gamble raised prices on 25% of its product lines, citing a $1 billion annual tariff cost
- Fortune's state-by-state study found tariffs "dealt an economic blow to all 50 states"
- March CPI jumped 0.9% month-over-month — the largest monthly increase in over a year — with gasoline up 21.2% (driven by the Hormuz shock) and energy overall up 10.9%
- Q1 2026 bankruptcy filings spiked 14%, with roughly 41% of filers citing tariff and cost-of-living pressures

### Myanmar: resistance forms new alliance; junta controls under half of territory

Myanmar's civil war, at 78 years, is the longest-running civil war in modern history.

### US measles cases hit 1,748, worst pace since 2000

The CDC reports 1,748 confirmed measles cases across 33 jurisdictions year-to-date, with 94% (1,637 cases) linked to 19 active outbreaks. At the current pace, 2026 is on track to be the worst measles year in a generation — the full year 2025 ended with 2,288 cases. States affected span the country, including Texas, Ohio, Florida, and New York. Kindergarten MMR coverage has fallen to 92.5%, down from 95.2% in 2019–20, leaving an estimated 286,000 unprotected children in the pediatric cohort alone.

### Oral GLP-1 orforglipron delivers 11% weight loss in Phase 3

A Phase 3 trial (ATTAIN-1) published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* reports that once-daily oral orforglipron 36 mg produced a 11.2% mean body-weight reduction vs. 2% for placebo over 72 weeks in 3,127 adults with obesity without diabetes. In the top-dose arm, 54.6% of participants lost at least 10% of body weight. A companion *Lancet* trial (ATTAIN-2) confirmed efficacy in type 2 diabetes patients. This is the first oral small-molecule GLP-1 agonist to demonstrate efficacy on par with injectable agents (Ozempic, Wegovy). Access implications are substantial — if priced accessibly, an oral formulation could dramatically expand global reach; under the current pharmaceutical-capitalist model, the drug will be priced to extract maximum surplus from insurers and governments before generics arrive.

### H5N1 in US: 71 human cases, CDC scales back surveillance

CDC now reports 71 total US human H5N1 cases since February 2024 (40 from dairy-cow exposure, 23 from poultry, 4 unknown), with dairy-herd infections confirmed in 19 states. A new H5N1 genotype has been identified in US dairy cattle. The agency has stopped reporting animal-outbreak data on its own site, redirecting to USDA; the CDC H5N1 page has not been updated since March 6, 2026. Public-health experts warn the surveillance rollback reduces transparency during an active, evolving zoonotic threat.

### 3D-printed neurons communicate with real brain cells

Northwestern University engineers report that 3D-printed artificial neurons have successfully formed functional connections and communicated bidirectionally with biological brain cells under lab conditions — a first for bioelectronic interfaces. The result has significant implications for brain-computer interface medicine (paralysis, neurodegenerative disease), with the potential to bypass the limitations of traditional implanted-electrode approaches.

### Greenland's Prudhoe Dome fully melted 7,000 years ago

Ice cores drilled from the Prudhoe Dome — a major high point of the Greenland Ice Sheet — show that the dome disappeared completely roughly 7,000 years ago during a Holocene warm period, then reformed. The finding implies that sea levels rose several meters more than current IPCC models assume, and that Greenland ice is materially more sensitive to warming than projections indicate. This is a direct challenge to the baseline sea-level-rise projections underpinning coastal-adaptation planning worldwide.

### White House AI policy stalls, global rules multiply

The White House's National AI Policy Framework (released March 20, 2026) has stalled in Congress over Democratic opposition to accountability gaps, particularly around federal preemption of state AI laws. Meanwhile, Vietnam's AI law took effect March 1, South Korea's AI Basic Act is live, and the EU AI Act's transparency Code of Practice applies from August 2026. California, Texas, and New York all have state AI rules in force. The NIST AI Agent Standards Initiative is seeking industry input. The global AI regulatory landscape is fragmenting rapidly — which in practice means that major transnational AI firms will continue setting de facto policy in the regulatory vacuum.

### WSWS recommends six essential Iranian films

Amid the ongoing US–Israeli war on Iran — 1,500 dead, 3.2 million displaced, most of the country's oil infrastructure under bombardment — WSWS film critic Zac Corrigan today published a short list of six Iranian films he considers indispensable viewing for workers in the imperialist centers: three by **Abbas Kiarostami** (1940–2016), whom Corrigan calls "perhaps the most important filmmaker in the world" during the 1987–1997 decade, and three by **Asghar Farhadi** (b. 1972), whose *A Separation* (2011) became the first Iranian film to win the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film in 2012.

The political point of the list is explicit: the function of imperial war is to dehumanize the population of the target country, and cinema is one of the principal means by which that dehumanization can be contested. Kiarostami's patient, contemplative realism — the Koker trilogy, *Close-Up*, *Taste of Cherry* — renders a living, breathing Iranian society of villagers, children, students, and workers, flatly incompatible with the abstracted "regime" that US war planners require the public to accept as a target. Farhadi's moral dramas — *About Elly*, *The Salesman*, *A Separation* — do the same work through urban middle-class Tehran.

The piece is a modest intervention, but its method is correct. Against a sustained propaganda effort to reduce 88 million people to a single silhouette of Khamenei's successors, Corrigan offers the concrete humanity of the Iranian artistic tradition.

### Reed Hastings steps down as Netflix chairman

Co-founder **Reed Hastings** announced his departure as Netflix chairman, effective following the April 18 earnings release that disappointed markets and sent the stock lower. Hastings, who founded Netflix in 1997 as a DVD-rental service before driving its transformation into the dominant global streaming platform, exits at a moment of significant industry contraction. The streaming-era investment boom has given way to the "streaming wars" consolidation phase — subscriber growth is flat, content budgets are being slashed, writers and directors are being squeezed, and the industry as a whole is being subordinated to the same "mega layoff" logic the WSJ celebrated this week. Hastings himself oversaw the decisive pivot from a content-as-public-service model to a content-as-financialized-asset model; the conditions of his departure are an indictment of the model, not a rebuttal.

### LVMH sales miss reflects retrenchment in luxury sector

**LVMH** — the French luxury conglomerate that owns Louis Vuitton, Dior, Moët, Hennessy, and dozens of other brands — reported Q1 sales below analyst estimates, with management citing the Middle East conflict and the US tariff regime as drags. The disappointing print formalizes a cultural shift that has been developing through the post-COVID "revenge spending" boom: the global upper-middle consumer base that LVMH assembled during the decade of zero-rate finance has been battered by inflation, tariffs, and now the Iran war's cost-of-living shock. The "aspirational luxury" category — buyers who stretch to afford a Dior handbag or a bottle of Dom Pérignon — is being hollowed out. What remains is a smaller, wealthier ultra-luxury consumer for whom the war is a market opportunity rather than a burden. The sector's retrenchment is itself a cultural datum: the "democratization of luxury" narrative that ran through the 2010s is being reversed in real time.

### 7. Benin's 94% election result and the hollowing out of African electoral democracy

Benin's certified 94.27% presidential margin for the handpicked Talon successor Romuald Wadagni is the latest example of a pattern across the Sahel and West Africa: elections stripped of meaningful opposition, managed transitions that preserve incumbent blocs, and mass disengagement (turnout 58.78%). A WSWS piece placing Benin in its regional context — alongside recent events in Senegal, Togo, and Côte d'Ivoire — would serve the WSWS's under-developed African coverage.
