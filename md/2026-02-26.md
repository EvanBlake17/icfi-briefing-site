### 1. Trump delivers longest-ever State of the Union, threatens Iran war and announces global tariffs

Trump delivered a nearly two-hour State of the Union address on the evening of February 24 — the longest ever — in what the SEP characterized as "a grotesque and degrading spectacle." The speech combined explicit threats of military action against Iran, a sweeping new tariff proposal, racist attacks on Minnesota's Somali community, celebration of the military invasion of Venezuela, and the complete erasure of Gaza and the Palestinian people from any mention throughout the address.

On trade, Trump announced a worldwide 10 percent tariff via Section 122 authority after the Supreme Court blocked his previous sweeping tariff policy. He demanded tariffs eventually replace income tax as America's primary revenue source — a proposal that would shift the tax burden decisively onto working-class consumers while relieving corporations and the wealthy of direct taxation. Canada's finance minister Francois-Philippe Champagne responded immediately that the US is "unlikely to lift tariffs," signaling that trade conflict between the two countries will deepen.

On domestic policy, Trump unveiled the "Save America Act" to bar "illegal aliens and others" from voting — a measure the SEP described as "a blueprint for voter suppression, intimidation and the criminalization of opposition." He described the military invasion of Venezuela as a "triumph" without mentioning civilian casualties or the seizure of a sovereign nation's government. The word "Palestinian" did not appear once in his speech; Gaza was entirely absent, even as the humanitarian catastrophe there continues.

Trump devoted the final 30 minutes of the address to threatening military action against Iran, calling Iran's regime "the world's number one sponsor of terrorism." The WSWS compared this language to the WMD fabrications that preceded the 2003 Iraq invasion, noting that the rhetorical escalation is designed to prepare the American public for a war that has never been meaningfully debated.

Trump used explicitly racist language against Minnesota's Somali community, describing them as "pirates" who have "pillaged" tens of billions of dollars. The SEP statement called this the same language "the Nazis used against Jews as they laid the groundwork for the Holocaust." The SEP statement observed that "the Democrats fear that if they do anything that undermines the obsolete procedures of congressional decorum, it will encourage popular opposition and mass working class action." The Democratic response was delivered by Virginia Governor Abigail Spanberger, an ex-CIA officer, who criticized Trump for "ceding economic power and technological strength to China" but did not mention the invasion of Venezuela, Gaza, the Epstein files, or ICE killings.

### 3. Russia pounds Ukraine with missiles and drones as US–Ukraine meet in Geneva

Russia launched overnight missile and drone attacks across Ukraine on February 26 as the US and Ukraine held high-level talks in Geneva aimed at ending the war, now in its fifth year. The simultaneous bombardment and diplomacy encapsulate a conflict that has killed an estimated 600,000 people while all bourgeois "peace" frameworks have failed to halt the carnage.

At least 25 people were wounded: 16 in Kharkiv and the surrounding region, 7 in Zaporizhia where 19 apartment buildings were damaged, and 2 in Kryvyi Rih. The dispute illustrates the fracturing of European unity over the war as energy interests and domestic politics increasingly diverge from the unified front that marked the early years of the conflict. Hungary trails in polls ahead of elections next month.

The Lancet published a four-year anniversary editorial documenting more than 47,000 Ukrainian healthcare workers killed, wounded, or displaced since Russia's full-scale invasion, calling it "a catastrophe on every level, not least for health."

### 7. AI fears trigger 800-point Dow drop after Citrini Research report

A research report by Citrini Research circulated widely on February 25 and contributed to sharp market losses: the Dow fell over 800 points (1.7 percent), the S&P 500 dropped 1 percent, and the NASDAQ fell 1.1 percent. The report posed a question that struck at the heart of the AI investment thesis: "What if our AI bullishness continues to be right ... and what if that's actually bearish?"

The argument: AI productivity tools could massively reduce labor costs and time, destroying the valuations of existing software and information companies — including the very tech giants spending trillions on AI data centers. The Wall Street Journal noted the report "tapped into a new strain of fears about AI." The core concern is whether hyperscaler spending by Meta, Google, Amazon, and Microsoft will generate sufficient returns, or whether the technology they are building will undermine the profit structures on which their valuations rest.

The market reaction illustrates the extreme speculative instability of the AI investment boom, where trillions of dollars in capital expenditure rest on assumptions about future returns that even AI proponents now acknowledge may not materialize. As the WSWS noted, the technology may succeed in its stated purpose — increasing productivity — while simultaneously destroying the profit basis of the companies investing in it, a contradiction rooted in the capitalist mode of production itself. The Citrini report's core insight is that AI may be the rare technology that harms its own investors by succeeding too well.

Israeli forces abducted a young Syrian man while he was tending sheep — an act of kidnapping on sovereign Syrian territory. The abduction of a civilian farmer tending livestock underscores the brazenness of these operations: this is not a security sweep but an assertion of territorial control over a population that has no means of resistance.

Separately, the Syrian government and Druze factions carried out a prisoner swap in the Suwayda region, reflecting the complex mosaic of internal negotiations as Syria's post-Assad government attempts to consolidate authority while foreign military forces operate freely within its borders.

### 30,000 LAUSD support workers vote 94 percent to authorize strike

Thirty thousand classified workers in the Los Angeles Unified School District voted 94 percent to authorize a strike, following a 94 percent strike authorization by UTLA teachers three weeks earlier. Together, 65,000 LAUSD employees — bus drivers, cafeteria workers, custodians, classroom aides, and teachers — are poised to strike in the nation's second-largest school district.

The average classified worker salary is $35,501, classified as "extremely low income" in Los Angeles, where the cost of living far exceeds that figure. Ninety percent are Latino or African American, and many are immigrants. Internal union surveys show a large portion of classified staff are housing insecure, with many living in overcrowded multi-family dwellings or commuting long distances. As the WSWS noted: "Schools cannot function without them. Yet they are paid poverty-level wages."

The LAUSD struggle is part of a broader wave of labor conflict in California: 40,000 UC graduate workers are also set to strike when their contract expires March 31, 35,000 Kaiser healthcare workers recently waged a month-long strike, and CSU skilled trades workers are also striking. The convergence of these struggles represents the most significant labor upsurge in California in years.

The Lancet's willingness to publish such a direct critique of US military action in a major journal reflects growing alarm within the global medical community about the weaponization of healthcare infrastructure in conflicts worldwide.
