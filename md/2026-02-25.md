## IEB Discussion Points

**Global situation.** The financial markets opened sharply lower Tuesday as new economic data from the eurozone signaled contraction for the third consecutive quarter. The European Central Bank is expected to respond with emergency rate cuts at its Thursday meeting.

**United States.** The UAW has announced an emergency strike authorization vote at three major auto manufacturers following the collapse of contract negotiations. Union president Shawn Fain called the breakdown a "deliberate provocation" by management, citing the refusal to address outsourcing clauses.

**Middle East.** Humanitarian organizations reported that aid deliveries into Gaza dropped to their lowest levels in two months amid renewed border closures. The UN Emergency Relief Coordinator called the situation "catastrophic and preventable."

---

## Perspective Contribution

The trade union bureaucracy's role in containing the strike wave deserves examination this week. Three major labor actions — at Boeing, in the ports, and now potentially at the automakers — have each been met with the same pattern: initial mobilization of rank-and-file sentiment, followed by backroom negotiations that trade the most significant gains for modest wage increases.

This is not incidental. The AFL-CIO and affiliated unions operate as what the ICFI has analyzed as a labor apparatus integrated into the management of capitalism. Their function is not to advance the independent interests of the working class but to channel discontent into forms that leave the profit system intact.

The question that must be posed to workers is: under what conditions is it possible to break from this apparatus and build genuinely independent forms of struggle?
