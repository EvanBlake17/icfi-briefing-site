### 2. Strait of Hormuz closure triggers worst oil supply crisis in history

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global seaborne crude and LNG transits — has produced what IEA chief Fatih Birol called "the worst energy supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Birol warned that "April will be much worse than March."

Since the war began, 4.5–5 million barrels per day have been lost (~5% of global supply), a figure projected to double by mid-April. Brent crude peaked at $126/barrel, with analysts now modeling scenarios approaching $200/barrel. The US and allies have released 400 million barrels from strategic reserves — the largest release on record — but a mid-April supply cliff looms when emergency reserves run thin.

Key facts:
- Jet fuel has more than doubled; Air New Zealand, Vietnam Airlines, and Korean Air are cutting flights
- Philippines president says grounding planes is a "distinct possibility"
- Iraqi southern oil output down over 70%; Qatar LNG capacity down ~17% after March 18 attack on Ras Laffan
- Asian LNG spot prices up 140%+
- Gulf nations backed a UN resolution authorizing "all necessary measures" to reopen the Strait

### Magnitude 7.4 earthquake strikes Indonesia's Molucca Sea

A magnitude 7.4 earthquake struck the Molucca Sea approximately 127 km west-northwest of Ternate, North Maluku province, on April 1–2 — described as the strongest in the region in six years. One person was killed: a 70-year-old woman in a building collapse in Manado, North Sulawesi. Tsunami waves were recorded at five locations, the highest at 0.75 meters in North Minahasa, before warnings were lifted.

Approximately 50 aftershocks followed, the largest at magnitude 5.8. Initial warnings had modeled 0.5–3 meter waves. Minor to moderate damage to houses and a church was reported, with a full assessment ongoing.

### Death of Rohingya refugee left by Border Patrol ruled homicide

A New York medical examiner ruled the death of Nurul Amin Shah Alam, a 56-year-old nearly blind Rohingya refugee, a homicide. Shah Alam was found dead on a Buffalo street on February 24, five days after Customs and Border Protection officers left him outside a closed Tim Hortons coffee shop on a freezing night. Shah Alam came to the United States from Myanmar in 2024. A CBP spokesperson claimed his death "had NOTHING to do with Border Patrol," noting he died nearly a week after release — a defense that underscores the agency's indifference to the fate of those in its custody. The Erie County district attorney and New York Attorney General Letitia James are both investigating.

### Eli Lilly's oral GLP-1 drug shows 11.2% weight loss in phase 3 trial

Results published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* from the 72-week ATTAIN-1 phase 3 trial show that Eli Lilly's oral GLP-1 drug orforglipron, at its 36 mg daily dose, produced −11.2% mean body weight loss compared to −2.1% on placebo in non-diabetic adults with obesity. More than half (54.6%) of patients in the high-dose arm lost at least 10% of body weight. A companion *Lancet* paper (ATTAIN-2) showed similar results in patients with type 2 diabetes.

Unlike injectable semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy), orforglipron is a once-daily pill — a potentially major access breakthrough that could transform obesity treatment by eliminating injection barriers. The development raises sharp questions about pricing and accessibility given the pharmaceutical industry's record of extracting monopoly profits from breakthrough therapies.

### BSO dismissal of Andris Nelsons continues to provoke uproar

The Nelsons affair illustrates a pattern visible across major American cultural institutions: boards dominated by financial elites treat orchestras, museums, and theaters as investment vehicles or branding opportunities, not as custodians of humanity's artistic heritage. The 13-year tenure of one of the world's leading conductors was ended by email.

### Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin's most significant piece of the past 48 hours is Stephen Semler's **"The War on Iran Is More Expensive Than You Think"** (April 2), which argues the daily cost of the war is approximately $2.1 billion — far higher than official Pentagon estimates, which use outdated unit costs rather than replacement costs. The article demonstrates how military accounting systematically conceals true expenditure. While the fiscal analysis is useful, the political framework is characteristically limited: Semler treats the war as a budgetary misallocation (money for war instead of daycare) rather than an expression of the imperialist system that cannot be resolved through congressional spending priorities. The implicit solution — pressure Democrats to redirect funds — accepts the framework of capitalist politics.

Branko Marcetic's **"The US and Israel Are Making Gaza-Style War the New Normal"** (April 1) argues that Gaza was a "dress rehearsal" for the Iran campaign and documents the normalization of civilian targeting. The analysis approaches the WSWS position on the continuity between Gaza and Iran but stops short of identifying the class forces driving this escalation, treating it instead as a policy choice that could theoretically be reversed within the existing state framework.

On labor, Caitlyn Clark and Lisa Xu's **"The Biggest US Meatpacking Strike in 40 Years Is Still On"** covers the 3,800-worker JBS Greeley strike with strong on-the-ground reporting, celebrating the multinational character of the workforce (57 languages) and UFCW solidarity. However, Jacobin's coverage treats the union bureaucracy as a natural and positive vehicle for working-class struggle, with no examination of the UFCW's decades-long record of concessions and its role in suppressing the very militancy the article celebrates.

Overall orientation: Jacobin continues to function as the intellectual organ of left-Democratic politics, channeling genuine working-class anger into reformist and electoralist frameworks. The Iran war coverage exposes the fiscal obscenity of militarism but cannot identify imperialism as a systemic phenomenon requiring revolutionary working-class opposition.

### Left Voice

Left Voice has published several pieces on the Iran war, most notably **"Defend Iran, but Don't Support the Theocracy"** and **"Educators Must Take the Lead in Opposing the Imperialist War on Iran."** The former correctly identifies the imperialist character of the war and calls for Iran's defense while warning against political support for the Islamic Republic — a position that parallels the Trotskyist framework of military defense without political support. However, Left Voice's practical program channels opposition through existing union structures (particularly educators' unions), adapting to the bureaucracies that have done nothing to mobilize against the war.

Left Voice's **"Permanent Revolution in Iran"** invokes Trotsky's theory but applies it in an abstract, propagandistic manner disconnected from any concrete strategy for building revolutionary leadership in the Iranian or international working class. The PTS/FT tendency's characteristic combination of revolutionary rhetoric with adaptation to existing institutional structures — particularly trade union bureaucracies and academic milieus — is evident throughout their Iran coverage.

The overall orientation reflects a tendency that uses Marxist vocabulary while failing to develop the political conclusions that follow from its own analysis: namely, that opposition to imperialist war requires the building of an independent revolutionary party of the working class, not appeals to union officialdom.

### Revolutionary Communist Party / IMT

The IMT's longstanding substitution of organizational self-promotion for serious political analysis — "join the RCP" as the answer to every question — is on full display. ## What the WSWS should cover today

### 1. Working-class cost of the Hormuz crisis — airlines, food prices, and global fuel poverty

The oil shock from the US-Iran war is rapidly translating into fare hikes, flight cuts, and fuel price spikes that fall disproportionately on workers and the global poor. Airlines from New Zealand to Vietnam are cutting routes; the IEA projects April will bring the deepest supply crunch yet. The strategic reserve releases primarily benefit wealthy consuming nations, while developing economies face acute energy poverty. A detailed WSWS analysis connecting the Hormuz crisis to its specific impact on working-class living standards — not just macroeconomic data but household-level effects on food, fuel, and transportation costs — would fill a major gap in coverage.
