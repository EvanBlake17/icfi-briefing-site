### 1. Trump weighs 60-day Iran ceasefire as Hormuz crisis enters fourth month

US and Iranian negotiators have reached a tentative 60-day memorandum of understanding to extend the ceasefire and open formal nuclear negotiations, but President Trump had not signed as of Sunday morning. "The president relayed to the mediators that he wants a couple of days to think about it," a US official told reporters. The deal would end a war that began February 28, 2026, when the US-Israel air campaign opened, Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz, and Supreme Leader Khamenei was assassinated.

The standoff has now dragged into its fourth month, with the global economy absorbing a sustained energy shock and the US maintaining a naval blockade that has repeatedly skirted direct, wider war — including a May 28 Iranian retaliatory strike on a US base in Kuwait.

Key facts:
- Terms include unrestricted Strait of Hormuz passage, lifting of the US port blockade, Iranian removal of sea mines within 30 days, and an Iranian commitment not to pursue nuclear weapons; roughly **440 kg of highly enriched uranium at 60% purity** must be disposed of.
- Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth: "They're coming in our direction. The talks have been productive" — while warning the US military is "more strongly placed to resume combat than on day one."
- The US has redirected 116 vessels and disabled six ships attempting to breach the blockade over three months; the Treasury seized roughly **$1 billion in Iranian cryptocurrency** under the "Economic Fury" campaign.
- Iran's Strait of Hormuz authority, itself sanctioned, vowed operations would continue "without interruption"; a floating object believed to be a naval mine was sighted in the strait.

### 2. Israel seizes Beaufort Castle in southern Lebanon

Israeli troops captured Beaufort Castle, the 900-year-old strategic ridge fortress in southern Lebanon, on May 31 — the deepest advance of the offensive, roughly 7 miles past the Litani River, despite a ceasefire nominally declared April 16. The seizure confirms that the truce has collapsed in all but name as Israel exploits the cover of the Iran war to press its campaign against Hezbollah.

Key facts:
- **3,324 people killed and over 10,000 wounded** since the conflict began March 2; more than 1 million displaced; an average of **11 children killed or wounded daily** last week.
- Saturday saw one of the heaviest days of Hezbollah rocket and drone fire toward northern Israel since the April ceasefire; Israel closed schools in border communities, capped outdoor gatherings at 50 people, and moved one hospital underground.
- On May 19, Israeli airstrikes killed at least 22 people, including women and children, in Deir Qanoun an-Naher, Nabatieh and Kfar Sir; on May 22, six Lebanese paramedics were killed in two separate strikes.
- Secretary of State Rubio held a call with Lebanese President Aoun blaming Hezbollah as "entirely responsible for the ongoing fighting." Hezbollah was never a formal signatory to the truce, which was extended for 45 days on May 15.

### 3. Colombia votes in presidential first round; Cepeda leads

An estimated 41.4 million Colombians were eligible to vote May 31 in the first round of a presidential election widely framed as a referendum on outgoing leftist President Gustavo Petro's four-year term — the first left-wing presidency in Colombian history. With 14 candidates contesting, a runoff between the top two is scheduled for June 21 if no one wins a majority. The new president takes office August 7; Petro is constitutionally barred from re-election.

- US Senator Bernie Moreno traveled to Colombia ahead of the vote; the US Embassy issued an election-day security alert.
- The campaign has unfolded against the backdrop of failed peace negotiations with FARC dissidents and persistent inequality — the material record of Petro's "leftism."

### 4. Russian drone hits Romanian apartment block — first civilian casualties on NATO soil

A Russian drone targeting Ukrainian positions crashed into a 10-story apartment building in Galați, eastern Romania — about 10 miles from the Ukrainian border — injuring two people and igniting a roof fire. It is the 28th confirmed drone breach of Romanian airspace since Russia's 2022 invasion, but the first to injure civilians inside an allied state.

Key facts:
- Romania's president expelled Russia's consul and ordered the closure of the consulate in Constanța; the Foreign Ministry called the crash "a serious violation of international law."
- Romania did not shoot the drone down, exposing NATO's unresolved rules of engagement: downing Russian drones risks Article 5 escalation, but inaction exposes civilians.
- European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen: "Russia's war of aggression has crossed yet another line." NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte called it "a danger to us all."

### 5. Gaza aid collapses ~80% under cover of the Iran war

- Food aid now reaches just over **1.1 million people monthly**, far below need; the UN warns famine risk is re-emerging after being averted.
- The UN Security Council held an open debate on consolidating the ceasefire and halting West Bank settlement expansion.

## Science, technology, and public health

- [Measles tops 1,983 cases](#science-technology-and-public-health) — worst US year in decades; kindergarten vaccination coverage has slipped to 92.5%
- [First severe human H5N5 infection reported in NEJM](#science-technology-and-public-health) — a previously uncharacterized avian-flu subtype causes critical illness in a 75-year-old
- [Trump pulls AI security executive order](#science-technology-and-public-health) — pre-deployment cyber-review gate dropped hours before signing as CAISI signs voluntary deals

**Measles** continues its worst US year in decades: as of May 28, the CDC reported **1,983 confirmed cases across 40 jurisdictions**, with 93% (1,847) outbreak-associated across 30 discrete outbreaks; no deaths so far in 2026. Kindergarten vaccination coverage has slipped to **92.5%** (from 95.2% in 2019–20), leaving an estimated 286,000 children unprotected and sustaining transmission chains — a direct product of the anti-vaccine policy now entrenched in the federal public health apparatus.

In avian influenza, the **NEJM** published the first documented severe human **H5N5** infection: a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman developed acute hypoxemic respiratory failure after an initially negative influenza A nasal swab. H5N5 was a previously uncharacterized subtype in humans, broadening the roster of avian-flu variants capable of critical illness. Separately, the CDC's **H5N1** human case count reached **71** since 2024 across 13 states (2 deaths, 3 of unknown exposure); conjunctivitis remains the predominant presentation, with no human-to-human transmission detected.

Roundup:
- **COVID-19** is at its lowest level since spring 2021: national test positivity 0.83%, 0.2 hospitalizations per 100,000, and record-low wastewater readings. A new variant, **BA.3.2 ("Cicada")**, shows slow growth in the Northeast, with upticks in Georgia, Florida, Louisiana and Hawaii. (*The Sick Times*)
- **Long COVID:** A large neuroimaging study found no evidence of widespread brain inflammation in patients; instead, the most severe cases showed heightened hippocampus and amygdala activity, redirecting therapy toward stress/emotional regulation rather than anti-inflammatories. (*ScienceDaily*)
- **Brain aging:** Stanford researchers, using the turquoise killifish, found ribosomes collide and stall during translation elongation in aging brains, promoting aggregates linked to Alzheimer's, ALS and Parkinson's — identifying a druggable bottleneck more precise than amyloid or tau. (*ScienceDaily*)
- **AI policy:** A planned White House executive order requiring frontier developers to submit models for federal cybersecurity review before release was **pulled hours before the May 21 signing**, with Trump citing competitiveness. Meanwhile the government's AI safety institute (CAISI) signed voluntary pre-deployment evaluation deals with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI — replacing a mandatory gate with softer oversight.
- **AI and labor:** OpenAI released a 13-page "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age" proposing a public AI wealth fund, a 32-hour/4-day workweek pilot, and broad low-cost AI access — even as Altman and Amodei walked back earlier "jobs apocalypse" predictions ahead of blockbuster IPOs, drawing criticism that it is regulatory positioning, not governance.

## Arts and culture

The **79th Festival de Cannes** closed with the Palme d'Or awarded to *Fjord*, a Norway-set drama by Cristian Mungiu (a previous Palme winner for *4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days*), starring Sebastian Stan and Renate Reinsve. The choice of a director whose work has consistently dramatized the corrosive pressures of bureaucracy, poverty and moral compromise on ordinary people is significant at a moment of war and deepening social crisis — a counterweight to the festival's celebrity spectacle, which this year included honorary Palmes for Peter Jackson, John Travolta and Barbra Streisand.

The jury, presided over by Park Chan-wook and including Demi Moore, Ruth Negga, Chloé Zhao and Stellan Skarsgård, distributed its major acting prizes in ways worth noting:
- **Best Actor** went jointly to Valentin Campagne and Emmanuel Macchia in Lukas Dhont's *Coward*, continuing Dhont's preoccupation with masculinity, shame and adolescent vulnerability.
- **Best Actress** was shared by Virginie Efira and Tao Okamoto for Ryusuke Hamaguchi's *All of a Sudden* — Hamaguchi again probing chance, intimacy and the fragility of bourgeois domestic life.
- **Best Screenplay** went to Emmanuel Marre's *A Man of His Time*.

The festival unfolds against the backdrop of an industry reshaped by streaming consolidation, AI anxieties and the political pressures of the present conjuncture. Cannes has historically been a venue where serious filmmaking — engaging war, class and historical memory — competes for attention against the marketing machinery of the culture industry. The honorary tributes to aging Hollywood and blockbuster figures reflect that tension: genuine artistic achievement (Mungiu, Hamaguchi, Dhont) sharing a stage with the nostalgia economy.

Broader cultural notes:
- The selection of European auteurs working in a humanist, socially attentive register — rather than the nationalist or militarist themes increasingly promoted in state-backed cultural production — is a welcome sign, though the festival remains an elite marketplace insulated from the conditions of working-class audiences priced out of cinema.
- The continued prominence of films interrogating private life and moral failure (Hamaguchi, Dhont) points to a cinema reaching for psychological truth in the absence of a broader political vocabulary — a limitation, not merely a virtue, of much contemporary art-house work.

The task for a Marxist criticism is to assess these works not as festival trophies or lifestyle content but in their social and historical context: what they reveal about consciousness under capitalism in crisis, and where they retreat from the great questions of war, inequality and historical reckoning that define the moment.

### The Nexteer rebellion: The working class vs the trade union apparatus — Perspective

### Labor share of income hits record low as corporate profits soar — Andre Damon

The headline data and the Benioff quote are integrated into the US section. ### Kenyan court blocks US offshore Ebola camp as epidemic accelerates across central Africa — Evan Blake

Kenya's High Court on May 29 froze a US plan to open a 50-bed offshore Ebola facility at Laikipia Air Base — built by the US military and staffed by US Public Health Service officers — with Judge Patricia Nyaundi barring any such facility pending a June 2 hearing. The article's political conclusion: the dismantling of global public-health infrastructure under both parties has manufactured the conditions for catastrophe, and only an internationally coordinated, working-class response can contain emerging epidemics.

### Left Voice

Left Voice continued to develop its line on the Iran war in **"The War Against Iran Shows the Failure of Western 'Liberation'"**, arguing that Washington's offensive failed to trigger an internal uprising and instead accelerated regional rejection of Western leadership, with "Palestine now occupying the political and moral center of the Middle East." Orientation for the day: anti-imperialist in rhetoric, but oriented to protest-movement pressure rather than the construction of a revolutionary party.
