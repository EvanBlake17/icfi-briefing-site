### 2. Strait of Hormuz closure triggers worst energy crisis since the 1970s

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has produced the most severe disruption to global energy markets since the 1973 oil embargo. US crude settled at $99.64/barrel on March 27 — briefly touching $100 intraday — up more than 51% in a single month. Brent crude closed at $112.57/barrel, its highest level since July 2022, having peaked at $126/barrel earlier in March.

Before the war, 130 ships per day transited the Strait; the figure has collapsed to six or fewer, with over 500 tankers anchored outside. Approximately 17.8 million barrels per day of oil and fuel flows have been disrupted, with a total of roughly 500 million barrels lost since the closure began. Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has ordered the Strait to remain closed, and Iran's parliament is moving to formalize transit fees for passage.

- The IEA announced its **largest-ever coordinated strategic reserve release**: 400 million barrels across 32 member nations, with the US contributing 172 million barrels
- Gulf Cooperation Council states face a **"grocery supply emergency"**: over 80% of caloric intake transits the Strait; 70% of food imports disrupted; consumer food prices spiked **40–120%** in the region
- European gas storage stands at **30% capacity** after a harsh winter; Dutch TTF gas prices nearly doubled to €60+/MWh; the ECB postponed rate cuts and raised its 2026 inflation forecast
- Southeast Asian nations — Philippines, Vietnam, Thailand — are among the hardest hit globally as near-total import-dependent economies; protests erupted near Manila's Malacañang presidential palace over fuel and food prices
- Australia's PM Anthony Albanese announced the government will **underwrite private-sector fuel purchases** to guarantee national supply security
- UNICEF reports **$15.7 million in lifesaving supplies** destined for Somalia are at risk of non-delivery due to the blockade
- Goldman Sachs estimates a **$14–18/barrel geopolitical risk premium** embedded in current prices

### 7. UN General Assembly declares transatlantic slave trade "gravest crime against humanity"

The UN General Assembly voted 123–3 on March 25 to declare the transatlantic slave trade a "crime against humanity" and demand reparations — the starkest Global South vs. Western divide on international justice in years. Only the United States, Israel, and Argentina voted against. All 27 EU member states and the United Kingdom abstained.

- The resolution calls for "a full and formal apology, measures of restitution, compensation, rehabilitation, satisfaction, guarantees of non-repetition, and changes to laws, programs and services"
- It demands **"prompt and unhindered restitution"** of cultural artifacts, artworks, monuments, and national archives without charge
- Ghana's President John Dramani Mahama, speaking for the 54-member African Group: **"Today, we come together in solemn solidarity to affirm truth and pursue a route to healing and reparative justice"**
- US opposition statement: Washington "does not recognize a legal right to reparations for historical wrongs that were not illegal under international law at the time they occurred"
- The resolution is **nonbinding** but could elevate legal standards for crimes against humanity and enable new restitution claims

### Denmark election inconclusive: Frederiksen's Social Democrats suffer historic losses

Denmark's March 24 snap election — called by PM Mette Frederiksen after a polling surge driven by her defiance of Trump's Greenland annexation threats — produced a hung parliament. The Social Democrats won just 38 seats in the 179-seat Folketing, down from 50 seats in 2022, their worst result since the early 20th century.

- Neither the left nor right bloc won a majority; former PM **Lars Løkke Rasmussen** (Foreign Minister) holds the kingmaker role
- Voters prioritized **cost of living, clean water, pensions, and a potential wealth tax** — not Greenland
- Political scientist Peter Thisted Dinesen: the Greenland crisis "definitely helped her a little bit in the polls" but was ultimately not decisive
- Denmark increased spending in Greenland **tenfold** over the past year, an unexpected side-effect of Trump's threats

The result is a case study in how nationalist "resistance" to Trump cannot substitute for material improvements in working-class living standards. Despite leading Europe's most visible anti-Trump moment, Frederiksen's party was punished for its record on economic inequality.

The crew escaped but the vessel was damaged. The incident represents a significant escalation in NATO's campaign against Russia's so-called "shadow fleet."

- The vessel was linked through sanctions to figures connected to Iranian leadership, drawing the Ukraine and Iran conflicts into direct intersection

As the WSWS's Barış Demir noted, the multiple simultaneous war fronts — Ukraine entering its fifth year, the US-Israel war on Iran, and now active naval confrontation in the Black Sea — risk uncontrolled escalation toward a broader world war.

### Orforglipron oral GLP-1 results published in NEJM and Lancet

Two major Phase 3 trials of Eli Lilly's oral GLP-1 receptor agonist orforglipron were published simultaneously in the New England Journal of Medicine and The Lancet. ATTAIN-1 (n=3,127, obesity) showed the 36 mg dose produced 11.2% mean weight loss vs. 2.1% for placebo over 72 weeks, with 54.6% of participants losing at least 10% of body weight. ATTAIN-2 (n=1,613, type 2 diabetes) demonstrated 9.6% weight loss and HbA1c reduction of 1.8 percentage points. A separate head-to-head trial showed orforglipron outperformed oral semaglutide on both weight (9.2% vs. 5.3%) and HbA1c. If approved, it would be the first once-daily oral GLP-1 for obesity — a pill rather than an injection — potentially transforming access. Lilly has filed in 40+ countries.

### World Theater Day 2026: Willem Dafoe delivers official message

March 27 marked the 64th annual World Theater Day, with this year's official international message delivered by Willem Dafoe — actor and co-founder of the Wooster Group, one of the most influential avant-garde theater companies of the past half-century. Dafoe's selection is notable precisely because the Wooster Group represents the antithesis of the commercial theater machine: deconstructionist, non-narrative, politically confrontational. In a cultural moment dominated by franchise adaptations and jukebox musicals, the choice of Dafoe is a quiet rebuke to the commodification of the stage.

Theater's position as the most immediately social of art forms — performed live, for a gathered audience, in shared physical space — gives it a unique role in periods of political crisis. The art form's capacity for direct engagement with contemporary reality stands in tension with the commercial pressures that increasingly reduce it to entertainment product.

### Three Broadway musicals launch previews simultaneously

Three new Broadway productions began previews on March 27: *The Lost Boys* (an adaptation of the 1987 cult vampire film) at the Palace Theatre, *Beaches: A New Musical* at the Majestic Theatre, and a revival of Noël Coward's *Fallen Angels* starring Kelli O'Hara and Rose Byrne at the Todd Haimes Theatre. The simultaneous opening signals Broadway's peak spring season push ahead of Tony Award nominations.

The programming choices are telling. *The Lost Boys* and *Beaches* represent the now-dominant tendency of adapting existing intellectual property for the stage — a process that treats theater as a subsidiary of the film industry rather than an independent art form. The Coward revival, by contrast, reconnects with the tradition of theatrical wit and social observation, though Coward's drawing-room comedies inevitably reflect the concerns and limitations of their upper-class milieu.

### Dallas Theater Center and regional theater developments

Incoming Artistic Director Jaime Castañeda announced Dallas Theater Center's 2026–27 season, anchored by three world premieres including *Reykjavik86*, a Cold War thriller dramatizing the 1986 Reagan-Gorbachev nuclear summit in Iceland. The choice of subject matter — arms control negotiations at a moment of existential nuclear danger — resonates unmistakably with the present moment, as the US wages simultaneous wars and the risk of broader conflagration grows.

Cleveland's Playhouse Square revealed its 2026–27 season featuring the Cleveland debut of *Oh, Mary!*, the breakout off-Broadway cult comedy, alongside *Hadestown*, *Wicked*, and a 20th anniversary tour of *Jersey Boys*. The Milwaukee Film Festival unveiled its 2026 lineup headlined by the world premiere of *Ueck*, a documentary on legendary Brewers announcer Bob Uecker, alongside the Sundance Audience Award–winning *American Pachuco: The Legend of Luis Valdez* — the latter significant as a treatment of Chicano cultural history and the farmworker movement.

A Billie Eilish concert film directed by James Cameron, *Billie Eilish: Hit Me Hard and Soft*, was announced for theatrical release in immersive 3D. The pairing of a generation-defining pop artist with Hollywood's foremost technical filmmaker marks a continued resurgence of the concert film format — though the marriage of spectacle technology to pop music production raises questions about whether the format serves artistic depth or merely enhances sensory overwhelm.

## What the WSWS should cover today

### 1. The global working class bears the cost of the Iran war's energy shock

While coverage focuses on oil price benchmarks and geopolitical dealmaking, the concrete impact on workers has received minimal systematic coverage. Food prices are up 40–120% in the Gulf states. UK inflation is forecast to breach 5%. European recession risk is mounting. The IEA's coordinated 400-million-barrel reserve release primarily protects speculative markets and large consumers, not working-class households dependent on daily energy affordability. The WSWS is uniquely positioned to document how the costs of imperialist war are being transferred directly to the international working class.

### 2. Afghan civilian deaths in Pakistan's cross-border bombing campaign

### 6. The 123-nation reparations vote vs. Western elite opposition

The UN reparations resolution passed 123–3, with the US, Israel, and Argentina the only outright opponents and the entire EU and UK abstaining. The vote reveals a stark Global South vs. Western divide on international law and historical accountability. Legal scholars note the resolution could open new pathways for restitution claims even as nonbinding. The WSWS could analyze the class dynamics behind both the vote and the abstentions, the limitations of a reparations framework that does not challenge capitalist property relations, and why the Western powers — including those that profess commitment to racial justice — refused to support the resolution.
