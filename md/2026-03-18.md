### 2. Strait of Hormuz crisis drives oil above $100 as Iran expands strikes to Gulf states

Iran expanded its retaliatory strikes beyond Israel to target Gulf states that host US military infrastructure. ### 6. JBS meatpacking strike enters day 2 in Greeley, Colorado

Some 3,800 meatpacking workers at the JBS plant in Greeley, Colorado entered the second day of their strike, with picket lines stretching nearly half a mile around the facility. Workers are demanding wages rise from $23 to $33/hour after the company offered a 60-cent increase. Between 80 and 90 percent of the workforce are immigrants, with more than 50 languages spoken inside the facility.

Workers describe near-daily injuries — "cut, stabbed, poked" — $100 charges for replacement safety gloves, and Greeley rents of $1,800–$2,000/month. Haitian workers recruited via TikTok were housed in conditions where 30 people shared one bathroom. Unmarked vans were reportedly stationed near the strike vote location, and ICE is operating at least nine secret detention sites in Colorado. As the WSWS Perspective by Tom Hall documented, the UFCW bureaucracy has already declared the strike will last "only two weeks" — a pre-planned signal to management that a retreat is being prepared.

Key facts:
- JBS is a Brazilian multinational employing 270,000–280,000 workers globally; BlackRock and Vanguard hold significant ownership stakes
- The UFCW reached a national JBS agreement covering 26,000 workers last year but deliberately excluded Greeley
- JBS has preemptively transferred cattle to its Canyon, Texas plant
- At least six workers died during the first year of the pandemic; in 2021 a worker fell into a chemical vat and died

### Jürgen Habermas (1929–2026): death of the last Frankfurt School philosopher

Jürgen Habermas died on March 14 in Starnberg, Germany at the age of 96. The most cited humanities scholar of the postwar era and the last surviving representative of the Frankfurt School, Habermas is best known for his two-volume *Theory of Communicative Action* and his theories of the public sphere and deliberative democracy. Born in Düsseldorf in 1929, he was drafted into the German military in 1944 and later recalled the Nazi atrocities as a formative moment that guided him toward philosophy.

Habermas's relationship to Marxism was complex: the Frankfurt School was critical of capitalism but also of orthodox Marxism, and Habermas's later work on communicative rationality explicitly departed from the class-struggle framework of historical materialism. His theory of the public sphere, while influential, idealized bourgeois democratic discourse in a manner that obscured the class character of the institutions he studied. Jacobin published a sympathetic obituary praising his "profound critique of irrationality" — a framing that elides his distance from revolutionary politics. His ideas shaped constitutional law scholarship and debates over European integration, but the fundamental contradictions of bourgeois democracy that he sought to resolve rationally remain unresolvable within the capitalist framework.

### Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin's most politically significant article of the past 24 hours is **"A Post-Order World"** by Juliano Fiori, which argues that US hegemony has collapsed not because a new hegemon has emerged but because consent for American empire has broken down, producing "enduring hegemonic crisis" and "zonal geopolitics." The article employs Gramscian theory and criticizes US military interventions in Venezuela and Iran, but its political conclusion — that regional blocs might constrain great-power ambitions — amounts to a prescription for a multipolar imperialist order rather than the revolutionary overthrow of imperialism by the international working class. This is the characteristic Jacobin method: sophisticated academic analysis that stops precisely where Marxist politics begins.

**"Thousands of Colorado Meatpacking Workers Are on Strike"** by Alex N. Press covers the JBS Greeley walkout sympathetically, emphasizing worker militancy and conditions. Notably absent is any examination of the UFCW's pre-announced two-week time limit on the strike or the union's deliberate exclusion of Greeley from the national contract — the very mechanisms by which the bureaucracy is positioning to strangle the strike. This silence on the role of the union apparatus is consistent with the DSA's structural relationship to the trade union bureaucracy.

**"Jürgen Habermas Showed What Philosophy Could Be"** offers a sympathetic obituary praising Habermas's "profound critique of irrationality" as "an indispensable guide in the struggle against oppression" — a framing that erases the fundamental class divide between Habermas's communicative-rationalist project and the Marxist-Trotskyist understanding of the state, class struggle, and revolution.

The day's overall orientation: Jacobin functions as a left-academic organ that absorbs genuine working-class struggles into a reformist framework, combining sophisticated international analysis with systematic evasion of the trade union question.
