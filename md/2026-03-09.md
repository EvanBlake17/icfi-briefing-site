### 1. Iran war enters day nine as death toll surpasses 1,400

The US-Israeli war on Iran entered its ninth day on March 9 with continued airstrikes against Tehran, Qom, and oil infrastructure. The preliminary death toll has surpassed 1,400 — approximately 1,255 killed in Iran (including at least 742 civilians and 176 children), 200+ in Lebanon, 13 in Israel, 8 US soldiers, and 14+ in Gulf states. The UN declared a "major humanitarian emergency" on March 6. An eighth US service member death was confirmed on March 9, and the 82nd Airborne Division has cancelled all training operations — widely interpreted as preparation for ground deployment.

Iran has partially closed the Strait of Hormuz, suspending approximately 20% of global daily oil and gas supply. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei accused the US of trying to "partition our country to take illegal possession of our oil riches." As Patrick Martin argued in today's WSWS Perspective, the administration's rhetoric about "limited operations" follows the precise ideological script used to initiate every major American ground war of the last century: "The 'military advisers' sent to Vietnam became 550,000 troops."

Key facts:
- US and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury on Feb. 28, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei on the first day
- Trump stated: "I said unconditional. It's where they cry uncle or when they can't fight any longer"
- US airstrikes destroyed a girls' elementary school, killing 175 children; a separate school strike killed 160+ children and 185 teachers
- The conflict region hosts approximately 25 million displaced people
- Three US aircraft carriers now deployed to the Middle East

### 3. Global oil crisis as prices hit $116/barrel; G7 announces emergency reserve release

The Iran war has triggered the fastest oil price surge in four decades. WTI crude surged 66% in nine days — from $67/barrel on Feb. 27 to an intraday peak of $111.24 on March 8; Brent hit $114.38. Iran's partial closure of the Strait of Hormuz suspended approximately 140 million barrels of daily output from Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iraq, and Kuwait. Qatar declared force majeure on LNG exports, and European LNG prices surged 50%.

G7 finance ministers and IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol held an emergency call on March 9, announcing a coordinated release of approximately 400 million barrels from strategic reserves — the sixth such action in IEA history and the first since Russia's 2022 Ukraine invasion. Prices fell approximately 11% after the announcement.

Key facts:
- US national average gasoline price reached $3.41/gallon — up $0.43 in one week
- Qatar's energy minister predicted oil could reach $150/barrel and warned the conflict could "bring down the economies of the world"
- Goldman Sachs warned sustained $100+/barrel oil would add ~1 percentage point to inflation and subtract 0.25–0.4 points from global GDP growth
- Egypt's president warned of a "state of near-emergency" from inflation
- The supply shock is described as "17 times worse" than the post-Ukraine energy crisis

### 4. Global stock markets in freefall as war drives risk-off panic

Global equity markets experienced their sharpest declines since the 2020 COVID crash as the Iran war entered its second week. Dow futures sank 1,000 points on Sunday night ahead of the Monday open. The S&P 500 declined approximately 2% over the prior week, with the Nasdaq down 1.59%.

- The shock hits a US economy already strained by tariffs, persistent inflation, and a deteriorating labor market

### 5. US economy loses 92,000 jobs in February as Fed faces impossible dilemma

The US unexpectedly lost 92,000 net jobs in February — the first net monthly job loss outside a recession in years, against expectations of +59,000. Unemployment ticked up to 4.4%. The Bureau of Labor Statistics also revised December's figure downward by 65,000, from +48,000 to -17,000, revealing the labor market was weaker than believed for months.

The Fed now faces what Morgan Stanley's chief economic strategist called being caught "between a rock and a hard place" — weak employment data supports rate cuts, but oil-driven inflation from the Iran war argues forcefully for holding rates steady or raising them.

Key facts:
- Healthcare led losses at -28,000 (strike activity); manufacturing -12,000; transportation/warehousing -11,000; federal government -10,000
- The Supreme Court's Feb. 20 ruling that IEEPA tariffs were unconstitutional forced Trump to pivot to a 10–15% flat tariff under Section 122 of the 1974 Trade Act
- The tariff package represents the largest US tax increase as share of GDP since 1993
- Average additional cost per US household from tariffs: $1,500 in 2026 (Tax Foundation)
- Goldman Sachs found tariff incidence split: 37% paid by US consumers, 51% by US businesses, 9% by foreign exporters
- Recession probability currently estimated at ~30% (Morgan Stanley/Goldman Sachs)

### 8. Nepal's Gen Z revolution delivers election landslide for ex-rapper Balen Shah's RSP

Nepal's Rastriya Swatantra Party (RSP), founded only four years ago, won 103 of 165 directly elected seats and led in 21 more in parliamentary elections — the country's first since the 2025 youth revolt that ousted Prime Minister Khadga Prasad Oli. The RSP also holds approximately 51% of proportional representation seats. The result swept aside Nepal's two dominant parties — the Nepali Congress and the Communist Party of Nepal (UML) — which had alternated power for decades.

RSP leader Balendra "Balen" Shah, 35, is a former rapper turned Kathmandu mayor who led the 2025 Gen Z uprising. He campaigned on health and education for poor Nepalis. The vote reflects mass rejection of entrenched political elites, though the class character of Shah's populist anti-corruption platform requires scrutiny.

### US measles cases near 1,300 as elimination status comes under threat

The US measles total has reached 1,281 confirmed cases across 31 jurisdictions as of March 5, with 12 active outbreaks. South Carolina leads with 991 cases from an October outbreak; Utah has 358, Florida 124, and Texas 93. A full 93% of cases involve unvaccinated or unknown-status individuals, and 77% involve people under 19.

The Pan American Health Organization has delayed its ruling on US measles elimination status until November — the country hasn't lost that designation since it was first declared in 2000. The erosion of vaccination coverage, exacerbated by anti-vaccine political movements and public health funding cuts, threatens a return to endemic measles transmission.

The Iranian regime's crimes do not "shrink" for Iranian workers who face imprisonment for striking.

Woods's analysis of the war's strategic dimensions is superficial, relying primarily on assertions that Trump "lacks a realistic war strategy" — a military-technical critique that evades the deeper political-economic forces driving the war. The article concludes with organizational self-promotion for the newly launched "Revolutionary Communist International" rather than a concrete program for working-class action.

*Overall orientation:* The IMT/RCP's response to the Iran war reveals its characteristic political adaptation: opposition to imperialism that slides into apologetics for the targeted regime, combined with bombastic organizational claims ("Revolutionary Communist International") that substitute branding for political program.
