### 1. US-Iran ceasefire unravels as Strait of Hormuz remains closed

The two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan and announced April 8 — barely 90 minutes before Trump's deadline, after which he warned "a whole civilization will die tonight" — is fraying over the status of Lebanon. Pakistan's ceasefire language explicitly included Lebanon; Trump and Netanyahu both publicly declared Lebanon excluded. Iran says this discrepancy voids the agreement and has refused to reopen the Strait of Hormuz.

As of April 11, more than 100 commercial vessels remain stalled near the strait, which normally handles roughly 20 percent of global oil supply and significant LNG volumes. Reports indicate Iran is conditioning or charging fees for passage, and possible mines were detected in the waterway as of April 9. Brent crude has fallen from a war peak of $119 per barrel to approximately $95, but remains 35 percent above pre-war levels of roughly $70 in late February.

Key facts:

- Trump stated US military forces will remain "near Iran" until a "real agreement" is honored and warned any breach triggers a response "larger than anything seen before"
- Russia and China blocked a UN Security Council resolution on the Strait of Hormuz on April 7
- Iran struck UAE gas infrastructure, Kuwait oil facilities (causing "severe material damage" to oil, power, and desalination plants), and Saudi Arabia's east-west pipeline during the conflict
- UAE intercepted 17 ballistic missiles and 35 drones in a single engagement
- US military conducted strikes on Kharg Island, Iran's main oil export terminal

### 2. Israel continues Lebanon assault, strikes government building in Nabatieh

Israel continued its bombardment of Lebanon on April 10, killing at least 14 people. Thirteen of the dead were security personnel struck in an attack on a government building in Nabatieh. Lebanon's President Joseph Aoun condemned the strike, stating that "targeting state institutions would not deter Lebanon from defending its sovereignty." The attack on a state building — not a Hezbollah installation — marks an escalation in Israel's willingness to target Lebanese government infrastructure directly.

The assault follows the deadliest single day of the Lebanon campaign on April 8, when Israeli forces launched strikes on more than 100 Hezbollah targets in approximately 10 minutes using 50 fighter jets and roughly 160 munitions, killing at least 303 people and wounding 1,165. Netanyahu declared Lebanon operations fall outside the US-Iran ceasefire — a position that has become the central obstacle to reopening the Strait of Hormuz, since Iran refuses to comply with a ceasefire that excludes Lebanon.

Key facts:

- Netanyahu stated Israel "supports Trump's decision to suspend strikes against Iran" but declared Lebanon operations continue independently
- Lebanon's ceasefire exclusion is the principal reason Iran has not reopened the Strait of Hormuz

### 4. North Korea conducts 3-day weapons blitz with cluster-warhead missiles

### 5. US inflation surges to 3.3% as war-driven fuel costs soar

Consumer prices rose 0.9 percent in March — triple the prior month's rate — pushing annual inflation to 3.3 percent, driven overwhelmingly by a 21.2 percent monthly surge in gasoline prices, the largest single-month increase since the Bureau of Labor Statistics began tracking the series in 1967. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) rose a more modest 0.2 percent, with the annual core rate at 2.6 percent, indicating the headline spike is almost entirely energy-driven.

The surge reflects the Strait of Hormuz closure and the broader disruption caused by the US-Iran war. California pump prices averaged $5.92 per gallon, with some counties reaching $6.91. As Jacob Crosse noted on the WSWS, the inflation falls hardest on the 26.6 percent of civilian workers who depend on driving for income, while over 50 million workers earn $20 per hour or less against a federal minimum wage of $7.25 that has not increased in years.

Key facts:

- March CPI: +0.9% month-over-month, +3.3% year-over-year
- Core CPI: +0.2% month-over-month, +2.6% year-over-year
- Gasoline: +21.2% in March — largest monthly increase on record
- Billionaire wealth rose 120% from 2017–2025 versus 45% for the prior equivalent period; the top 1% holds approximately 50.2% of corporate equities

### 6. Gaza: 571,000 in emergency hunger as IPC projection period ends

The IPC food security projection period for Gaza (December 2025–April 15, 2026) is ending with approximately 571,000 Palestinians in emergency (Phase 4) hunger conditions and roughly 1,900 in catastrophic (Phase 5) conditions. An estimated 101,000 children aged 6–59 months suffer acute malnutrition, with more than 31,000 cases classified as severe.

Famine was technically averted after the October 2025 ceasefire allowed limited humanitarian access, but the IPC warns that "renewed hostilities or a halt in commercial inflows could trigger famine across the entire Strip rapidly." The Iran war and Strait of Hormuz crisis have further complicated regional supply chains, disrupting humanitarian logistics at a critical juncture.

Key facts:

- 571,000 in IPC Phase 4 (emergency); ~1,900 in Phase 5 (catastrophe)
- 101,000 children under five in acute malnutrition; 31,000+ severe
- No international mechanism exists to prevent a return to famine after the projection period lapses
- Regional supply chain disruptions from the Hormuz closure are compounding the crisis

### Chinese researchers face McCarthyite persecution at US universities

Youhuang Xiang, a 32-year-old Chinese postdoctoral researcher at Indiana University, was sentenced to time served after four months in detention and ordered deported for receiving plasmid DNA — circular, non-infectious molecules routine in biological research — from a colleague in China. The Justice Department pursued felony charges carrying up to 25-year sentences for a matter that would normally be handled as an administrative Agriculture Department issue.

Similar cases at the University of Michigan saw five Chinese researchers charged over harmless materials including plant fungi and roundworms; three had charges dismissed. One University of Michigan researcher died by suicide in March after what has been described as "severe and hostile questioning" by federal agents. Both universities reportedly cooperated with authorities, immediately revoking positions and visas.

The pattern reflects the escalation of anti-Chinese targeting in the context of US-China great-power rivalry, echoing the McCarthy-era persecution of scientists.

### US measles surges to 1,714 cases across 33 states

The United States has confirmed 1,714 measles cases as of April 9, making 2026 one of the worst years for measles since the disease was declared eliminated in 2000. Ninety-four percent of cases are outbreak-associated, and roughly 92 percent involve unvaccinated individuals or those with unknown vaccination status. No deaths have been reported in 2026. The country is now at risk of losing its measles elimination status, which requires keeping case counts below a sustained threshold over 12 months. The surge is directly linked to declining vaccination rates, exacerbated by anti-vaccine sentiment promoted by figures in and around the Trump administration.

### Afrika Bambaataa, founding figure of hip-hop, dies at 68

Afrika Bambaataa, one of the three founding figures of hip-hop alongside DJ Kool Herc and Grandmaster Flash, died April 9 in Pennsylvania of prostate cancer at age 68. His 1982 track "Planet Rock" — which fused hip-hop with Kraftwerk-influenced electro-funk — was a landmark recording that helped define an entire genre and influenced generations of electronic and hip-hop artists. He founded the Universal Zulu Nation, originally conceived as a vehicle for channeling Bronx gang culture into artistic expression.

His legacy was profoundly tarnished after multiple men accused him of sexually abusing them when they were boys during the 1980s and 1990s. The accusations confronted hip-hop culture with the contradiction between its origins as an art form of the oppressed and the predatory behavior of one of its founders — a contradiction the industry largely avoided reckoning with. His death will inevitably produce hagiographic retrospectives that elide the abuse; the fuller truth is that Bambaataa's story illustrates how cultural movements born from genuine social need can be deformed when individual power goes unchecked.

### 1. Strait of Hormuz closure and the global working class: who pays for imperialist war?

The Strait of Hormuz closure has raised global oil prices 35 percent or more since late February, driving inflation in fuel, food, and transportation costs that disproportionately fall on workers worldwide. Insurance costs, shipping delays, and the potential for Iran-imposed transit fees represent a massive transfer of wealth from workers to energy and finance capital. While the WSWS has covered the war itself extensively, a dedicated piece on the mechanisms by which the costs of imperialist war are systematically shifted onto the working class — through gas prices, food costs, and insurance premiums — would connect the geopolitical crisis to everyday economic experience.
