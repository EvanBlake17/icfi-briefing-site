### 1. Xi-Putin Beijing summit declares "multipolar world" as bilateral trade hits $240 billion

Vladimir Putin and Xi Jinping concluded a state visit in Beijing this week with the signing of a 47-page joint declaration on building "a multipolar world and a new type of international relations" — an explicit and unprecedented public targeting of US global dominance. The visit came only days after Xi's separate summit with Donald Trump, underlining China's calibrated balancing role between the rival great powers. Putin and Xi finalized over 40 agreements, including the long-delayed Power of Siberia 2 pipeline, which will carry 50 billion cubic meters of Russian gas annually to China via Mongolia, and an extension of visa-free travel for Russian citizens through end of 2027.

The economic data behind the declaration are the most consequential element. Bilateral Russia-China trade reached $240 billion in 2025 and rose another 20% in the first four months of 2026. Russia now imports more than 90% of sanctioned technology through China, and nearly all transactions are settled in roubles and yuan, bypassing the dollar. The summit's substantive achievement is the consolidation of an alternative payments, supply-chain and energy infrastructure that has functionally defeated the post-2022 Western sanctions regime.

Key facts:
- Xi: "The tide of unilateral hegemony is running rampant." Putin: "We have created a stable system of mutual trade protected from external influence."
- Both leaders designated 2026 the "Year of Education"; Putin invited Xi to visit Russia in 2027.
- The Siberia 2 pipeline had been stalled for over a decade by pricing disputes — its agreement signals Beijing's willingness to lock in long-term dependence on Russian hydrocarbons even as Western governments insist Russia is "isolated."
- The summit produced no public comment on the US-Iran war, despite both governments having major interests in its outcome.

### 3. Trump creates $1.776 billion "Anti-Weaponization Fund" by personal deal with Attorney General

The Trump administration this week formally established a $1.776 billion "Anti-Weaponization Fund" to compensate individuals who claim they were "unfairly investigated or prosecuted" under the Biden administration. The fund was created through a settlement between Trump and his former personal lawyer, now acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, in which Trump agreed to drop a spurious $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS over the leak of his tax returns to the *New York Times*. The transaction was conducted entirely within the executive branch with no congressional authorization, no public rulemaking, no defined class of eligible claimants, no judicial review and no right of appeal.

A separate one-page addendum to the same deal — written in Trump's favored all-caps style — "RELEASES, WAIVES, ACQUITS, and FOREVER DISCHARGES" Trump, his sons and his business entities from any federal claim that "has been or could have been asserted." It effectively terminates any remaining federal tax-fraud or financial investigations of the Trump Organization.

As Patrick Martin notes in today's WSWS Perspective, "Trump is now immunized for both his public and private actions" — adding a new layer to the immunity granted by the 2024 Supreme Court ruling in *Trump v. United States*. Martin argues the structure of the fund is designed to channel hundreds of millions of dollars to the pardoned January 6 attackers and assorted fascist allies, building "the American equivalent of Hitler's brownshirts" as a paramilitary auxiliary to be deployed against strikers and anti-war protesters.

Key facts:
- A five-member committee — all appointed by the Trump-appointed Attorney General, all removable by Trump — governs disbursements; only three members constitute a quorum.
- The fund is auditable "only at the Attorney General's direction."
- Article I of the Constitution: "No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law" — a clause the deal flatly disregards.
- Senate and House Democrats criticized the fund as "corruption" on Thursday but, per Martin, "retreated to promises that a midterm election victory would allow them to hold Trump accountable" rather than calling for mass mobilization.

### 4. WHO declares Ebola PHEIC in DRC and Uganda — no vaccine exists

The World Health Organization Director-General declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) on May 16-17 in response to an Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo that has now spread to Uganda. The outbreak involves the Bundibugyo virus strain, for which **no licensed vaccine and no specific therapeutics exist**. Historical case fatality rates for the Bundibugyo strain run between 30% and 50%.

As of May 20, DRC authorities have reported approximately 600 suspected cases and 139 deaths, with 51 confirmed. Two imported cases — including one death — have been confirmed in Kampala, Uganda. At Mongbwalu hospital in Ituri Province, four healthcare workers died within four days of one another. A four-week interval elapsed between the first symptom onset (April 25) and laboratory confirmation (May 14) — a detection lag that allowed the virus to seed in three provinces before containment could begin.

Key facts:
- This is the 17th Ebola outbreak in the DRC since 1976.
- Over 60% of cases are female, with most between ages 20 and 39 — a profile consistent with infection within healthcare and caregiving settings.
- The outbreak has spread from Ituri Province to North Kivu, including the cities of Butembo and Goma.
- The PHEIC is the highest alert level under the International Health Regulations and is rarely declared; it is an explicit admission that available countermeasures are inadequate.

### 5. Bolivian capital besieged as workers' blockades enter third week

Bolivia's capital La Paz has effectively been cut off from the rest of the country for two weeks as road blockades led by the Bolivian Workers' Central (COB), peasant unions and mining federations have emptied markets and depleted hospital oxygen reserves. Approximately 5,000 vehicles are stranded along Bolivia's highways, including fuel and food trucks. Business organizations estimate the blockades are costing the economy more than $50 million per day. At least three people have died after emergency vehicles were prevented from reaching medical centers.

The political crisis facing right-wing President Rodrigo Paz, less than six months into his term, has now escalated beyond the original trigger — a land mortgage law that he formally annulled on May 13. Worker and peasant demands have expanded to include higher wages, labor-law reform and Paz's resignation. Paz announced a cabinet reshuffle Wednesday, but the strike continues. Analysts describe the underlying economic situation as "the worst in 40 years": inflation hovered near 20% last year, fuel shortages are chronic and foreign reserves are depleted.

Key facts:
- The COB-led mobilization has the character of a general strike combined with a nationwide blockade — a level of working-class action not seen in Bolivia since the 2003-2005 gas-wars period that ousted two presidents.
- Paz's government has so far avoided declaring a state of emergency, evidently fearing military fragmentation if troops are ordered against the mineworkers.
- The Movement for Socialism (MAS) of Evo Morales is reportedly attempting to channel the movement back into parliamentary opposition, but rank-and-file committees in the highlands are operating outside of MAS control.

### 6. Ukraine strikes Russian drone training hub, killing 65 cadets

Overnight May 20-21, Ukrainian forces launched 11 precision strikes — using medium-range weapons with 100 kg warheads — on a Russian drone pilot training center in Snizhne, occupied Donetsk Oblast. Ukrainian military intelligence reported that 65 cadets of the "Sever-Akhmat" 78th Special Purpose Motorized Regiment were killed in their barracks, along with their commander, Lt. Colonel "Buryi," identified as a PhD at the Russian Academy of Missile and Artillery Sciences. The targeted two-story complex, roughly 2,484 square meters, was reportedly used for UAV and warhead assembly as well as personnel quarters. Russia has not officially confirmed the strike.

The operation is part of a sustained Ukrainian campaign against Russian drone infrastructure. Ukrainian military figures report Russia lost over 19,000 troops in early May. In parallel, Russia has launched the largest deep-strike campaign of the war: approximately 20 combined drone-and-missile attacks on Kyiv during May alone. The qualitative escalation comes precisely as Trump has promised a diplomatic settlement of the war — and as Ukrainian forces have been expanding the reach of indigenous strike weapons.

Key facts:
- Ukrainian intelligence claims the cadets were killed in their barracks before the warhead-assembly section was hit, suggesting the strike was timed for maximum personnel casualties.
- The "Akhmat" formations are nominally Chechen units under Kadyrov's command structure, repurposed for drone warfare.
- The Russian Defense Ministry has imposed an internal information blackout on training-center casualties since a November 2025 strike on a similar facility.

### 7. Gaza phase-two ceasefire stalls as Israel refuses withdrawal from "yellow line"

Israeli forces recovered the body of Ran Gvili — the last Israeli hostage in Gaza — from a cemetery in northern Gaza on January 26, and Israel formally announced the recovery this week. The development was supposed to trigger phase two of the Gaza ceasefire framework, which requires full Israeli military withdrawal from Gaza, Hamas disarmament, deployment of an International Stabilization Force and transition of governance from the "Board of Peace" to a reformed Palestinian Authority.

Israel has now told UN mediators it will not withdraw from the so-called "yellow line" within Gaza, directly blocking phase-two implementation. Israeli air strikes, shelling and small-arms fire continue across the strip despite the formal ceasefire. The UN Security Council held an emergency debate on May 21 urging consolidation of the ceasefire and warning of the "horrendous conditions" facing Palestinian civilians.

In parallel, Israel's war on Lebanon continues. Since March 2, when the war began, at least 2,846 people have been killed in Lebanon and more than a million displaced. A 45-day ceasefire extension brokered by the United States on May 15 has been repeatedly violated; Israeli strikes killed at least five in Lebanon on May 17 alone, damaging a hospital. A US-facilitated security track is scheduled to begin May 29, with the next round of direct US-Israel-Lebanon talks planned for June 2-3 in Washington.

Key facts:
- The "Board of Peace" — a Trump-administration-led body chaired by Trump himself — retains effective veto power over Palestinian self-governance arrangements.
- Hamas retains its political and military presence in Gaza despite the formal disarmament requirement; no enforcement mechanism has been agreed.
- Iran has tied the Lebanon war to its own conflict with the United States: Tehran warned the wars will "spread beyond the Middle East" if Washington resumes large-scale strikes.

### 8. DNC chair disavows party's own 2024 election autopsy on day of release

DNC Chair Ken Martin released the Democratic National Committee's official 2024 election post-mortem on Thursday under duress, after CNN obtained and reported on the report's contents. In a Substack post accompanying the release, Martin wrote: "I am not proud of this product; it does not meet my standards and it won't meet your standards." - The report avoids all discussion of the war on Gaza, on which Harris repeatedly affirmed her support for "Israel's right to defend itself" while the Democrats lost decisive margins among Arab-American, Muslim and youth voters in Michigan, Wisconsin and elsewhere.
- The report does not examine the Biden administration's role in suppressing labor militancy — including the imposition of a no-strike contract on rail workers in 2022.
- Multiple DNC members had pushed for an outside, independent investigation in the months after the election; the leadership rejected the request.

### Australia cuts university research funding by 4.3% while military spending climbs

The Australian federal budget released this week increases overall higher-education teaching and research funding by 5% to $12.6 billion in nominal terms — but a 4.1% rise in domestic enrollments means per-student funding falls in real terms. Some 4,000 university staff positions have been eliminated over the past 18 months. Federal funding for domestic enrollments has fallen from approximately 80% of costs in 1987 to 40% today, with the gap filled by debt-financed student fees.

### US measles cases hit 1,893 across 27 outbreaks as kindergarten vaccination dips to 92.5%

As of May 14, the CDC has confirmed 1,893 measles cases across 27 outbreaks in the United States — already on pace to exceed any year in decades. 93% of cases (1,761) are outbreak-associated, indicating sustained chains of transmission rather than isolated imports. Kindergarten MMR vaccination coverage has fallen to **92.5%**, below the 95% threshold required for herd immunity. Roughly 286,000 kindergartners are now unprotected. The trajectory reflects the cumulative effect of the Trump administration's promotion of vaccine skepticism through HHS Secretary RFK Jr. and the systematic dismantling of CDC outbreak-response capacity.

### First documented human H5N5 infection reported in NEJM

The *New England Journal of Medicine* published this month the first documented human infection with highly pathogenic avian influenza A(H5N5). The case involved a 75-year-old immunocompromised woman in North America who developed fever and diarrhea after bird exposure in October 2025. Antiviral treatment was delayed because initial upper-respiratory tests came back negative, complicating her course. The case confirms that fully avian H5 lineages beyond H5N1 can spill over into humans — broadening the surveillance and pandemic-preparedness target.

In parallel, WHO reported 13 avian and swine influenza human cases in Q1 2026, including one previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child from Bangladesh. None of the 13 case contacts became ill. CDC surveillance shows no unusual influenza activity in the US population as of May 1. WHO's TAG-CO-VAC group this month recommended monovalent **LP.8.1** as the preferred antigen for next-cycle COVID-19 vaccines, reflecting continued antigenic drift away from XBB/JN lineages. The dominant US variant is currently XFG.1.1 (32% of cases). National test positivity is approximately 1% (week ending May 2), with infections declining or stable in 37 states.

### Trump signs AI oversight order after Anthropic "Mythos" model autonomously identifies thousands of OS vulnerabilities

The Trump administration moved this week to sign an executive order creating a "voluntary" framework requiring AI developers to provide frontier models to the US government 90 days before public release. The shift was catalyzed by Anthropic's unreleased "Mythos Preview" model, which reportedly identified thousands of critical vulnerabilities in major operating systems and browsers without human prompting. The Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) has already signed pre-deployment evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI. The order represents a sharp reversal from the administration's earlier anti-regulation stance — and a precedent for government pre-screening of frontier models, with obvious military and intelligence implications.

### EU finalizes AI Omnibus deal — significantly lightens AI Act burden

The European Council and Parliament agreed on an AI Omnibus package that reduces recurring compliance costs, extends simplified requirements to small mid-caps, expands access to regulatory sandboxes (including a new EU-level sandbox) and adds a specific prohibition on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery. The deal substantially softens implementation pressure on smaller developers and harmonizes enforcement across member states ahead of key AI Act deadlines.

### Emperor penguins and Antarctic fur seals added to IUCN Red List

The International Union for Conservation of Nature's updated Red List — now listing 48,600 threatened species (28% of all assessed) — has added emperor penguins and Antarctic fur seals. Penguin populations are down roughly 10% (over 20,000 adults) between 2009 and 2018, from a current base of ~595,000, with populations projected to halve by the 2080s. Antarctic fur seals have declined from 2,187,000 mature individuals (1999) to 944,000 (2025), with specific colonies declining 47-86% as warming waters displace krill. Antarctica is warming at three times the global rate and losing 100 billion metric tons of ice annually. At the 48th Antarctic Treaty Consultative Meeting (May 11-21), China and Russia vetoed Specially Protected species designations in order to defend industrial krill fishing.

In other science news, astronomers using the James Webb Space Telescope this week published the clearest map yet of the universe's large-scale "cosmic web," the filamentary structure linking galaxies across billions of light-years — enabling new tests of dark-matter distribution models. Separately, researchers published a superconducting sensor capable of detecting energy below one zeptojoule (10⁻²¹ J), a record sensitivity that may enable direct detection of dark-matter candidates and pushes quantum sensing into fundamental-physics territory.

## Arts and culture

The political climate of war abroad and oligarchic consolidation at home has continued to shape the cultural moment. Two threads dominate the past 24 hours: a series of attempts by Hollywood and the streaming industry to engage with the Iran war and broader Middle East violence, often in evasive or jingoistic forms; and the unfolding crisis at the great cultural institutions, which are being squeezed simultaneously by Trump-era cultural decrees and by their own donor classes.

The 79th Cannes Film Festival, which concluded last weekend, continues to reverberate through the trade press, with critics noting the unusual political weight of this year's selection — a Brazilian-Argentine-French co-production on the suppression of striker mobilizations during the 1973 Pinochet coup, an Iranian-French film smuggled out of Tehran during the war, and a Ukrainian documentary built from frontline footage. The jury's choice to award the Palme d'Or to the Iranian work was both a recognition of the courage of its makers and a tacit rebuke of the western military campaign now underway against the country in which it was filmed. That such a gesture is necessary at all is a measure of how thoroughly the broader film industry has abandoned political seriousness; the festival was otherwise dominated by big-budget franchise sequels, biopics of inoffensive figures and an unmistakable nostalgia for the Cold War period.

Hollywood's relationship to the Iran war has been openly opportunistic. Within the past two months, multiple studios have green-lit projects framed around US naval operations in the Persian Gulf — variants of *Top Gun*-style militarist entertainment that present US strikes as inherently righteous and Iranian civilians as either invisible or as obstacles to be overcome. As the WSWS arts pages have observed repeatedly, this is the same cultural-industrial complex that gave us the genre of post-9/11 "war on terror" cinema two decades ago: militarist propaganda dressed as middlebrow drama. The current cycle is being accelerated by Pentagon technical-support agreements with major studios and by the integration of military-industrial-funded "consultants" into writers' rooms.

In the institutional space, the Smithsonian continues to face direct political pressure under a Trump executive order requiring the removal of exhibitions deemed insufficiently celebratory of American history. Curators have reported the removal of materials on slavery, the labor movement and the civil rights struggle — even from museums founded specifically to document those histories. The Metropolitan Opera in New York has announced significant cuts to its 2026-27 season, citing donor losses; the New York City Ballet has reduced its corps positions. These institutional retrenchments are occurring in parallel with the cultural strangulation of public broadcasting, with PBS and NPR facing further congressional funding reductions in the reconciliation package now stuck in Senate negotiations.

Among the deaths this week, the death of the Russian poet **Olga Sedakova**, 76, has been noted in literary publications worldwide. Sedakova — one of the major Russian-language religious poets of the late Soviet and post-Soviet period, a translator of Rilke and Dante, and a public opponent of the Putin government's war on Ukraine — wrote some of the most spiritually serious verse of her generation. Her death removes a distinct moral voice from the contemporary Russian literary scene.

In book publishing, **Hisham Matar's** new novel on the Libyan revolution and its aftermath has received significant attention in the British and US press. Matar, whose previous work *The Return* won the Pulitzer Prize, continues to engage seriously with the legacy of imperialist intervention in North Africa.
