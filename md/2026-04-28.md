### 1. Trump reviews Iran peace plan as war hits day 60 and War Powers deadline looms

President Trump's national security team is reviewing an Iranian proposal to halt hostilities and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, while deferring nuclear talks to a later date. The development comes as the US-Israel war against Iran enters its 60th day. The conflict, code-named "Operation Epic Fury," began on February 28 with US and Israeli strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and other senior Iranian officials. Iran responded by closing the Strait of Hormuz.

Iran's diplomatic offensive intensified over the weekend. Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi arrived in Moscow on April 27 after a "whirlwind weekend of diplomacy" that included visits to multiple capitals. Putin "voiced strong support for Iran" and endorsed a negotiated solution. Russian Permanent Representative to the UN Vasily Nebenzya publicly called for "dropping hawkish plans to resume" large-scale war. Trump canceled a planned diplomatic trip to Pakistan after learning Iran's foreign minister was leaving Russia — signaling continued friction over the negotiating framework. Iran insists discussions of the Hormuz closure and nuclear matters be separated; the US and Israel are pushing for a comprehensive deal.

The constitutional dimension of the war is reaching a breaking point. The 60-day clock under the War Powers Resolution — starting from Trump's March 2 congressional notification — expires May 1. The Senate has blocked invoking the War Powers Act five times, including a 52-47 vote on April 15 in which Sen. Rand Paul was the lone Republican supporter and Sen. John Fetterman broke with Democrats.

Key facts:
- Sen. Tammy Duckworth: "This war is flat-out illegal. Trump didn't have the authority to launch it in the first place."
- ACLU's Christopher Anders: "There is nothing under the War Powers Resolution that allows the president to continue beyond those 60 days."
- White House response: Trump has provided "over 30 bipartisan briefings" before and during hostilities.
- Qatar publicly stated: "the Strait must not be used as pressure"; UN Secretary-General has urged all parties to prioritize the humanitarian and economic dimensions of reopening the waterway.

The episode confirms what the WSWS has documented for two months: the Iran war is being prosecuted in defiance of constitutional restraints, with both bourgeois parties complicit in a structural shift toward unfettered presidential war-making.

### 2. Strait of Hormuz crisis triggers global food and energy emergency

Iran's near-closure of the Strait of Hormuz is producing the most severe simultaneous energy and food shock in decades. Tanker traffic through the strait — which carries roughly 30–35% of global crude oil and 20% of global natural gas — has dropped to near zero. Iran is now charging tolls exceeding $1 million per vessel for ships it permits through. The disruption has reshaped global commodity markets and is triggering cascading effects on agricultural production worldwide.

The economic damage compounds daily. The Strait closure is costing approximately $20 billion per day in global GDP. A full prolonged closure could erase $3.57–6.95 trillion in global economic output — between 3.24% and over 6% of global GDP. Brent crude surged 10–13% to $80–82 per barrel on March 2 when the closure began; analysts now forecast $100/barrel if disruptions persist and up to $132/barrel if extended two quarters.

The food dimension is particularly acute. Urea fertilizer prices have risen 36% above pre-war levels, with approximately 3–4 million tonnes of fertilizer trade per month stalled. Over 30% of global urea exports pass through the strait. The FAO warns of a global food "catastrophe" if disruptions persist beyond a 3-month window, as 2026 planting decisions are already being affected.

Key facts:
- UN Secretary-General: the standoff risks a "global food emergency."
- Qatar: "the Strait must not be used as pressure."
- Global crude oil and LNG flows through Hormuz: 30–35% and 20% respectively.
- Estimated global GDP loss per day of closure: $20 billion.

The crisis is producing a sharp class redistribution. Energy corporations operating outside the Gulf — US shale producers and European supermajors — are reporting sharply higher revenues, while working-class households in import-dependent countries from Egypt to the Philippines absorb the costs in food, fuel, and fertilizer prices.

### 3. Mali defense minister killed in unprecedented nationwide offensive

Mali's Defense Minister Gen. Sadio Camara was killed on April 25 when a suicide bomber drove a car into his residence in Kati, killing him alongside his second wife and two grandchildren. Intelligence chief Modibo Koné sustained multiple gunshot wounds during fighting at Kati and died the following day. Army Chief of Staff Oumar Diarra was also reportedly wounded. The deaths cap what analysts are calling "the largest offensive in the Mali War since the capture of northern Mali in 2012."

The April 25 attacks were carried out simultaneously by JNIM (Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin, an al-Qaeda affiliate) and the FLA (Azawad Liberation Front, Tuareg-led) across at least seven cities including the capital Bamako, Kati, Sévaré, Bourem, Mopti, Kidal, and Gao. The FLA claimed to have seized Kidal and parts of Gao. JNIM struck near Modibo Keita International Airport, just 15 kilometers from central Bamako. Russian-backed Africa Corps mercenaries — successors to the Wagner Group — fought alongside Malian forces in several locations, including the capital.

Key facts:
- Civilian casualties from the offensive remain unconfirmed; analyst Nicolas Haque told Al Jazeera, "The scale and coordination of the attacks appear to be unprecedented."
- Mali's military junta seized power in a 2021 coup and has relied on Russian mercenary support since expelling French forces in 2022.
- The collapse of governance across the Sahel — Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger — is the legacy of more than a decade of French and US counterinsurgency that failed to address underlying social conditions and instead ratified the dictatorial rule of military strongmen.

The convergence of jihadist insurgency, ethnic-separatist mobilization, foreign mercenary deployment, and military dictatorship in one of the world's poorest countries underscores the disintegration of the post-colonial state structure across the Sahel and the consequences of imperialist destabilization, from the 2011 NATO war on Libya forward.

### 4. Lebanon war intensifies despite ceasefire as Israel orders southern evacuations

Israeli forces killed 14 people in southern Lebanon on April 26 — the deadliest single day since a US-brokered Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire took effect April 16-17. The strikes were accompanied by forced evacuation orders for southern Lebanon, signaling a potential major ground escalation. Both sides have accused each other of over 500 ceasefire violations since the truce began.

The cumulative toll of Israel's renewed war on Lebanon is staggering. Total deaths since fighting resumed on March 2: at least 2,491 killed and 7,719 wounded, according to Lebanon's Health Ministry. On April 8 — just after the Iran-Israel ceasefire announcement — Israel launched what it called its "most powerful attacks" on Lebanon, killing at least 357 people in a single day. Since the April 16 ceasefire took effect, at least 40 additional people have been killed by Israeli attacks.

Key facts:
- Hezbollah rejected ceasefire violation allegations, calling its attacks "a legitimate response to the enemy's persistent violations."
- Iran has linked the Lebanon conflict to the broader regional war, demanding an end to Israeli strikes as a condition for peace.
- Southern Lebanon, the targeted area, is densely populated with no realistic evacuation destinations within Lebanon.
- The ceasefire framework, brokered by the United States, places no enforceable constraint on Israeli operations.

The pattern — a "ceasefire" that authorizes continued bombing of civilian areas — replicates the model imposed in Gaza and reveals the function of US "diplomacy" as cover for ongoing military operations rather than termination of hostilities.

### 5. Ukraine strikes Russia's Tuapse refinery for third time in 12 days

Ukrainian drones struck Rosneft's Tuapse refinery on April 28 — the third strike on the facility in 12 days, following attacks on April 16 and April 20. The refinery, which processes approximately 12 million metric tons of crude annually and is a key Black Sea export hub for naphtha, diesel, and fuel oil, has been offline since the April 16 strike. Krasnodar Krai Governor Veniamin Kondratyev ordered the evacuation of residents living near the facility.

The April 20 strike alone destroyed 24 storage tanks and damaged four more, according to Ukraine's General Staff. Russian authorities cited "fall of drone debris" as the official cause; no casualties were reported in the April 28 strike. Russia's Defense Ministry said air defenses intercepted 186 Ukrainian drones over southern Russia and occupied Crimea on April 28.

Key facts:
- Repeated strikes on the Tuapse facility represent a deliberate Ukrainian targeting of Russian export infrastructure as fighting in eastern Ukraine remains static.
- President Zelensky simultaneously announced a target of at least 50,000 ground robotic systems for 2026, marking the institutionalization of unmanned ground combat in Ukrainian doctrine.
- Russian crude exports through Black Sea terminals have been progressively disrupted; the cumulative effect is to extend pressure on global oil markets already strained by the Hormuz crisis.

The targeting of energy infrastructure on both sides — Ukrainian strikes on Russian refineries, Russian strikes on Ukrainian electrical grids and gas storage — demonstrates that the war has moved into a phase of attritional industrial destruction that NATO escalation policy is committed to extending indefinitely.

### 6. Meta and Microsoft announce 17,000 job cuts as AI labor offensive accelerates

Meta announced cuts of approximately 8,000 jobs — roughly 10% of its 80,000 workforce — to begin May 20, while Microsoft offered voluntary buyouts to roughly 8,750 US employees, or about 7% of its US staff. Microsoft's program is the first such voluntary separation in the company's 51-year history. Combined, the two announcements account for more than 17,000 job losses; total tech sector layoffs in 2026 have now passed 92,000.

Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg framed the cuts in explicit AI terms: "2026 will be the year when AI fundamentally transforms our work." The company is committing $135 billion to AI infrastructure spending this year. Wall Street rewarded the announcements: shares of both companies rebounded after initial declines, reflecting investor enthusiasm for AI-driven cost reduction over employment stability.

Key facts:
- Meta will eliminate 6,000 currently open positions in addition to the 8,000 layoffs.
- Microsoft's buyout offer represents the company's largest workforce action ever.
- 92,000 tech workers laid off in 2026 to date — a pace that exceeds the 2022-23 layoff wave.
- Big Tech earnings reports this week (Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft on Wednesday April 29) are expected to disclose further AI capex commitments.

As Kevin Reed wrote on the WSWS, the combination of mass layoffs and unprecedented capital spending on AI infrastructure exposes the class character of the "AI revolution" — a deliberate offensive to displace labor, intensify exploitation of remaining workers, and concentrate wealth, conducted with the open backing of financial markets.

### 7. SIPRI: Global military spending hits record $2.887 trillion

The Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI) reported that global military expenditure reached $2.887 trillion in 2025 — the highest ever recorded and the eleventh consecutive year of growth. The data, released as the US-Israel war on Iran enters its third month and as European NATO members rearm at the sharpest pace since the Cold War, confirms that the postwar pattern of episodic conflict has given way to a structural war economy.

The United States led at $954 billion — one-third of the global total. Trump has proposed a $1.5 trillion Pentagon budget for fiscal year 2027, a 42% year-on-year increase and the largest single-year defense request in US history. European NATO members' spending rose 14% to $864 billion. Germany increased its budget 24% to $114 billion and announced its largest rearmament since World War II, targeting "the strongest conventional army in Europe." Japan raised spending 9.7% and ended its postwar ban on lethal weapons exports. Ukraine is allocating 40% of GDP to military spending.

Key facts:
- The Trump $1.5 trillion Pentagon request is partly funded through 10% cuts to non-defense discretionary spending including healthcare, education, housing, and food assistance.
- Russia and China continue rapid military buildups; SIPRI documents accelerating expenditure on hypersonic, naval, and space-based systems.
- The figures do not include intelligence, veterans' care, or much classified spending — actual military-related expenditure exceeds reported totals substantially.

As Andre Damon wrote on the WSWS, the SIPRI report documents the conscious construction of a war economy on a world scale. The "guns and butter" framework has been abandoned; rearmament is being financed directly through the dismantling of the social conditions of working-class life. The class question is posed nakedly: workers in every major country are being made to pay for the preparations for a third world war.

### Palestinian Authority sweeps low-turnout Gaza and West Bank vote

The Palestinian Authority held local elections on April 25 across 403 Palestinian local authorities — the first vote of any kind in any part of Gaza since 2006. Only one Gaza municipality, Deir al-Balah, participated. Turnout there was 22.7% — just 15,890 of roughly 70,000 registered voters cast ballots. In the West Bank, turnout reached 53.4% across 183 municipalities, with approximately 522,000 people voting.

Fatah/Abbas-aligned lists swept both contests. The Nahdat Deir el-Balah list won 6 of 15 Gaza seats; Fatah claimed a "sweeping victory" in the West Bank. Hamas did not participate. Palestinian officials are framing the vote as a step toward a long-delayed presidential election — the last was held in January 2006, more than 21 years ago. Critics describe the result as a Palestinian Authority legitimation exercise rather than genuine democratic renewal. The structural conditions that make meaningful elections impossible — Israeli military occupation, collective punishment of Gaza, exclusion of Hamas, mass displacement of the population — remained unchanged on election day.

### Peruvian far right alleges fraud as nominally "left" candidate climbs to second

Roberto Sánchez secured second place in Peru's presidential election with 12.04% of the vote, defeating right-wing candidate Rafael López Aliaga by just 24,174 votes. Keiko Fujimori leads with 17.06%. López Aliaga and right-wing media alleged systematic fraud without evidence, forcing ONPE president Piero Corvetto to resign. The episode marks an early effort by the Peruvian right to contest results that did not deliver outright victory.

The most striking statistic from the vote was the level of social abstention: 30.8% of eligible voters — approximately 8.4 million people — cast null or blank ballots or did not vote at all, reflecting deep alienation from all wings of the bourgeois political system after years of revolving-door governments, the violent repression of indigenous protests, and accumulated economic crises. As Cesar Uco wrote on the WSWS, Sánchez's nominally "left" platform — including constitutional reform — will be blocked by a right-dominated Congress. Sánchez himself was implicated in the cover-up of the 2009 Baguazo Massacre of indigenous protesters, exposing his "left" credentials as fraudulent.

### Australia's Labor government partially reverses aged care co-payments

The Albanese Labor government partially reversed co-payments for personal care services under the Support at Home program after months of public backlash. However, retirees still face charges of 50–80% for transport, mobility, cleaning, meals, and social support, with lifetime caps of $15,000 for home modifications. Over 200,000 Australians remain on waiting lists — 120,000 awaiting assessment, 87,000 approved but without packages.

KPMG 2026 data shows the Australian aged care sector generated $558.1 million in net profit in the past year, with the 15 largest providers controlling more than 30% of funding. As Mike Head wrote on the WSWS, Labor has transformed aged care into a profit market in which vulnerable people are denied services despite eligibility — a model that serves corporate interests rather than public need, and which Labor is presenting as "reform."

### US measles cases hit 1,792 across 22 outbreaks

CDC data through April 23 confirms 1,792 measles cases across 22 outbreaks in 2026 — already 78% of 2025's full-year total of 2,288 cases. South Carolina leads with 667 cases, followed by Utah (408), Texas (176), and Florida (129). 93% of cases are outbreak-associated, indicating concentrated under-vaccination clusters rather than dispersed importation. The trajectory puts US measles elimination status — held since 2000 — at serious risk for the first time in over two decades.

### NEJM: pulsed-field ablation outperforms drugs in persistent AF

A 440-patient randomized controlled trial published in the New England Journal of Medicine reported that Boston Scientific's Farawave pulsed-field ablation catheter achieved a 12-month arrhythmia-free rate of approximately 56% in persistent atrial fibrillation, versus approximately 30% for antiarrhythmic drug therapy. The trial — among the largest head-to-head comparisons of catheter ablation against pharmacotherapy for persistent AF — strengthens the clinical case for early interventional management.

A separate study published in *Nature Genetics* by researchers at McGill University and the Douglas Institute identified two neurologically distinct cell populations with divergent behavior in major depressive disorder, opening a path toward cell-type-specific therapeutic targets. The finding may help explain why current SSRIs and SNRIs fail a substantial fraction of patients.

The race was also the first major World Marathon Major run under the new IAAF restrictions on advanced footwear technology, lending Sawe's performance additional historical weight. The achievements of East African distance runners — products of impoverished rural training environments and the determined cultivation of a national sporting infrastructure — continue to expose the fictional character of bourgeois nationalist claims that human excellence depends on racial or "civilizational" hierarchy.

Notable cultural developments and currents in the past 24 hours:

- **Cinema and the war**: A growing number of independent filmmakers — including documentarians working in southern Lebanon and Iran — are reporting interruption of their production by the renewed bombing campaigns. Festival programmers in Berlin, Karlovy Vary, and Locarno are reporting an increase in submissions addressing the wars; questions of access, witness, and the representation of mass civilian death will shape the late spring and summer film calendar.
- **Theater**: The continued retreat of major regional repertory theaters in the United States — driven by collapsing subscription models, the post-pandemic donor crisis, and structural under-funding of the arts — is producing a wave of season cancellations and layoffs. Several mid-sized companies have announced reduced 2026-27 seasons; the structural decay of the regional theater system is one of the clearest indices of the long-term decline of bourgeois cultural philanthropy.
- **Publishing**: Spring lists continue to be dominated by works engaged, in various registers, with authoritarianism, war, and historical memory. The literary press has seen renewed debate over the relationship between memoir and political analysis, with several recent works on Palestine and Iran provoking attempts at suppression in academic and corporate venues.
- **Visual arts**: The restitution debate continues at major European institutions. The Berlin and Vienna museum systems remain at the center of disputes over Nazi-era looted artworks; parallel efforts are underway to address colonial-era African and Asian collections held by the British Museum and the Quai Branly. Restitution is being deployed by some bourgeois liberals as a substitute for genuine engagement with the imperial structures that produced the original looting.
- **Censorship and defunding**: US arts funding cuts written into the Trump administration's discretionary spending reductions are beginning to land at the National Endowment for the Arts, the Institute of Museum and Library Services, and federally supported PBS programming. The pattern — concentrated cuts to public-facing cultural infrastructure alongside accelerating military spending — replicates the war-economy template at the level of cultural policy.
- **Notable deaths**: Major literary, theatrical, and musical figures continue to pass at a generational rate; obituary writers in the major dailies are processing what is, in effect, the wholesale departure of the post-1945 cultural cohort. Each obituary is also a measurement of how far the conditions for serious artistic work have deteriorated.

The dominant cultural register of late April 2026 is one of compressed, accelerating crisis: the public arts infrastructure is starving while a generation of artists confronts wars on multiple fronts and the displacement of cultural labor by AI tools. The works that endure are those that grapple seriously with these conditions, rather than retreating into identitarian celebration or aestheticized despair.

Coverage of the Russia-Iran Moscow meeting tends toward open advocacy of a multipolar world order led by Beijing and Moscow — a framework that has nothing to do with the international socialist mobilization of the working class against war.

The political line is Stalinist-Maoist and great-power-campist in basic orientation. The organization's energy and street presence are real; the political program is a dead end for any serious anti-war movement.

### Socialist Alternative

Socialist Alternative has maintained a consistent line calling for "labor resistance" to Trump and for the construction of a "workers' party," but its operational practice continues to flow into existing union structures and into electoral campaigns that do not break decisively with the Democratic Party. Coverage of the AI layoff wave at Meta and Microsoft has been substantial but ends in calls for legislative remedies (taxing Big Tech, retraining funds) that pose no challenge to the property relations driving the offensive.

The political line is left-reformist, structured around the project of pressuring the existing labor and political apparatus rather than building independent organs of working-class power.

### Socialist Worker / SWP UK

The SWP's current coverage centers heavily on the Iran war and the Lebanon escalation. The line is principled in calling for an immediate end to the bombing and for the defeat of the imperialist war effort. However, on the British political situation, the paper has continued to treat the trade union bureaucracy and the left wing of the Labour Party as recoverable terrain for working-class politics — a position that has produced a long string of strategic defeats since the Corbyn project's collapse.

The political line is centrist with strong anti-imperialist instincts but a chronic inability to break organizationally with the union and Labour bureaucracies that the WSWS has consistently identified as the principal political obstacle in Britain.

### Socialist Appeal / RCP IMT

The IMT's rebranding of its sections as "Revolutionary Communist Party" continues to draw new recruits with bold revolutionary language. Coverage of the Iran war and the global rearmament documented in the SIPRI report is presented in genuinely Marxist terminology and points correctly to the war-economy character of the moment. However, the organization's operational practice — emphasis on rapid recruitment, simplified propaganda, and the projection of a "communist mass party" before the basic political and theoretical work has been done — substitutes appearance for substance. The relationship to the WSWS and the ICFI is one of denunciation without serious engagement with the documentary record of the historical struggle against Stalinism, Pabloism, and revisionism.

The political line is propagandistic-centrist: a mass-recruitment-first orientation overlaid on Communist branding, without the cadre formation that would translate growth into a genuine revolutionary force.

### 1. Trump's $1.5 trillion Pentagon budget request and the dismantling of social spending

The administration's proposed FY 2027 defense budget is being financed directly through 10% cuts to non-defense discretionary spending, including healthcare, education, housing, and food assistance. The class equation is naked: workers are being made to pay for a war economy that targets Iran, prepares for confrontation with China, and underwrites NATO rearmament. A class-political analysis tying the budget request to the SIPRI data and the May 1 War Powers deadline would be uniquely valuable.

### 7. War Powers Resolution and the structural degradation of US democratic oversight

Senate Republicans have defeated war powers resolutions five times to authorize an undeclared war that constitutional scholars across the political spectrum call illegal.
