Radev has previously called EU sanctions on Russia *"counterproductive,"* opposed sending weapons to Ukraine, and advocated resuming Russian gas imports. Russia is described in European Council on Foreign Relations analysis as having *"regained leverage inside the European Union"* through the result — Bulgaria is both an EU and NATO member. The outcome reflects political disillusionment with EU austerity and the war drive, and signals a major Eurosceptic and pro-Russian breakthrough on the EU's eastern flank.

### US measles cases reach 1,792 — second-worst year since 2000

The CDC reports **1,792 confirmed measles cases** across 22 outbreaks as of April 23, 2026, with 93% outbreak-associated. State leaders: South Carolina (667), Utah (408), Texas (176), Florida (129). The weekly growth rate has slowed sharply to ~0.4%, down from ~6% earlier in the year, but 2026 is already the second-worst measles year since 2000, trailing only 2025's full-year total of 2,288 cases. The collapse of childhood vaccination rates in the wake of the Trump administration's restructuring of CDC and HHS continues to threaten US measles elimination status — a designation the country has held since 2000.

### H5N1 acquires cattle-specific NeuGc-binding mutations

Newly identified H5N1 mutations, reported through UNMC's transmission tracking project, allow the virus to bind **NeuGc**, a sugar receptor found in cattle mammary glands but absent in humans and birds. The adaptation helps explain the virus's persistence in dairy herds. The US has now recorded **71 total human A(H5) cases** since February 2024, including one confirmed case of likely mammal-to-human transmission in Texas in early April. The cattle-specific adaptation is not expected to increase human transmissibility, but ongoing viral evolution in mammals remains the key pandemic risk.

### "Cicada" BA.3.2 COVID variant in 31 US states

The heavily mutated BA.3.2 "Cicada" variant has now been detected in **31 US states and 33 countries**, accounting for ~7% of national wastewater samples. CDC projections for the week ending April 25 show ~1,400 new COVID hospitalizations (range 750–3,100); the test-positivity rate as of early April was 1.8%, with COVID declining or likely declining in 37 states. Despite the novel variant's spread, overall COVID burden remains very low — but the heavy mutation load warrants continued immune-evasion monitoring.

### NEJM: Paxlovid fails in vaccinated patients; once-monthly obesity drug succeeds

The *New England Journal of Medicine* published two open-label trials this week showing nirmatrelvir–ritonavir (Paxlovid) did **not** reduce hospitalization or death among vaccinated, higher-risk COVID patients — a significant reassessment that challenges current prescribing guidelines for vaccinated populations. Separately, a Phase 2 trial of once-monthly **maridebart cafraglutide** (a GLP-1 class agent) demonstrated significant weight reduction on a monthly injection schedule — a major convenience advance over current weekly GLP-1 protocols.

### DeepSeek V4 releases as Meta announces 8,000 layoffs

Chinese AI lab **DeepSeek** released its **V4** model this week, claiming state-of-the-art open-source performance in reasoning and agentic tasks — sustaining pressure on US labs to keep flagship open-weight models competitive. The release came as **Meta announced approximately 8,000 job cuts** while simultaneously increasing AI infrastructure investment. Meanwhile, the bipartisan **CHAT Act (SB 1700)** passed its state legislature 31–0 in the Senate and 90–0 in the House, becoming one of the most broadly bipartisan AI safety bills enacted in 2026. The UN's first Independent International Scientific Panel on AI is convening its inaugural in-person summit; Nobel laureate Geoffrey Hinton publicly described AI as *"a fast car with no steering wheel."*

## Arts and culture

### Michael Tilson Thomas (1944–2026)

The most significant cultural loss of the past 24 hours is the death of American conductor **Michael Tilson Thomas** at age 81. Tilson Thomas, a protégé of Leonard Bernstein, served as music director of the San Francisco Symphony from 1995 to 2020 and helped re-establish the Bay Area as a major center of American orchestral life. He was also founder of the New World Symphony in Miami Beach, an academy that trained an entire generation of American musicians.

Tilson Thomas's significance lies less in any single recording than in his lifelong commitment to a *democratic* approach to concert culture. He worked deliberately to widen audiences — addressing them directly from the podium, championing accessible programming, and producing television and online documentaries that introduced classical repertoire to viewers far outside concert-hall demographics. His advocacy for **Charles Ives** in particular — a composer historically marginalized as eccentric — embodied a serious effort to recover the indigenous American tradition in classical music as an artistic patrimony belonging to working people, not merely to the donor class. As Fred Mazelis wrote in his WSWS obituary, Thomas's career exemplified the broader humanist musical culture *"under threat from commercial and political pressures."*

His death comes at a moment when American orchestras and arts institutions are confronting massive funding cuts under the Trump administration's restructuring of the National Endowment for the Arts, the Kennedy Center, and federal cultural programs more broadly — a context in which Tilson Thomas's model of public-facing serious music feels at once more urgent and more precarious.

### Chernobyl at 40 — the cultural reckoning

Beyond the Slavutych vigil (see Top Stories), the 40th anniversary has produced a small but noteworthy wave of cultural projects. **Chornobyl Children International** opened an exhibition in Cork displaying artwork created by children of the affected region — a direct response to the elision of liquidator and survivor experience in the dominant Western narrative of Chernobyl as a tale of Soviet incompetence. The persistent Western dramatization of the disaster — most prominently in HBO's 2019 *Chernobyl* — was almost entirely silent on the **600,000 working-class men** who were drafted from across the USSR and exposed to radiation doses without warning, and on the systematic abandonment of those liquidators by post-Soviet states. The class-inflected truth of the disaster is one that anniversary coverage in 2026 has continued to bury.

### State pageantry and cultural diplomacy

The King Charles state visit (see Top Stories) is itself a cultural event — and one that should be read with skepticism. The British monarchy is being deployed as a soft-power instrument to stabilize an Atlantic alliance fractured by Trump's open hostility to Starmer over the Iran war. The deliberate scheduling around the Semiquincentennial ties American republican mythology to British monarchic ceremony in a way that reflects the deep ideological stagnation of the late-imperial Atlantic order — and the absence of any cultural form within either liberal democracy or the monarchic tradition that can credibly speak to the social crisis tearing both countries apart.
