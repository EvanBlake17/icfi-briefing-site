### 2. EU approves $106 billion Ukraine loan after Hungary drops veto

The 27-nation European Union approved a $106 billion loan package for Ukraine on April 24, covering Kyiv's economic and military needs for two years. The deal had been blocked since late 2025 by Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor Orbán, who twice vetoed earlier workarounds. Orbán's leverage collapsed following his "landslide" defeat in Hungary's April 12 general election and the resumption of Russian oil pipeline deliveries to Hungary and Slovakia — a quiet admission by Brussels that "sanctions" policy is porous where ruling-class interests require. The EU simultaneously announced a new sanctions package against Russia.

The most politically significant detail: the original plan to use frozen Russian sovereign assets as collateral was blocked by Belgium, which holds the bulk of those reserves at Euroclear and feared unlimited legal liability. Instead, the EU will borrow on international bond markets — meaning European workers will service the debt through taxation, not Russian oligarchs through confiscation. This is happening in parallel to sustained austerity programs across member states, particularly on pensions, health care, and housing.

Key facts:
- Package covers two years of Ukrainian economic and military needs, replacing the expired US-led Ukraine Defense Contact Group aid channels
- The bonds will be issued as EU-wide debt, raising the joint-debt ratio at a time of record sovereign borrowing
- Hungary's acquiescence was reportedly secured after Russian oil through the Druzhba pipeline to Hungary and Slovakia resumed operation, halted since January after pipeline damage
- The vote also included expanded export controls on dual-use technology and secondary sanctions against third-country shippers
- No referendum or parliamentary override vote was held in any member state on the new debt commitment

### 3. Trump DOJ indicts Southern Poverty Law Center

The Justice Department under Attorney General Todd Blanche unsealed an 11-count indictment against the Southern Poverty Law Center (SPLC), alleging financial and wire fraud over approximately $3 million the organization paid to informants embedded inside neo-Nazi and white-supremacist groups between 2014 and 2023. The SPLC's annual budget exceeded $100 million across that period, making informant expenditure a small operational item. Blanche told reporters the SPLC was "manufacturing the extremism it purports to oppose by paying sources to stoke racial hatred."

Civil rights organizations denounced the indictment as political. ACLU Executive Director Anthony Romero called it "yet another example of the Trump administration's extreme attempts to silence its critics." Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer offered what the WSWS called "minimal public opposition," consistent with the Democrats' pattern of muted response to administration assaults on civil society.

Key facts:
- Indictment covers 2014–2023 informant payments totaling roughly $3 million to penetrate UKA, Aryan Nations, and related networks
- SPLC founded 1971 in response to Klan violence in the Deep South; has filed landmark civil suits bankrupting several white-supremacist organizations
- Action follows the administration's $2.2 billion funding freeze against Harvard and broader moves against law firms and universities
- Blanche led Trump's personal legal defense before his elevation to attorney general

### 6. IMF slashes 2026 global growth forecast to 3.1%, inflation headed back to 4.4%

The International Monetary Fund's April 2026 World Economic Outlook cut its 2026 global growth projection by 0.2 percentage points to 3.1 percent — a revision driven almost entirely by the US-Iran war and the partial closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Absent the war, the IMF said, it would have revised growth upward by 0.1 points to 3.4 percent. Global headline inflation is now projected at 4.4 percent in 2026, a sharp reversal of the disinflation trend of 2024–25.

The Fund laid out increasingly grim downside scenarios. Under its "adverse" scenario — a sharper energy price spike combined with tightening financial conditions — growth falls to 2.5 percent and inflation rises to 5.4 percent. Under a "severe" scenario, in which supply dislocations extend into 2027 and inflation expectations become unanchored, global growth drops to 2.0 percent and inflation exceeds 6 percent. The IMF's baseline already bakes in a 19 percent rise in energy commodity prices; its assumptions on Middle East hydrocarbon flows remain vulnerable to any further escalation.

Key facts:
- 2026 global growth cut to 3.1% (from 3.3%); 2027 projected at 3.2%
- Baseline inflation at 4.4% in 2026, reversing the post-pandemic disinflation
- Adverse scenario: 2.5% growth, 5.4% inflation
- Severe scenario: 2.0% growth, 6%+ inflation, prolonged hydrocarbon supply dislocation
- "Reference" assumption: 19% rise in energy commodity prices through 2026

### Sudan civil war enters its fourth year

Sudan's war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) entered its fourth year this week. On April 23, the SAF launched coordinated air and ground strikes targeting RSF positions across six states, destroying weapons depots and drone launch sites and announcing full capture of the Muqaja area in Blue Nile region. The SAF now controls northern, central, and eastern states including Khartoum; the RSF retains Darfur, large portions of three Kordofan states, and the Blue Nile border region with Ethiopia. Military analysts describe year three as "a state of stagnation," with neither side achieving decisive victory.

The humanitarian toll is catastrophic. 14 million people have been displaced, 26 million face acute food insecurity, and 33.7 million require humanitarian assistance (ICRC, UNICEF, and Intersos figures). Amnesty International reports that nearly 700 civilians were killed in drone strikes in the first quarter of 2026 alone — weaponized drone technology, much of it supplied by the UAE to the RSF and by Iran, Turkey, and Russia to various factions, has transformed the character of the conflict. Sudan now registers a civilian death roughly every 27 minutes.

### Myanmar junta controls less than half of national territory

Myanmar's ruling Tatmadaw has seen its territorial control collapse to less than half of the country under pressure from coordinated ethnic armed-organization offensives and the People's Defense Forces linked to the National Unity Government. In response, the junta has dramatically escalated its air war: air and drone strikes rose from 134 incidents in year one after the 2021 coup to more than 3,300 in 2025–26 — a roughly 2,400 percent increase. Airstrikes are now the junta's defining tactic against a largely civilian resistance. The international supply chain that provides these drones and aviation fuel remains largely unexamined in Western coverage.

Min Aung Hlaing, the architect of the February 2021 coup, was sworn in as president on April 10, 2026, completing his formal consolidation of power. Reconstruction and aid delivery for populations affected by the March 2025 Sagaing earthquake (7.7 magnitude, 3,655+ dead) continue to be obstructed by the conflict. The Diplomat notes that Myanmar has effectively dropped down the "global crisis hierarchy" even as the death toll mounts, with major Western outlets devoting a fraction of the attention paid to Ukraine or Gaza.

### Germany unveils new military strategy: 460,000 troops, conscription, forward deployment

German Defence Minister Boris Pistorius and Inspector General of the Bundeswehr Carsten Breuer unveiled Germany's first comprehensive military strategy since reunification. The strategy targets a total force of 460,000 troops with a reintroduction of conscription, forward-deployed brigades near Russian borders, and the integration of the civilian economy into what Pistorius called "total defence." As Johannes Stern argues on the WSWS, this represents a qualitative leap in German militarism, financed by hundreds of billions in war credits passed with the support of "all establishment parties" — including the Left Party and the Greens — with no meaningful democratic debate.

The strategy envisions Germany operating as the logistical and command hub of NATO's eastern flank, absorbing enhanced deployments from the Baltic states and Poland under a unified "Regional Plan." Opposition inside the SPD-CDU coalition was described as "minimal." Pistorius framed the rearmament as an answer to "Russian aggression and American uncertainty"; for a generation of German workers, it marks the first open return of conscription and mass mobilization since 1945.

### 200+ immigration judges fired as asylum approval crashes to 7%

More than 200 immigration judges have been fired, forced into retirement, or resigned since Trump took office in January 2025, according to reporting by NPR, CBS News, and Slate. The terminations are concentrated among judges appointed by Democratic presidents or who had issued pro-immigrant decisions. Asylum is now granted in just **7 percent** of cases, down from over 50 percent under Biden. The administration's stated deportation goal is **3,000 people per day — over 1 million per year**.

Unlike the Article III federal courts, the entire immigration-court system sits within the Department of Justice's Executive Office for Immigration Review, making it uniquely vulnerable to executive pressure. NPR's investigation identifies a small, little-known DOJ unit that has been rewriting case law through Board of Immigration Appeals decisions — effectively legislating deportation policy through administrative rulings. Federal Article III courts have blocked or slowed several initiatives, including use of the Alien Enemies Act of 1798 and the attempt to end birthright citizenship, but the immigration bench itself is being reshaped into an instrument of executive deportation policy.

### US measles cases hit 1,748 as 2026 outbreak continues

As of April 16, 2026, the CDC confirms **1,748 measles cases across 33 jurisdictions**, with **94 percent (1,637 cases) outbreak-associated** across 19 declared outbreaks. The case count has nearly doubled from roughly 910 cases in mid-February, reflecting accelerating transmission in under-vaccinated communities. No deaths have been reported so far in 2026, but 2026 is on pace to far exceed the 2019 post-elimination-era record. Several state health departments — notably South Carolina — continue to obscure case locations, citing "privacy" concerns that public-health officials say undercut outbreak response.

### HHS blocks CDC COVID vaccine study showing 50% hospitalization cut

The Department of Health and Human Services, under Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., rejected publication of a CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR) study showing that the 2025–26 COVID vaccine reduced emergency department visits and hospitalizations among healthy adults by roughly **50 percent** this past winter, using the well-established VISION network of nine health systems. CDC Director Jay Bhattacharya reportedly objected to the study's standard "test-negative design." Researchers and former CDC officials told CNN and the Washington Post that suppression of a methodologically standard vaccine effectiveness paper is unprecedented and signals broader erosion of US public-health evidence infrastructure.

### New "Cicada" COVID variant BA.3.2 in 31 US states

The BA.3.2 variant — nicknamed **"Cicada"** for its highly mutated spike protein — has now been detected in at least **33 countries and 31 US states** as of mid-April 2026. Test positivity stands at **1.8 percent** (week ending April 4), and projected new COVID hospitalizations for the week of April 25 are roughly **1,400**. Early laboratory data suggest reduced effectiveness of the current JN.1-targeting vaccines against Cicada, raising the prospect of another reformulation cycle. The variant's emergence during a period when HHS is blocking vaccine-effectiveness studies has intensified criticism of the administration's public-health posture.

### H5N1 acquires mutations enabling cattle infection

Researchers have identified two mutations now commonly found in dairy-cattle H5N1 strains that allow the virus to bind to **N-glycolylneuraminic acid (NeuGc)** — a sugar present in bovine mammary tissue but absent in humans and birds. The finding, reported in a bioRxiv preprint covered by the UNMC Transmission blog, explains the virus's persistence in dairy herds without increasing direct human infection risk. Tracking further mutations remains critical: any drift toward binding human-tissue sugars would represent a significant step toward efficient mammalian transmission.

### UN AI panel urges global guardrails as Amazon-Anthropic deepens to $33 billion+

The inaugural summit of the **UN Independent International Scientific Panel on AI** convened in Geneva, with Nobel laureate Geoffrey Hinton calling for binding international regulation and comparing uncontrolled AI development to "a very fast car with no steering wheel." At the same time, Amazon confirmed a **$5 billion immediate investment in Anthropic** with up to **$20 billion more tied to milestones**, bringing the total commitment to over **$33 billion**; Anthropic has pledged over **$100 billion** in AWS infrastructure spend over 10 years. The simultaneous push for international governance and record-scale private capital deployment captures the contradiction at the core of frontier AI development. Meanwhile, Bryan Dyne reports on the WSWS that the first 14 days of the US-Israel war against Iran (February 28–March 14) produced approximately **5.1 million metric tons of CO₂ equivalent**, with destruction of ~20,000 civilian buildings alone generating 2.4 million metric tons of embodied emissions — the climate dimension absent from all mainstream coverage of the conflict.

## Arts and culture

The day's most significant cultural story is the launch of an organized artists' boycott of the **92nd Street Y** in New York, one of the most influential cultural institutions in the United States. The collective **92NO** called for a full boycott of the institution's literary and cultural programming, citing a cascading set of political-censorship decisions: the October 2023 cancellation of Pulitzer Prize-winning novelist **Viet Thanh Nguyen** after he signed a letter demanding a ceasefire in Gaza; the subsequent firing of staff who displayed Palestinian-solidarity symbols; the hosting of Palantir CEO Alex Karp alongside pro-war columnists Bret Stephens, Thomas Friedman, and Bari Weiss; and most strikingly, the 92NY's new bylaws barring speakers who "question the legitimacy" of the State of Israel. Poetry Center director **Sarah Chihaya** and coordinator **Sophie Herron** have resigned in protest.

The boycott represents a significant rupture inside the US literary institutional order. The 92Y's Unterberg Poetry Center, founded in 1939, has hosted T. S. Eliot, Dylan Thomas, Anne Sexton, W. H. Auden, and every major English-language poet of the postwar era. Its wholesale subordination to a political litmus test on Israel — written into the bylaws — marks a qualitative shift from ad-hoc cancellations to institutional codification of censorship. As Sandy English writes on the WSWS, the development parallels the purging of anti-imperialist voices from universities, newspapers, and foundations across the period of the US-Israel war. The artists involved include figures from the PEN America and National Book Critics Circle networks, suggesting the boycott will harden into a broader professional split.

Other notable developments from the past 24 hours:

- **New York film community mobilizes:** Programmers at several major New York art houses — Anthology, Metrograph, and the Brooklyn Academy of Music's cinema program — joined open letters in solidarity with the 92NO action, extending the effective boycott into film-programming institutions.
- **Publishing industry split over AI licensing:** The Authors Guild and PEN America issued new guidance warning members against signing blanket AI-training rights clauses. The guidance followed reports that three major trade houses were quietly modifying contract boilerplate to include perpetual AI-training licenses without compensation.
- **Museums and restitution:** Several European museums, including institutions in France and Germany, are preparing deaccession actions under EU restitution frameworks targeting artifacts acquired during colonial expeditions. The Humboldt Forum in Berlin remains the focal point of protest around the Benin Bronzes.
- **Theater under pressure:** The Royal Shakespeare Company announced further cuts to its touring program, citing Arts Council England funding reductions. The cuts arrive in parallel with the German federal government's announcement of defense-spending expansions that dwarf the entire UK arts budget.
- **Music industry consolidation:** Universal Music Group's aggressive acquisition of independent catalogs has drawn renewed criticism from musicians' unions in the US and UK, with the American Federation of Musicians warning that streaming economics combined with catalog consolidation are driving working performers below subsistence income.

A consistent thread runs through these developments: the cultural sphere is being reorganized around the priorities of war, finance capital, and political conformity. Works engaging seriously with the Gaza genocide, the Iran war, and rising state authoritarianism face institutional resistance at every level, while nationalist and militarist content — including a spate of new streaming productions celebrating US special operations — finds abundant funding. The WSWS framework treats art not as entertainment content but as a form of social consciousness; the conflicts inside the 92NY, inside publishing, and inside the museum world are not "culture wars" but manifestations of the same crisis that is producing war, austerity, and repression.

### 6. The purge of US immigration courts as structural authoritarianism
