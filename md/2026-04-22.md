### 1. Iran war day 53: ceasefire extended, Vance peace trip scrapped

President Trump extended the US-Iran ceasefire — originally set to expire at 4:50 am PST on April 22 — hours before the deadline, after Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif personally requested additional time. Trump set no new deadline. Vice President JD Vance's planned trip to Islamabad for a second round of peace talks was postponed indefinitely after Iran refused to send a delegation while the US naval blockade on Iranian ports remains in place.

Iran's IRGC-affiliated Tasnim news agency confirmed Tehran had told US mediators through Pakistan there was "no prospect for participation" while the blockade continues. Iran's Foreign Minister called the blockade "an act of war" and a ceasefire violation. The US separately seized an Iranian vessel in the Strait of Hormuz this week — Tehran called it "piracy" — and Iran responded by firing drones at US ships, with no damage reported.

Writing on the WSWS, Patrick Martin noted that Trump's public posture masks strategic weakness: a CSIS analysis cited in the article reports the US military has depleted **45% of its precision-strike missiles and nearly 50% of its Patriot interceptors** in 53 days of war. Nearly two-thirds of Americans oppose the war, and 59% question Trump's nuclear judgment. Democratic commentary has shifted from condemning Trump's war crimes to warning he might exit prematurely — exposing the bipartisan commitment to imperialist objectives against Iran and China.

Key facts:
- Trump threatened: "if they don't [reach a deal], the United States is going to knock out every single Power Plant, and every single Bridge, in Iran"
- International Crisis Group analyst Ali Vaez identified the core impasse: "whether the US will ease pressure to make diplomacy credible and whether Iran will curb leverage to keep talks alive"
- The ceasefire extension contradicted Trump's own social media posts earlier in the week declaring no extension would occur

### 2. Germany summons Russian ambassador as Berlin escalates toward direct war

Germany's Foreign Ministry on Monday summoned Russia's ambassador in a sharp escalation, citing "direct threats by Russia against targets in Germany" intended to weaken Berlin's support for Ukraine. The summons followed Russia's publication of the addresses of German arms manufacturers — itself a response to Berlin's announcement that it would supply Ukraine with drones and long-range strike weapons capable of hitting Russian territory.

The escalation unfolds alongside a dramatic buildup of European nuclear coordination. French President Emmanuel Macron and Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk met in Gdańsk to discuss nuclear cooperation. Tusk declared: "Our cooperation, whether in the nuclear field or in joint maneuvers … is a cooperation that knows no limits." Macron indicated "concrete progress" in nuclear deterrence, including possible "deployments" of French nuclear-armed aircraft to Poland. Chancellor Friedrich Merz signaled German participation in European nuclear initiatives, and voices in German political-military circles are openly advocating acquisition of independent German nuclear weapons.

In today's WSWS Perspective, Johannes Stern wrote: "Nearly 85 years after the invasion of the Soviet Union by the Nazi regime, Germany is once again de facto at war with Russia." Stern frames Ukraine as functioning as "a geostrategic bridgehead of German imperialism — comparable to Israel's role in the Middle East for US imperialism."

Key facts:
- Germany is deploying the 41st Panzergrenadier Brigade and additional forces — **over 5,000 troops total** — to Lithuania as part of what the Merz government calls an "offensive military strategy"
- Foreign Minister Johann Wadephul stated Germany's intent to be "massively expanding the military strength of Europe, with Germany taking the lead"
- Zelensky's recent visit to Berlin produced a "strategic partnership" enabling "long-range strikes deep into Russian territory"
- Conscription — discussed in German political circles — would funnel "hundreds of thousands of young people as cannon fodder" into the conflict

### 3. IMF cuts global growth forecast to 3.1%, warns of recession in severe scenario

The International Monetary Fund's April 2026 World Economic Outlook — titled "Global Economy in the Shadow of War" — cut the global growth forecast to **3.1% for 2026**, down from 3.4% in 2025, citing the Middle East conflict as the primary driver. Headline global inflation was revised up to **4.4%** in the base scenario.

The fund laid out two alarming downside scenarios. An "adverse scenario" projects growth at 2.5% and inflation at 5.4%. A "severe scenario" projects growth at just **2%** — the conventional threshold for a global recession — with inflation exceeding 6%. The IMF cited "rising commodity prices, firmer inflation expectations, and tighter financial conditions" driven by the Iran war, alongside US trade fragmentation from Trump's tariff regime as compounding factors.

The IMF Spring Meetings (April 14) coincided with the ceasefire period but warned that even a short conflict would leave lasting economic scars. The fund emphasized that supply-chain disruptions, rerouted shipping, and energy-price pass-through would ripple through food, transport, and utilities costs for years — falling hardest on the working class and the Global South.

Key facts:
- G20 inflation expected at **4.0%** in 2026, subsiding to 2.7% only by 2027
- Euro-area Q4 2025 GDP came in at +0.2% QoQ / +1.2% YoY — below expectations
- China's official manufacturing PMI remained **below 50** in March (contraction); property and labor-market weakness persist

### 4. IEA calls Hormuz disruption "worst energy crisis ever faced by the world"

The head of the International Energy Agency explicitly labeled the disruption caused by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and attacks on energy infrastructure **the worst energy crisis in the agency's history — surpassing the 1970s oil shocks**. Shipments through the Strait — which carries roughly 20% of the world's oil trade — averaged only 3.8 million barrels per day in early April, compared to more than 20 mb/d in February before the war began.

Brent crude surged more than 55% from pre-war levels, hitting nearly **$120 per barrel** at the height of the conflict. As of April 22, Brent was trading at approximately $98/barrel after a brief dip below $91. Even after the April 7-8 ceasefire announcement, commercial shipping through the Strait remained far below pre-war levels. The IEA's April Oil Market Report flags that any return to open hostilities would send prices back toward or above the prior peak.

Global natural gas markets have also been severely disrupted. Liquefied natural gas tanker rerouting is adding weeks of delivery time and significant cost premiums for European and Asian importers, compounding winter fuel-poverty pressures already gripping working-class households across the OECD.

### 6. Harvard graduate workers launch indefinite strike over wages, ICE, academic freedom

Approximately **4,000 Harvard Graduate Student Union–UAW members** launched an indefinite strike on April 21, halting teaching, grading, and laboratory research across Cambridge and the Medical School. Teaching Fellows earn $18-21/hour — qualifying many for state assistance — against Harvard's **$53.2 billion endowment**. The university is offering only 2.5% annual raises when workers say they need roughly 74% to reach wage parity with peer institutions and local cost of living.

The strike's demands extend well beyond pay: protections for non-citizen workers against ICE detention and deportation, academic freedom safeguards, and resistance to military-intelligence integration in university research. The action follows mass ICE raids on international students at multiple campuses earlier in the spring.

WSWS reporter Mike Ingram noted that UAW presidential candidate Will Lehman has warned that union leadership delayed the strike nearly a year to "contain the struggle," while Harvard separately negotiated with different campus unions to prevent solidarity — a pattern of management dividing university workers to suppress unified action.

### 7. Japan ends ban on lethal weapons exports, preparing for Taiwan war

Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi announced that Japan will permit arms manufacturers to export lethal weapons to 17 treaty partners — including the US, UK, and Australia — reversing the 1967/1976 export ban rooted in Article 9 of Japan's pacifist postwar constitution. Major contractors Mitsubishi Heavy Industries and Kawasaki are positioned to profit, including a reported **$15-20 billion frigate deal with Australia**.

On the WSWS, Mike Head argues the export shift reflects Japan's preparation for a US-led confrontation with China over Taiwan. The move follows Shinzo Abe's 2014 and Fumio Kishida's 2023 partial expansions of the export framework — each stepping stone having paved the way for today's decisive break with the postwar pacifist order.

Head connects the weapons export shift to simultaneous moves toward anti-espionage legislation and emergency powers, framed as preparation for suppressing domestic opposition to war. Takaichi, Japan's first female prime minister and an acolyte of Abe, has pursued the most aggressive rearmament program since 1945.

### 8. IMF and World Bank restore ties with Venezuela's acting president Delcy Rodriguez

On April 17, IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva announced that the Fund is recognizing the government of acting President Delcy Rodriguez — the first woman to lead Venezuela's government — following a majority-weighted vote of IMF member nations. The World Bank immediately followed with its own recognition, restoring ties broken for approximately 20 years.

Rodriguez assumed power after Trump ordered the arrest and extradition of former President Nicolás Maduro to the US, where he faces drug-trafficking and weapons charges. The move could unlock **billions in frozen special drawing rights (SDRs)** and paves the way for Venezuela's first full IMF economic assessment in two decades — a prelude, critics warn, to structural adjustment and privatization.

Key facts:
- Rodriguez, a long-time Chavista and former foreign minister, called it "a great achievement of Venezuelan diplomacy"
- A new central bank president was simultaneously appointed, signaling alignment with Western financial institutions
- The opening sets the stage for IMF conditionalities that will fall on Venezuelan workers already pauperized by years of sanctions and economic crisis

### Benin's Wadagni wins 94.27% in ballot without real opposition

Benin's Constitutional Court confirmed on April 16-17 that Finance Minister Romuald Wadagni, 49, won the April 12 presidential election with **94.27%** of the vote. His only opponent, former culture minister Paul Hounkpè, received 5.73%. Voter turnout was 63.57%. No credible opposition candidates were permitted on the ballot.

The election occurred amid a deteriorating security situation in northern Benin, linked to Sahel jihadist spillover from Burkina Faso and Niger. The result fits a regional pattern of IMF-aligned technocrats cementing power through managed elections across Francophone West Africa.

### 110 protesting coal miners detained after 112-mile march to Ankara

Turkish police detained 110 coal miners for 14 hours in Ankara after they arrived from a nine-day, 112-mile march from Eskişehir. A socialist publication should systematically document the class character of the war's toll.
