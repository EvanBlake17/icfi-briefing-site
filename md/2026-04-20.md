### 1. Trump threatens to destroy Iran's power plants and bridges as Hormuz blockade enters third week

The threat followed the seizure by the USS Spruance of an Iranian-flagged cargo ship in the Gulf of Oman after Iran's Revolutionary Guard re-closed the Strait of Hormuz and fired on two India-flagged tankers. Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Baqer Ghalibaf declared the strait would remain closed so long as Iranian vessels were themselves blocked by the US Navy, and Iran withdrew from the Islamabad negotiating track.

As Andre Damon wrote on the WSWS, Trump's escalation expresses "a bipartisan ruling-class consensus." The Hormuz corridor carries roughly 20 million barrels of oil per day, about 20 percent of global seaborne oil trade. Twenty-five commercial vessels have been turned back under the blockade. Brent crude rebounded to $95 a barrel on the latest seizure, and major shipping companies have suspended operations in the Gulf.

Key facts:

- US intelligence reports China is preparing to deliver air defense systems to Iran within weeks, raising the prospect of a direct Great Power military confrontation in the theatre.
- Documented casualties stand at 2,076–3,636 Iranian dead and 2,167 killed in Lebanon, including 172 children.
- The background: US–Israeli strikes on Iran began February 28, 2026; Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was assassinated; his son Mojtaba Khamenei has been named successor.
- Trump told reporters, "We're offering a very fair and reasonable deal"; Iran's demand for 440 kg of enriched uranium rights and the US port blockade remain the critical sticking points.

### 2. 7.5 earthquake strikes northern Japan, triggering tsunami warning and mass evacuations

A magnitude 7.5 earthquake — initially reported as 7.4 — struck off the coast of Iwate prefecture at 4:53 p.m. local time (07:53 GMT), shaking northeastern Honshu and Hokkaido. The Japan Meteorological Agency issued a three-metre (ten-foot) tsunami warning for Iwate, Hokkaido and Aomori prefectures and ordered the evacuation of 171,957 people across five prefectures. Initial waves of 0.8 metres struck Kuji Port and 0.4 metres hit Miyako Port, with NHK reporting that water levels were still rising at the time of the last broadcast cycle.

Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi activated the government's crisis response team and issued a public appeal: "Please evacuate to higher, safer places such as higher ground." Initial reports recorded some 200 power outages but no confirmed fatalities or major structural damage. The quake epicentre lies within the Japan Trench subduction zone, the same system that generated the 2011 Tōhoku disaster — a fact cited in early Japanese media coverage as officials warned against complacency during the evacuation window.

Key facts:

- Evacuation orders spanned Hokkaido, Aomori, Iwate, Miyagi and Fukushima prefectures.
- Japan's Nuclear Regulation Authority confirmed no abnormalities at Tōhoku Electric's Onagawa plant, although backup monitoring was activated.
- Transportation disruption included suspension of Tōhoku Shinkansen services between Morioka and Shin-Aomori.
- The Pacific Tsunami Warning Center extended monitoring advisories to Russia's Kuril Islands and parts of the Aleutians.

### 3. IMF cuts global growth forecast to 3.1 percent, warns of 2.0 percent "war scenario" near-recession

The International Monetary Fund released its April 2026 *World Economic Outlook* under the headline "Global Economy in the Shadow of War," cutting projected global growth to 3.1 percent for 2026, down from the 3.4 percent projected in January and the 3.4 percent registered in 2025. In its adverse scenario — modelling a prolonged shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz — growth falls to 2.5 percent with global inflation rising to 5.4 percent. In the severe scenario, growth drops to 2.0 percent, a threshold the global economy has crossed only four times since 1980. Baseline global inflation is revised up 0.6 percentage points to 4.4 percent.

The Fund's research finds that emerging-market and developing economies, especially commodity importers, bear the brunt of the adjustment. IMF chief economist Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas stated oil-price normalization depends on "meaningful ship traffic" resuming through Hormuz. The report represents the sharpest downgrade of a spring Outlook since the 2020 pandemic contraction, and arrived as equity markets priced in optimism that has now been reversed overnight by the USS Spruance seizure.

Key facts:

- Advanced-economy growth is cut to 1.6 percent (from 1.9 percent).
- The adverse-scenario inflation figure of 5.4 percent would wipe out most real wage gains registered since 2023.
- Developing-economy sovereign debt servicing costs are projected to consume 21 percent of revenue in 2026, the highest share since the 1980s debt crisis.

### 4. Balikatan 2026 begins: 17,000 troops, Japanese combat forces on Philippine soil for first time since World War II

The 41st iteration of the US–Philippines Balikatan exercise opened in Manila today, the largest ever, with 17,000 troops from seven countries — the US, the Philippines, Japan, Australia, Canada, France and New Zealand — plus 17 observer nations. Running through May 8, the exercise marks the first deployment of Japanese combat-capable units on Philippine soil since 1945; Tokyo has sent 1,400 troops, a decisive step in the remilitarization of Japanese foreign policy under Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi. Ahead of the exercise, the JMSDF destroyer *Inazuma* transited the Taiwan Strait on April 17 — the first such transit under Takaichi's government.

Drills include anti-submarine warfare, coastal defense, maritime security, and ship-to-shore logistics — all capabilities tailored to a Taiwan contingency. China has responded by installing a 1,150-foot floating barrier at Scarborough Shoal on April 10–11 and deploying roughly 100 coast guard and naval vessels in the East and South China Seas, double the normal operational baseline. Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. described Balikatan as a "deterrence posture"; US Indo-Pacific Command characterized the exercise as "full-spectrum readiness" training.

Key facts:

- Japan's combat deployment includes a Type-12 anti-ship missile unit, a direct response to Chinese naval expansion.
- China issued an unusual 40-day airspace restriction notice over the Yellow Sea and East China Sea (March 27 to May 6), without announcing any drills.
- Balikatan 2026 overlaps the final phase of the US blockade against Iran, a convergence WSWS analysts describe as the concretization of a two-front imperialist offensive.

### 5. Sudan enters year four of civil war with 150,000 dead, 19 million facing acute hunger

April 15 marked three years since the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) plunged Sudan into civil war. The country now enters year four with no resolution in sight. More than 150,000 people have been killed, more than 14 million displaced internally and externally — roughly a quarter of Sudan's 52 million population — and an estimated 19 million people face acute hunger. The SAF controls Khartoum and the eastern and central regions; the RSF holds Darfur and parts of Kordofan.

The conflict has become an overt proxy war between regional capitalist states. Yale's Humanitarian Research Lab confirmed via satellite imagery that Ethiopia's military is supporting RSF operations and has allowed cross-border staging from an Ethiopian army base. Egypt is conducting drone strikes on RSF supply convoys near its own border. The United Arab Emirates continues to supply the RSF with weapons through multiple deniable corridors, according to UN expert panel findings cited in recent reporting.

Key facts:

- The US State Department issued fresh sanctions and called for a "humanitarian truce" on the April 15 anniversary.
- Only one of Sudan's 18 national hospitals is operating at full capacity; Médecins Sans Frontières has withdrawn from Darfur for the second time.
- The proxy war character — four separate capitalist states arming one or both sides — is occluded in Western media coverage that frames the conflict as an ethnic feud.

### Mass strikes by North Indian contract workers enter second week

Tens of thousands of unorganized contract workers across India's National Capital Region — a 4.5-million-worker zone spanning 15,000 manufacturing industries around New Delhi — have been on strike since April 10, demanding a uniform monthly minimum wage of Rs. 23,000 ($247) against the Rs. 10,000–15,000 most currently earn for six-day, 10-to-12-hour shifts. Kranti Kumara's reporting for the WSWS links the uprising directly to Persian Gulf supply disruptions and the inflation shock caused by the US–Israeli war against Iran, which has devastated India's import-dependent economy. The Stalinist-led CITU and AITUC confederations, caught off guard by the spontaneity of the movement, have moved to contain rather than extend the strike wave — a stance consistent with their long-term orientation toward Congress-led coalition politics.

### Trump tariffs deliver $1,500 average household blow as CBP launches refund portal

A Yale Budget Lab analysis, corroborated by research from the Tax Foundation and by Fortune's 50-state survey based on Ohio State/Cornell data, finds that the Trump tariff schedule now constitutes the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993. The average US tariff rate exceeds 12 percent, up from roughly 2.5 percent at the January 20, 2025 inauguration. The average additional cost to US households is projected at $1,500 in 2026, with the impact falling most heavily on lower-income households whose consumption patterns are weighted toward imported consumer goods. The Ohio State/Cornell study finds "immediate shocks" in all 50 states, with distinct trade vulnerabilities exposed in each.

US Customs and Border Protection today launched the Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries (CAPE) system, intended to process IEEPA tariff refund requests — a politically significant rollout timed to the one-year anniversary of the major tariff implementation. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent told reporters that gasoline prices could fall to $3 per gallon between June and September if Middle East shipping lanes reopen — a forecast that hinges directly on the reversal of policies the administration is currently defending. Real US GDP is projected to remain 0.10–0.16 percent smaller in the long run as a result of the duties.

### USPS warns of insolvency within 12 months as mail volume collapses by 104 billion pieces

Postmaster General David Steiner warned Congress this week that the United States Postal Service "will be out of cash in less than 12 months." Mail volume has collapsed from a peak of 213 billion pieces annually to 109 billion, representing more than $80 billion in lost revenue across the post-2010 period. The agency has suspended its biweekly employer pension contributions of approximately $200 million in order to preserve operating cash. The magnitude of the liquidity crisis, combined with the $25 billion "Delivering for America" restructuring package and its positioning of USPS as a privatized last-mile contractor for Amazon, confirms the direction of structural privatization.

As Tom Hall and co-authors reported on the WSWS, APWU President Jonathan Smith publicly dismissed the crisis as "fiction," offering management-aligned solutions — higher borrowing limits, accounting adjustments — while endorsing 1.3–1.5 percent annual wage increases that amount to real pay cuts. The roots of the crisis lie in the 1971 Postal Reorganization Act, which converted the Postal Service from a cabinet department into a self-funding corporation and created the competitive pressure that Amazon, FedEx and UPS have exploited.

### US measles cases reach 1,748 for 2026

The CDC's April 17 update records 1,748 confirmed US measles cases across 33 state and territorial jurisdictions, with 94 percent (1,637) linked to 19 separate outbreaks. The 2025 full-year total was 2,288. At current pace, 2026 will surpass that figure well before summer. Outbreak clusters are heavily concentrated in under-vaccinated religious and rural communities across Texas, New Mexico, Oklahoma and Michigan, and public-health officials have flagged specific data-suppression practices in several state health departments that obscure the true outbreak footprint.

### H5N1 passes 71 US human cases as D1.1 genotype spreads through migratory birds

The CDC reports 71 total confirmed US human H5 cases since February 2024 — 64 detected via occupational monitoring and 7 via flu surveillance — with two deaths. All cases have been traced to dairy or poultry exposure; no person-to-person transmission has been observed. A new *Nature Medicine* paper documents rapid expansion of the D1.1 A(H5N1) genotype in wild North American birds through the 2024 migratory season, widening the geographic footprint for spillover into both poultry operations and dairy herds. The D1.1 lineage carries mutations associated with increased mammalian adaptation, according to the authors.

### BA.3.2 "Cicada" variant now detected in 33 countries

The heavily mutated BA.3.2 variant — dubbed "Cicada" — has now been detected in at least 31 US states and 33 countries as of April 13. CDC models project approximately 1,600 US hospital admissions for the week ending April 18, down from ~1,800 the prior week. 33 states show declining activity, 1 growing. BA.3.2's mutation profile improves immune escape from the 2025-2026 vaccine formulation, but current hospitalization data do not warrant alarm, according to CDC spokespeople. The WHO Technical Advisory Group on SARS-CoV-2 Virus Evolution continues to monitor BA.3.2 under variant-of-interest status.

### NEJM publishes Phase 3 results for Eli Lilly's oral GLP-1 orforglipron

*The New England Journal of Medicine* today published Phase 3 results for Eli Lilly's oral GLP-1 agonist orforglipron (ATTAIN-1 trial, n=3,127). At 72 weeks, the 36 mg daily dose achieved a mean body-weight reduction of −11.2 percent versus −2.1 percent for placebo, with 54.6 percent of patients losing at least 10 percent of body weight. *The Lancet* simultaneously published the ATTAIN-2 trial, showing similar efficacy in patients with type 2 diabetes. The scientific significance is that orforglipron is the first oral small-molecule GLP-1 agonist to approximate the efficacy of injectable semaglutide. The class-wide question of pricing, access and the political economy of chronic obesity treatment in a US healthcare system that leaves 29 million uninsured is, as ever, foregrounded — and not by the journals.

### Stanford 2026 AI Index shows US–China gap at 2.7 percent, 89 percent collapse in AI researcher immigration

The Stanford Institute for Human-Centered AI (HAI) released its 2026 AI Index on April 13. Key findings: the performance gap between the top US and Chinese AI models has compressed to just 2.7 percent, down from double digits in 2023; generative AI adoption in the US population has reached 53 percent in three years — faster than personal computers or the internet. AI researcher immigration to the United States has collapsed by 89 percent over seven years, with an 80 percent drop in the past year alone, tied to the new $100,000-per-hire H-1B employer fee. The Foundation Model Transparency Index average score fell from 58 to 40, meaning the most powerful models are becoming *less* open at the precise moment their societal impact is accelerating.

Over 600 state AI bills targeting private entities have been introduced in 2026 US state legislative sessions. Nebraska passed a chatbot-disclosure law, Maryland passed an AI-pricing transparency law, and Maine banned AI from providing psychotherapy unless supervised by a licensed professional.

### *Michael* biopic opens Friday into a debate over the "rehabilitation" of Jackson

Lionsgate's *Michael*, directed by Antoine Fuqua and starring Jaafar Jackson (Michael Jackson's nephew) as the pop singer, opens in US theatres on April 24. The film traces Jackson's career from childhood stardom to global icon. It has prompted significant commentary — with some outlets observing that a theatrical reintroduction of Jackson occurs while civil litigation over child sexual abuse allegations remains active and while the documentary *Leaving Neverland* has shaped a widely shared public reckoning with the singer's legacy. The more serious critical question — which prestige entertainment journalism has largely avoided — is what it means for the culture industry to mount a $150-million celebration of a figure whose treatment by his own estate and record labels was itself an instance of capitalist exploitation of a child performer. The social and political interest here is not reducible to the abuse allegations: it lies in the commodification of Jackson's image itself, and the film will demand extended treatment in arts reviewing.

### *The Pitt* reaches ~12 million US viewers per episode

The HBO Max medical drama *The Pitt* continues to register extraordinary cultural traction. The Season 2 premiere drew nearly 200 percent more viewers than the series launch. The show now averages approximately 12 million US viewers per episode and logged 1.19 billion streaming minutes weekly by mid-January. As Isabel Roy and David Walsh argue in today's WSWS Perspective, this viewership is significant because the show treats the daily working life of an understaffed emergency department with social realism and without identity-politics framings. The Season 2 storyline — set over a single July 4th — includes ICE agents kidnapping a nurse from hospital grounds and a diabetic patient leaving against medical advice because she cannot afford continued care. Treated as a social phenomenon rather than a television product, *The Pitt* demands the kind of extended criticism practiced in the classical Marxist tradition: a serious attempt to read popular culture as a symptom of social contradictions.

### *The Mummy* reboot and *The Super Mario Galaxy Movie* mark another quarter of franchise dominance

Lee Cronin's *The Mummy* opened last Friday as a supernatural-horror reimagining of the Universal franchise; *The Super Mario Galaxy Movie* (Horvath/Jelenic) continues in wide release after opening April 1, buoyed by voice performances from Chris Pratt, Anya Taylor-Joy and Jack Black. Together with *Michael*, these titles dominate the April 2026 theatrical calendar. The pattern — horror reboot, video-game animated spin-off, estate biopic — represents the further consolidation of intellectual-property exploitation as the principal economic logic of American film production. The space for original dramatic production continues to contract in direct proportion to the consolidation of the streaming industry, a development that has material implications for writers, actors and crews still living through the aftermath of the 2023 writers' and actors' strikes.
