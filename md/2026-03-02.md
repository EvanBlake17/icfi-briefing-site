### 1. Iran war Day 3: Hezbollah enters the conflict, RAF Akrotiri struck, three F-15s downed by friendly fire

The US-Israeli war on Iran escalated dramatically on Day 3 as the conflict spread beyond Iran's borders into Lebanon, Cyprus, Iraq, and Kuwait. Hezbollah broke the 15-month ceasefire that had been in place since November 2024, launching what it called "a barrage of precision missiles and a swarm of drones" at a military base near Haifa in northern Israel. Hezbollah framed its attack as "revenge for the blood of the Supreme Leader of the Muslims, Ali Khamenei." Israel responded with what IDF Chief Lt. Gen. Eyal Zamir said would be a multi-day "offensive campaign" against Hezbollah targets in Lebanon, striking Beirut's southern suburbs and killing at least 31 people and wounding 149, according to Lebanon's Health Ministry.

Iran struck the British RAF Akrotiri base in Cyprus with Shahed-type kamikaze drones overnight, marking the first attack on a British military installation since 1986. Two drones hit the runway; no casualties were reported. The UK is evacuating families and non-essential staff. Home Secretary Yvette Cooper confirmed the strike involved "Iranian kamikaze drones," while insisting Britain is "not at war." The attack followed PM Starmer's statement that UK bases could be used for US "defensive strikes" on Iranian missiles.

In a separate incident, three US F-15E Strike Eagles were shot down by Kuwaiti air defense in a friendly fire incident while flying support for Operation Epic Fury during an Iranian missile and drone barrage. All six aircrew ejected safely. The Pentagon separately confirmed three US service members killed and five seriously wounded in an earlier Iranian missile strike on a Kuwait base. Iraq's Iran-backed militias also entered the conflict, firing on a US base in Erbil and claiming a drone attack on Baghdad airport.

Trump told the New York Times the operation would last "four to five weeks," adding: "Unfortunately there will be more casualties. That's how it is." Netanyahu said Israel "will strike thousands of targets in the coming days." Iran reported strikes across 24 of its 31 provinces. Iran's ambassador to the IAEA confirmed the Natanz nuclear facility was struck on March 1; a monument to Islamic Republic founder Khomeini was toppled by crowds in southern Iran the same day.

Key facts:
- **Lebanon:** 31 dead, 149 injured in Beirut (Lebanon Health Ministry); IDF offensive expected to last "several days"
- **RAF Akrotiri:** First attack on British military base since 1986; two Shahed drones hit runway; no casualties
- **Friendly fire:** Three US F-15Es shot down by Kuwaiti air defense; six aircrew safe
- **US casualties:** Three US service members killed, five seriously wounded in separate Iranian missile strike
- **Iran:** 24 of 31 provinces struck; Natanz nuclear facility confirmed hit
- **Trump timeline:** "Four to five weeks" for the operation (NYT interview)

### 2. IAEA emergency session: Natanz nuclear strike risks radiological catastrophe

The IAEA Board of Governors convened an emergency special session on March 2 after Iran's ambassador Reza Najafi confirmed that Natanz — Iran's primary uranium enrichment site with deep underground centrifuge halls — was struck on March 1. IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi stated the agency currently has "no indication" of structural damage to nuclear installations and radiation levels remain at normal background in neighboring countries.

However, Grossi issued an explicit warning: if attacks on nuclear sites continue, a radiological release "cannot be excluded," and the fallout could require evacuations of areas "as large or larger than major cities." Iran's ambassador condemned the strikes as deliberate attacks on facilities under IAEA safeguards, constituting war crimes under international law. The strike on Natanz represents one of the most serious nuclear security crises since Chernobyl in terms of potential risk — the deliberate military targeting of active nuclear facilities under international safeguards is virtually unprecedented.

The irony is glaring: Operation Epic Fury was ostensibly launched to eliminate Iran's nuclear "threat," yet the bombing of nuclear facilities creates precisely the radiological catastrophe it claimed to prevent. As the SEP National Committee statement noted, Trump himself had previously declared Iran's nuclear capacity "obliterated" following June 2025 strikes, while Defense Intelligence Agency assessments showed Iran was "years, if not a decade" from intercontinental missile capability.

Key facts:
- **Natanz struck March 1:** Iran's primary enrichment facility, housing deep underground centrifuge halls
- **Radiation levels:** Currently normal in neighboring countries; no confirmed structural damage
- **Grossi warning:** Radiological release "cannot be excluded"; evacuations of "major city" scale areas possible
- **Iran position:** Strikes on safeguarded facilities constitute war crimes

### 4. Congress challenges Iran war powers with bipartisan resolutions

Operation Epic Fury was launched without congressional authorization. The White House notified top lawmakers shortly before strikes began but did not seek approval. Bipartisan war powers resolutions have now been introduced in both chambers: Senator Tim Kaine (D-VA) and Senator Rand Paul (R-KY) introduced a Senate resolution requiring explicit congressional authorization before further hostilities; Representatives Thomas Massie (R-KY) and Ro Khanna (D-CA) introduced a parallel House resolution to compel a halt to unauthorized military action.

Public opinion is strongly against the war. A University of Maryland poll found only 21% favor the attacks, with 49% opposed. A CBS/YouGov poll found 74% said Trump needed congressional approval. Rep. Jim Himes (D-CT) said: "We have not heard articulated a single good reason for why now is the moment to launch yet another war in the Middle East." However, most Republicans continue to back Trump. Senator Tom Cotton called the operation "ending 47 years of terror"; Senator Ted Cruz called it "the single most important decision of his presidency."

Even if the resolutions pass, Trump would veto, and a two-thirds override majority does not exist. The War Powers Act of 1973 was designed precisely to prevent unauthorized military campaigns, yet it has been routinely circumvented by presidents of both parties for decades — a pattern the SEP statement characterized as exposing the "constitutional order's collapse under imperialist war-making."

Key facts:
- **Senate:** Kaine/Paul resolution requiring congressional authorization
- **House:** Massie/Khanna resolution to halt unauthorized hostilities
- **Polls:** 21% favor attacks (UMD); 74% say Trump needed approval (CBS/YouGov)
- **Veto reality:** Even if passed, Trump would veto; two-thirds majority unavailable

### 5. Iran inside: Khomeini monument toppled as protests collide with US-Israeli bombardment

The pre-existing 2025-2026 Iranian protest movement — the largest since the 1979 revolution, sparked by economic collapse in December 2025 — is intersecting with the US-Israeli bombardment in politically contradictory ways. HRANA (the Human Rights Activists' News Agency) estimates 7,015 deaths in the protests as of February 5; the Iranian government acknowledges 3,117. On March 1, a crowd in southern Iran toppled a monument to Khomeini, the Islamic Republic's founder — a symbolically devastating act during the regime's moment of maximum vulnerability.

The internal dynamics are complex. Many anti-regime Iranians refuse to celebrate the US strikes, recognizing that imperialist intervention serves Washington's strategic interests, not the Iranian people's democratic aspirations. Meanwhile, the foreign bombardment may generate nationalist rallying around the regime among segments of the population that might otherwise oppose it. Outside Iran, diaspora protests — including celebrations of Khamenei's death in Los Angeles — are politically driven primarily by monarchist and pro-regime-change forces aligned with exiled crown prince Reza Pahlavi, who has intensified his international political activity.

Trump reportedly offered three options for new Iranian leadership, signaling the regime-change dimension of the operation that the administration publicly denies. Iranian artists continue to participate in cultural resistance anonymously through encrypted networks.

Key facts:
- **Protest toll:** HRANA estimates 7,015 killed as of Feb. 5; government says 3,117
- **March 1:** Khomeini monument toppled in southern Iran
- **Diaspora:** Celebrations in LA and other cities, driven primarily by monarchist forces
- **Reza Pahlavi:** Exiled crown prince increasing international political activity

### E3 leaders (UK, France, Germany) warn Iran of military response

Britain, France, and Germany issued a joint E3 statement on March 1 warning Iran that "indiscriminate and disproportionate" missile attacks would be met with military defense: "We will take steps to defend our interests and those of our allies in the region." The statement effectively aligned the three largest European NATO powers behind the US-Israeli operation without formally joining it.

Germany's new Chancellor Friedrich Merz went further, declaring "the days of the Iranian regime are numbered" — the most bellicose statement from a German leader on the conflict. Merz explicitly endorsed the "illegal military assault" and said Germany would not "lecture" allies, despite acknowledging that international law "has relatively little effect." The WSWS characterized this as a qualitative shift in German foreign policy, connecting the Iran war, Gaza, and Ukraine as expressions of imperialist powers' strategy to maintain dominance through force.

Starmer maintained the fiction that Britain is "not at war," even after Iranian drones struck RAF Akrotiri. The UK position — allowing US offensive operations from British bases while claiming non-belligerent status — mirrors the legal gymnastics used during prior US wars.

### Natanz nuclear strike and radiological risk

As detailed in Top Stories above, the Natanz strike confirmed by Iran's IAEA ambassador triggered an emergency Board of Governors session on March 2. From a technical standpoint, the risks are severe. Natanz's underground centrifuge halls contain cascades of spinning centrifuges enriching uranium hexafluoride (UF6) — a compound that, if released by structural damage, reacts with moisture to produce hydrofluoric acid and uranyl fluoride, both acutely toxic. A breach of containment would not produce a nuclear explosion but could create a chemical-radiological contamination zone requiring mass evacuation.

The IAEA's monitoring capacity is itself under threat: inspector access depends on physical presence at facilities now under active bombardment. If IAEA personnel are evacuated — a real possibility — the international community loses its only independent verification of what is happening inside Iran's nuclear infrastructure. The precedent is dangerous: no state has deliberately targeted active, safeguarded nuclear facilities on this scale since Israel's 1981 strike on Iraq's Osirak reactor, which was still under construction and contained no enriched material.

### Frederick Wiseman, chronicler of American institutional life, dies at 96

Frederick Wiseman, whose 45 observational documentaries over six decades constituted the most sustained cinematic examination of American institutional life ever undertaken, died at 96 at his Cambridge, Massachusetts home. Working without narration, formal interviews, or musical scores, Wiseman turned his camera on the institutions that constitute the actual fabric of social life: schools ("High School," 1968), hospitals ("Hospital," 1970; "Near Death," 1989), welfare offices ("Welfare," 1975), courts ("Juvenile Court," 1973), police departments ("Law and Order," 1969), and public libraries ("Ex Libris," 2017).

His 1967 debut "Titicut Follies" — exposing conditions at Bridgewater State Hospital for the criminally insane in Massachusetts — was banned from public screening for 25 years, a nearly unprecedented act of government censorship of an American film. He received an Honorary Academy Award in 2016. His death coincides with the destruction of the very public institutions he spent his life documenting — libraries defunded, hospitals privatized, schools stripped of resources — under the regime now launching war abroad.

### This week in history: March 2-8

**Fanglin fireworks explosion (25 years ago, March 6, 2001).** An explosion at a Jiangxi province elementary school in China killed 50 children aged 9-11 and 4 adults who had been assembling fireworks as unpaid sweatshop labor. The tragedy exposed a systemic practice: approximately 93% of Chinese schools operated commercial enterprises to address budget shortfalls caused by the restoration of capitalism. The school's children — some as young as nine — assembled explosive fireworks for a local manufacturer, their labor treated as a subsidy for an educational system hollowed out by market reforms. PM Zhu Rongji initially denied the children were making fireworks, blaming a "deranged man," before evidence became overwhelming.

**Vitoria Massacre (50 years ago, March 3, 1976).** Spanish Armed Police fired tear gas and live ammunition on workers peacefully assembling at a church in Vitoria-Gasteiz during the post-Franco transition. Five workers were killed and over 150 wounded. The massacre triggered solidarity strikes involving more than 500,000 workers across Spain — one of the largest strike waves in modern European history. The events demonstrated both the revolutionary potential of the Spanish working class and the role of the Stalinist and social-democratic leaderships in channeling working-class anger into the bourgeois "democratic transition."

**Assassination of Iranian Prime Minister (75 years ago, March 7, 1951).** PM Haj Ali Razmara was assassinated by Khalil Tahmassebi, an Islamic fundamentalist, removing the last parliamentary obstacle to oil nationalization. Iran's parliament unanimously voted to nationalize the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company — the act that led directly to the 1953 CIA-backed coup overthrowing Mossadegh. This history is acutely relevant: the current US war on Iran is the latest chapter in 75 years of imperial intervention that began with the attempt to maintain control over Iranian oil.

**Germany and the League of Nations (100 years ago, March 2, 1926).** Chancellor Hans Luther demanded a permanent Council seat for Germany, warning of non-membership if competing nations received equal status. German imperialism was reclaiming Great Power status barely eight years after its defeat in World War I — a trajectory that within seven years would lead to the Nazi seizure of power.
