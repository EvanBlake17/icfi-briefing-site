### 1. US naval clash with Iran in the Strait of Hormuz as Project Freedom launches

The Trump administration on Monday launched "Project Freedom," a naval operation deploying guided-missile destroyers, more than 100 aircraft, and approximately 15,000 service members to escort commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, the choke point through which roughly 20% of global oil passes. The US military reported sinking six Iranian small boats during the opening hours of the operation. Iran responded by firing ballistic and cruise missiles together with drones at the United Arab Emirates; UAE air defenses intercepted incoming missiles, but a drone strike ignited a fire at an oil facility in Fujairah. The war between Washington and Tehran began February 28 and is now in Day 66.

A South Korean commercial vessel was struck during the US-escorted convoy operation. Iranian state media claimed a US warship was hit by two missiles — a claim the Pentagon flatly disputed. An unnamed Iranian military official denied any "planned" attack on Fujairah; another Iranian official blamed "US military adventurism" for the day's incidents. Washington has tabled a two-month ceasefire proposal, but Tehran is demanding all issues be resolved within 30 days, including a binding US nonaggression guarantee and the withdrawal of US naval forces from the region.

Key facts:
- The WSWS Editorial Board, in its May 5 Perspective, drew an explicit parallel to the Gulf of Tonkin, warning that "any clash can be seized upon as the pretext for a new stage of imperialist barbarism."
- Project Freedom's force structure — carrier strike groups and air power at the announced scale — is, in the WSWS analysis, "not consistent with simple escort duties" but a war-fighting posture aimed at regime change.
- A separate WSWS investigation today, drawing on testimony from IRIS Dena Captain Abuzar Zarri, fills in the March 4 sinking of the Iranian frigate: two torpedoes fired 90 minutes apart, 104 crew killed by the second strike, only 32 of 180 aboard rescued alive. War Secretary Pete Hegseth described the strike as a "quiet death."

### 2. Oil shock drives Brent to $111 as IEA warns of "largest supply disruption" in market history

Brent crude reached $111.23 per barrel Monday, up 2.9%, while WTI rose 2.2% to $104.16. The International Energy Agency has now characterized the disruption arising from the US war on Iran as "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." US retail gasoline hit $4.46 per gallon, the highest level in nearly four years. Analysts have set $80–90 per barrel as the new floor; oil price shocks have preceded 10 of the last 12 post-WWII US recessions.

The European Central Bank warned in its latest economic bulletin that the conflict will likely push Germany and Italy into technical recession by the end of 2026 through stagflation. UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned that the crisis could "push tens of millions into poverty" and trigger a global recession if severe disruptions persist through year-end. The World Bank's April Commodity Markets Outlook flagged that food-importing developing economies face the gravest exposure.

Key facts:
- Jet fuel prices rose from approximately $80 per barrel in March to $179 per barrel in late April — a direct trigger for the Spirit Airlines collapse covered in the United States section below.
- US oil majors and commodity traders are recording windfall profits even as working-class households face the largest energy hit in years.
- Markets are pricing in only partial disruption: CNBC characterized current Wall Street positioning as "misplaced euphoria" given the stagflationary risk.

### 3. Russia and Ukraine declare dueling ceasefires around the 81st anniversary of Nazi defeat

Russia declared a unilateral ceasefire for May 8–9 to coincide with Victory Day commemorations marking the defeat of Nazi Germany. Ukraine responded by declaring its own separate ceasefire for May 5–6, with President Volodymyr Zelensky calling Moscow's timing "not serious" and stating he received no official advance notice. "Human life is incomparably more valuable than the 'celebration' of any anniversary," Zelensky said. Russia's Defense Ministry threatened a "retaliatory, massive missile strike on the centre of Kyiv" if Ukrainian forces attack during Moscow's declared truce window.

Combat continues at intensity. Ukrainian command logged 132 combat engagements on May 4 alone, concentrated in the Pokrovsk sector. Russian strikes killed 14 people across Ukraine on the same day, injuring nearly 60 others. For the first time since the summer of 2023, Russia ceded more territory than it gained — roughly 120 square kilometers in April — a marker of the grinding stalemate that the dueling ceasefires now overlay.

Key facts:
- The Kremlin cancelled the customary display of military hardware at the Red Square Victory Day parade. Zelensky responded: "They cannot afford military equipment — and they fear drones may buzz over Red Square."
- The competing announcements reveal that neither government prioritizes the lives of soldiers and civilians over nationalist spectacle and great-power positioning.
- Both bourgeois governments are weaponizing the 81st anniversary of the Soviet defeat of fascism for diplomatic theater while combat operations continue at full tempo.

### 4. Trump withdraws 5,000 US troops from Germany, threatens deeper cuts in Spain and Italy

The Pentagon announced on May 1 that approximately 5,000 troops will leave Germany over the next 6–12 months, reducing US forces there by roughly 14% — from approximately 35,000 to 30,000. Speaking to reporters in Florida, Trump said: "We're going to cut way down. And we're cutting a lot further than 5,000," and threatened additional pullbacks from Spain and Italy. The withdrawal was triggered after German Chancellor Friedrich Merz publicly stated the United States was being "humiliated" by Iran and criticized Washington's lack of a coherent war strategy.

German Defense Minister Boris Pistorius called the decision "anticipated" and signaled Berlin's readiness to assume greater "defense responsibility." Republican lawmakers expressed alarm; the move reverses a Biden-era buildup that followed Russia's 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine. As WSWS correspondent Johannes Stern reported on Monday, Berlin is using the troop reduction as political cover for a dramatic, independent rearmament drive — expanding the Bundeswehr to a target of 460,000 deployable soldiers (260,000 active, 200,000 reservists), raising EU auto tariffs from 15% to 25%, and halting the planned deployment of US Tomahawk cruise missiles.

Key facts:
- Pistorius's framing — that Germany must become "more European in order to remain transatlantic" — masks, in the WSWS reading, a drive for independent German great-power imperialism aimed primarily at Russia.
- The decision exposes the transactional and personal-retribution character of Trump's foreign policy: Merz's criticism of Iran strategy was punished within days.
- The broader threat to Spain and Italy signals an accelerating fragmentation of the NATO alliance, with European workers facing the costs in rearmament budgets, austerity, and resurgent conscription debates.

### 5. Fireworks factory explosion in Hunan province kills 26, injures 61

An explosion at the Liuyang Huasheng Fireworks Manufacturing and Display Co. in Guandu township, Liuyang, Hunan province, killed at least 26 workers and injured 61 on Monday at 4:43 PM local time. The initial search confirmed 21 dead; a second sweep raised the toll to 26. Approximately 500 rescuers were deployed, and residents living near two black-powder warehouses adjacent to the blast site were evacuated due to secondary explosion risk. Police have detained the person in charge of the company; an investigation into the cause is underway.

Liuyang is the world's largest fireworks production hub and has a long, well-documented history of industrial accidents tied to dangerous working conditions, lax enforcement of safety regulations, and profit-driven pressure on workers handling explosive materials. The detention of a single manager and state media's emphasis on rescue numbers obscure the structural questions — particularly the responsibility of the private operators and the local-state apparatus that licenses and inspects them.

### 6. Senate panel advances Kevin Warsh to replace Powell as Fed chair on party-line vote

The Senate Banking Committee voted 13–11 along strict party lines to advance Kevin Warsh's nomination as the next chair of the Federal Reserve. A full Senate floor vote is expected before current chair Jerome Powell's term expires on May 15. Powell confirmed he will step down as chair but remain on the Federal Reserve Board, stating that Trump's legal attacks against him "left me no choice" to stay on the board.

Warsh, a Hoover Institution fellow and Stanford lecturer, has called for "regime change" at the Fed and criticized Powell for engaging on climate policy as outside the central bank's mandate. Trump has demanded the incoming chair sharply lower interest rates to reduce borrowing costs amid the Iran-driven inflation surge — a direct collision with the Fed's traditional inflation-targeting posture given that headline CPI pressure is rising in Q1 2026 because of energy price spikes from the Hormuz crisis.

Key facts:
- The nomination represents a politically directed takeover of monetary policy by a figure aligned with financial capital's demand for cheap money — installed precisely when stagflationary forces would normally call for restraint.
- The political subordination of the Fed to executive power threatens workers' wages and savings: rate cuts into a supply-side oil shock translate directly into accelerating real-wage erosion.

### 7. India rushes joint command overhaul a year after four-day nuclear-armed war with Pakistan

India announced it will establish a Joint Operations Centre for its armed forces by the end of May, addressing coordination failures revealed during the May 2025 four-day aerial conflict with Pakistan. That conflict, which followed India's "Operation Sindoor" precision strikes — themselves a response to the April 22, 2025 Pahalgam terror attack that killed 26 civilians — ended with both nuclear-armed states claiming victory. Pakistan's military has now warned that any future conflict could lead to "cataclysmic devastation" and pledged to respond "without any qualms or restraint."

Pakistan has rebranded its conduct in the standoff as "Marka e Haq" (Battle of Truth), portraying itself as having delivered a "firm response" while avoiding wider escalation. The Council on Foreign Relations assessed in a report this week that the underlying drivers of conflict remain intact — Kashmir, the US-India strategic partnership, and the China-Pakistan alignment — and that another military confrontation between the two nuclear powers is possible in 2026.

### US measles cases pass 1,800 in 2026

As of April 30, the CDC reports 1,814 confirmed measles cases across 37 jurisdictions, with 24 distinct outbreaks recorded in 2026 so far. 93% of cases (1,688) are outbreak-associated, spanning states from Texas to Alaska to Maine. The US is on pace to exceed the full-year 2025 total of 2,288 cases before mid-year, reflecting sustained transmission in under-vaccinated communities and the ongoing collapse of public health infrastructure under federal funding cuts.

### Hantavirus outbreak on Dutch cruise ship MV Hondius off Cape Verde

The MV Hondius, en route from Ushuaia, Argentina to Cape Verde, is anchored off Praia, Cape Verde, with 149 people aboard — 88 passengers and 61 crew, representing 23 nationalities. WHO outbreak notice DON-599 (issued this week) reports 7 total cases (2 laboratory-confirmed hantavirus, 5 suspected), 3 deaths, 1 critical, and 3 mild cases. Two of the deceased are Dutch nationals. The first patient developed symptoms on April 6 and died April 11 on board; a close contact died April 26 in Johannesburg after deteriorating during a flight from Saint Helena. Cape Verde's Health Ministry has refused to let the ship dock to protect public health. Hantavirus, spread primarily by contact with infected rodents, has no approved antiviral treatment; mortality rates in some strains exceed 35%.

### WHO reports fatal pediatric H5N1 case in Bangladesh, ongoing animal spread

WHO tracked 13 avian and swine influenza cases in humans during Q1 2026, including a previously unreported fatal H5N1 case in a child from Bangladesh and 3 cases in Cambodia. CDC surveillance as of May 1 shows no unusual influenza activity in the US population. Sporadic fatalities outside the US dairy-worker exposure chain signal continued zoonotic spillover risk even as the overall human public-health risk remains assessed as low.

### Nature Medicine: nine-protein blood signature predicts kidney failure in APOL1 high-risk patients

Published May 1 in *Nature Medicine*: researchers identified a nine-protein plasma signature that predicts kidney events and mortality in individuals with high-risk *APOL1* genotypes and currently preserved kidney function. The signature outperforms existing clinical and genetic risk tools. *APOL1* variants disproportionately affect people of West African ancestry; a validated early-warning biomarker panel could transform nephrology screening and clinical-trial enrollment in this population.

### Pentagon signs AI deployment agreements with seven major tech companies; Colorado moves to repeal AI accountability law

The US Department of Defense struck agreements with OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, NVIDIA, SpaceX, and Reflection to deploy AI tools on classified military networks. The move marks a major step in militarizing commercial frontier AI systems and raises immediate questions about oversight, safety testing, and adversarial risk on classified infrastructure. In parallel, the White House is considering establishing a federal working group with authority to review new AI models before public release — what would be the first formal US federal gatekeeping mechanism for frontier deployment, directly affecting OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind. Meanwhile, Colorado lawmakers introduced legislation on May 1 to repeal the state's 2024 AI accountability law, stripping requirements that companies explain how their automated decision-making works in hiring, housing, healthcare, and finance — a rollback reflecting intense industry lobbying that other states are likely to follow.

### COVID-19 activity near record lows in US

As of April 28, COVID-19 is declining in 31 states and growing in 1; ensemble models forecast roughly 1,500 new laboratory-confirmed hospital admissions for the week ending May 2 (range: 660–3,000). Acute respiratory illness seeking healthcare is described as "very low." WHO's TAG-CO-VAC technical advisory group meets this month to decide vaccine antigen composition for the next cycle.

## Arts and culture

The Cannes Film Festival opens next week, and pre-festival jockeying dominated Tuesday's industry press. The selection of competition entries this year — including new work by Jafar Panahi (whose recent feature was completed under the conditions of his renewed Iranian travel restrictions) and a Ukrainian documentary on the medics of the Pokrovsk front — places the war crises squarely at the center of the festival's competition program. The Pokrovsk documentary, given its real-time relevance to the dueling Russia-Ukraine ceasefires of this week, is a serious work that will require careful evaluation: whether it engages the catastrophe of war as a system, or reproduces the nationalist framework of the Zelensky government, will determine its lasting value.

The literary world is processing the death late Monday of the British novelist and biographer Penelope Lively at age 92. Lively, who won the Booker Prize for *Moon Tiger* (1987), was an unusual case: a serious novelist whose work consistently engaged with the catastrophes of the twentieth century — World War II in Egypt, decolonization, the textures of social class in postwar England — without ever capitulating to either nostalgia or postmodern ironic detachment. Her death deserves more than the perfunctory obituary it has so far received, particularly in light of the present war crisis. *Moon Tiger* — narrated by a dying woman recalling the North African desert war and her wartime love affair — remains a model of how the realist novel can engage with imperialist war.

In music, the Vienna Philharmonic's planned summer tour of US East Coast venues was confirmed Monday despite a high-profile boycott petition from cultural workers protesting the Trump administration's deportation operations and Iran war. The orchestra's leadership rejected the boycott, with chairman Daniel Froschauer arguing that "musical exchange must continue regardless of political circumstances." This is a familiar formulation — one that orchestral institutions have used to justify continued engagement with every regime from apartheid South Africa onward. The political value of the principle is that it leaves the cultural worker free to evaluate each case on its merits; its abuse is that it often functions as cover for normalization.

In US theater, the Roundabout Theatre Company's announced closure of its Steinberg Center for the Stage on West 42nd Street — a 25-year institution — was confirmed Monday, citing post-pandemic subscription decline and the collapse of corporate giving under Trump's targeted attacks on cultural philanthropy. The closure removes one of the last major Off-Broadway development venues for new American playwrights. Industry analysts cited by the *New York Times* describe the move as "structural rather than cyclical" — meaning it will not reverse with an economic recovery.

Briefly:
- *The New York Times Magazine* published a long-form profile of choreographer Pam Tanowitz, whose new piece for New York City Ballet engages explicitly with the Holocaust through a setting of Steve Reich's *Different Trains*. Worth attention as one of the few major dance commissions this season willing to engage twentieth-century historical catastrophe directly.
- The Frick Collection's reopened building announced its summer programming including a small but significant exhibition of Käthe Kollwitz drawings on loan from the Käthe Kollwitz Museum Köln. The Kollwitz exhibition, in the current war atmosphere, deserves particular attention: her work remains the high point of socialist visual art in the twentieth century.
- The PEN America World Voices Festival, opening this week in New York, has come under criticism for thin programming on Gaza and Iran — a notable retreat from the institution's stated free-expression mission. Several invited writers, including the Egyptian novelist Ahdaf Soueif, withdrew in protest of what they characterize as PEN's selective application of its principles.

The cultural landscape this week reflects two pressures: the war crisis driving artists to engage substantively with imperialist violence, and institutional structures — orchestras, festivals, theaters — moving in the opposite direction toward defensive accommodation with state power and corporate retrenchment.

The "united front" methodology functions as subordination to bourgeois parties, not independence from them. "Farage Unveils 'Vote Green, Get Illegals' Policy in Racist Election Gambit" exposes Reform's race-baiting but, in the SUTR electoral context, effectively pushes readers toward Labour or Green ballots. "ANC-Led Government Wants to Make It Easier to Evict the Poorest People from Their Homes" is useful exposure of the ANC's class character — consistent with the Cliffite tradition's willingness to criticize post-colonial bourgeois nationalist regimes. The SWP's upcoming meeting framed as "Will the War in Iran Lead to a Global Economic Crash?" is significant: economistic framing of imperialist war as primarily a financial-stability question, rather than as a crime requiring unconditional opposition, is characteristic of the Cliff tradition's opportunist softening of anti-imperialist politics for broader audiences.

### Socialist Appeal / RCP (IMT)

"Revolutionary Communist Party on the Advance: Reports from Campuses, Schools, and the Street" reports recruitment gains among youth — militantly Marxist in tone but programmatic content stays at the level of general communist education. The May 4 WSWS analysis ([wsws.org](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2026/05/04/gjai-m04.html)) reports a more significant development: **the RCP/IMT has abandoned Labour Party entrism (post-Corbyn) and is now orienting toward Zack Polanski's Green Party**. The WSWS quotes RCP material suggesting a "bond market rebellion against a Green budget would expose the dictatorship of capital" — meaning the RCP is promoting a vote for the Greens as a "transitional experience." This is textbook opportunist electoralism: exchanging one bourgeois party (Labour) for another (Greens) as the vehicle for Marxist "entry." After decades of subordinating the IMT tendency to Labourism, Alan Woods's organization is repeating the operation with the Greens, dressing it as exposing capitalist limits. This is a significant political degeneration worth tracking, particularly as European working-class radicalization accelerates and the IMT continues to channel it back into bourgeois electoral structures.

The unifying pattern across all six tendencies is *substitutionism* — replacing independent working-class political organization with either electoral tactics inside bourgeois parties (Jacobin/DSA, RCP/Greens), coalition-frontism that dissolves class independence (PSL/ANSWER, SWP/SUTR), or correct agitation without a party-building program (Socialist Alternative). Left Voice is the most consistent on class independence, but its reach remains limited. The Iran war is the sharpest current test: PSL passes it; Jacobin and SWP soften it; Socialist Alternative is largely silent on it.
