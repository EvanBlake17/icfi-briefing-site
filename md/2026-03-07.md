### 1. US-Israel war on Iran enters day eight: death toll surpasses 1,332

The US-Israeli bombing campaign against Iran entered its eighth day on Saturday with no sign of de-escalation. The Iranian Red Crescent documented at least 1,332 killed, over 6,500 wounded, and more than 3,600 civilian sites damaged in Iran since strikes began on February 28. An additional 217 have been killed in Lebanon and 11 in Israel, with 6 US service members killed in the conflict.

US CENTCOM reported it has struck over 3,000 targets across Iran and destroyed 43 Iranian warships since the launch of "Operation Epic Fury." Israeli Army Chief Eyal Zamir claimed Israel has destroyed "80% of Iran's air defenses" and "at least 60%" of Iran's missile launch capability. Trump declared on social media: "There will be no deal with Iran except UNCONDITIONAL SURRENDER!" Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi rejected ceasefire talks outright and, when asked about the prospect of a US ground invasion, responded: "No, we are waiting for them."

Key facts:
- Iran is operating under near-total internet blackout (1% connectivity); the rial has collapsed to 1,660,000 per dollar
- An Israeli strike on a girls' school in Minab killed 165+ children
- Israel has simultaneously launched a major Lebanon offensive, ordering evacuation of entire Beirut districts, while Gaza remains under a total aid blockade since March 1
- Hezbollah entered the conflict on March 2; Iran-backed militias attacked a hotel in Irbil, Iraq
- UAE intercepted 109 drones and 9 ballistic missiles; Qatar intercepted 9 of 10 Iranian drones on Friday
- Trump met privately with executives from seven major defense contractors — Lockheed Martin, Boeing, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, BAE Systems, L3Harris, and Honeywell — with Lockheed agreeing to quadruple munitions production

As Andre Damon noted on the WSWS, the war's cost has already reached approximately $3.7 billion in its opening days, while Trump simultaneously proposes a $1.5 trillion military budget — a 50% increase.

### 2. Khamenei assassination triggers succession crisis

The assassination of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in Israeli airstrikes on February 28 — confirmed by the Iranian Supreme National Security Council on March 1 — has opened a succession crisis at the apex of the Iranian state during wartime. An Interim Leadership Council took power under Article 111 of the Iranian constitution, comprising Chief Justice Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Eje'i, Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, and President Masoud Pezeshkian.

IRGC commanders are now "repeatedly pressuring" Assembly of Experts members to vote for Mojtaba Khamenei, the supreme leader's son — a move that risks contradicting the Islamic Republic's anti-monarchical founding ideology. Trump openly inserted himself into the process, stating: "We're going to have to choose that person along with Iran," and dismissed Khamenei's son as "a lightweight."

Key facts:
- The Assembly of Experts, an 88-member clerical body, is constitutionally responsible for selecting the next Supreme Leader
- IRGC pressure for a dynastic succession represents an unprecedented politicization of the selection process
- The succession fight is unfolding while Iran is under active bombardment and near-total communications blackout
- Washington Post analysis suggests the leadership vacuum has complicated Iran's military command structure

### 5. February jobs report: US economy sheds 92,000 jobs

The US economy lost 92,000 jobs in February — dramatically worse than the consensus expectation of a 60,000 net gain — while unemployment rose to 4.4%. This marks the third monthly contraction in five months, with the US labor market having shed jobs in five of the past nine months and losing a net 19,000 jobs since Trump's major tariff announcements in May 2025.

The report lands amid an accelerating wave of corporate layoffs. Hiring announcements collapsed 55% in a single month, reaching the lowest level since 2010. Wages rose modestly — average hourly earnings increased 0.4% month-over-month and 3.8% year-over-year — but these gains are being eroded by the war-driven energy price spike.

Key facts:
- Deepest sectoral declines: healthcare (down 28,000), leisure and hospitality (down 27,000), construction (down 11,000)
- 2025 saw 1.2 million announced job cuts — highest outside a formal recession since 1989
- Major layoffs: Oracle (30,000), Amazon (30,000 since October), UPS (tens of thousands after a prior 48,000-person reduction), Block (eliminated half its workforce in a single day)
- The Trump administration has eliminated 317,000 federal workers
- Microsoft's AI chief has predicted "most, if not all, professional tasks" will be automated within 18 months; Amazon plans to automate 75% of warehouse operations, putting 600,000 jobs at risk

As Tom Hall argued in today's WSWS Perspective, the war and the economic crisis are not separate events but "expressions of the same class offensive by the capitalist ruling elite."

### 6. Senate defeats war powers resolution on Iran 47-53

The Senate rejected a war powers resolution sponsored by Sen. Tim Kaine that would have constrained Trump's authority to prosecute the Iran war, failing 47-53 in a procedural vote. Sen. Rand Paul was the only Republican to vote with Democrats; Sen. John Fetterman (D-PA) was the lone Democrat to oppose the measure. A parallel House resolution also failed the previous day, 212-219.

Trump launched the war without a congressional declaration of war, invoking the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force — a framework designed for the post-9/11 Afghanistan campaign, now being stretched to cover a full-scale air war against a sovereign state. Republicans argued that withdrawing authorization while Iran was launching missiles at US bases would be "irresponsible."

Key facts:
- The bipartisan refusal to invoke war powers follows the same pattern from the Iraq and Libya wars
- Congressional debate has been confined entirely to questions of military effectiveness, not the legitimacy of the aggression itself
- Polling shows only 38% of Americans approve of the Iran war while 49% disapprove — yet both parties voted to sustain it

### 7. Stand Up for Science rallies in 46 locations nationwide

## Pseudo-left press review

- [Jacobin / DSA](#jacobin--dsa) — channels antiwar sentiment into Democratic Party proceduralism and AIPAC-focused lobbying; avoids class analysis of war's origins in capitalist crisis
- [Left Voice](#left-voice) — takes more radical anti-imperialist positions than Jacobin but still lacks a Trotskyist program for working-class power; notable polemic against Eric Blanc's reformism on military spending
- [PSL / Liberation News](#psl--liberation-news) — Stalinist-oriented war opposition that defends the Iranian regime as anti-imperialist without class analysis
- [Socialist Alternative](#socialist-alternative) — reformist framework channels opposition through abstract "movement" building rather than revolutionary party construction

### Jacobin / DSA

Jacobin's war coverage reveals the fundamental limitations of Democratic Socialist politics when confronted with imperialist war. Four significant pieces published in the past 24 hours:

"US, Israel Target Civilians as Iran Escalates War to Survive" covers civilian casualties but frames the conflict through the lens of Iranian "survival" — as though the war were a conflict between two belligerents rather than an imperialist assault on a semi-colonial nation. The article avoids any class analysis of the war's origins in the crisis of American capitalism.

"Trump's War on Iran Makes a Mockery of American Democracy" channels opposition through concern for democratic procedure — noting that only 21% of the public supports the war — but proposes no path beyond Congressional mechanisms. The implied solution is better Congressional oversight, not independent working-class action.

Most revealing is "The Iran War Shows Why It's Time for Chuck Schumer to Go," which channels genuine antiwar sentiment into an internal Democratic Party leadership fight. The argument that replacing Schumer with a more "progressive" Senate leader would change the trajectory of US imperialism exposes Jacobin's essential function: keeping working-class opposition trapped within the two-party system.

"AIPAC Is Influencing Trump's War in Iran" focuses on the Israel lobby's influence — a framework that implies removing AIPAC's money from politics would alter the war's trajectory, obscuring the fact that the war serves fundamental US imperialist interests regardless of any individual lobby's influence.

**Overall orientation:** Jacobin is performing its characteristic function during wartime — expressing limited opposition while channeling it into safe Democratic Party proceduralism and single-issue lobbying frameworks. The class character of the war and the need for independent working-class action are entirely absent.
